## My Humble Submission

Appayya Dikshitar has authored a work called Nayamyukhamalika, which is a commentary on the Brahma Sutras. This is published in Devanagari script. It was not composed according to his own doctrine. It was largely composed following the Sri Bhashyam. He himself has stated this in a verse: "Lakshmanarya Hrudayanusarini Rachyata Nayamyukhamalika". It is considered novel to write a work on a different doctrine, setting aside one's own. In this manner, we have published this history of Desika's divine life many times in our style, with beautiful words, as it was written by others. However, we have been explaining for a long time in separate books that many concocted stories are included in them. Such books are also widespread. Modern scholars, however, are delighted that their imaginative stories have been validated by considering our written history of Desika, based on Appayya Dikshitar's Nayamyukhamalika, as the supreme authority. This causes no harm to us. We have already refuted such unauthentic stories. Since these refutations are scattered in various books and not in a single volume, people do not examine all of them to understand the truth! It is with this regret, and with the desire that seekers of truth should find clarity on all matters in one book, that this book is being published.

There is no doubt that the matters we have written here are based on evidence, and thus this will bring supreme joy to the authentic scholars.

Thus,

# Vedanta Desika Vaibhavam
Compiled by Sri Kanchi Prativadi Bhayankaram Annangaracharyar.

### Avatharam (Incarnation)
The place of Vedanta Desika's incarnation is the divine abode of Tirutthanka, near the Sannidhi of Vilakkoli Emperuman in Kanchi Kshetra, which is praised as "Among all good cities, Kanchi is unparalleled like the dark-bodied Lord" and "Kanchi is the chief among the seven cities that grant salvation". His father was Ananta Somayajis of Tirutthuparanar, and his mother was Todathtri Ammai. His teacher (maternal uncle) was Kidambi Appullar, who was also Ramanuja Pillan. It is said that he appeared as the divine gem (Tirumani) of Tiruvengadamudaiyan. Swami himself, in the prelude to the Sankalpa Suryodaya Natakam, when speaking about himself, made the statement: "Vidrasithah Vibudavairi Varu Thrinaan Padyasakenaparichara Vidoutprayukta, Utprekshyathe Padajanair Upapatti Bhumna Kanta Hares Samajashta Yadatmaneti"[^3_1]. This verse is relevant here.

### Tirunamakaranam (Naming Ceremony)
Since this Acharya incarnated on the Purattasi Tiruvonam day, which is the Avabhruta Dinam of Tiruvengadamudaiyan's Mahotsavam, he was named Sri Venkatanatha Sarma. The title 'Vedanta Acharyar' was later bestowed upon Swami. [Scholars state that his incarnation was in Kali year 4370, Salivahana Saka 1191, corresponding to 1268 A.D. in Vibhava Samvatsaram.] His lifespan is thus indicated to be approximately 595 years ago, since he lived in this world for a hundred years and then ascended to the divine abode. No difference of opinion or sectarian dispute seems to exist on this. Nor is it appropriate for there to be.

### Early Grace and Education
This Acharya was a recipient of the immense affection of his maternal uncle, Appullar. He was engaged in studying Sri [[P4]]
Bhashyam with his maternal uncle, who brought this child, then about four years old, to the Kalakshepa Goshti (assembly for scriptural study). His maternal uncle, observing the extraordinary brilliance and intellectual prowess of the child, exclaimed, "Indeed, this is the First Lord!" and showered special blessings. This matter is alluded to by the Acharya himself in the beginning of Adhikaranasaravali: "Srimadbhyam Syadasavidhyanu Pati Varadacharya Ramanujabhyam Samyakdrishtena Sarvam Sahita Chitthiya Venkataschend". In due course, after undergoing all the customary samskaras like Chaulopanyanam, he began to study the Vedas. There is a very wonderful verse in the Acharya Champoo regarding his proficiency in Vedic study: "Ahanya Vedankilan Acharyadagrahitasau. Punarucharanakankshi Padanam Veshtane Param"[^4_1]. In the Vedas, there are certain terms called "Veshtana Padas". [For example, "Prajapatiriti Praja - Pati: Bhakateya Niti Bhaka - Teyena; Agnishtomanaam Ityaknishtoma - Naamam"]. These "Veshtana Padas" require repetition and re-recitation. The verse implies that the Swami had to repeat only these "Veshtana Padas," but other terms he grasped with a single recitation, indicating his unparalleled ability to grasp and retain knowledge. In this manner, having mastered all the shastras, including the Vedangas, within twenty nakshatras (days) under his maternal uncle, and having received instruction in special mantras, he became proficient. At the appropriate time, he embraced the Grihasthashrama (householder's life) and began giving discourses on general and special shastras in Sri Kanchi itself.

### An Important Interjection
We must write the history of Swami, who lived six hundred years ago, based on reliable sources. Nowadays, there are four or five historical works written by others. We have explained repeatedly that these are mutually contradictory and lack authentic sources. Seventy years ago, the disciples of the renowned Sri Thirupputkuzhi Appa Swami of Kanchipuram, and Chetlur Narasimhacharyar of Thiruvaheendrapuram, published works with critical observations, making an authentic history of Desika rare. The Acharya Champoo, which we mentioned earlier, is a highly esteemed work. It is famous by the name 'Vedantacharya Vijayam'. Since this is a Sanskrit work, there is no scope for interpolation in it. We studied it in our youth and hold it in high esteem. There is a great scholar and poet of immense literary prowess who composed this work, so much so that he could be compared to Desika. The Champoo works of [[P5]]
Arasanippalai Venkatadhvari are far superior. It does not contain any criticism of other Acharyas. Whatever historical facts are there about Desika are included in it, to that extent. If we extract only the historical portions, setting aside the descriptive and metaphorical parts, which are in accordance with Kavya tradition, we get the true history of Desika to the extent we present it here. Observe how we present it without any exaggeration or distortion:

### Desika's History as found in Acharya Champoo
That Champoo work contains six Stabakas. The first Stabaka comprises: elaborate invocations, the vow to narrate the history of Vedantasuri, the author's lineage, a description of Kanchipuram, the place of Desika's incarnation, and a description of Pundarikaksha Yajva, Desika's paternal grandfather.

The second Stabaka details: the praise of Ananta Suri, the revered son of Pundarikaksha Yajva and Desika's father; his consort becoming pregnant; and the entry of Tirumalai Mal's divine gem into that pregnancy – thus much is narrated. (Hereafter, the history begins with his incarnation).

The third Stabaka describes: 1. Desika's incarnation, 2. his childhood, 3. his reception of blessings from his maternal uncle, Kidambi Appullar, who studied Sri Bhashyam at the feet of Nadathur Ammal, and Desika accompanying him to Ammal's residence to receive her blessings, 4. his speedy completion of Vedic studies and general shastras in due course, 5. his marriage, 6. his japa of Hayagriva mantra, 7. his victory over disputants, 8. his composition of works like Nyaya Siddhantanjanam, 9. his reception of the title 'Kavitaarkika Kesari' – thus much is narrated.

The fourth Stabaka describes: 10. a description of Devaperumal's Vaisakha Utsavam, 11. Desika's composition of Varadaraja Panchasat, when honored on the Garuda Utsavam day during that festival, 12. his defeat of Vidyaranya, an Advaita scholar, who had come for the Vaisakha Utsava Seva and challenged him in a debate, 13. his eagerness to witness Tiruvengadamudaiyan's Utsavam in Purattasi month, whereupon he set out – thus much is narrated.

The fifth Stabaka details: 14. Desika's pilgrimage to Tiruvengadam, 15. his composition of Dayashatakam there, 16. his receipt of an invitation from Vidyaranya to attend the royal court in Vijayanagara, Desika's reply in the form of 'Vairagya-Panchakam', disdaining riches and royal patronage, his pilgrimage to North Indian Kshetras, and his return to Kanchi, and his delivering judgment [[P6]]
in the debates between Vidyaranya and Akshobhya Muni (a Dvaita scholar) - thus much is narrated. 17. Desika’s travel to northern regions for Ganga bath and his return to Kanchi where he stayed for some days. 18. The debate between Vidyaranya and Akshobhya Muni in the court of the Vijayanagara king where the king wished to know whose argument was superior, Desika was asked to mediate, and he sent a verse: "Asina Tatvamasi Na Parajiva Prabetina, Vidyaranya Maharanyam Akshobhya Munirachint". The king, pleased with this, honored Akshobhya Muni. 19. Desika’s desire to visit Sriranganatha, his departure from Kanchi, his stay in Tiruvahindrapuram for a long time, his performance of Hayagriva Upasana, his composition of Hayagriva Stotra, Devanaika Panchasat, Gopala Vimshati, Mahavira Gadya, Achyuta Shataka, Paramatabhangam, Virodhi Pariharam, etc., due to the command of the Lord who manifested to him during Japa. 20. The arrival of some Srivaishnavas from Srirangam, while Desika was in Srimushnam and was about to proceed to Srirangam, and their submission of an invitation letter for a debate, Desika’s hurried return to Srirangam – thus much is narrated.

The sixth Stabaka describes: 21. Swami’s visit to Sriranganatha and his composition of hymns like Bhagavad-Dhyana Sopanam. 22. His victory over Krishnamiśra, a disputant, in an eighteen-day debate, at the request of the Bhagavatas there. 23. His reception of titles like 'Vedantacharya' and 'Sarvatantra Svatantra'. 24. His composition of numerous works like Shatadushani, Adhikaranasaravali, Tatvamuktakalapam, Sarvarthasiddhi, Sesvaramimamsa, Sankalpa Suryodayam, Yadavabhyudayam, Tatparyachandrika, Mimamsapaduka, Tatvadika, Sri Stuti, Bhu Stuti, Yatirajasaptati. 25. His composition of Padukasahasram to subdue the arrogance of a proud poet. 26. His completion of Samasya Sahasram. 27. His composition of Abhitistava for the cessation of the Yavanas' invasion there. 28. His pilgrimage to Tiruvanaimalai. 29. His visit to Divyadesams like Azhwar Tirunagari, Tirunarayanapuram and his return to Kanchipuram, 30. Swami's subduing a magician who, unable to tolerate his titles, challenged him to a snake charming contest by composing Garudadandaka. 31. His son's incarnation. 32. His composition of Rahasyatrayasara – thus much is narrated and the work is concluded. [[P7]]

The above account from the work mentions thirty-two incidents related to Desika's life. Some Srivaishnavas say that they hung his sandals (padarakshai) to touch his head, and that those invited for shraddha betrayed him, and that during Ekadashi, while he was worshipping Perumal in the sannidhi, they offered pongal, and that while he was doing granthakalakshepa in his house, they dragged him by his feet into the street. These and many other such stories written by modern scholars are fabricated to defame others. This not only defames others, but also harms the great honor of Swami Desika, which discerning people will easily understand.

* "Ramo Ramo Rama iti Prajanam Abhavan Kathah" (people's words became "Rama Rama Rama") – just as this verse is quoted, the verse "Vedanta Surir Nigamantasuri Trayyantasuri: Kavitarkikendra:, Ityeva Sarvatra Sadaiva Sarve Janah Samantat Kathayanta Asan" (Vedanta Suri, Nigamantasuri, Trayyantasuri: Kavitarkikendra, everywhere, at all times, all people always said this) should be quoted proudly. That is, instead of proclaiming his greatness to the world, stating that everyone was speaking his praises due to his divine qualities, they propagate that he was disliked by all, including his own followers and opponents. Is there anything more lamentable than these authors of 'Vaibhava Granthas' who, without the slightest knowledge that this causes disrespect to Swami, write such trivialities?

For the Sraddha, those invited would be close relatives and not mere passers-by. Is there any doubt in this? If such close relatives, having agreed, failed to come at the appointed time, what does that imply? These stories are mere pretexts to defame others. Such fabricated stories lead to greater disrespect, not honor. Many people, having heard and repeated such stories for six hundred years, believe them to be true, following the principle of "Durvasatradimata" (the words of Durvasa). No matter how many times the truth is explained, they dismiss it and cling to their ignorance. However, such arguments have become prevalent and are widely used even today. This is what we lament. They fill the works with absurdities, stating that Desika considered those Acharyas whose divine utterances he held on his head and whose works he always followed, as his opponents, and that his main task was to criticize their divine utterances. Discerning people desire to know the true history, free from these absurdities and based on authentic sources.

If some people, motivated by devotion, invent and write various stories to glorify the Acharya with extraordinary divine powers, and then others follow suit and write them, it eventually leads people to believe these fabricated stories as true. ### Desika's Pilgrimage to Tiruvahindrapuram
While these events were taking place, one day, desiring to worship the Southern Divyadesams, Desika, having received the command of Perarulalan, set out. On the bank of the pure Garudanadi, he resolved to stay for some time in the sacred abode of Devanaika and the mouth of Oushadadri. While performing the regular avritti (recitation) of the Vainateya mantra, which he had received from his Acharya earlier, one day, the great Garuda, who manifested with a gracious face, imparted the Hayagriva mantra to him. After that, while he was reciting the Hayagriva Mahamantra for a long time in front of the beautiful Sringara, the white-faced Hayagriva manifested with a gracious face, granted grace by showering his nectar-like words, and disappeared. Then the Acharya, immersed in the ocean of supreme bliss, composed Hayagriva Stotram and Garuda Panchasat, offering them to Devanaika. He also composed Devanaika Panchasat, Achyuta Shatakam (a work in Prakrit language), Gopala Vimsati, and Mahavira Vaibhavam (which is known as Raguvira Gadya).

### Mangalashasanam at Tiruvarur
After that, having received the command of Devanaika Perumal, desiring to worship Ayanar at Tiruvarur, the holy place where Poikai Azhvar, Bhutam Azhvar, and Peyazhvar enjoyed and rejoiced together, as described by "Pavaru Tamizhal Peruperu Panuvalar Pathi Naliravil, Muvaru Nerukki Mozhivillamchetri Mukundanai Tozhuta Nannadu" (the good land where the three, pressing close, adored Mukundan in the middle of the night, with the glorious Tamil songs), he resided there for some time and composed "Dehalisa Stuti" in praise of that Perumal. In verses 6 and 7 of that work, the interaction between the first three Azhvars and Emperuman is beautifully described.

(1) [Deepena Kenachidhasrucha Nishithe Sneho Panibaddya Parisuddha Gunarpitena, Tatravakas Nibidam Tatruchur Bhavantam Swadhyaya Yoga Nayanasucaya: Kavindras:] The meaning is that the great poets, who are chief among yogis, saw Ayanar in their hearts, having lit a unique lamp in the middle of the night. [[P10]]
(2) [Kasarapurva Kavimukhyavimarda Janma Punyatate kshepa bhagavaraso Bahutuste, Tvadpada Padmaduti Tvadananyabhogye Nutanam Samashrayati Nutanam Sarkaratvam] The meaning is that the juice produced by the pressing of Poikai Azhvar and other three Azhvars on the bank of the Pennai river, which flows like sugarcane, is mixed with sugar in the nectar flowing from his lotus feet, according to the Shruti "Vishno: Pade Parame Madva Utsa:". These two precious verses are to be meditated upon daily by devotees.

### Return to Kanchipuram
In this manner, having resided in Tiruvarur for some time, and having given discourses on Vedanta works to the people there, he later returned to Kanchipuram, his birthplace, in the course of his travels. While giving special discourses there, he composed Sri Stuti in praise of Perundevi Tayar, Varadaraja Panchasat in praise of Perarulalan, Vegasetu Stotram in praise of Yathotthakari Emperuman, Ashtabhujashtakam in praise of Attapuyakkarathu Emperuman, Saranagati Dipika (also known as Dipaprakasha Stotram) in praise of Vilakkoliyemperuman, Kamasik Ashtakam in praise of Velukkai Emperuman, and Paramarthastuti in praise of Tirupputkuzhi Emperuman.

### The Special Circumstances of the Composition of Vairagya Panchakam
"Vishnukantavathara Butarana Nam Vedantasikah Sarvatobhagam Gnanam Padayathi Eva Viraktim Lokavilakshanm Praptavan" (our Vedanta Desika, an incarnation of Vishnu, possessed all-encompassing knowledge as well as detachment, which was extraordinary). Therefore, like Koorathazhvan, he lived by begging for alms and performed the worship of Perumal without any deficiency. He was residing in Kanchipuram, the abode of Perarulalan, who is dark-hued, and was performing Mangalasasanam. At that time, a person named Vidyaranya, who had great influence with the Vijayanagara king and had the means to support many scholars, remembered our Sri Desika and, intending to alleviate his poverty and bestow wealth, sent an invitation letter to him to come to the Vijayanagara king. Swami Desika, realizing that Vidyaranya, though aware of his steadfastness, was inviting him to such a futile venture, felt disappointed and in response, [[P11]]
composed the verse: "Na Mebhitarjitam Kinchit Na Maya Kinchidarjitam! Asti Me Hastisailagre Vastv Paitamaham Danam!"[^11_1] and sent it. Vidyaranya, seeing this, and driven by the desire to somehow bestow immense wealth upon Desika due to his great affection for him, again sent a persistent invitation [through the king, with great respect]. It was then that the verses of Vairagya Panchakam, received as grace by Swami, became famous.

### An Amazing Act of Swami
One day, a Mayavadi Sannyasi came to Kanchi and debated with Swami. Being defeated, he was vexed and intended to cause some trouble to Swami. Using his knowledge of black magic, he caused the water in a pond near Swami's house to enter Swami's stomach, making it swell. Swami, realizing this magical act, scratched the pillar at his house entrance with his nail, and water flowed from it, and his stomach returned to normal. Seeing this, the Mayavadi was greatly astonished by Swami's extraordinary power and bowed at his feet before leaving.

### The Greatness of Sri Stuti, also known as Kanakadhara Stava
One day, a Brahmachari came to Swami's presence, bowed down, and prayed: "I am very poor; I wish to get married, Swami, please help me with wealth." Swami smiled and asked, "My boy! You know that I live by begging for alms and performing Perumal's worship; why do you come to me asking for money instead of going to rich people?" The Brahmachari replied, "I have heard from many elders that if Swami desires something, nothing is unattainable, and wealth can be obtained." Hearing this, Swami said, "Is that so? If the Bhagavatas bless, wealth will surely be obtained; do not worry from now on; recite Sri Stuti and circumambulate Perundevi Tayar; grace will be bestowed." Saying this, he taught him the Sri Stuti, a hymn he had already composed in praise of Perundevi Tayar. The Brahmachari recited that hymn throughout the day. The next morning, when Swami had finished his daily rituals and was proceeding to Perarulalan's Sannidhi for Mangalasasanam, the Brahmachari followed him, reciting Sri Stuti in a melodious voice, and circumambulated Perundevi Tayar. ### The Incarnation of His Son
In the Nala Samvatsaram, in the month of Avani, on the Rohini Nakshatra, a son was born to Swami, who was equal to him in all respects. He was named Varadachariyar. He too, having undergone the Upanayanam and other samskaras at the appropriate time, mastered all the shastras in Swami's presence and shone like a lamp lit in an assembly. He came to be known as Nayanaracharyar, Nayanavaradacharyar, and Kumara Varadacharyar.

### Swami's Titles
Swami Desika received many titles like 'Vedantacharya', 'Kavitarkika Kesari', 'Sarvatantra Svatantra', and 'Samasya Sahasri'. Among these, the title 'Vedantacharya' was bestowed by Lord Ranganatha himself, as revealed in the opening verses of Swami Desika's 'Sankalpa Suryodaya'. The words 'Deva' used at the end of 'Dayashatakam' and 'Hari' at the end of 'Stotra Bhashyam' refer to Lord Ranganatha alone. Other titles like 'Sarvatantra Svatantra' (master of all arts and crafts) were bestowed upon Sri Desika by learned men, as stated by Sri Desika himself in 'Sankalpa Suryodaya' and 'Stotra Bhashyam'. Some say the title 'Sarvatantra Svatantra' was given to him by Sriranganachiyar, the divine consort of Lord Ranganatha. It is understood that in learned assemblies, Sri Desika was acclaimed as 'Samasya Sahasri'. 'Samasya' means the incomplete part of a verse, and 'Samasya Purana' is the feat of completing that verse in the assembly of poets, when only the first, middle, or last line is mentioned. This great poetic feat was performed by Sri Desika with unrivaled ease and excellence, which earned him the distinction of 'Samasya Sahasri'. Sri Desika himself mentions this in the concluding verse of his 'Stotra Bhashyam': "Aganhi Sadasi Sadbhih Yassamasya Sahasri Kavikathakam Mrigendras Sarvatantra Svatantrah" (அகணி ஸதஸி ஸத்பி: யஸ் ஸமஸ்யா ஸஹஸ்ர கவிகதகமிருகேந்த்ர: ஸர்வதந்த்ர ஸ்வதந்த்ர:)[^12_2], enumerating some of the titles bestowed on him, and 'Samasya Sahasri' is one of them. Yet, in one of the books written by no less a person than Panditabhushanam Chetlur Narasimhacharyar Swami, it has been stated that Sri Desika had written a precious book called 'Samasya Sahasri', which is the eightieth in the order of the works composed by him! It is indeed difficult to comprehend how a scholar of his eminence could mistake Sri Desika's title for a book written by him. Unfortunately, we have no direct evidence in the form of the verses which were completed by Sri Desika and which got him this title. This is not so bad if we consider the numerous anecdotes spun around Sri Desika's title 'Sarvatantra Svatantra', all aimed at showing that Sri Desika was not merely harassed by the Thenkalai Srivaishnavas, who were his sworn enemies, but also by all sections of the public, high and low, masons, goldsmiths, shoemakers, snake-charmers and so on, each one challenging Swami's right to the title 'Sarvatantra Svatantra'. Is it not preposterous to imagine for a moment that the meanest artisans in the country could have had access to one of Desika's eminence and stature, day in and day out, and drawn him out for a challenge, as if the title in question [[P13]]

was conferred by good men of learning to denote mastery over every known art like shoemaking, masonry, snake-charming and what not? And on Desika's own part, was it necessary for him to secure earthly fame and approbation by taking up the challenge every time and proving his all-worthiness? An emphatic 'No' is the reply and, in fact, Sri Desika, who shunned all worldly things including name, fame and wealth (Eashanatraiyamuktha), would have been in no mood to take the gauntlet from all and sundries and establish his mastery over them all. Sri Desika could have composed 'Garuda-Panchasat' like many other Stotras in the ordinary course of events. The interpolation of the episode of the snake-charmer is just a piece of dramatization of the event, harmless though, playing on the credulity of the public to whom miracles have a special appeal, inspiring them with awe and reverence. This incident finds mention in the 'Acharya Champu' also and if only the erudite and enlightened author of the Champu had been aware of the other anecdotes, he would have certainly mentioned them with all the poetic embellishment he was capable of. Surely, he would not have been aware of the umpteen anecdotes, not related to the actual life of Sri Vedanta Desika, but constituting the figments of imagination of the writers of the later days. Even so, it may be noted that while the Champu and other 'Vaibhava-Granthas' of the later writers, have described the venue of the snake-charmer's episode as 'Kanchi', the Pandita-Bhushanam has stated that 'Garuda-dandaka and Garuda-panchasat' were composed at Ahindrapuram. ### Important Research Refuting the Arguments of the Advocate
There was no animosity or sectarian difference among the Acharyas during Desika's time or even one or two hundred years later. There was only some difference of opinion on a few matters among some Acharyas. Even today, we see that among common traditionalists, there are differences in opinion and practice regarding certain matters like Upakarma, Jayanti, Krittika Deepam. It is revealed through research that even during the time of Emperumanar, there were some differences of opinion among those who directly took refuge at his feet. Desika himself has indicated this in 'Rahasyatrayasara' in the verse: "Ittham Arthavisheshepi Yojanabhedamatrataha, Pracham Vivadas Samvrittho Bhashyakarai: Avaritaha."[^22_1] (Thus, even in this matter, there is only a difference in interpretation; the disputes of the ancient ones were covered up by the Bhashyakara.)

It is evident that Somasyandana, who served Emperumanar's feet, also had some differences of opinion with Acharyas like Azhvan, Andan Pillan, and Kidambi Achchan, without having a complete unity of thought.
