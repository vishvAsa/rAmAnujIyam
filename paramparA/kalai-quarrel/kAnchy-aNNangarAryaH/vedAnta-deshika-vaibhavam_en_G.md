## My Humble Submission
Appayya Dīkṣitar has composed a work called Nayamaayūkhamālikā as a commentary on the Brahma Sūtras. This is published in Devanagari script. It was not composed according to his own doctrine. It was largely composed following the Śrībhāṣya. He himself stated this in a verse: "Lakṣmaṇārya Hṛdayānusāriṇī Racya Nayamaayūkhamālikā". Composing a text according to a different doctrine, abandoning one's own, is considered a unique feat. In this manner, we have often published the divine history of Desika, written with beautiful words, in our style. However, we have long been pointing out in individual books that these contain many fabricated stories. Such books are also widely spread. Modern scholars, on the other hand, rejoice, believing that their imaginative stories have been validated by our written history of Desika, which is based on Appayya Dīkṣitar's Nayamaayūkhamālikā tradition. There is no harm to us in this. We have already refuted unauthentic stories. Since these refutations are scattered in various books, people do not examine them all and understand the truth! Due to this sorrow, and with the desire that seekers of truth should find clarity on all matters in a single book, this book is being published.
Since all the matters we write about here are supported by evidence, there is no doubt among the authentic ones that this will bring supreme joy.
Thus,
P. B. Aṇṇangarāchāryar.

## Life story
### Incarnation
The place where Vedānta Deśika incarnated is the divine abode known as Tiruttaṇkā, located in Kāñcī Kṣetra, which is praised as "among all auspicious cities like Kāśī, there is none comparable to Kāñcī where the dark-hued Lord resides" and "Kāñcī, the foremost among the seven cities that grant salvation". He was born in the vicinity of the illuminated Lord of Tiruttaṇkā. His father was Ananta Somayājī Tirusuppanār, and his mother was Tōtādriyammā. His teacher (maternal uncle) was Kiḍāmbi Appuḷḷār, who was also Rāmānuja Piḷḷāṇ. They say that he was the very gem of Tiruvēṅkaṭamuḍaiyān (Lord of Tiruvengadam) who appeared as this teacher. The Lord Himself, in the prologue to His drama Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, states about Himself: "Vītrāsī Vibudhāri Varga Dr̥ṇām Padmāsano Paricāra Vidhau Prayuktā, Utprekṣyate Padalaya Rupatti Bhūmkā Kaṇḍā Hareḥ Sama Jriṣṭhiḥ Yadātmaneti", a verse that can be recalled here.

### Naming Ceremony
Since this teacher incarnated on Purattāci Tiruvōṇam, the day of Tiruvēṅkaṭamuḍaiyān's grand festival, he was named Śrī Vēṅkaṭanātha Śarmā. The title Vedāntāchārya was later bestowed upon him by the Lord. [It is stated by elders that his incarnation occurred in Kali Yuga 4370, Śālivāhana Saka 1191, i.e., 1268 CE, in the Vibhava year.] Since he remained in this world for a hundred years and then ascended to the divine abode, his period of existence is roughly 595 years ago from now. There appears to be no difference in opinion or doctrine regarding this.
### Early Blessings and Education
This teacher was greatly loved by his maternal uncle, Appuḷḷār. At the abode of Natādūrammā, Śrī [[P4]]

The teacher, who was engaged in the Śrībhāṣya Kalakṣepam, once took this four-year-old child to the assembly for Kalakṣepam. His mother, observing the child's extraordinary brilliance and radiant presence, exclaimed, "Indeed, this is the foremost one!" This fact is mentioned by the teacher himself in the beginning of his Adhikaraṇa Sārāvaḷi: "Śrīmadbhyām Syād-asāv-idyanu-pati Varadāchārya Rāmānujābhyām Saṁyag-dṛṣṭena Sarvaṁ-saha Nicitathiyā Veṅkaṭeceṅ". At the appropriate time, after undergoing all the necessary sacraments like Caulōpāyana, he began studying the Vedas. A verse in Āchārya Caṁpū beautifully describes his proficiency in Vedic study: "Aṅgāya Vedāṅgān Akhilān Āchāryād Agr̥hīta Sau. Punar-uccāraṇāgaṅgal Padānām Veṣṭane Pām". There are certain terms in the Vedas called Veṣṭana Padams. For example, "Prajāpatir-iti Prajā - Patiḥ Bhāgadeye Neti Bhāga - Devena; Agniṣṭoma Sāṁ Mithy-Agniṣṭoma Sāmam". Since these terms need to be recited repeatedly, it is stated that the teacher had to repeat only these Veṣṭana Padams, while other terms were grasped by him with a single recitation, indicating his unparalleled ability to grasp and retain knowledge. In this manner, he mastered all the Śāstras, including the Vedāngas, within twenty days under his maternal uncle. After receiving initiation into sacred mantras and becoming proficient, at the appropriate time, he entered the Gṛhastha Āśrama, performed Āhitāgni, and began teaching both general and special Śāstras in Kāñcī itself.
### An Important Interjection
We must write the history of the Swāmi, who lived six hundred years ago, based on reliable sources. There are five historical texts written by contemporary authors. These are contradictory to each other and are not based on authentic sources. Seventy years ago, the disciples of the universally renowned Śrīmat Tirupuṭkuli Appā Swāmi who lived in Kāñcīpuram, and Śēṭlūr Ramanujam Āchāryar of Tiruvayindrapuram, published rebuttals with specific reasons, so reliable historical texts of Deśika are rare. The Āchārya Caṁpū, which we have mentioned before, is a very elevated work. This is known as Vedāntācārya Vijayam. Since this is a Sanskrit work, there is no scope for interpolations. We had studied this in our youth. We have great reverence for it. A great scholar and poet, endowed with such eloquent prowess that he could challenge Deśika, composed that work.

### Deśika's pilgrimage to Tiruvahīndrapuram
As these events were unfolding, one day, desiring to perform Maṅgalāśāsanam in the southern divine abodes, he departed with the Lord's command. When he reached the bank of the sacred Garuḍa River and desired to reside there for some time, contemplating the divinity of the Lord and the sanctity of Oṣadhādri, he began to regularly recite the Vainateya Mantra which he had received from his teacher in Tirumala. One day, the greatly benevolent Lord Hayagrīva appeared and instructed him on the Hayagrīva Mahāmantra. Thereafter, he remained in front of the beautiful Śrīsiṁha, reciting the Hayagrīva Mahāmantra for a long time. Then, the white-faced Lord appeared, blessed him with the nectar of His words, and disappeared. The teacher, immersed in supreme bliss, then composed and offered two stotras, Hayagrīva Stotra and Garuḍa Pañcāśat, and worshipped the Divine Lord. He then composed and offered stotras like Devānāyaka Pañcāśat, Acyuta Śatakam (a prākr̥ta-language stotra), Gopāla Viṁśati, and Mahāvīra Vaibhavam (known as Raghuviira Gadyam).

### Return to Kāñcī
Thus, after residing for some time in Tirukkovalur and discoursing on Vedāntic texts for the residents there, he returned to the Perumāḷ Temple, his birthplace, and performed discourses on various texts. During this time, he composed the Śrīstuti on Perundēvī Tāyār, the Varadarāja Pañcāśat on Perarūḷāḷan, the Vēgasetu Stotra on Yadōttakāri Emperumāṇ, the Aṣṭabhujāṣṭaka on Aṭṭapuṟakkarattu Emperumāṇ, the Saraṇāgati Dīpikā, also known as Dīprapakāśa Stotra, on Viḷakkōḷiyemperumāṇ, and the Kāmāsikāṣṭaka on Vēḷukkai Emperumāṇ, and the Paramārthastuti on Tirupuṭkuli Pōrēṭru Perumāṇ.
### The Origin of Vairāgya Pañcakam
Our Vedānta Deśika, born as an incarnation of Viṣṇu, was endowed with all-encompassing knowledge, and was also uniquely detached from worldly matters, just like Kūrattāḻvān. Therefore, he resided in Kāñcī, where the dark-hued Lord dwells, living by alms alone, performing the worship of Perumāḷ perfectly, and discoursing on both Tamil and Sanskrit Vedāntic texts to scholars of both languages. At that time, a person named Vidhyāraṇya, who enjoyed great favor with the Vijayanagara Emperor and had the means to support many scholars, intended to alleviate our Śrī Deśika Śikāmaṇi's poverty and bestow wealth upon him. He sent a letter to that Vijayanagara king, requesting him to come. When Swāmi Deśika saw this, he wondered, "This Vidhyāraṇya knows my spiritual state, yet he invites me in vain!"
He sent back a response in the form of a verse to him to emphasize his steadfast detachment.

### One of Swāmi's Miraculous Feats
One day, an Advaita Sannyāsī came to Kāñcī and, being defeated in a debate with the Swāmi, decided to cause him some trouble. Using his subtle magical powers, this sorcerer made the Swāmi's stomach swell by making him drink water from a pond near the Swāmi's residence. The Swāmi, aware of this magical act, drew a line on a pillar at the entrance of his residence with his fingernail, and water began to flow from it, restoring the Swāmi's stomach to its normal state. Witnessing this, the sorcerer was greatly astonished by the Swāmi's powers and prostrated before him before departing.
### The Greatness of Śrīstuti, known as Kanakadhārāstūavam
One day, a Brahmachari came to the Swāmi's presence, prostrated, and prayed, "I am very poor; I wish to marry, Swāmi, please help me with wealth." The Swāmi smiled and asked, "Child! You know that I live by alms and perform the worship of Perumāḷ. Why then do you come to me asking for money, instead of approaching wealthy people?" The Brahmachari replied, "I have heard many elders say that if the Swāmi wills it, nothing is unattainable, and wealth can be obtained. That is why I prayed to you." Hearing this, the Swāmi said, "Is that so? If devotees say so, wealth will certainly be obtained; do not worry anymore; recite Śrīstuti and circumambulate Perundēvī Tāyār; you will be blessed." He then taught him the Śrīstuti, a prayer he had composed earlier on Perundēvī Tāyār. That Brahmachari spent the whole day reciting that stuti. The next morning, when the Swāmi, after completing his daily rites, went to the presence of Perarūḷāḷan for Maṅgalāśāsanam, the Brahmachari followed him, reciting Śrīstuti melodiously, and circumambulated Perundēvī Tāyār.

### Birth of a Son
A son, equal to the Swāmi in all respects, was born in the Naḷa year, in the month of Āvaṇi, on Rōhiṇī Nakṣatra. He was named Varadāchāryar. He too, at the appropriate time, underwent the Upanyana and other sacraments, and mastered all Śāstras in the Swāmi's presence, shining brightly like a lamp. He was known as Nayanārāchāryar, Nayanavaradāchāryar, and Kumāravaradāchāryar.
### Swāmi's Acquisition of Titles
Swāmi Deśika acquired various titles such as Vedāntāchārya, Kavitārkika Keśarī, Sarvatantra Svatantra, and Samasyāsahasrī. Among these, the title Vedāntāchārya was bestowed upon him by Śrī Raṅganātha, as is evident from his divine works; for instance, in the prologue to Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, he states "[Śrī Raṅgarāja Divyājñā labdha Vedāntāchārya padaḥ:]", and in the beginning of Adhikaraṇa Sārāvaḷi, he mentions "[Svasti Śrī Raṅgabhartuḥ:]" in the verse "[Detha Devetha Dattāṁ Vedāntāchārya Saṁjñām]". It is to be understood that the Lord of Śrī Raṅgam is implicitly referred to by the terms Hari and Deva in the verse "[Svakam-iti Haritattvam Yāmuna Stōtra Vṛttyā Vyākūṇuta Nigamāntāchāryakaṁ Veṅkaṭeśa]" at the end of Dayāśatakam, and in the verse "[Vedāntāchārya pade Viniveśya Bālaṁ Devo Dayāśatakam Etad Avātayān Mām]" at the end of Dayāśatakam. The identity of the subject (Dharmi Aikya) is to be understood. The other titles like Sarvatantra Svatantra were conferred by scholars. This is also evident from Śrī Deśika's divine works; for example, in Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, he states "[Kavitārkika Siṁha iti Prakhyāta Guṇasamākhyaḥ:]", and in the conclusion of the Stotra Bhāṣya, he mentions "[Agaṇī Satali Satbhir Yas Samasyā Sahasrī Kavīta Katamr̥keśvaraḥ Sarvatantra Svatantraḥ:]" in the Śrī Sūktis. ### Swāmi's Poetic Prowess
Even though there were many great poets in the world who were highly skilled in composing, the Swāmi's poetic prowess was exceptionally unique. The Swāmi was unparalleled in all four types of poetry: 1. Āśu Kavi (extempore poet), 2. Madhura Kavi (sweet poet), 3. Chitra Kavi (चित्रकवि - चित्र-कविता), and 4. Vistāra Kavi (extensive poet). It is not that he composed verses by painstakingly stringing together a few words, as a "Haṭādākṛṣṭānāṁ Katipayapadānām Racayitā" would. His ability to compose many verses in a moment, all filled with meaning and aesthetic pleasure, and to arrange verses in various patterns like Padma Bandha, Chakra Bandha, Muraja Bandha, etc., at the mere thought of it, and the astonishing fact that these were countless, leads one to conclude that he was an incarnation of the Goddess of Poetry (Kavitāvatāra Bhūmi).

### Review of Others' Writings
We have cited verbatim the history of Deśika as described in the Āchārya Champu, also known as Vedāntāchārya Vijayam [pages 5, 6 below]. Since that book alone is authentic and ancient, no one should write histories that are not mentioned in it. Due to certain specific reasons, those who developed a new relationship with Deśika have completely ignored that Āchārya Champu and published several fabricated notes, adding contradictory and unmentioned details from it, calling them Deśika's history and Deśika Vaibhavam. We have discussed this repeatedly below.

One of the lawyers who wrote this, or rather, the leader, attempting to explain the reason for Deśika's incarnation, wrote, "Other Āchāryas wrote misrepresentations about the Goddess (Prāṭṭi) and diminished her greatness," and "Deśika incarnated and refuted all those misrepresentations, establishing the Goddess's greatness wonderfully." This is also the prevailing discourse in his circle. What is the true greatness of the Goddess? How did Deśika clarify it? What is the difference between the views of other Āchāryas and Deśika on this matter? It is truly regrettable that people, without understanding or researching these matters, have become confused and are confusing others. Is it so difficult to understand Deśika's doctrines? His divine works have not been lost. What he clearly stated is briefly elucidated here.
Deśika did not at all delve into establishing the Lord's being the cause of the universe, the bestower of liberation, His omnipresence, etc., as exclusively belonging to the Goddess. We will explain this clearly and simply. The Mahākāvya called Yādavābhyudaya was composed by Swāmi Deśika; its twelfth canto deals with Rukmiṇī, the Goddess. In its 1st and 2nd verses, it is stated that Śrī Mahālakṣmī herself incarnated as Rukmiṇī. The very next verse states:
"Śikhaṇḍakaṁ Niṣpratimaṁ Śr̥tīnāṁ Śṛṅgāra Līlopama Viśvakṛtyaṁ, Adhīyate Tannmitunaṁ Svabhāvād Anyōnya Jīvātu Madhanya Bhōgyam". The following verses exclusively describe Rukmiṇī. The first two verses are also of the same nature. The third verse, however, mentions "mitunaṁ" (couple). The commentary on "Śṛṅgāra Līlopama Viśvakṛtyaṁ" states, "The creation, sustenance, and dissolution of the universe, which is like a playful act of romantic dalliance, easily achieved." Deśika used the term "mitunaṁ" in this verse, thus making it unique, because he did not wish to attribute these acts (creation, sustenance, etc.) solely to the Goddess. If one keenly observes that he included both (the Lord and the Goddess) in this one verse, while in the preceding and succeeding verses he composed only about the Goddess, it becomes clear that Deśika held that the Goddess has no separate role in the creation and sustenance of the universe.
In Śrīstuti, the verse "*Yatsaṅkalpāt Bhavati Kamalē Yatra Dehitvam-īṣāṁ Janma Sthema Pralaya Racatā Jaṅgamājaṅgamānām; Tat Kalyāṇaṁ Kimapi Yaminaṭam Ekalakṣyam Samarat-au Pūrṇaṁ Tejaḥ Sphurati Bhavatīpādalākṣārasāṅgam*" is sufficient to understand Deśika's true view on the Goddess.

### Some Important Research Refuting the Arguments of D.R.

Such differences of opinion existed and grew in our tradition even during Emperumāṇār's time. In this way, it can be assumed that Deśika also had some differences of opinion. Therefore, there is no room to speak of doctrinal differences. It cannot be said that the few differences of opinion that Deśika held persisted until the end. Nor is there any harm in assuming that they did. Vādi [[P23]]
