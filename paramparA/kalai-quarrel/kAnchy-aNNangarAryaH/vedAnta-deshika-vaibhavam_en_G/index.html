My Humble Submission

Appayya Dīkṣitar has composed a work called Nayamaayūkhamālikā as a commentary on the Brahma Sūtras. This is published in Devanagari script. It was not composed according to his own doctrine. It was largely composed following the Śrībhāṣya. He himself stated this in a verse: “Lakṣmaṇārya Hṛdayānusāriṇī Racya Nayamaayūkhamālikā”. Composing a text according to a different doctrine, abandoning one’s own, is considered a unique feat. In this manner, we have often published the divine history of Desika, written with beautiful words, in our style. However, we have long been pointing out in individual books that these contain many fabricated stories. Such books are also widely spread. Modern scholars, on the other hand, rejoice, believing that their imaginative stories have been validated by our written history of Desika, which is based on Appayya Dīkṣitar’s Nayamaayūkhamālikā tradition. There is no harm to us in this. We have already refuted unauthentic stories. Since these refutations are scattered in various books, people do not examine them all and understand the truth! Due to this sorrow, and with the desire that seekers of truth should find clarity on all matters in a single book, this book is being published. Since all the matters we write about here are supported by evidence, there is no doubt among the authentic ones that this will bring supreme joy. Thus, P. B. Aṇṇangarāchāryar.

Life story

Incarnation

The place where Vedānta Deśika incarnated is the divine abode known as Tiruttaṇkā, located in Kāñcī Kṣetra, which is praised as “among all auspicious cities like Kāśī, there is none comparable to Kāñcī where the dark-hued Lord resides” and “Kāñcī, the foremost among the seven cities that grant salvation”. He was born in the vicinity of the illuminated Lord of Tiruttaṇkā. His father was Ananta Somayājī Tirusuppanār, and his mother was Tōtādriyammā. His teacher (maternal uncle) was Kiḍāmbi Appuḷḷār, who was also Rāmānuja Piḷḷāṇ. They say that he was the very gem of Tiruvēṅkaṭamuḍaiyān (Lord of Tiruvengadam) who appeared as this teacher. The Lord Himself, in the prologue to His drama Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, states about Himself: “Vītrāsī Vibudhāri Varga Dr̥ṇām Padmāsano Paricāra Vidhau Prayuktā, Utprekṣyate Padalaya Rupatti Bhūmkā Kaṇḍā Hareḥ Sama Jriṣṭhiḥ Yadātmaneti”, a verse that can be recalled here.

Naming Ceremony

Since this teacher incarnated on Purattāci Tiruvōṇam, the day of Tiruvēṅkaṭamuḍaiyān’s grand festival, he was named Śrī Vēṅkaṭanātha Śarmā. The title Vedāntāchārya was later bestowed upon him by the Lord. [It is stated by elders that his incarnation occurred in Kali Yuga 4370, Śālivāhana Saka 1191, i.e., 1268 CE, in the Vibhava year.] Since he remained in this world for a hundred years and then ascended to the divine abode, his period of existence is roughly 595 years ago from now. There appears to be no difference in opinion or doctrine regarding this.

Early Blessings and Education

This teacher was greatly loved by his maternal uncle, Appuḷḷār. At the abode of Natādūrammā, Śrī [[P4]]

The teacher, who was engaged in the Śrībhāṣya Kalakṣepam, once took this four-year-old child to the assembly for Kalakṣepam. His mother, observing the child’s extraordinary brilliance and radiant presence, exclaimed, “Indeed, this is the foremost one!” This fact is mentioned by the teacher himself in the beginning of his Adhikaraṇa Sārāvaḷi: “Śrīmadbhyām Syād-asāv-idyanu-pati Varadāchārya Rāmānujābhyām Saṁyag-dṛṣṭena Sarvaṁ-saha Nicitathiyā Veṅkaṭeceṅ”. At the appropriate time, after undergoing all the necessary sacraments like Caulōpāyana, he began studying the Vedas. A verse in Āchārya Caṁpū beautifully describes his proficiency in Vedic study: “Aṅgāya Vedāṅgān Akhilān Āchāryād Agr̥hīta Sau. Punar-uccāraṇāgaṅgal Padānām Veṣṭane Pām”. There are certain terms in the Vedas called Veṣṭana Padams. For example, “Prajāpatir-iti Prajā - Patiḥ Bhāgadeye Neti Bhāga - Devena; Agniṣṭoma Sāṁ Mithy-Agniṣṭoma Sāmam”. Since these terms need to be recited repeatedly, it is stated that the teacher had to repeat only these Veṣṭana Padams, while other terms were grasped by him with a single recitation, indicating his unparalleled ability to grasp and retain knowledge. In this manner, he mastered all the Śāstras, including the Vedāngas, within twenty days under his maternal uncle. After receiving initiation into sacred mantras and becoming proficient, at the appropriate time, he entered the Gṛhastha Āśrama, performed Āhitāgni, and began teaching both general and special Śāstras in Kāñcī itself.

An Important Interjection

We must write the history of the Swāmi, who lived six hundred years ago, based on reliable sources. There are five historical texts written by contemporary authors. These are contradictory to each other and are not based on authentic sources. Seventy years ago, the disciples of the universally renowned Śrīmat Tirupuṭkuli Appā Swāmi who lived in Kāñcīpuram, and Śēṭlūr Ramanujam Āchāryar of Tiruvayindrapuram, published rebuttals with specific reasons, so reliable historical texts of Deśika are rare. The Āchārya Caṁpū, which we have mentioned before, is a very elevated work. This is known as Vedāntācārya Vijayam. Since this is a Sanskrit work, there is no scope for interpolations. We had studied this in our youth. We have great reverence for it. A great scholar and poet, endowed with such eloquent prowess that he could challenge Deśika, composed that work.

The Caṁpū work of [[P5]] Vedānta Deśika Vaibhavam in Acharya Champu

6 far superior to the Arasanipālai Vengatadvari’s work. It does not contain any criticism of other Āchāryas, and it records only as much of Deśika’s history as is authentic. If we extract only the historical parts, omitting the descriptive and imaginative sections composed in a poetic style, we arrive at the true history of Deśika as we present it here. Observe how we present it without any additions or deletions:

Deśika’s History as Found in Acharya Champu

That Champu work contains six sections (stabakas). The first section includes an elaborate auspicious invocation, the resolve to narrate the history of Vedānta Sūri, the author’s family lineage, a description of Kāñcīpuram, Deśika’s birthplace, and a description of Puṇḍarīkākṣa Yājvā, Deśika’s paternal grandfather. The second section describes the glory of Ananta Sūri, the son of Puṇḍarīkākṣa Yājvā and Deśika’s father, his wife’s pregnancy, and how Śrīmal Tirumaṇi (the jewel from Tirumala) entered that womb – that’s all that’s described. (From now on, the history begins with his incarnation.) The third section describes: 1. Deśika’s incarnation, 2. Childhood, 3. His study of Śrībhāṣya under Natādūrammā, and how Deśika, accompanied by his maternal uncle Kiḍāmbi Appuḷḷār, went to Ammāḷ’s residence and received her blessings, 4. His speedy completion of Vedic and general śāstra studies at the appropriate time, 5. His marriage, 6. His chanting of the Hayagrīva Mantra, 7. His victory over opponents, 8. His composition of several works like Nyāya Siddhāñjanam, 9. His acquisition of the title Kavitārkika Siṁha – that’s all that’s described. The fourth section describes: 10. A description of Devapperumāḷ’s Vaikhānosatsava, 11. Deśika being honored during that festival on Garuḍotsava day and composing Varadarāja Pañcāśat Stōtram; 12. Vidhyāraṇya, who came for the Vaikhānosatsava service, being unable to bear Deśika’s title Kavitārkika Siṁha, challenged him to a debate, and Deśika defeated that ascetic with fierce arguments. 13. His eagerness to worship Tiruvēṅkaṭamuḍaiyān during the Purattāci month’s festival – these are described. The fifth section describes: 14. Deśika’s pilgrimage to Tiruvengadam. 15. His composition of Dayāśatakam at Tirumala. 16. Vidhyāraṇya, who had been defeated in Kāñcīpuram, joined the king of Vijayanagara and, exaggerating Deśika’s incomparable prowess, [[P6]] 6 Vedānta Deśika Vaibhavam in Acharya Champu summoned him there and honored him greatly, and after obtaining the king’s permission, sent a message to Deśika, who resided on Tiruvēṅkaṭa Hill, who then composed and sent the Vairāgya Pañcakam, thereby demonstrating his unparalleled detachment. 17. He then journeyed north, performed a pilgrimage to the Gaṅgā River, and returned to Kāñcīpuram, residing there for some days. 18. During a debate between Vidhyāraṇya and Akṣōbhya Muni in the Vijayanagara king’s court on Dvaita-Advaita philosophy, the king wished to know whose arguments were superior, so he had both their arguments written down and sent them to Deśika, requesting his impartial opinion. Deśika then composed a verse: “Asinā Tattvam-asinā Para-jīva-prabhedinā, Vidhyāraṇya Mahāraṇyam Akṣōbhya Munir-acidyat”, and the king, receiving it, praised Akṣōbhya Muni greatly. 19. Desika, desiring to worship Śrī Raṅganātha, departed from Kāñcī and traveled to Tiruvahīndrapuram, where he resided for a long time. While performing the Hayagrīva Mantra recitation with discipline in the Ōṣadhādri, during one recitation, the perfectly benevolent Lord Hayagrīva appeared and instructed him to compose the Hayagrīva Stōtram. He then composed Hayagrīva Stōtram, Garuḍa Pañcāśat, Mahāvīra Gadyam, Acyuta Śatakam, Paramata Bhaṅgam, Virōdha Parihāra and other works, and offered them to the Divine Lord and His Consort. 20. Later, when he proceeded to Śrīmuṣṇam and from there to Śrīraṅgam, some Śrīvaiṣṇavas from Śrīraṅgam came and presented him with an invitation letter for a debate, so he immediately went to the temple. The sixth section describes: 21. Swāmi worshipped Śrī Raṅganātha and composed prayers like Bhagavad Dhyāna Sōpānam, 22. At the request of the devotees there, he debated with a wicked debater named Kṛṣṇa Miśra for eighteen days and emerged victorious, 23. He was awarded the titles Vedāntāchārya and Sarvatantra Svatantra, 24. He composed many works such as Śatadūṣaṇī, Adhikaraṇa Sārāvaḷi, Tatvamuktākalāpa, Sarvārtha Siddhi, Īśvara Mīmāṁsa, Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, Yādavābhyudaya, Tātparya Candrikā, Mīmāṁsā Pādukā, Tatva Ṭīkā, Śrī Stuti, Bhū Stuti, Yati Rāja Saptati, 25. He composed Pādukā Sahasram to quell the arrogance of a wicked debater who came boasting of his poetic skill, 26. He completed a thousand samasyas, 27. When a Yavani invasion occurred there, he composed Abhiiti Stava to ward it off, 28. He then went on pilgrimage to Tiruvāṇaiyāttirai. 29. After visiting Tirunagari, Tirunārāyaṇapuram, etc., he returned to Kāñcī. 30. There, Swāmi [[P7]] 7

When a sorcerer, unable to tolerate his titles, challenged him to a snake charming contest, he composed the Garuḍa Daṇḍaka and subdued the sorcerer’s arrogance. 31. Afterwards, his son was born. 32. He composed the Rahasyatraya Sāram – thus the work concludes. The thirty-two topics related to Deśika’s history described above are all that is found in that book. Some Śrī Vaiṣṇavas say that they hung old sandals so that they would touch the Swāmi’s head, and that those who had accepted invitations for a Śrāddha were cheated, and that they were served pongal on Ekādaśī while they were worshiping Perumāḷ in the temple, and that while he was reviewing books in his residence, they pulled his feet and made him fall in the street. Such stories written by modern authors are fabricated to cast blame on others. Not only does this cast blame on others, but discerning people understand that it also greatly harms the reputation of Swāmi Deśika. Instead of composing verses like “Rāmo Rāme Rāma Iti Prajānām Abhavat Kathā” and “Vedānta Sūrir Nigamānta Sūris Trayyanta Sūriḥ Kavitārkika Siṁhaḥ, Ity-eva Sarvatra Sadai-va Sarve Jñās Samantāt Kathayanta Āsan” (meaning, the whole world praised him with his divine qualities), it is regrettable that those who write books titled “Vaibhavam” (glory) and compose such trivial accounts, depicting him as hated by all, including his own followers and opponents, do not realize that this diminishes the Swāmi’s greatness. What a pity! Those who are invited for a Śrāddha are close relatives and not mere passers-by. Is there any doubt in this? If it is written that such close relatives, having agreed, cheated by not coming at the appointed time, does this even hold true for Deśika, who possessed such great glory? They do not even consider this. It is even more astonishing that if one person cheated out of envy, they write that three people cheated! This story is found in the history of Appayya Dīkṣitar. It is heard and seen that such incidents occur in the Śrāddhas performed by some people who have been outcast as fallen due to many शास्त्र-prohibited actions. It is utterly preposterous for modern authors to fill their “Desika Vaibhavam,” “Desika Prabhavā,” and “Desika Divya Charitram” with such absurdities as claiming that Desika treated as adversaries those Āchāryas whose divine works he always revered and embraced in his own compositions, and that his main task was to refute their divine works. All these absurdities should be strictly avoided, and one should strive to understand the authentic history cherished by the devout. Out of excessive devotion, if some individuals fabricate various stories to highlight the unique glory of the Āchārya, and if subsequent authors endorse them, these fabricated stories eventually come to be believed as true. If there is no incongruity in these fabricated stories, then devotees might accept them. Deśika’s pilgrimage to Tiruvahīndrapuram

As these events were unfolding, one day, desiring to perform Maṅgalāśāsanam in the southern divine abodes, he departed with the Lord’s command. When he reached the bank of the sacred Garuḍa River and desired to reside there for some time, contemplating the divinity of the Lord and the sanctity of Oṣadhādri, he began to regularly recite the Vainateya Mantra which he had received from his teacher in Tirumala. One day, the greatly benevolent Lord Hayagrīva appeared and instructed him on the Hayagrīva Mahāmantra. Thereafter, he remained in front of the beautiful Śrīsiṁha, reciting the Hayagrīva Mahāmantra for a long time. Then, the white-faced Lord appeared, blessed him with the nectar of His words, and disappeared. The teacher, immersed in supreme bliss, then composed and offered two stotras, Hayagrīva Stotra and Garuḍa Pañcāśat, and worshipped the Divine Lord. He then composed and offered stotras like Devānāyaka Pañcāśat, Acyuta Śatakam (a prākr̥ta-language stotra), Gopāla Viṁśati, and Mahāvīra Vaibhavam (known as Raghuviira Gadyam).

Maṅgalāśāsanam at Tirukkovalur

Then, receiving the command from the Divine Lord, he composed “Dēhaḷīśa Stuti” during his stay there, wishing to worship the Lord of Tirukkovalur, a divine abode where the Āḻvārs, Poigai Āḻvār, Bhūtat Āḻvār, and Pey Āḻvār, had joyfully experienced the Lord together, as described in the verse “Pāvaru Tamilāl Per Peru Panuvalar Pātināḷiravil, Mūvaru Nerukki Moḻi Viḷakkēṭri Mukundanait Toḻuda Nannādu”. In its 6th and 7th verses, the interaction between the first Āḻvārs and the Lord is beautifully described. (1) [Dīpena Kedacidaci Taruṣā Nīśīte Stheya Upapattya Pariśuddha Guṇārpiteta, Dahravakāśa Nibhiḍam Tatra Karbhavantam Svādhyāya Yoganayanāḥ Śucayaḥ Kavīndrāḥ:] The meaning is that the chief poets, who are like the first Āḻvārs, saw the Lord in their hearts by lighting a unique lamp on a moonless night.

The idea is that the chief poets, who are like Yogis, saw the Lord in their hearts by lighting a unique lamp on a moonless night. (2) [Kāsarapūrva Kavimukhya Vimṛṣṭa Janma Puṇyā (puṇṇā) Taṭe-kṣaṇaṁ Paka Svayaraso Bahu haste, Tvātpādapadmadhuti Tvādananyabhokye Nūnam Samāśrayati Nūtanasarkarātvam] The meaning is that the essence produced by the compression of Poigai Āḻvār and others, who were born on the banks of the Pālār river like sugarcane, was mixed with the sugar in the honey that flowed from His lotus feet, as stated in the Śruti “Viṣṇoḥ Pade Parame Madhva Utsaḥ”. These two jewel-like verses are eternally cherished by devotees.

Return to Kāñcī

Thus, after residing for some time in Tirukkovalur and discoursing on Vedāntic texts for the residents there, he returned to the Perumāḷ Temple, his birthplace, and performed discourses on various texts. During this time, he composed the Śrīstuti on Perundēvī Tāyār, the Varadarāja Pañcāśat on Perarūḷāḷan, the Vēgasetu Stotra on Yadōttakāri Emperumāṇ, the Aṣṭabhujāṣṭaka on Aṭṭapuṟakkarattu Emperumāṇ, the Saraṇāgati Dīpikā, also known as Dīprapakāśa Stotra, on Viḷakkōḷiyemperumāṇ, and the Kāmāsikāṣṭaka on Vēḷukkai Emperumāṇ, and the Paramārthastuti on Tirupuṭkuli Pōrēṭru Perumāṇ.

The Origin of Vairāgya Pañcakam

Our Vedānta Deśika, born as an incarnation of Viṣṇu, was endowed with all-encompassing knowledge, and was also uniquely detached from worldly matters, just like Kūrattāḻvān. Therefore, he resided in Kāñcī, where the dark-hued Lord dwells, living by alms alone, performing the worship of Perumāḷ perfectly, and discoursing on both Tamil and Sanskrit Vedāntic texts to scholars of both languages. At that time, a person named Vidhyāraṇya, who enjoyed great favor with the Vijayanagara Emperor and had the means to support many scholars, intended to alleviate our Śrī Deśika Śikāmaṇi’s poverty and bestow wealth upon him. He sent a letter to that Vijayanagara king, requesting him to come. When Swāmi Deśika saw this, he wondered, “This Vidhyāraṇya knows my spiritual state, yet he invites me in vain!” He sent back a response in the form of a verse to him to emphasize his steadfast detachment. [[P11]]

11 “Na Me Bhūmīdhārādarjiṭam Kiñcid Na Mayā Kiñcid Arjiṭam! Asti Me Hastīśailāgre Vastu Paitāmahaṁ Dhr̥tam.” He sent this verse, and Vidhyāraṇya, seeing it, was so delighted by the extraordinary devotion the Lord had for Desika that he again sent an insistent invitation letter [having obtained the king’s permission and respect]. At that time, the verses sent by the Swāmi are known as Vairāgya Pañcakam.

One of Swāmi’s Miraculous Feats

One day, an Advaita Sannyāsī came to Kāñcī and, being defeated in a debate with the Swāmi, decided to cause him some trouble. Using his subtle magical powers, this sorcerer made the Swāmi’s stomach swell by making him drink water from a pond near the Swāmi’s residence. The Swāmi, aware of this magical act, drew a line on a pillar at the entrance of his residence with his fingernail, and water began to flow from it, restoring the Swāmi’s stomach to its normal state. Witnessing this, the sorcerer was greatly astonished by the Swāmi’s powers and prostrated before him before departing.

The Greatness of Śrīstuti, known as Kanakadhārāstūavam

One day, a Brahmachari came to the Swāmi’s presence, prostrated, and prayed, “I am very poor; I wish to marry, Swāmi, please help me with wealth.” The Swāmi smiled and asked, “Child! You know that I live by alms and perform the worship of Perumāḷ. Why then do you come to me asking for money, instead of approaching wealthy people?” The Brahmachari replied, “I have heard many elders say that if the Swāmi wills it, nothing is unattainable, and wealth can be obtained. That is why I prayed to you.” Hearing this, the Swāmi said, “Is that so? If devotees say so, wealth will certainly be obtained; do not worry anymore; recite Śrīstuti and circumambulate Perundēvī Tāyār; you will be blessed.” He then taught him the Śrīstuti, a prayer he had composed earlier on Perundēvī Tāyār. That Brahmachari spent the whole day reciting that stuti. The next morning, when the Swāmi, after completing his daily rites, went to the presence of Perarūḷāḷan for Maṅgalāśāsanam, the Brahmachari followed him, reciting Śrīstuti melodiously, and circumambulated Perundēvī Tāyār. When the verses “Ārtatrāṇavratapīru Mṛtāsāranīlāmbuvāhaiḥ Ambojaānāmuṣasi Miṣatā Mandaraīr-apāṅgaiḥ, Yasyāṁ Yasyāṁ Diśi Viharate Devi! Dṛṣṭis Tvadīyā Tasyāṁ Tasyāṁ Mahamahikaaraṁ Tatvate Sampado Kāḥ Yogārambhatvarita Manaso Yūṣmad-ekāntiyuṣṭaṁ Dharmam Prathamiha Ye Dhārayante Dhanāyāṁ! Teṣām Bhūmau Tat-apatiguha Tāmbarād Ambudeḥ Vā Dhārā Nir-yanty-adhika Matikam Vāñchitānām Vasūnām” were being recited, a shower of golden coins fell in front of the Tāyār’s shrine, astonishing everyone. The Brahmachari, filled with gratitude, took leave and departed.

Birth of a Son

A son, equal to the Swāmi in all respects, was born in the Naḷa year, in the month of Āvaṇi, on Rōhiṇī Nakṣatra. He was named Varadāchāryar. He too, at the appropriate time, underwent the Upanyana and other sacraments, and mastered all Śāstras in the Swāmi’s presence, shining brightly like a lamp. He was known as Nayanārāchāryar, Nayanavaradāchāryar, and Kumāravaradāchāryar.

Swāmi’s Acquisition of Titles

Swāmi Deśika acquired various titles such as Vedāntāchārya, Kavitārkika Keśarī, Sarvatantra Svatantra, and Samasyāsahasrī. Among these, the title Vedāntāchārya was bestowed upon him by Śrī Raṅganātha, as is evident from his divine works; for instance, in the prologue to Saṅkalpa Sūryōdaya, he states “[Śrī Raṅgarāja Divyājñā labdha Vedāntāchārya padaḥ:]”, and in the beginning of Adhikaraṇa Sārāvaḷi, he mentions “[Svasti Śrī Raṅgabhartuḥ:]” in the verse “[Detha Devetha Dattāṁ Vedāntāchārya Saṁjñām]”. It is to be understood that the Lord of Śrī Raṅgam is implicitly referred to by the terms Hari and Deva in the verse “[Svakam-iti Haritattvam Yāmuna Stōtra Vṛttyā Vyākūṇuta Nigamāntāchāryakaṁ Veṅkaṭeśa]” at the end of Dayāśatakam, and in the verse “[Vedāntāchārya pade Viniveśya Bālaṁ Devo Dayāśatakam Etad Avātayān Mām]” at the end of Dayāśatakam. Swāmi’s Poetic Prowess

Even though there were many great poets in the world who were highly skilled in composing, the Swāmi’s poetic prowess was exceptionally unique. The Swāmi was unparalleled in all four types of poetry: 1. Āśu Kavi (extempore poet), 2. Madhura Kavi (sweet poet), 3. Chitra Kavi (चित्रकवि - चित्र-कविता), and 4. Vistāra Kavi (extensive poet). It is not that he composed verses by painstakingly stringing together a few words, as a “Haṭādākṛṣṭānāṁ Katipayapadānām Racayitā” would. It is truly regrettable that people, without understanding or researching these matters, have become confused and are confusing others. Is it so difficult to understand Deśika’s doctrines? His divine works have not been lost. What he clearly stated is briefly elucidated here. Deśika did not at all delve into establishing the Lord’s being the cause of the universe, the bestower of liberation, His omnipresence, etc., as exclusively belonging to the Goddess. We will explain this clearly and simply. The Mahākāvya called Yādavābhyudaya was composed by Swāmi Deśika; its twelfth canto deals with Rukmiṇī, the Goddess. In its 1st and 2nd verses, it is stated that Śrī Mahālakṣmī herself incarnated as Rukmiṇī. The very next verse states: “Śikhaṇḍakaṁ Niṣpratimaṁ Śr̥tīnāṁ Śṛṅgāra Līlopama Viśvakṛtyaṁ, Adhīyate Tannmitunaṁ Svabhāvād Anyōnya Jīvātu Madhanya Bhōgyam”. The following verses exclusively describe Rukmiṇī. The first two verses are also of the same nature. The third verse, however, mentions “mitunaṁ” (couple). The commentary on “Śṛṅgāra Līlopama Viśvakṛtyaṁ” states, “The creation, sustenance, and dissolution of the universe, which is like a playful act of romantic dalliance, easily achieved.” Deśika used the term “mitunaṁ” in this verse, thus making it unique, because he did not wish to attribute these acts (creation, sustenance, etc.) solely to the Goddess. If one keenly observes that he included both (the Lord and the Goddess) in this one verse, while in the preceding and succeeding verses he composed only about the Goddess, it becomes clear that Deśika held that the Goddess has no separate role in the creation and sustenance of the universe. In Śrīstuti, the verse “Yatsaṅkalpāt Bhavati Kamalē Yatra Dehitvam-īṣāṁ Janma Sthema Pralaya Racatā Jaṅgamājaṅgamānām; Tat Kalyāṇaṁ Kimapi Yaminaṭam Ekalakṣyam Samarat-au Pūrṇaṁ Tejaḥ Sphurati Bhavatīpādalākṣārasāṅgam” is sufficient to understand Deśika’s true view on the Goddess.
