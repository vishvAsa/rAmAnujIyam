Traditional Śrīvaiṣṇava works are written not only in Sanskrit, Tamil, Maṇ ipravāḷ am and Telugu, but they have also been printed in various scripts \(for instance, the Telugu script with its *grantha* letters has been used for Maṇipravāḷam texts\). To facilitate access to Śrīvaiṣṇavism for those who have only a general knowledge about Hinduism, I have favoured Sanskrit spellings for words that are Tamilised Sanskrit \(thus, Rāmānuja instead of Rāmānujar or Irāmānujar, or *sabhā* instead of *sabhai*, or *maṭha* instead of *maṭam* \[Tamil\]\). I give Sanskrit and Tamil equivalents on first use for key terms. 

In general, I have used standard Sanskrit and Tamil 

diacritical marks for words that appear in historicalsources, such as names of deities, technical theological and philosophical terms, and people’s names \(unless there is another standard spelling in the scholarly literature\). After the first citation of a name, I have offered shortened versions of many long names and eliminated titles to make it easier for those not familiar with these languages and subjects to read the book. I have used English spellings of names that appear in English-language sources in Tamil Nadu. In some cases where an English equivalent exists, I have used the English word. In 


addition, I have added an ‘s’ to Tamil plurals to Anglicise the word. Moreover, I have used the spellings of my informants’ names provided by them.

Because place names have changed over time, I generally 

use the name appropriate to the historical context, for example, Madras Presidency in the early twentieth century but Tamil Nadu after Indian independence, or in general comments. Because my interviewees alternated between the names of Madras or Chennai and Puducherry or Pondicherry, both appear. 

I do not use diacritical marks for caste names; rather, I 

use the version common in English language works, but have given Tamil, Telugu or Sanskrit variants in a note. Nor do I use diacritical marks for names of villages, towns and cities; for these names, I follow the spellings of place names on current Tamil Nadu maps with one exception. I use ‘T’ and not ‘Th’ in names such as Tanjavur or Tiruchirappalli. However, in Appendix 1 on the location of places, I provide variant spellings.

For the discerning eye, there will be anomalies in the use or 

non-use of diacritical marks \(for instance, the names of some organisations \(*sabhā*s\) will appear with diacritical marks but others will not, depending on my source. Words and names that are a combination of Sanskrit and Tamil may have features of both languages, as in Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, where Ekāṅgi is Sanskrit and Svāmikaḷ is a Sanskrit word with a Tamil suffix. It is impossible to be absolutely systematic with the linguistic complexity of this subject matter.

The *Nāmam*: A Note





The book cover features a Śrīvaiṣṇava mark worn by men that sanctifies the body in twelve places or just the forehead. It is called the *tirunā mam* \(sacred name\) or simply *nā mam* \(name\) because one utters the name of God when applying it. It is also called the *ūrdhva-puṇḍra*, which means a mark \(*puṇḍra*\) that rises upward \(*ūrdhva*\) and thereby signifies the spiritual upward path. The two vertical white lines symbolise the two feet of God, which means the way and the goal, but also purity, because they are white in colour. The central red mark between them, made of turmeric and vermillion \(*kuṁkum*\), signifies the Goddess \(Śrī-Lakṣmī\) and her compassionate grace. 

By extension, the *nāmam *is used to sacralise any place and 

identify its association with Śrīvaiṣṇavism. The shape of the *nāmam* with a pedestal is that of the Teṉkalai subsect; when the bottom is rounded, it signifies the Vaṭakalai subsect. Most non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas are Teṉkalais, although some are Vaṭakalais.
