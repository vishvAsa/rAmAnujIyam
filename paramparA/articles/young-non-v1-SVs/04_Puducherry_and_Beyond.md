Oral Histories from Puducherry and Beyond



I refer loosely to the general area around Puducherry. This region includes the former British area of South Arcot and the former French colony of Pondichéri \(traditionally known as Putuvai\), which then became Pondicherry. Today, India has redefined South Arcot as the districts of Cuddalore and Villupuram, and Pondicherry as a Union Territory \(a special status to recognise its French heritage\). This general region is significant for my purposes here, because it was for several centuries an area of Śrīvaiṣṇava proselytism and non-Brahmin leadership in the late nineteenth century. When Pondicherry emerged as the cultural and intellectual metropolis of this area, after joining India in 1954 as a Union Territory, Bhāgavata academics from other regions joined local traditional scholars and literary figures to further the strong non-Brahmin presence in the city, with links to the surrounding villages and the *maṭha* in Tirukkoyilur.



The Octogenarian’s Flashbacks

In Puducherry, I met M. Radhakrishnan, who is also known as Pāvalar Maṇī Cittaṉ, Visnuchittan or just Chittan. He told 







me many stories, some about Śrīvaiṣṇavism in Pondicherry in his parents’ generation and others about Vaiṣṇava caste inclusiveness, from traditional lore. This elderly yet still energetic man had been a traditional dentist, although he is now more famous as a Tamil poet and a prominent Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavata. 

I am a third-generation Śrīvaiṣṇava. In my father’s 

time, a non-Brahmin called Ramanuja Swami came 

from Srirangam to Pondicherry, gave public talks and 

converted many people. Another Bhāgavata leader, 

Varadarājulu Naidu, also lectured on Vaiṣṇavism. My 

father, whose name was Mudaliyantan \(after Rāmānuja’s 

nephew\), was a worker in a government hospital and after 

his conversion, was also initiated with the *samāśrayaṇam*

ritual by Manjakkupam Svamikal, the non-Brahmin 

swami of Manjakkupam \(Cuddalore\) and a disciple of 

Bhuvanagiri.1

My father was a staunch Vaiṣṇava. He would not take food from outside, in fact not even from his son or daughter, only his wife. After marriage, his brother’s sons lived in Chennai. Once, he visited them. To avoid their food, he went to the Parthasarathy temple instead to take the *prasādam*. The priest said that there was none. Then someone intervened and told the priest to give him food. My father thought that this person was God himself. 

My wife was stauncher than I was. When she was dying, 

Venkataswami Reddiyar, a Bhāgavata who had published the *Divyaprabandham*,2 took her to a famous *siddha* doctor. He came and gave her medicine. A Murukaṉ devotee, the doctor told her first to wear *vibhūti* \(sacred ash\) and then take the medicine. She refused, because this was the emblem of another god. She subsequently died in the local hospital. She made no compromises. In fact, she made many sacrifices in her life for her faith.

Recollecting Śrīvaiṣṇava practices in his own family 

reminded Chittan of other Śrīvaiṣṇavas he had known.



162







Great Śrīvaiṣṇavas lived here in the past. For instance, 

there was Manika Kannan, belonging to the Vanniyar 

caste, who was a constable. He would wear a *nāmam* on 

his forehead. When the chief opposed this, he resigned his 

post and lived by begging. Once, when Manika Kannan 

went on the street where Bharati-dasan,3 a famous poet 

who was staunchly atheistic, was living, Bharati asked 

his wife to fill a pot with rice and give it to him. Bharati 

later told his followers that he had given rice because this 

man had a very strong character. Manika Kannan made a 

garland of flowers and *tulasī* and offered it to the Lord at 

Varadarāja Perumāḷ temple in Pondicherry daily until his 

death. He used to do *bhajana*s on important holy days. If 

people offered money, he said, ‘Please put it in my pot’, 

but would not touch it. He would cook rice and offer it to 

God at his home shrine. Manika Kannan was my father’s 

best friend. 

Then there was a pious Śrīvaiṣṇava lady, a Naidu 

named Etiraja Valli. After her husband died, she refused 

to remove her *t* *āli* \[the necklace symbolising her status 

as a married woman\], saying, ‘my husband is living 

in Paramapadam \[supreme heaven\], so why should I 

remove it?’

Antal Amma, too, was a great Śrīvaiṣṇava. A woman of 

little education from the Reddiyar community, she would 

chide her daughter-in-law if she put a pot of very hot milk 

before the deities in the home shrine, saying, ‘Can you 

drink extremely hot milk? If not, how can the Lord drink 

it? We must give it at the right temperature.’ 

I remember being told about a very pious woman who 

refused to use the vegetables that she had just bought 

at the market because a male friend of her husband 

had asked for them, and so she thought that they were 

polluted. She gave the greens to her own female friend 

instead. \[In other words, as a pious wife, she would not 

touch anything that had been touched by a man other 

than her husband.\]

More memories surfaced, this time of Śrīvaiṣṇava Harijans.



163







My father and his friends converted Harijans in this 

area to Śrīvaiṣṇavism. The leader of the Harijans who 

encouraged this was Glovis Varadarajan. He had the 

French name Glovis because he had French citizenship on 

account of his military service. He was a well-off grocery 

merchant and wore a big *n* *āmam*. Glovis lived in Uppalam 

in Pondicherry, which is a Harijan colony. He would come 

on his bullock cart. My father and his friends would eat 

with this Harijan, invite him to all the functions and give 

the first *pras* *ādam* to him. Whenever the *sabhā* organised 

a conference, it would get donations from Glovis. In the 

Perumāḷ temple, there were many high castes; still they 

honoured Glovis by asking him for donations, and he 

would give large ones. After that time, there was a Harijan 

*sabhā*. The main person is now gone. He maintained a 

temple and arranged for *bhajana*s, lectures and festivals. 

Many Harijans were initiated by Krishna Premi Jīyar 

near Paranur or Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar in Sriperumbudur. 

I have been told that in Ethiraja’s will, he has requested 

that the Dalits should perform the rites after his death.

In Vaiṣṇavaism, there are ten *avatāra*s. Rāma and 

Krishna are the most important. According to the epics, 

these two gods don’t belong to the Brahmin caste. Rāma is 

a Kṣatriya and Krishna belongs to the Yadava \(cowherd\) 

clan. The majority of the *āḻvār*s are non-Brahmins. They 

put the Vedas into Tamil. Of the 10, only Periyāḻvār and 

Toṇṭaraṭippoṭi are Brahmins. 

‘Now there are no Harijans in the *sabhā*s,’ Chittan told me, before narrating the story of Periya Nampi, ‘the guru of Rāmānuja, a great person who honoured the “outcastes”’. 

When Periya Nampi built a new house, he had to fix an 

auspicious date for a ‘house-warming’. When an outcaste 

named Māṟannēri Nampi arrived at the house, Periya 

Nampi said that there was no need to fix an auspicious 

date, because Māṟannēri was there, which by itself 

created an auspicious time and place \[in other circles, as 

an outcaste, he would have had to stay away from the 

house altogether\].



164







Māṟannēri is an important symbol for the *sampradāya*’s concept of an inclusive community. He is remembered as an ascetic outcaste disciple of Yāmuna at Srirangam in the eleventh century. Another story about Māṟannēri tells how, when he was dying, he asked Periya Nampi to perform his last rites since his relatives were not Śrīvaiṣṇavas. When Periya Nampi did this, other Śrīvaiṣṇavas complained vociferously to Rāmānuja. Periya Nampi defended his action by citing many prior examples from the scriptures, such as Rāma doing the final rites for the vulture Jaṭāyu in the *Rāmāyaṇa*, or the many *Divyaprabandham* verses such as *Tiruvāymoḻi* 3.7.9 that honour outcastes, which later *ācārya*s in their commentaries linked to the stories of Māṟannēri.4

Remembering Māṟannēri inspired Chittan to tell the 

tale about what happened when the twelfth-century *ācārya* Parāśara Bhaṭṭar committed a small mistake. 

Regretting it, he bathed and prayed. Still he had no peace 

of mind. He told his learned motherāṇṭāḷ \(not the *āḻvār*

Āṇṭāḷ\) his problem and asked her what to do. His mother 

told him to wash the feet of a pious and learned non-

Brahmin and drink that water as sacred water \(*t* *īrtha*\). 

Bhaṭṭar pointed out that a non-Brahmin might not allow 

this and asked her to prepare him to do this, just as one 

calms a cow before milking it. After Bha ṭṭar brought a 

non-Brahmin home, his mother did this, and so a non-

Brahmin agreed to have his feet washed by a Brahmin.

Chittan narrated yet another anecdote, this one about Rāmānuja walking on a *bund* \(raised path\), across a huge irrigated field belonging to the temple.

An outcaste woman was approaching him. Because the 

bund was narrow, someone needed to get down so the 

other could pass. Although Rāmānuja asked the lady 

to get down, she refused, even though the custom was 

that the outcaste should do so. She said, ‘I cannot move, 

because outcastes should not put their feet on temple 

land. If I get down from the bund, I’ll have to put my 

feet there.’ Then Rāmānuja realised that she was a great 

165







lady and asked her to join him and his disciples. After 

her death, Rāmānuja honoured her and had a statue of 

her installed under the foot of Lord Perumāḷ. Since then, 

whoever worships God, worships the outcaste lady. 

‘This happened at Tiruma ṅkaiāḻvār’s birthplace,’ commented Chittan, ‘which is now the Tiruvāli-Tirunagari temple at Cirkali, beyond Chidambaram.’ 

To illustrate the inclusiveness of the religion, Chittan 

told me: ‘There are four *varṇa*s. Below them are the *cāṇṭāḷa*s \(outcastes\). They used to eat the meat of dead animals floating in the river, and so they were considered very inferior.’ After telling me the etymology of the name Toṇṭaraṭippoṭi—*poṭi* \(dust\) on the feet \(*aṭi*\) of the servants \(*toṇṭar*\)—he pointed out that even a Brahmin *āḻvār* should worship dust on the feet of devotees \(including the feet of *cāṇṭāḷa*s\). Chittan continued: ‘Toṇṭaraṭippoṭiāḻvār, a Brahmin, said that an upper-caste person may marry an outcaste, if that person is a devotee of Lord Perumāḷ and also good and pure.5 Even a Brahmin girl may marry a low-caste devotee.’ 

I asked Chittan whether this was a fact or a story, because 

I had never encountered this idea in the texts or interviews. Chittan replied that it once was ‘fact’, although that changed after ‘the thirteenth-century Brahmin commentator Periyavāccaṉ Piḷḷai altered the meaning of Toṇṭaraṭippoṭi’s poems to eliminate the idea of a Brahmin marrying a pure, albeit low-caste, woman’. ‘Although inter-caste marriages were no longer done,’ Chittan continued, ‘today there is inter-caste marriage; partners might be chosen on the basis of income and education. The Dravidian movement has been against caste and religion.’ 

After a pause in the conversation, I asked Chittan about his 

political affiliations.

I had joined Periyar’s Self-Respect movement, as had 99 

per cent of the non-Brahmins, when I was twenty-seven. 

My father protested because Periyar was an atheist and 

chided me. Once I went as a dentist to a fisherman’s 

colony and saw a man from Periyar’s movement at 

166







a local temple there. I noticed that he was wearing the 

Śaivite marks of *vibhūti* and *kuṁkuma*. I wanted to know 

why he did this as an atheist, which followers of Periyar 

supposedly were. The man said that his son and wife had 

made him do it. I then decided that Periyar and members 

of his party were pseudo-atheists. I told my father, and he 

agreed. That was the end of my attraction to Periyar. Many 

Vaiṣṇavas and Śaivas who joined Periyar’s Self-Respect 

Movement found out that it was not a true movement. K. 

M. Balasubramaniam, a populariser of Maṇikkavācakar’s 

*Tiruvācakam*, was with Periyar. Later, he returned to 

Śaivism, realising that it was right. For forty to sixty years, 

Vaiṣṇavism declined because of this atheistic movement. 

Now Dravidian atheism has declined. Vaiṣṇavism is 

becoming popular once more. Some non-Brahmins even 

give initiation.

This idea of non-Brahmins giving initiations was nothing new for Chittan. After all, the non-Brahmin Manjakkupam, who was Bhuvanagiri’s disciple, had initiated his father.6



The Carpenter’s Masterful Chanting

Hoping to find some Bhāgavatas to interview, I sat on the steps of a pillared hall in the courtyard of a Perumāḷ temple, which had been built about 100 years ago, on the outskirts of Puducherry. After a while, I noticed a man moving from one sub-shrine to another, bowing deeply. When he had finished praying, I approached him. Ramanuja Nayakar agreed to an interview. 

A carpenter by profession, I live with my family nearby 

and come to the temple daily. Like so many other non-

Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas in this region, I belong to a 

long-standing Śrīvaiṣṇava family. It had founded the 

Muthial Manjini Kuppa, a *ma* *ṭha* for chanting *bhajana*s. 

My father was well-versed in chanting the verses of the 

*Divyaprabandham*, and I learned to chant from him. I have 

memorised all 4,000 verses. 

167







I rise at 4:00 am and take my bath. I chant about 200 

verses, changing them each day until I have chanted a 

cycle of all 4,000. Then, I worship the image of Perumāḷ in 

my home shrine. On weekdays, my children chant with 

me. Because of all the chanting, my sons and daughters 

are now educated and getting good jobs. In fact, they 

receive offers of employment even before completing 

their courses. Whenever my children wrote their exams, 

I would sit at home and chant. Or when they went for 

job interviews. Therefore, they received all the benefits of 

chanting. I myself have no other goal than to surrender at 

the Lord’s feet. Because I am a daily wage labourer, I have 

not had formal initiation into Śrīvaiṣṇavism. I cannot 

maintain the purity of that position. I am, however, a pure 

vegetarian. My wife, who is from a non-vegetarian family, 

is not. Neither are my children.

Ramanuja Nayakar suddenly began chanting. When he stopped, he told me more about his life. 

I was once in the Communist Party, learned all its 

theories and taught them to the labourers. But the leaders 

gave me such trouble that I had to leave, even though 

I was a staunch party leader and union organiser. My 

disillusionment began when I noticed the hypocrisy 

of the leaders. During one conflict, they supported the 

capitalists. When I left the Party, I started chanting again. 

By chanting, you can transform even your enemies. 

Because I chanted, a Member of the Legislative Assembly 

who had given me trouble surrendered to the Lord after 

he had an accident.

Ramanuja Nayakar interrupted the conversation again by chanting, in a beautiful voice, one verse from Tirumaṅkaiāḻvār’s *Tirukkuṟuntāṇṭakam*. When he finished, he elaborated on its meaning with great erudition. 

The conversation turned to problems that Ramanuja 

Nayakar faced when teaching Tamil hymns at the temple.

Both Brahmins and non-Brahmins, including boys and 

girls, are my students. I teach for free. Occasionally, they 

offer me gifts. The Brahmin priests are not too keen about 

168







my activity. I think that this might be due to discrimination, 

or because the temple is becoming increasingly Vedic and 

therefore not so interested in the Tamil *Divyaprabandham*.



The Professor’s Nostalgia for a Lost World

Dr A. Pandurangan, a retired professor emeritus of Tamil literature at Pondicherry University, was often my guide in Puducherry, thanks to a request by K. K. A. Venkatachari. We met Bhāgavata notables in the city and went on several car trips in the countryside, which led us to the traces of Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri and Nārāyaṇa Dāsar \(discussed in Chapter 2\). There was plenty of time for conversation on these journeys of discovery, and the professor’s life story gradually unfolded.

I am a ninth-generation Śrīvaiṣṇava. My children 

and grandchildren make eleven generations in all. 

The oldest ancestor in our lineage was namedāḻvār, 

after Nammāḻvār. He was from a small village called 

Maruthan Valvu, which is near Nammāḻvār’s birthplace, 

Alvartirunagari in Tirunelveli district. My father left this 

village and migrated to a village called Subbulapuram 

in Madurai district, where he obtained a large tract of 

land for agriculture and brought many people from his 

previous village to help him open up the new fertile area.

The men in my family maintained their Śrīvaiṣṇava 

identity by marrying only Śrīvaiṣṇava women, 

worshipping at the Narasiṁha Perumāḷ temple at 

Jambuliputtur, a small village nearby, and going on 

pilgrimage to places such as Srirangam. My grandfather, 

Ramaswami, was a trustee of the Narasiṁha-Perumāḷ

temple and is remembered fondly by all. He used to stay 

in Srirangam for several months every year and also went 

during the Cittra festival to the Tirumaliruncolai temple 

near Madurai. Brahmin *ācārya*s from Srivilliputur visited 

his home and gave initiation to family members. I never 

met my grandfather, who was a staunch Śrīvaiṣṇava.



169







In my boyhood, an old relative taught me Periyāḻvār’s 

*Tiruppa* *ḷḷāṇṭu*. He did *bhajana*s and took me to visit nearby 

villages. Some of my other relatives would not accept 

food from this old relation’s family, only from initiated 

Śrīvaiṣṇavas. As a result, the old man and I had to bring 

our own food and cook it there. These same relatives 

would not allow others into their home shrine. They were 

very fond of me, however, because my mother had died 

when I was only ten days old, and so they let me enter. 

The family’s faith was so strong that when the goddess 

Kālī’s procession came down their street, they closed their 

doors because they refused to worship any deity who was 

not associated with Viṣṇu. I was especially impressed by 

an elderly widow in my family. She had no children and 

was extremely poor. She never complained and always 

refused help, saying that everything is God’s grace and 

her only husband is Lord Mahārānī. This strong religious 

tradition virtually died out with my father, who was not 

interested in religion. Because of the family’s wealth, my 

father, who was the eldest son, ignored the care of the 

lands. Other relatives helped out my grandfather, but 

then they siphoned away the wealth.

Because my family was very rich, it had close relations 

with local Brahmins and often gave them rice. In my 

boyhood, I ate in Brahmin homes. There was no conflict 

of purity rules because my own family, as ‘staunch’ 

Vaiṣṇavas, maintained these rules themselves.

Educated at the primary level in the local village 

school, I did my secondary school in Teni. Although 

I started in science, which gave me an interest in the 

scientific method, I did a B. A. in Madurai. At that time, 

Madurai College was controlled by Brahmins, mainly 

Śrīvaiṣṇava Ayyaṅkars, who were the teachers. Because 

I was a Vaiṣṇava, the Ayyaṅkars liked me. When they 

quoted *āḻvār* hymns, I knew them and was asked to recite 

them. When I was a student in Madras doing my M. A. in 

Tamil literature at President’s College, I stayed in a non-

Brahmin student hostel for one year. When the warden, a 

former Indian Oil officer, heard my name and recognised 



170







me as a Vaiṣṇava, he became very affectionate. He created 

a strong Vaiṣṇava influence in the hostel. 

I stood first in my M. A. class, winning two gold 

medals. In 1956, I was forced to marry by my uncle, who 

had educated me, because he thought that he was going 

to die and wanted to fulfil his Hindu responsibility as my 

substitute father after my father had died, of arranging 

my marriage. I was married to my aunt’s daughter. In 

1961, I moved to Pondicherry, where I became a professor 

of Tamil literature.

My family had been Gandhians and Congress followers. 

My father and uncles wore only *khadi* cotton cloth, as I did 

before I became a professor. By that time, it was expensive 

and difficult to wash, and so I used it for a while only on 

ritual occasions. 

Many of my fellow students had joined the Dravidar 

Kazhagam \(DK\) in the 1950s, when I was in high school 

in Teni. The DK spread like wildfire. Most young people 

were attracted to the speeches of its leaders. One of my 

uncles became a staunch DK member. He brought DK 

books to me. I was not attracted to them, especially after 

contempt for C. N. Annadurai, who broke away from 

Periyar’s DK and started the DMK, became strong. One 

day, a DMK student attacked me, and nobody helped me. 

Almost all castes backed the DMK. By joining it, people 

were connecting with those in power. That was good for 

jobs. Some of those who joined these movements later 

returned to their religions. 

When I was a student at the University of Madras, one 

of my teachers was Sangara Rajalu, a Telugu man. After 

being a student leader of the Communist Party of India, 

he became a staunch Vaiṣṇava and developed an interest 

in the *Divyaprabandham*.

After I came to Pondicherry, I joined a Vaiṣṇava *sabhā*

with southern traditions—The Southern Acarya Sabai. 

It was headed by Rajagopalan, a Brahmin civil-supplies 

officer in Pondicherry. Although members were very 

fond of me and wanted me to be the secretary, I refused 

because it would be hard to obtain donations as a non-

native in Pondicherry. 

171







The *sabhā* tried to introduce the custom of Brahmins 

and non-Brahmins singing together in the main Perumāḷ

temple. We were not allowed to chant in its inner 

sanctuary, only in the pillared hall of the first courtyard. 

We conducted Nammāḻvār’s week-long celebration and 

arranged for endowments. The temple priests refused 

to give us *tīrtha* \(sacred water\). With its members 

disillusioned, the *sabhā* disbanded. 

The names of my children and grandchildren are all 

Vaiṣṇava, which has helped to maintain their religious 

identity. No one received initiation in my generation 

because of my father’s negative influence. Still, I decided 

to do it. 

If they wanted initiation, Bhāgavatas went to any *ācārya*

who was willing to perform it. I first considered going 

to Desikapillai, a Vaṭakalai *ācārya*. Then I thought about 

having initiation from a Srivilliputur *ācārya*, because that 

had been my family’s tradition. The tradition had been 

lost by my father and brothers, and I could no longer 

trace our *ācārya* family. They had become mere priests. 

Other *ācārya*s did not want to initiate non-Brahmins. I 

thought also about going to Srirangam Narayana Jiyar, 

but he was not a real spiritual leader, merely the head of 

a *maṭha*. Because I believe that a guru should be above 

ordinary people, I wanted a very spiritual *ācārya*. I heard 

about the Vijawada *jīyar* in Andhra \[Pandurangan was 

probably referring to Chinna Jeeyar Swami; I discuss him 

in Chapter 6\], but it was too far away. Finally, I went to 

Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar in Sriperumbudur and was deeply 

impressed by his affection. I had met him many years 

before and found him vigorous, smart, and the only one 

who has real compassion for non-Brahmins. 

About ten years ago, my wife, my daughter and I 

were initiated by Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar. He refused any 

*dakṣiṇa* \(gift\) for performing the ritual and said, ‘If you 

insist, 4 annas \(a few cents\).’ I gave him more anyway. 

When this *jīyar* receives a donation, he gives a receipt. He 

insists. Many Brahmins are against him, denying him all 

rights and honours. Processions in Sriperumbudur used 

to stop at his ashram. Now they refuse to do so. If you 

172







visit the *jīyar*, he always asks, ‘Have you had any food?’ 

Even if you say ‘yes’, he still prepares at least coffee. 

Other Brahmins won’t cook or feed non-Brahmins with 

their own hands. I recently visited him in the hospital, 

after he fell and broke his hip. 

I am vegetarian. My own relatives eat meat ‘for good 

health’. These days, in the morning, I worship the 

*śālagrāma* stone and chant the *Tiruppāvai* and *Tiruppaḷḷāṇṭu*

along with verses from the *Tiruvāymoḻi*, tasks that take me 

about an hour. I eat by 10 am. My children don’t want 

to wait, because they must go to the office or have other 

things to do. My wife and daughter also don’t chant, 

saying that their voices are not good enough. 

Now I think a lot about the village of my childhood. 

My house there is more than 200 years old. It once had 

murals. One was of Krishna stealing the saris of *gopī*s. 

Another was of devotees offering fruits to the reclining 

Lord Raṅganātha. Yet another was of Nammāḻvār. I knew 

who these figures were only later in life. The walls have 

been whitewashed since then. I plan to restore them. I 

am now the only one who remembers these things. As 

the eldest son, I inherited the property. It is a huge house. 

Three to 400 people can sit in its courtyard. In the past, 

many people had their weddings there. 

My own family tradition in my native village is now 

only for festivals and singing in the *rāmānuja* *kūṭam*. It is 

not very serious. As an elder who is trusted, I still remain 

in charge of many village affairs and return periodically. 

For the festival of the goddess Kālī, the deity who is 

important for the villagers, the festival ornaments are kept 

in my house. I am the custodian of them, even though 

they are not rich ornaments. I manage the revenues for 

the Kālī temple, even though I am a Vaiṣṇava. I levy the 

taxes for festivals and have increased the revenue. Four 

hundred participating couples pay Rs 100 and sometimes 

give offerings too. From these funds, I also give to needy 

people.

Speaking of caste and current politics, Pandurangan remarked that:

173







My own caste, the Naidu, are no longer politically 

powerful, despite the fact that they once had high status as 

non-Brahmins. During Nayak rule, many Telugu Naidus 

entered Tamil Nadu as warriors and were rewarded 

with lands. Even though they claim a common Telugu 

identity, Naidus are not politically united because people 

from various places claim descent from different kings, 

and these then form sub-sects such as the Gauravas and 

Kamalas. 

Even though they are often rich landlords, Naidus 

have not managed to become a political force and belong 

to various political parties. By contrast, the Vanniyar and 

Kallar castes don’t claim royal links; therefore, it is easier 

for them to unite politically. Now, there are two famous 

Naidus, Vijaya Kanth and Vaikō, who are developing 

political identities. Vaik ō, for instance, has a big following

Pandurangan’s comments reflected the fluidity of non-Brahmin caste status and the importance of caste solidarity for political and economic goals. Most of my informants were not so explicit on this subject of the relation of caste support for politicians.

The Epigrapher’s Dream of Reform

I am to interview Dr G. Vijayavenugopal at his office at École Française d’Extrême-Orient \(EFEO\) in Puducherry. Scholars at this institute, which has a large collection of Indological books and palm-leaf manuscripts in its library, do research on the literature, archaeology, history, art history and epigraphy of Tamil Nadu. Dr Vijayavenugopal, a senior research fellow, is the epigrapher and historian here. After I questioned him about several of my own historical research interests—he is a great source of information—we started the interview.

I was born in Trichi \(Tiruchirappalli\) into a Vaiṣṇava 

Telugu-speaking family. My great-grandfather had 

created a *r* *āmānuja* *kūṭam*, a charity that offers food, on 

Sattani Street, which my uncle’s son maintains to this 

day. When I was very small, every morning at 5:00, my 

grandfather took all the boys in the family, dressed in 

silk *dhoti*s and with *nāmam*s on their foreheads, by horse 

and cart to the Srirangam temple for *pūjā*. After returning 

home, we did more *p* *ūjā*. By 7:30, we had breakfast.

Our ancestors were originally bangle sellers. An old 

mother goddess, Aṅkaḷa Parameśvarī \(Pārvatī\), is our 

family deity; this gives a clue to our ancient family 

tradition. I think that she was originally a jungle goddess 

\(Koṟṟavai\) of the chieftains in ancient times; warriors used 

to cut off their own heads for an offering to this goddess 

to ensure the king’s victory. Goddesses like her were the 

original Dravidian deities. They were reinterpreted as 

176







the pan-Indian goddess Durgā who was the protector of 

jungle forts. Later, they were linked with Śaiva myths, 

turning the mother goddess into the Śaiva goddess 

Pārvatī.

My grandfather did a B. A. degree at St. Joseph’s 

College in Srirangam. The most prosperous non-Brahmin 

parents tried to get their children into this school; it was 

known for providing a good education. My grandfather 

was a stationmaster at Tiruchirappalli town station, 

division headquarters of the southern railroad. Most of 

his relatives were uneducated and got into the railroad 

system at various levels. My grandfather owned some 

fertile lands. One of his younger brothers was an 

administrative officer of the Srirangam temple. He was 

also a Trichi district cooperative bank director.

My father studied at St. Joseph’s as well and specialised 

in chemistry and math. He then did a degree in teaching. 

He was the only non-Brahmin in this degree programme. 

Madras Medical College invited him to study there, but 

his grandmother didn’t like this idea; ‘You’ll be a butcher,’ 

she said, ‘doing post-mortems.’ Therefore, my father 

rejected the invitation to study medicine and became a 

high-school teacher. 

My father’s first wife died, and there were no children. 

His second wife, my mother, was born near Anantapur 

in what became known as Andhra Pradesh after the 

linguistic division of Madras Presidency into two states. 

My mother came alone to Trichi; her brothers stayed 

behind.

When it was time to go to school in Tiruvarur, where 

my father got a job, I was initiated into education by 

wearing religious marks, receiving a notebook and slate, 

going in procession with other children to the school and 

prostrating myself before the teacher. The teacher took my 

index finger and wrote the alphabet on the slate. Along 

with the other children, I received sweets. 

Attracted to the Dravidian movement, my father was 

a member of the South Indian Welfare Association, later 

renamed the South Indian Liberal Foundation, which 



177







was a precursor to the Dravida Kazhagam \(DK\) and the 

Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam \(DMK\).

At the time, some non-Brahmin leaders were emerging 

and joined the Justice Party. Brahmins were advisors to 

the British and were IAS \(Indian Administrative Service\) 

officers. Therefore, the social structure, with power 

monopolised by the Brahmins, remained the same as 

under the former rulers. Rich and poor non-Brahmins 

were kept out of choice positions. Although my father 

was attracted to the Justice Party, he didn’t join it because 

of its atheist position. By this time, my father was not 

going to the temple, which my grandfather had done. Still 

he had some piety.

My own wife is a Śaiva Brahmin. We were classmates 

and had a ‘love marriage’. The Dravidian movement had 

given people new hope to get out of the caste system 

through inter-caste marriage. Therefore, my love marriage 

was backed by the new ideology. 

This reform orientation was different from the 

International Congress Party. Congress preached 

democracy, yet allowed school hostels to be divided 

between Brahmins and non-Brahmins. Periyar broke 

away and formed his own movement \(the Self-Respect 

Movement\).10 My father’s younger sister’s son was active 

in it. All party members wore black shirts. My cousin 

made me wear one and go to a Periyar lecture. One of 

my classmates, Nedumaran, was in the DMK and was a 

proponent of Tamil nationalism. 

In 1949, when C. N. Annadurai left the DK to form a 

branch of the party in Madurai, which became the DMK, 

I became the DMK’s official propaganda secretary and 

wrote the messages for billboards and pamphlets. When 

I was doing my M. A. in Tamil language and literature, 

my teacher T. P. Meenakshisundaram11 belonged to the 

Indian National Congress Party. I stood first in his class. 

He tried to attract me and other students to Congress, but 

I was not interested. 

My father taught M. Karunanidhi of the DMK, who 

became the Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu in 1969 when 

C. N. Annadurai, the DMK’s founder, died.12 My father 

178







helped the teachers to get better pensions by going with 

them to Karunanidhi’s office. When Karunanidhi saw my 

father, his former teacher, he helped them.

I became a teacher because I wanted to be objective. 

When I did a paper in philosophy, comparing Advaita, 

Viśiṣṭādvaita and Vīraśaivism, I saw contradictions 

between their philosophies and their temple cultures. As 

intellectuals, philosophers were in an ivory tower. Through 

education, I wanted to make reforms. I still struggle in 

my heart between an anti-caste, socialist revolution and 

a religious philosophy. For instance, Mayawati, the Dalit 

Chief Minister of Uttar Pradesh, often13 gave reservations 

to Brahmins. \[That is, even though Mayawati, a Dalit, 

came to power, Brahmins have still managed to hold onto 

power through political alliances with Dalits.\]

In my paper for the 2007 symposium, ‘Sri Vaisnavism—

Tomorrow’, held at the University of Madras,14 I argued 

that we must make everyone equal. The DMK denied 

God to get equality. But Periyar substituted a human 

political leader for God as the rallying point. People 

became devotees of Periyar or Gandhi. This is a religious 

phenomenon. It is also a family phenomenon. The leader 

is like the mother of children: ‘I will take care of you’. In 

Śrīvaiṣṇavism, surrender or *prapatti* is absolute surrender. 

For women, this blends with their traditional chastity, 

that is, absolute surrender to their husbands. Therefore, 

this creates a beautiful conceptual matrix or ideological 

structure, which the leader can manipulate. People want 

a leader. If equalisation equals democratisation, then 

there will be no leader, and people won’t be attracted. 

Even now, women can’t get a ration card because they do 

not head their families. Therefore, we have a God-*darśana*

structure and a family model of society. 

Even though Periyar was an atheist, he remained a 

patron and trustee of a temple. This suggests that he was 

a hypocrite. I now vote according to the leader and have 

no party affiliation. Parties are all the same these days. I 

have no belief in the leadership.

I no longer do *pūjā*. My wife does her Brahmin *pūjā*

by herself. I won’t join. I am sometimes invited to give 

179







lectures because of my knowledge. I try to provide 

political ideas for a new path. Therefore, I don’t follow the 

tradition. Vedic tradition merely protects the jobs of priests 

in the temples, and the only way the Sanskrit tradition is 

maintained today is by Brahmins knowing a few mantras. 

There is no real Vedic knowledge now. Brahmin priests, 

for example, sometimes doāgamic rituals for the village 

goddess Mārīyammaṉ. And if they are asked, ‘What 

is the source of these rituals?’ they say the source is in 

an ‘appendix’ to theāgamas, which implies a later and 

therefore non-authoritative addition, especially because it 

is about goddess worship. 

In the twelfth century, Rāmānuja attempted to convert 

non-Brahmins to Brahminism. After the fifteenth century, 

no non-Brahmin had an *ācārya* or formal initiation, 

*samāśrayaṇam*.15 The twentieth-century figure Jayakanthan 

\(b. 1934\), who became a Marxist and a novelist,16 wanted 

to convert all non-Brahmins to Brahminism. In their 

hearts, however, Brahmins still think that there is a 

radical difference. Non-Brahmins can’t be upgraded. 

Brahmins make the \(religious\) law, and therefore they 

have to be the ones to change it. The poet Subramania 

Bharati, an Iyer Brahmin, initiated a Harijan.17 This was a 

minor exception to the general caste ideology. Therefore, 

there is lots of rhetoric but no practice. Nevertheless, 

non-Brahmins played important roles. The non-Brahmin 

Kallars \(weavers\), for instance, protected the temple 

Alakarkōyiḻ against the Muslims.

In his symposium paper,18 Vijayavenugopal argues that even before the twelfth century, inscriptions referring to donations to Viṣṇu temples always said that the Śrīvaiṣṇavas should protect them, even though Brahmins controlled the temple administration, rituals, and so forth. This, according to Vijayavenugopal, suggests a distinction between these two groups of Vaiṣṇavas—Brahmins and other Vaiṣṇavas— although Śrīvaiṣṇavas ideally are a casteless and classless society, staunch devotees of Viṣṇu, and respectful not only to humans but also to animals. Unfortunately, this broad 



180







concept of Śrīvaiṣṇavism was abandoned, he noted. The whole Vaiṣṇava tradition became:

caste-centred \(that is, Brahmin-centred\) and logo-centred 

\(that is, Sanskrit-centred\). I feel that if we want to create 

a broad base to continue the Viṣṇu faith, this kind of 

exclusiveness has to be discarded and ways and means 

have to be found to restore this glorious tradition.19

Vijayavenugopal argues that he was not the first to call for reform. By the late thirteenth century, Piḷḷai Lokācārya and his younger brother Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Perumāḷ Nāyaṉār called for reform. Their works, Vijayavenugopal thinks, represent a Vaiṣṇava tradition that is casteless and classless, which must be distinguished from the Vedic tradition. 

Vijayavenugopal offered examples of how these authors 

refute the Vedic-oriented objections to a casteless and classless tradition. For instance,

these Śrīvaiṣṇava authors argue that the hymns of 

the *āḻvār*s are not inferior to the Vedas. Even though 

Nammāḻvār was a *śūdra*, his hymns are divine. There 

are no caste restrictions for *prapatti* because everyone 

who surrenders will reach the divine. Even animals 

have attained liberation. Brahmins who chide low-caste 

devotees will themselves become outcastes. 

Low castes who are pure and properly behaved should 

be worshipped by others. The devout are not *śūdra*s, but 

like Brahmins. Even earlier, Rāmānuja, who ate with 

low castes, thereby breaking the taboo on inter-dining, 

had endorsed this perspective by arguing that teaching 

Vedānta is not restricted to Brahmins. 

After all these examples, Vijayavenugopal concluded:

Unfortunately, this tradition was abandoned. How can it 

be revived? How are we to make people feel that they are 

Srīvaiṣṇavas and not identified with their castes? How 

are we to realise this in today’s culture of reservations in 

the name of castes? A sincere effort should be made to 

achieve these Śrīvaiṣṇava ideals. True, it will be difficult 

in the field of religion and caste. Even so, it should be easy 

181







to open up Sanskrit and Tamil education by teaching the 

religious and philosophical texts to all Śrīvaiṣṇavas who 

want to study them. Here, the Śrīvaiṣṇava *maṭha*s have a 

role to play. Their activities should be broad-based.20

Vijayavenugopal’s view of Śrīvaiṣṇava history was that there was once a golden age consisting of a casteless society, or at least leaders who believed in one. However, this declined over the centuries, despite periodic protests, and now, he argued, it has to be restored through reform.



The *Ācārya* Chosen by Lord Vēṅkaṭeśvara

In 1931, B. K. Rangachari wrote that:

The Prabandhic movement of the Tengalais ... led to the 

rapid spread of Vaisnavism among all grades of people, 

and it became a popular creed. As a logical result of this 

movement several Non-Brahman castes, such as the 

Vanniyans, began to claim superiority, or at any rate, 

equality with the Sri Vaisnava Brahmans. This view was 

carried to such an extreme that certain Vanniyans took to 

the sacred thread, to the study of the Vedas and even to 

the establishment of temples and temple worship. Such a 

group of Vanniyans exists in Kumilam \(Kumalam\) village 

in South Arcot District .... So far, they have remained 

isolated and no mixture with Sri Vaisnava Brahmans has 

been affected. In spite of the theoretical views expressed 

by the Tengalai Brahmans, they have not mixed with 

them in any way.21

Kumalam is a Vanniyar \(Nayakama\) village. I met two priests there wearing *lu ṅgi*s and sacred threads, with their hair tied back in a knot. People addressed them as ‘*swami*’. The women of their families had diamond earrings, nose jewels, and gold bracelets; they were obviously well-off. One of the priests, Kannapiran Nadaswami, told me the history of the temple.

My ancestor Ananta Nadaswami founded the temple. 

He used to visit Tirupati. According to both the family’s 

182







tradition and the *purāṇa* about the village’s temple,22

Lord Vēṅkaṭeśvara appeared in a dream one night to 

him and said, ‘Take the image of Vēṅkaṭeśvara that is 

in the Puṣkāraṇi temple tank and install it at a temple 

in your village.’ The Lord himself then initiated Ananta 

Nadaswami and taught him the mantras. 

Our village is a substitute for Tirupati and thus has 

regional importance. People from other villages and even 

Chennai come here because of its sacred reputation. If 

they can’t go to Tirupati for important rituals, they have 

them done here. The Tirupati influence is strong in Tamil 

villages. Members of all castes go there on pilgrimages. 

Sometimes, they walk there, which is over 200 kilometres 

from here. This is inspired in part by the fact that school 

children memorise an example of Tamil prose, written by 

a non-Brahmin, about a pilgrimage to Tirupati.

In the temple, I saw a 20-foot tall image of Lord Vēṅkaṭeśvara. It was coated in gold and adorned with many jewels. A statue of the family’s ancestor, Ananta Nadaswami, was positioned before the Lord. The priest provided a leaflet about their Brahmotsava, which reveals that this festival is like those of major temples. They raise the flag at the beginning and lower it at the end. They take the *cakra* image to the tank, moreover, and everyone has a sacred bath. At the end, they perform twelve propitiations to compensate for any omissions during the festival. The chariot festival too is very popular at this place. 

Kannapiran described the family’s priestly roles.

Four families act as priests for three months each. We wear 

sacred threads and call ourselves Banya Brahmins, that is, 

Brahmins who serve the Banyas \(Vanniyars\) or ‘Brahmins 

among the Vanniyars’. Members of the Vanniyar priest 

families in this village are strictly vegetarian. 

When I asked the village priests whichāgama tradition they followed for the temple rituals, they told me that they had Sanskrit texts written on palm leaves, and studied these before becoming priests. When I asked what kind of mantra they use, because this might identify theāgama, they did not tell me. 

183







In any case, the language of the *pūjā* combined Sanskrit and Tamil. Their language of conversation was colloquial, like that of Vanniyars in other villages. 

Kannapiran continued with his oral history by saying that

surrounding villages invite us to their temples to perform 

rituals, even though they are not Vaiṣṇava temples. We 

will conduct a *kumbha-abhiśekha* ritual or a *brahmotsava*

for their *amma* *ṉ* \(goddess\) temple. Every twelve years, 

temples need this ritual to recharge their energy. Acting 

as Brahmin priests for the area is good business for the 

non-Brahmin priests of Kumanam village, but there is a 

big project in store. We need to renovate our own temple.

When I talked to a woman of a priestly family, I learned that it has matrimonial alliances only with the priests and cooks of the village. They never give their daughters in marriage to other families, although they accept girls for their sons from outside, if they are strict vegetarians, have had *samāśrayaṇam*, and follow the precepts according to family customs. A priest from their own family gives *samāśrayaṇam* and the rites of passage called *saṃskāra*s to them. 

I noticed that there are hardly any middle-aged men in the 

village. Those men probably have other jobs.

worked for the government in personnel, tourism and

interested in rituals and temples. I am not against prayer; 

however, I think it is more important to love others and 

be kind. For this reason, I am fond of the philosophy of

Considering that compassion is the only path to

to me, five were Te ṉkalai and one Vaṭakalai. One Teṉkalai reported: ‘The Vaṭakalai Brahmins say that they alone are Brahmins; all Teṉkalais are non-Brahmins and therefore merely Bhāgavatas. Vaṭakalais often have the names Rāma and Krishna, moreover, whereas Teṉkalais use the name Bālāji.’

On the drive back to Puducherry, Kalladan and I discussed 

the function of the *sabhā*s, which were prevalent everywhere I went. Many, he remarked, are combinations of men’s clubs, charities and shared interests \(which vary from religion to poetry\). Judging from the number of older men I saw involved, the *sabhā*s also offer purposeful activities for seniors.

Kalladan had mentioned that his older brother Vanidasan 

\(1915–74\) had been a famous poet. At first, he wrote about nature and love. Then, after joining Periyar and C. N. Annadurai, he wrote political poems, eventually publishing more than seventeen collections. He was honoured by the Tamil Nadu and French governments.27

rebirth. I read many books on Advaita Vedānta. At that

very popular and had inspired my mother and then me 

to become vegetarian. Our household was like any other 

average Hindu household: we worshipped many deities, 

chanted hymns, performed vows and celebrated festivals. 

I began to study Sanskrit with a Śrīvaiṣṇava scholar.

In 2005, when I was in Europe, I was exploring 

Śrīvaiṣṇavism on the Internet. I began to interact with 

Sri Ranga Sri, the publisher and scholar in Srirangam. He 

told me that *samāśrayaṇam* was the only way to achieve 

liberation and sent me to a site that answered many 

questions about liberation. Later, someone suggested that 

I contact Dr V. Sadagopan, a Va ṭakalai living in New York, 

who was the moderator of a Yahoo group. He told me that 

Rāmānuja promised *mokṣa*.

Viṣṇu was always my personally chosen form of God 

\(*iṣṭadevatā*\) and I had been influenced by the television 

version of the *R* *āmāyaṇa*. One of my aunt’s colleagues was 

a Te ṉkalai Brahmin *ācārya*. He didn’t think that it was 

a good idea to have initiation because it would be very 

difficult to keep all the rules. Sadagopam convinced me, 

however, that it should be done, because even the desire 

for the ritual is installed in one’s mind by the Divine 

Couple.

In February 2005, I was in Paris. Because I was worried 

about the food rules and the fact that I could only worship 

Viṣṇu if I were initiated, I talked to my parents because 

initiation would add complications to our relationship in 

the future. Despite some initial opposition on their part, 

I decided to go ahead, worried that I could die before 

being initiated. My parents met Sriivan Sathakopa Sri 

Narayana Yatindra Mahadesikan, the forty-fifth *jīyar* of 

the Ahobila *maṭha*, to see if he were willing to perform 

the ritual. We fixed the auspicious time and place for it

when I was still in Paris. It would be during the retreat

After I returned from Paris, I went with my extended 

family there for *samāśrayaṇam* and the additional 

Vaṭakalai ritual. The *jīyar* was very flexible. He explained 

the importance of being vegetarian and then said that if 

I could not follow all the dietary restrictions, I could eat 

meat because ultimately it is the soul’s liberation that is 

important, not what one eats. One should not worship 

another god, however, and not hurt another devotee. The 

day I was initiated, I received my *jīyar*’s sandals \(*pāduka*s\), 

which he wears once and then gives to a disciple. The 

*pāduka*s are now in our household shrine. I saw my *jīyar*

just one more time before he died. 

At the time of my initiation, the *jīyar* chided my parents 

for not being Śrīvaiṣṇavas, because he knew that Yadavas 

have traditionally worshipped Viṣṇu, and therefore only 

I was being sensible. At first my father was indifferent 

to this goading; however, the next year both he and my 

mother were initiated.

I don’t chant all the verses of my *jīyar*’s lineage, just his. 

I have done several *kaiṅkarya*s or services; for instance, the 

day I was initiated, I did one. I have also visited many 

*divyade* ś*a*s. 

When I asked Suganya if she had experienced any discrimination as a non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava, she mentioned the following:

Once at Srirangam, the *pras* *ādam* was first given to 

Srirangam Brahmins, then other Brahmins and then 

only to non-Brahmins like myself. On another occasion, 

however, many people were waiting to enter the temple. 

For some reason, a Brahmin took me immediately into 

the sanctum all the way up to the deity. Perhaps it was 

because I looked wealthy and fair.

Colour-coding, as anthropologists have long warned, is a fallible guide to caste.

Before I arrived in Puducherry, I thought that one distinctive feature about Puducherry would be its French heritage. Coming from Quebec, the French part of Canada, I was curious how much French culture remained. From my everyday experience in the area, I encountered the proverbial *baguette* and *café au lait *for breakfast, and a few fine French restaurants in the colonial \(and tourist\) section of town, called ‘White Town’, along with architectural monuments such as Our Lady of the Immaculate Conception Cathedral, the wonderful colonial architecture, and a very active Alliance Française. 

I found little French still spoken, except by the many 

French tourists attracted here, visiting French scholars, people who had worked for the French government, or those whose ancestors had had an important position in the French East India Company, and who had opted for French citizenship, yet continued to live in Puducherry. More to the point, there seemed to be no connection of the French heritage with Śrīvaiṣṇavism, except for some research by scholars on this religious tradition \(such as the Vaṭakalai scholar Varada Desikan and the epigraphist Vijayavenugopal, at the École Française d’Extrême-Oriente\).

Aside from several Śrīvaiṣṇava temples in Puducherry and 

its outskirts, the hub for the general region—that extended beyond the Union Territory into Tamil Nadu proper—was the Emperumāṉār *maṭha* in Tirukkoyilur, which had non-Brahmin disciples from many castes, such as Vanniyars, Naidus, Reddiyars and Dalits. \(I will discuss this *maṭha* in another chapter.\)

Many Bhāgavatas, in Puducherry and its surrounding 

villages, whom I had interviewed, had been initiated by Srinivasa Ramanuja Jiyar of this *maṭha* \(or, if they were elderly, by his father Tiruvenkata Ramanuja Jiyar\). These disciples periodically visited their *jīyar* and maintained close relations. Śrīvaiṣṇavas had a special connection to places further away, such as Sriperumbudur. Dr Pandurangan, for instance, was the disciple of Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar and periodically visited him at Sriperumbudur. Others wanted to visit Rāmānuja’s 

195







birthplace at Sriperumbudur or the *samādhi* of Bhuvanagiri in a nearby field. Still others had a special connection with Tirumalai-Tirupati, despite the distance. Many had gone on pilgrimage there and one village, Kumanam, had established a local reputation as a Tirupati replica, as it were. The draw, therefore, seemed more towards the north rather than south to Srirangam, the other great Śrīvaiṣṇava centre. Nevertheless, many had gone on pilgrimage there too.

Speaking of sacred geographies reminds me of the 

one I discussed in Chapter 2, which was related to the late nineteenth-century non-Brahmin leaders Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri, and Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. As I noted in that context, there were temples and *samādhi*s connected with these figures: Nāvalar’s to the north of Puducherry, Bhuvanagiri’s near Cuddalore, and Nārāyana Dāsar’s near Tirukkoyilur. Today, these are functioning as local temples. Although Bhuvanagiri’s shrine was on the verge of historical oblivion, Nāvalar’s shrine, where an annual festival is held in his honour, maintains some links with the past, and continues to serve as a local shrine.
