Stories from Chennai and Beyond

These oral histories emerged from the heart of Chennai, its surrounding suburbs, nearby towns such as Sriperumbudur, and villages between the hubs. They represent several generations—from ‘seniors’ to students— many castes, and both men and women. Each story expresses a lived experience, which is the raw material from which narratives are fashioned.

The Grandson and the Outcaste Hamlet

The old Ambassador car lurched through yet another pothole. The monsoon had reminded the city once again that natural forces can halt human progress. The pock-marked road wound to the northwestern edge of Chennai’s sprawling suburbs, relentlessly intruding on the paddy fields. Endless storefronts stacked with stainless-steel pots, tires or saris—this is the land of the small businessman—made it impossible to know where one municipality ended and another began. Occasionally, our driver stopped for directions. In a staccato voice, someone listed the turns and gesticulated toward the horizon. Again,

we bumped along for what must have been an hour in heavy traffic. As we neared our destination1 and entered a maze of alleys, we stopped for instructions more often.

And then, miraculously, we were there in what had once

been a paṟaiccēri (outcaste hamlet),2 now called Ekangipuram, adjacent to a huge cloth mill, the oldest and largest in the area near a major train station. Suburbanisation had engulfed the area with roads, schools and hospitals. Land in this once-despised hamlet was fetching a good price and many owners were selling out. Some Dalits had moved into suburban neighborhoods with a mix of castes, hoping to hide their identity and take advantage of new opportunities. Others took what little they had into the city core to start new lives, only to find themselves stuck in slums, their search for justice ending in the terrifying struggle for survival.

A man directed us down a tiny alley, almost impassable

because of motorcycles parked on the edge. At that moment, something caught our attention: a Śrīvaiṣṇava emblem on a house wall, the ubiquitous conch, nāmam and discus3 that adorn, sanctify and protect place or person. We had arrived. Our host ran forward to greet us, bowing with hands folded, uttering vaṇakkam (welcome).

This handsome young man is J. N. B. Chakravarthi

Ramanuja Dasar (Chakra for short). In his household also live his wife Mahalakshmi (who is pregnant with their first child; she had a son later that year), his mother Lokanayaki. and his maternal grandmother Kumbhavalai Nacciyar. Chakra works in an automobile engineering business on Mount Road in Chennai. Several years later, he worked in the accounts department of Surya TV. Mahalakshmi has a B.Com. degree. She is a medical transcriptionist. The transcripts are sent from London and the United States; when they arrive in oral form, she writes them into formal medical records on the computer, and then she sends the computer files back.

The family’s religious identity has been defined by

formal initiation (samāśrayaṇam) into Śrīvaiṣṇavism. The male members of the family had been the first to be initiated. Their

names include the initiation name Ramanuja Dasar. According to Mahalakshmi, the women were initiated only after marriage.

When Chakra’s mother, Lokanayaki, married, she did

this ritual and began to worship Perumāḷ whom her

husband’s family considered to be the supreme god.4

And then, so did her mother and her father but not her

brother. My own family worshipped Perumāḷ, although

only I have had initiation. This was after my marriage to

Chakra. A Brahmin ācārya initiated us.

We entered their home. A large house-shrine dominates the main room. Chakra prostrated himself before it and then honoured K. K. A. Venkatachari and me with ceremonial shawls. Photos of deities include Vēṅkaṭeśvara, Krishna, Rāma, Varadarāja, Perumāḷ, and alsoāṇṭāḷ, the female poetess who is also considered a goddess, the consort of Lord Raṅganātha. There is a small image of Rāmānuja, too. Chakra said, ‘The family does daily p ūjā, chanting together short poetic works of the āḻvārs5 or a selection of Nammāḻvār’s verses.’6 I asked about other rituals that require the chanting of āḻvār hymns. ‘We chant some at weddings,’ Chakra said, ‘and during a wife’s pregnancy.’7

My attention turned to the wall next to that shrine. As in

many Hindu household shrines, it displays pictures of earlier generations: deceased parents, grandparents, aunts and uncles. The conversation focussed on a picture of one of Chakra’s grandfathers, J. J. Thirunarayana Ramanuja Dasar (1901–83), a very distinguished looking man.

Thirunarayana, as I will call him here, came from a poor

family. Chakra told me the following: ‘My grandparents8 had a piece of agricultural land near Vellore, in a village called Kilamanavur. The village had a small Krishna temple. My great grandfather, Jaganathan, taught my grandfather with the help of local pandits.’

Vellore had absorbed Christian influence too, which explains its famous Christian Medical College and the fact that many Dalits had converted to Christianity. Other Dalits had turned to neo-Buddhism. Krishnaswamy (1916–73), for instance, studied in Madras, then returned to Vellore and, as a follower of Ambedkar’s neo-Buddhism, worked his entire life to improve Dalit well-being.9

Chakra told me more about the family’s history in Chennai.

My grandfather married Godai Nacciyar. They had

three sons and two daughters.10 The family lived outside

Chennai in this outcaste hamlet.11 My grandfather had a

good job. He was employed as a diesel-engine examiner

for the southern railway; his second son, my uncle

The British often argued that the railway, which they gradually introduced after 1853, would usher in capitalist modernity; it would create a work ethic, moreover, and dissolve caste. Even Marx claimed that it would bring progress to India by eliminating the hereditary divisions of labour. Nevertheless, it took over a century to achieve some progress. Dissolving caste was not part of this. In the meantime, railways created new hierarchies with Europeans and Eurasians at the top, Brahmins in choice ‘native positions’, such as stationmaster, and other castes in lower jobs, such as ironworkers. The obvious contradiction between the British rhetoric on equality and this workplace inequality12—status, pay, access to education, and other opportunities—contributed to agitation by Indian railway employees. By the 1920s, with a growing number of strikes, railway workers were becoming a dangerous force both for the Raj, which needed trains to transport goods and soldiers, and for Indian political parties.

For instance, to be successful, the Congress Party had

to compete with the Communist Party and other parties for the loyalty of these railway workers who daily experienced indignity. Mahatma Gandhi visited railway unions in order to win their support for Indian nationalism in general and for the Congress Party in particular. Many railway workers became Congress Party stalwarts, although they remained potential threats to any party in power even after Independence. Gradually, however, fair wages, good working conditions and democratic principles prevailed.

With that in mind, let us return to the interview. The

conversation turned to the family’s political affiliations. Chakra informed me about his family’s connection with the Congress Party.

My grandfather was a staunch member of the Congress

party, and his family had personal connections with

98

Mahatma Gandhi. When Gandhi came to Chennai, he

stayed with a Śrīvaiṣṇava family. The couple named

their daughter after Gandhi’s wife Kasturba. Later, this

daughter became the wife of J. N. Arangaram Ramanuja

Dasar, my uncle. The Congress connection has continued

into my generation.

Thirunarayana’s job in the railways and his political connection to Gandhi might have been related to Gandhi’s interest in winning support from railway workers. Now I understood the presence of a three-foot statue of Gandhi in Chakra’s home.

Chakra made it clear that his family has had additional

political connections:

My grandfather was close friends with Kamaraj. Known

for his honesty, Kamaraj worked in the Congress party and

went from the lowest level to the highest. He was from the

Nadar caste, which was prevalent in Ramanathapuram,

the southernmost district of Tamil Nadu. Nadars were

traditionally toddy tappers.13 Their status was not high;

they used to fight with outcastes over who had the right

to pull the festival chariot (on which the deity rides)

through the streets surrounding the temple. Kamaraj

became the Tamil Nadu Congress president and later

president of the National Congress Party. He had close

relations with Vaiṣṇavas because Nadar religion has some

Vaiṣṇava roots (see Appendix 6), but he also had good

relations with all religions.

C. Rajagopalachari—a Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava whose

daughter had married Mahatma Gandhi’s son—had been

Chief Minister in Tamil Nadu, Governor of Bengal and the

Governor-General of India. Then he became Chief Minister

in Tamil Nadu for a second time. People thought that with

his admirable statesmanship and good governance, he

was the only one who could handle the DMK, which was

becoming powerful. Kamaraj and other Congress non-

Brahmins protested his appointment on the basis that he

was a Brahmin. As a result, C.Rajagopalachari resigned

as Chief Minister, and Kamaraj became Chief Minister

in1954.

The family showed me a picture of Indira Gandhi with Kamaraj, who had just died and was lying in state (1975). Thirunarayana was there (even though he could barely be seen at the edge of the picture). ‘Ekangipuram still has some Congress party influence,’ Chakra said, ‘even though the DMK and AIDMK are also active in this area.’

Chakra remarked that his grandfather Thirunarayana was

also known as a very learned Śrīvaiṣṇava. ‘My grandfather was called a nityasūri (an eternal being) because of his exemplary behaviour, and an ubhayavedāntin because of his knowledge of both Tamil and Sanskrit scripture. He established a Vedic school where he and then later his sons taught Vedic hymns.’

As an ubhayavedāntin, Thirunarayana would have known

Tamil and some Sanskrit. There is another example of this phenomenon: members of a Dalit community learning and chanting Vedic hymns. Vasudha Narayanan describes one of these communities in Melkote. This group recites selections from the Vedas, including the Puruṣasūkta, and the Taittirīya Upaniṣad. I was told by others that Thirunarayana was in communication with this Melkote community, perhaps because the Melkote community’s spiritual advisor was Sri Govinda Ramanuja Dasar, who was from Ganesapuram in Madras,14 and Thirunarayana knew him. Aside from his religious knowledge, Thirunarayana knew several languages. His sons were also known for their linguistic skills: the first son was a scholar in Tamil, the second was good in English, and the third was proficient in Hindi.15

Thirunarayana was a leader in his neighbourhood.

According to Chakra, government documents show that Thirunarayana and one of his three sons fought with the municipality to change the name of the locale from the pejorative paṟaiccēri (outcaste hamlet) to Ekangipuram. After all, it contained the samādhi of (Kōyiḻ) Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, whom we have already encountered.16 Perhaps because Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ gave lectures and initiations in this paṟaiccēri to Dalits, he was buried here.

When the American scholar Raymond Williams17 visited

Ekangipuram in 1991, he interviewed two of Thirunarayana’s sons (who have since died). They told him that there were 15 Śrīvaiṣṇava Harijan families living there. They were strictly vegetarian, and, like orthodox Brahmins, did not even eat onions or garlic. They led bhajanas (devotional songs). One brother did the rituals for new temples in the area. The family, Williams wrote, took care of the burial place of the Ekāṅgi and his shrine in the adjacent building.

On the day of his visit, Williams witnessed a procession

of Śrīvaiṣṇavas at Ekangipuram. The people who gathered at the shrine were almost all Dalits, except for a few Brahmin visitors. They went through the colony with a group chanting some āḻvār verses at the beginning of the procession (the lead person was holding a copy of the Divyaprabandham, the Tamil Śrīvaiṣṇava scripture), and another group chanting bhajanas at the end of the procession and dancing. That day, the procession had two drummers, a person who played the cymbals and a harmonium player. The procession stopped at many houses and received honours: sacred water, sandal paste and kumkum on the feet. Women of the households prostrated themselves before those in the procession. By the time they returned to the samādhi, the group had grown to about 100 people. There was more chanting and dancing, followed by a lecture by a Brahmin aboutāṇṭāḷ.

Now back to the present. As we walked to the samādhi a

short distance away, Chakra said,

Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s disciple Appaṉ cared for the samādhi

after his death. After Appaṉ’s own expiry, his picture was

also hung in the shrine, near his master’s. My grandfather

and his family lived in a building adjacent to the samādhi,

caring for the shrine and tending to the daily worship of

the images. Members of my family continue to do this.’18

When we returned to Ekangipuram, a few weeks later, Śrīvaiṣṇavas had gathered in Chakra’s house for our visit. Chakra commented on the changes in this neighbourhood.

The honoured guest on this occasion was K. K. A. Venkatachari. His homily was on Nammāḻvār’s Tiruvāymoḻi 3.7.9.

This verse was apropos, given its focus on outcastes (here, the word is ca ṇṭāḷa) who are absorbed [in God], and even though they are the lowest of the low, inferior to the four castes (cāti; jāti)—that is, the four varṇas—that support the families on which society depends, they are our true masters. The implication is that even the Brahmins should serve them with humility. After reciting the verse, Venkatachari elucidated its meaning, drawing from commentaries by ācāryas between the thirteenth

102

and fifteenth century. Their basic point is that outcastes are pre-eminently qualified to serve God, because their essential nature is to serve others and therefore they exemplify the ideal of service, which others should imitate. As such, outcastes are the real masters or preceptors. Service to God and his devotees (kaiṅkarya) is, of course, a key concept of Śrīvaiṣṇavism. As the homily drew to a close, Chakra presented ceremonial shawls to Venkatachari and me, and performed pūjā. Then we examined the many family pictures.

At the end of our visit, we walked to Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s

samādhi. I learned that there is now a legal dispute.22 Someone had seized control of the property, which had become valuable as part of metropolitan Chennai. Several weeks later, I returned to Chakra’s and found that the entire samādhi had been bulldozed. One chapter of local history had disappeared. Now, not only was Chakra’s family almost the only Śrīvaiṣṇava one left in the area, but there was also no trace of Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ.

The Granddaughter and Rāmānuja’s House

N. D. Sabari Natchi is Chakra’s first cousin and the granddaughter of J. J. Thirunarayana Ramanuja Dasar. Her father23 followed in her grandfather’s footsteps, working for the southern railways, and eventually became a manager; her mother24 was a teacher for the Corporation of Madras. One of her brothers is a mechanical engineer and the other is a civil engineer. Her husband Sivagnanan is director of a Central Government department that promotes small enterprises throughout Tamil Nadu and Puducherry. Sabari Natchi works for the Central Government office of the Oil and Natural Gas Corporation as a purchase officer.

I first met Sabari Natchi and her husband at

K.K.A.Venkatachari’s house in Triplicane. It was the Tamil month of Mārkaḻi (December–January) when Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins meet in the early morning to chantāṇṭāḷ’s Tiruppāvai, 30 verses about maidens gathering in the early morning to go

103

to the temple to perform a vow.25 Ostensibly, the maidens do so to find good husbands. On a deeper level, though, they do so to serve the Lord. Sabari Natchi and her husband were invited guests on this occasion. After the recitation, Venkatachari introduced them to me. They invited us to visit them the following weekend.

And there we went. Another long sojourn took us to the

even more distant northwestern suburbs of Chennai, this time to a place named Athipet, part of Ambattur. As we neared our destination, the roads were so muddy that we almost gave up for fear that the old car would get stuck. We finally came to Mahatma Gandhi Road and an open field with a palatial new house. Checking the address, we realised that we had reached our destination. As we climbed out of the car, we saw the words ‘Rāmānujar Illam’ (Rāmānuja’s House) on the façade. And there were Sabari Nacchi, Sivagnanam and their two sons: S. Nedumaalavan and S. Shree Saranathaperumal. They greeted us and offered us ceremonial shawls.

Our conversation began with the story of Sabari Natchi’s

life. ‘Until marriage,’ she said, ‘I lived in Ekangipuram. My brother still lives there. My family could not find a suitable Śrīvaiṣṇava match for me, and so they turned to a Śaiva family that worships Murukaṉ.’ Sivagnanam chimed in: ‘I made it to the short list and promised to follow Śrīvaiṣṇavism and be a strict vegetarian. When I had samāśrayaṇam, I was renamed Srinivasin, but I don’t use that name except within the family.’

As we sat down after tea, I asked them: ‘What is the best

aspect of Śrīvaiṣṇavism?’ They eagerly responded.

Sabari Natchi: ‘We are very fond of Rāmānuja. He rejected

untouchability; no one is big or small. There is equality.

This allows us to live with satisfaction and in a decent

way, which is sometimes difficult to do in our community.’

Sivagnanam: ‘In the Epics, Krishna describes how to

follow values and morals. We find these in Rāmānuja

who says, “I can see the Lord in the form of lower caste

people.” I am impressed, moreover, that Rāmānuja

revealed the mantra om, namo nārāyaṇāya to everyone.’26

Despite successful careers, a happy home, and deep Śrīvaiṣṇava identity, the family occasionally encounters the old outcaste stigma. Sabari Natchi and her sons, for instance, have not yet had samāśrayaṇam. Nevertheless, they want it. Sabari Natchi explained:

When my grandfather was ill, he told his first son to get

initiation from the same ācārya who had performed the

initiation for him. Now that ācārya’s son is doing the

initiations. However, when I went to him, along with

others from my community, he refused to do it. I want

to follow the instructions of my grandfather and have an

ācārya from this family perform the initiation. I want to

chant from this lineage during the pūjā, moreover, in the

traditional way. I do not want to deviate from the family

tradition.

Sivagnanam remarked, ‘Go to the person who is willing to do the initiation. When driven away, why follow the forefather? Your grandfather would not go to such an ācārya if he were alive today!’ With the interview concluding, we went on a tour

of their new home, ‘Rāmānuja’s house’, including its shrine room with its contemporary pictures of the deities.

For centuries, the entry of Dalits into major Śrīvaiṣṇava

temples had been contentious, as the following story of Tiruppāṇāḻvār reveals:

One day a minstrel, duly aware of his outcaste status

at the temple, was singing to the Lord from outside the

Srirangam temple. A priest who was fetching water

from the Kaveri for the ritual tried to chase him away

from the river to prevent him from polluting it. The Lord

himself intervened and chastised the priest in a dream

for his behavior. The next day, the priest himself carried

Tiruppāṇ on his shoulder into the temple’s sanctum. On

seeing the Lord, the āḻvār sang the ten verses known as the

Amalanātippirāṉ.29

This story of Tiruppāṇ reveals the desire of some Brahmins in the past to keep outcastes outside the temples. The command of God was to bring them inside. This might well reflect a growing tension in the community at that time, between Brahmanical views on orthodox purity (which considered outcastes impure and therefore not allowed in the sacred temple space) and the inclusive Śrīvaiṣṇava theology (which argued that any devotee of God is to be accepted and honoured). The orthodox eventually won; outcastes could not enter the major temples until the mid-twentieth century.

Sriperumbudur, the birthplace of Rāmānuja, also excluded

outcastes from the temple. For this reason, the bronze image of Rāmānuja was taken in procession around the temple in the Tamil month of Tai (mid-January to mid-February), during a three-day festival at Sriperumbudur, which commemorates the installation of Rāmānuja’s image there.30 Śrīvaiṣṇavas have believed, after all, that Rāmānuja is both divine and the mediator between the Supreme God and humans; by surrendering to an ācārya or jīyar, they are in point of fact surrendering to Rāmānuja himself, which guarantees salvation.

During this festival, outcastes had the right to carry the

palanquin. Just as the gracious deity went outside his home or

107

temple on special occasions, so that everyone could see him, and he could proffer the opportunity for service, devotion and salvation, so did Rāmānuja. He had (and still has) an iconic body. The Lord going in procession outside the temple, which was common in most temples, was important, because even though outcastes could not go inside the temple proper, they could perform service outside the temple and have darśana of God there. With Dalit political mobilisation in the twentieth century, Dalits no longer accepted their exclusion from the temple and relegation of their darśana to the streets. Nonetheless, they have maintained the tradition of carrying the palanquin as an honour that belongs to their community.

‘My grandfather was instrumental,’ said Sabari Natchi,

‘in improving the relation of the Harijans with Śrī Rāmānuja’s temple authorities in Sriperumbudur and agitated for their right to enter the temple.’ In this activity, I might add, Thirunarayana could have been inspired by Gandhi’s visit to Vellore31 and Pallikonda. When Gandhi visited a temple in Pallikonda in 1934, he was told that the outcastes who had experienced severe discrimination in the area were not allowed to enter. Gandhi declared that he would not enter the temple until the outcastes too could go inside. Caste Hindus denied his request, and so Gandhi went away.32

Gandhi agitated for temple entry, not only at this temple

but also throughout India, including many places in Tamil Nadu. Thirunarayana had political and family connections with the Mahatma, and if Thirunarayana had been at this attempted temple entry, or at least heard about it or about one of the many other events when Gandhi agitated for temple entry in Tamil Nadu, he might have been inspired to work for the opening of the Sriperumbudur temple to Harijans. According to Sabari Natchi:

I became a clerk and then the superintendent and

I wanted fruitful gains; any religious act multiplies 100

I saw him last month and dedicated my book to him,

The interview drew to a close, and the mood changed. It was the Tamil harvest festival called Pongal, held in January (the Tamil month of Tai), which is when the sun begins its journey northward for the next six months. ‘Pongal’ means to boil over; it symbolises fertility and abundance. The season’s first rice—which is made into a sweet with milk, cardamom, jaggery, raisins, and cashews—is offered to Surya, the sun god. It is cooked outside in the sunlight in a decorated pot. I went with Dr Parandhaman, his wife, and other relatives to the rooftop, which had been decorated. We sat and ate our Pongal on banana leaves. On another occasion, I went with him to his native village Nariyampakkam and met his brothers and other Śrīvaiṣṇavas there.

* * *

Many topics were covered in my interviews in and around Chennai, from sectarian and personal religious beliefs and activities, to views of formal initiation, religious leadership, organisations with religious aspects (such as sabhās), caste (especially regarding Brahmins, but also other castes, including Dalits), and the relation of both religion and caste to politics. Because I found that many of these topics were covered in similar ways in my interviews from other regions of Tamil Nadu, I will save discussion of their similarities and differences for the book’s final chapter, and in this chapter and Chapters 3 and 4, I will conclude simply by noting some of the region’s highlights.

In the case of greater Chennai, several points stand out. One

is the fact that conversations often mentioned Sriperumbudur, the birthplace of Rāmānuja, the key figure of Śrīvaiṣṇavism. In fact, in the area surrounding this temple, including nearby Kanchipuram, were born many key Śrīvaiṣṇava figures: besides Rāmānuja and his family, there were Tirumaḻicaiāḻvār, Tirukkaccinampi, Mutaliyāṇṭāṉ, Empār, and Kūreśa. Ifound

153

that the people I interviewed from Chennai and beyond had far more connections with Sriperumbudur than with the famous temples in Kanchipuram. In part, this can be explained by the fact that the key organiser for my fieldwork was K.K.A.Venkatachari, whose ancestors go back to Rāmānuja’s nephew Mutaliyāṇṭāṉ. Venkatachari’s family has had a relationship with the Sriperumbudur temple over generations, including family members still living in Sriperumbudur. Many of the people I met had connections with this family.

There were other ācārya families who had their

own networks of disciples. A number of ma ṭhas exist in Sriperumbudur, such as the Varada Yadugiri Yatiraja Maṭha69 and the ma ṭha founded by J. J. Thirunarayana Ramanuja Dasar for Dalits, which his granddaughter N. D. Sabari Natchi was renovating.70 The maṭhas all had their own networks of family members, disciples, or caste associates, which extended into the surrounding villages where the ācāryas and jīyars had proselytised.

This certainly explains some of the emphasis on Sriperum-

budur that I encountered. Nevertheless, I think that this emphasis was also because of the disputes between Teṉkalais and Vaṭakalais in Kanchipuram at the Varadarajaswami temple. Moreover, the major Vaiṣṇava temple in Chennai, the Parthasarathy temple, has Vaikhānasaāgama priests, which occasionally has caused conflicts. At Sriperumbudur, by contrast, the Teṉkalai administrative control was aligned with the Pañcarātraāgama priesthood, and there were no major disputes with the Va ṭakalais, thereby providing a consolidated identity and therefore a hub for Teṉkalai networks in the Chennai region.

Another hub, albeit a remote one, for Teṉkalais has been

Tirumalai-Tirupati. The history of this sacred place predates the formation of Śrīvaiṣṇavism as a sect. Today its fame as a pilgrimage destination attracts Teṉkalais from the region around Chennai, such as Sudarshana’s disciples (as it attracts other Śrīvaiṣṇavas and Hindus in general). The fact that

154

Teṉkalai jīyars have special privileges and honours at these temples facilitates the Teṉkalai pilgrims and the larger Teṉkalai networks.

Finally, the fact that Chennai is the major metropolis of

Tamil Nadu helps to explain other aspects of the interviews from this region. Many of my interviewees were first-generation or second-generation migrants from the immediate region, although some had come from places across Tamil Nadu for education and job opportunities. They were part of the phenomenon of urbanisation and the growing middle class.
