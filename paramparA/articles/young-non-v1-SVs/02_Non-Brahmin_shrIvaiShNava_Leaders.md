Traces of Non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava Leaders



My study of Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins was to be about recent changes in Tamil society. Perhaps it was fortuitous that I wanted to interview some elderly Ś r ī vaiṣṇ avas, because their stories would soon be lost to history. My questions about memories of grandparents and parents took me back into late colonial times. 

This chapter is about the traces of some Bhāgavata 

leaders, ones that I discovered just in time—that is, before they are erased forever from the historical record. I searched for more details. Sometimes this took me to village shrines and tombs or memorial temples \(*samādhi*s\), to photographs near descendants’ household altars, to pamphlets forgotten in personal libraries, and occasionally to a court case. One trace sometimes led to another, such as a booklet containing a short biography. Other vestiges led nowhere. These included many references to published books that no longer exist, even in the libraries of Tamil Nadu.

I am surprised that only traces remain, especially because 

the past century has been a time of non-Brahmin political awakening, ‘subaltern’ studies and robust scholarship on the colonial period in general and religions such as Tamil 







Christianity and Śaivism in particular. Given all the changes surveyed in the Introduction, how were Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas affected by these changes during the nineteenth century? And why do only traces remain?

Turning first to traces of seven non-Brahmin leaders, I 

analyse how they tried to make Śrīvaiṣṇava theological claims of inclusiveness more substantive than rhetorical, and therefore offer Bhāgavatas more support than before. At the heart of this Bhāgavata chutzpah were the ostensibly subversive acts of lecturing on the meaning of the *Divyaprapandham* verses and Śrīvaiṣṇava theology, as well as performing the initiation ritual \(*samāśrayaṇam*\), which only Brahmin *ācārya*s and *jīyar*s performed. With these traces recovered, I discuss how the road to recognition for non-Brahmins was oddly connected to Dalit mobilisation, how they became part of *pulavar* \(poet\) culture, and how they eventually achieved recognition. After comparing Śaivism and Vaiṣṇavism on the topic of non-Brahmin leadership, I conclude the chapter by comparing the trajectories of Śrīvaiṣṇavism and Śaivism under late colonial rule, and offer reasons for why Śrīvaiṣṇavas were slower to awaken to possibilities for leadership. 



A Forgotten Colonial History

The seven pivotal non-Brahmin leaders are Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, Nārāyaṇa Dāsar, \(Kōyiḻ\) Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, Gōpāla Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai, Appāvu Mudaliyār and B. R. Purushottama Naidu. Some of the following descriptions of their lives are based on booklets that their followers wrote about them. These combine the modern genre of biography with the traditional one of hagiography to emphasise the special status of these Bhāgavatas. Little is written about the last two leaders, however, and my information is garnered from remarks in the introductions to their own books.

Rāmānuja Nāvalar

A. V. Rāmānuja Nāvalar \(1829–91\), also known as Putuvai Navalar, was first known as Muttu Ramanuja. According to the brief biography on Ramanuja Navalar,1 he was born in 1829 to a Balija Naidu couple2 at Eppakkam, which is north of Puducherry. After receiving *samāśrayaṇam* from the *jīyar* at Emperumāṉār Mutt in Tirukkoyilur, he went to Bhaṭṭarpirāṉ *jīyar* of the Vānamāmalai Mutt in the southern district of Tirunelveli. Mastering Tamil, Sanskrit and Telugu, he studied philosophical and other works about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. He strongly supported Teṉkalai views. Because of the high standard of his scholarship and his status as a poet, orator and philosopher, Śrīvaiṣṇava scholars gave him the title Rāmānuja Nāvalar \(one who is the master of his tongue, that is, a learned person\). 

Nāvalar’s biography describes how Poṉṉuraṅkam Piḷḷai, 

a devotee belonging to the Yadava caste, learned about the greatness of Nāvalar and asked him to live in Putuvai \(Puducherry\). After moving there, Nāvalar resided on Perumāḷ K ōyiḻ Street, where the Śrī Varadarāja Perumāḷ temple was \(and still is\) located, until the end of his life. He married and had one son, Śaṭakōpa Rāmānuja Naidu, who was also a good scholar and poet.

Once, when Nāvalar visited his *jīyar* at Tirukkoyilur, the 

*jīyar* gave him a copy of the Sanskrit *Padmottara-purāṇa* and instructed him to translate its 4,123 verses into Tamil and popularise it. When it was finished in 1869, Nāvalar presented it to a public gathering of Śrīvaiṣṇavas3 in Kanchipuram, which was customary for new works. It was then printed with the addition of 55 praise-poems in honour of the author and distributed by Po ṉṉuraṅkam Piḷḷai. Because only Brahmins knew Sanskrit works, says the biographer, when Nāvalar translated some into Tamil, people began to think that he was an incarnation of God.

According to the biography, sometime later Nāvalar 

dreamed that the Lord of Srimushnam temple had appeared 

53







to him and asked him to translate the *Śrīmuṣṇa-purāṇa* from Sanskrit into Tamil. He did that too, and then presented it to scholars in the presence of his *jīyar*. When Nāvalar was reciting the work, his disciples were surprised that a verse had forecast his birth: ‘though an eternal being \(*nityas ūri*\), by the grace of God, Nāvalar was born on earth’.

This translation was followed by translations of two more 

*purāṇa*s.4 In addition to these, he wrote forty-six prose works on Śrīvaiṣṇavism,5 and composed some devotional songs6 in Carnatic musical style about God and his divine places. The biography points out that these are sung in *bhajana ma ṭha*s in many towns and villages, because singing songs is the easiest way to pray during the Kaliy ūga. Nāvalar himself travelled to many places where he taught about the tradition, gave lectures and led *kīrtana*-style songs for learned and ordinary people alike, just as Nampiḷḷai \(1210–70\) had done centuries earlier.7

Next, the biography describes how Somasundara Nāyakar8

\(1846–1901\), a Śaiva, mocked Vaiṣṇavism, the *ācārya*s, the *āḻvār*s and especially the sectarian symbol, the *nāmam*, in his *Śaiva Cūtāmaṇi*. Nāvalar wrote a rejoinder to this, which he called the *Hari Darśana*. He filed a suit in the magistrate court, moreover, stating that he had been abused by Somasundara Nāyakar. After learning about the legal proceedings, Somasundara Nāyakar offered him 100 rupees, a written commitment that he would not abuse a Vaiṣṇava again, and an apology that appeared in a popular journal.9

Describing some miracles, the biography tells the following 

stories. One time, when Nāvalar was young and sleeping under a tree, his parents saw a serpent protecting his face from the sun’s heat by spreading its hood. Another time, when climbing Tirumalai hill, he became thirsty and lay down under a tree for a short nap. When he opened his eyes, he found a water pond and *tulasī* plant, an incident that he mentions in his *Bhāgavatam Nāma-saṅkīrtana*. On yet another occasion, when Nāvalar was giving a series of discourses on the *Bhāgavata-purāṇa*, a parrot used to sit on his lap and then fly away when he was done.

In 1891, Nāvalar died. He was remembered for decades. 

A dictionary of Tamil literature,10 published in 1904, contains references to several of his books. Another work11 includes his 1914 *Pañcaratnamālā* about Nammāḻvār. Several poets composed works about him.12 In 1925, a scholar, K. Rāmānuja Svāmi, established an organisation in Pannurutti,13 west of Cuddalore, called Śrīmad Rāmānuja Nāvalar Svāmikaḷ Sabhai. This organisation celebrated his birthday every year, and also honoured him at an annual conference. 

The biography says that his portrait, which was painted in 

Srirangam, was under worship in the house of the secretary, and that Nāvalar presided in this form over the annual conference. In Koyampalli village, where another image was established and daily *pūjā* performed on his birthday, people reported that a snake used to come and open its hood over Nāvalar’s image, staying for a week and not hurting anyone. There was yet another image in Vilakkoli Sannidhi, a *rāmānuja kūṭam* in Kanchipuram that had been built by a woman namedāṇṭāḷ-ammāḷ. There, daily and annual *pūjā*s were conducted.

I visited a temple dedicated to Rāmānuja Nāvalar in the 

village of Eppakkam, on the outskirts of Puducherry, where he had been born. Although this was once a Naidu village, most inhabitants today are Vanniyars and Yadavas. Śrīnivāsiṉ Piḷḷai, a wealthy merchant in Pannurutti, built the temple. He was an erudite scholar in the *Divyaprabandham* and would sing it with the melodic modes or *pāṇ*s, not the Brahmin style of chanting.14 He would also conduct an annual Śrīvaiṣṇava conference in Pannurutti and invite important Bhāgavatas to lecture.

Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ

a Naidu family.18 His biography says that his original name was Ra ṅgasvāmi, the name of the God of Srirangam. Here, I will call him Bhuvanagiri. After marriage, he became a farmer and weaver to support his wife and daughter. Following his family’s tradition, he was initiated by Emperumāṉar Jīyar of Tirukkoyilur. 

One day, continues the biography, Rāmānuja Nāvalar 

visited this family at Bhuvanagiri. Cinnaiyya Naidu and his son, the future Bhuvanagiri, prostrated themselves before him. Nāvalar selected a verse and asked Bhuvanagiri to read it. As if already familiar with it, he read it loudly and interpreted it. Impressed, Nāvalar decided that he should become a leader for the Śrīvaiṣṇava *samprādaya* and instructed his father to provide a good teacher, saying that his son would become a very important person.

Cinnaiyya Naidu sent Bhuvanagiri to study with Nāvalar 

himself, who was teaching other students. Besides Tamil works, Bhuvanagiri studied Telugu and Sanskrit. He so distinguished himself with his excellent memory, says the biography, that Nāvalar named him after Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Dāsar \(Piḷḷai Perumāḷ Ayyaṅgar\), author of the famous *Aṣṭa Prapandham* \(Eight Books\).19

Bhuvanagiri became very popular because of his teaching,20

composition of verses on particular temples, singing and rhetorical power. A number of people were jealous of him, says the biography, and challenged him to a debate. After he answered all their questions \(which were later published as a small book called *Ka ṭāviṭai Cattai*\), his defeated rivals started verbally abusing him, and even physically assaulting him. 

Vēnugōpāḷa Naidu, chairman of the Cuddalore municipality, 

intervened and struck a compromise. On another occasion, according to the biography, someone in Chidambaram composed a poem with the following line: ‘O god of death, be compassionate by ending the life of Bhuvanagiri’. Surprisingly, the person who composed that line became very sick. The doctors lost hope for him. Fearing that the bad karma created by insulting Bhuvanagiri caused his illness, he 

57







wanted to go to Bhuvanagiri and apologise, even though his condition prevented that. When he learned of Bhuvanagiri’s visit to Chidambaram, however, he waited at his house until Bhuvanagiri passed by and then prostrated himself, requesting forgiveness. Bhuvanagiri replied that the Lord would forgive him. 

Bhuvanagiri decided to work full-time for the Śrīvaiṣṇava 

*sampradāya*. His wife went to stay with their daughter, who was now married. He travelled from town to town giving discourses in a style of mixed prose and music with percussive rhythm \(the *harikathā* style of *bhajana*\). Arguing that all religions have truth, Bhvanagiri said that one should follow one’s own strictly. In an age when Christian missionaries claimed that Christianity was the only truth, this was an attempt to keep Hindus within their own tradition.

Having heard about Bhuvanagiri’s discourses, Damodharaṉ

Naidu, the sub-magistrate of Bhuvanagiri village, invited him home to lecture on *Kampa ṉ Rāmāyaṇam*. After Bhuvanagiri did this, the man was pleased and offered him a gift. Bhuvanagiri refused it, suggesting instead that he arrange for daily *pūjā* at the Rāmaṉ temple—presumably the Pattabi Rāmaṉ temple in Bhuvanagiri village that I refer to below. The sub-magistrate not only did that, but he also endowed the temple with 2 acres of land, a small tank and a grove of trees.

Once, the Vānamāmalai *jīyar*, by name Śaṭhakōpa 

Rāmānuja Jīyar, visited the town of Bhuvanagiri. After Bhuvanagiri prostrated himself, the *jīyar*, who was familiar with the work of Bhuvanagiri, advised him that he could not find liberation merely by *harikathā* and discourses on *purāṇa*s. He would need to learn the basic principles of reality \(*tattva-jñāna*\) and customary norms \(*ācāra*\). The *jīyar* instructed him to study with Amarāvati Tiruvēṅkaṭācāriya and Chinna Kaliyaṉ Rāmānuja Jīyar, the twenty-fifth head of the Vānamāmalai Mutt. Both asked Bhuvanagiri to lecture and found his knowledge impressive. 

In 1908, Bhuvanagiri went to Sriperumbudur during the 

festival celebrating Rāmānuja’s birthday and gave lectures.

After listening to one, a young man named Gōpāla composed two poetic works about Bhuvanagiri: *Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Mālai* and *A ḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Antāti*. \(The works were never published and disappeared.\) That same year, Bhuvanagiri decided to become an ascetic \(*ekāṅgi*\). Śrīnāth Bālakṛṣṇa Ekāṅgi at Sriperumbudur did the ritual and renamed him after his birthplace: Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ.

To counter Śaiva critics of the *Divyaprabandham*, Bhuvanagiri 

wrote and published the *Drāviḍaveda Vaibhava Prakāśika* \(1906\),21 which explains the glory of the *Divyaprabandham*. In 1908, he wrote the *Nālāyira Divyaprabandhamābhāsa Darpaṇa* to argue against a Śaiva work22 that interprets the *āḻvār*’s hymns as if they were about the glory of Śiva. He established that one cannot interpret Vaiṣṇava works by relying on Śaiva ones, or vice versa.

In Kallakurichi, Bhuvanagiri’s followers constructed 

an *ashram* and provided a cook for him. When Bhuvanagiri was writing the *Tirucitrakūṭa Mahātmyam*, he appointed one Devarājulu to help him, yet when Bhuvanagiri gave discourses on the *rahasya grantha*s \(secret works\), he would send him away, saying that he could not reveal the secrets to anyone except a Śrīvaiṣṇava who had completed the initiation ritual \(*samāśrayaṇam*\). Therefore, Devarājulu should go to the Emperumāṉār *jīyar* at Tirukkoyilur for initiation. After doing so, Devarājulu joined the group.

A member of the Tirukkulam \(the sacred clan, a euphemism 

for the outcaste community\) wanted to study with Bhuvanagiri, so Bhuvanagiri took him to Tirukkoyilur and asked the *jīyar* to initiate him. Instead, the *jīyar* gave Bhuvanagiri his own ritual utensils for branding, a key part of the ritual, and asked him to perform the ritual. Bhuvanagiri complied, and the outcaste was renamed Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. \(This implies that by having a non-Brahmin initiate an outcaste, the Brahmin *jīyar* thinks he will not be caste-ist or polluted.\) Nārāyaṇa Dāsar then studied about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. \(I will discuss his biography in the next section.\) To counteract the propagation of Śaivism in the area, Bhuvanagiri proselytised among the outcastes and initiated 

59







them into Śrīvaiṣṇavism. This activity generated enormous controversy, according to the biography. 

Bhuvanagiri became famous for his recitations of 

devotional songs and talks on Śrīvaiṣṇavism. Even so, he provoked criticism within Śrīvaiṣṇavism for arguing against Brahmanical positions. When he gave lectures at Nammāḻvār’s birthplace \(Alvartirunagari\) in Tirunelveli district, for instance, the local Brahmin leaders refused to give him accommodation or organise the events.23 In response, Bhuvanagiri’s disciples raised funds and built a *rāmānuja kūṭam*, where he stayed and lectured.24

Since the Śrīvaiṣṇava schools \(*pāṭhaśālā*s\)—which taught 

both the Vedas and the *Divyaprabandham*—were for Brahmins only, Bhuvanagiri founded his own school in 1912 in Srirangam. Called Rāmānuja Darśana Vidyāśālā, it taught Bhāgavatas the *Divyaprabandham*, Śrīvaiṣṇava theology, and Tamil literature. Negamam Zaimindar Ramakrishna Naidu rented a house for the school.25 The first year, it admitted ten students with full board and lodging. Many students attended, including B. R. Purushotamma Naidu \(whom I will discuss\). Brahmins vociferously criticised it. Presumably this was because Bhāgavatas were receiving knowledge that customarily belonged to Brahmins alone. When students dwindled and costs rose, the Zamindar closed the school. Bhuvanagiri moved it to Chidambaram, where it continues to this day, only as an elementary school.

In 1914, in Tuttukkuti village, the Śrīvaiṣṇava Mahāsabhā

was organised. When Bhuvanagiri gave a lecture, the Brahmin president Rāghava Ayyaṅgar, an authority on Tamil literature, found a mistake. At the end of the meeting, Bhuvanagiri stood up and said that there ‘was no mistake. I can support everything.’ The next day, in the Mahāsabhā, Bhuvanagiri refuted the president’s critique by quoting the relevant authorities. After listening to Bhuvanagiri, Rāghava realised his mistake and met him privately. Nevertheless, from that day on, Bhuvanagiri resolved not to give lectures in the presence of Brahmins.

When Bhuvanagiri went away on pilgrimage,26 Brahmins 

continued to criticise his views for threatening the duties of caste and stages of life \(*varṇa-āśrama-dharma*\) in general, and Brahmin control of Śrīvaiṣṇavism in particular. In fact, they complained to the Vānamāmalai *jīyar*. However, the *jīyar*, after scrutinising Bhuvanagiri’s activities, told him to continue his good work.27

A Srirangam *jīyar*, Ayodhyā Rāma Svāmi, sided with the 

opposing Brahmins. In 1918, he published *Tattvatraya Vyākhyāna* and *Tātparya Dīpikā* to argue that only Brahmin *ācārya*s had the right to perform initiations. Bhuvanagiri travelled around Tamil Nadu to counter the Srirangam *jīyar*’s attacks. His *Tattvatraya Vyākhyāna Tātparya Tīpikā Vicāram* \(1918\)28 and *Samāśrayaṇam Sarvatikaram* \(1921\) defend the idea that both non-Brahmins and women can perform *samāśrayaṇam*.29

One of the Brahmins with whom Bhuvanagiri debated 

was Śrīraṅga Rāmānuja Jīyar, who had argued in a book that because the ritual for initiation involved a sacred fire, the ritual was Vedic and therefore could be performed only by Brahmins. In 1919, at Tiruvahindrapuram, Bhuvanagiri countered this by arguing that the fire used for branding was a Vaiṣṇava fire, not a Vedic one, and so non-Brahmins could perform *samāśrayaṇam*.30

That same year, Bhuvanagiri gave a public lecture in 

Cuddalore on the Brahmins’ monopoly on initiation. Quoting elaborately from the *Divyaprabandham* and *sampradāya* works, he argued once again that initiation is not the exclusive right of Brahmins. Rather, anyone who has expertise in these works, is devoted to God, and follows the behavioural norms of the scriptures, has a right to perform it. That includes women.31 Although Brahmin scholars from several communities were invited to debate him, they requested a postponement and failed to show up even at the later date. 

After meeting Govindarājulu from Manakkuppam 

\(Cuddalore\), who had become a government servant at Tirukkoyilur, Bhuvanagiri asked him to teach deserving devotees about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. On a later occasion, Bhuvanagiri

asked him to initiate them and gave him the required conch and wheel-branding tools. 

In 1926, Bhuvanagiri established his own *maṭha* in 

Sriperumbudur. He called it Maṇavāḷa Māmunikaḷ Maṭam, after the famous *ācārya* Maṇavāḷa Māmunikaḷ \(whom I date 1370–1443\), and lived there until his death the following year \(11 June 1927\). In his will, he instructed that his *samādhi* should be built in the corner of a garden that belonged to his devotee Lakṣmiprapanna. Bhuvanagiri’s *samādhi* is now in an open field on the outskirts of Sriperumbudur, along with the *samādhi* of his disciple Prabandhasvāmi. 

A *maṭha* called Emmidi Maṭam, which a disciple established 

during Bhuvanagiri’s lifetime, exists in Sriperumbudur. Today, its head is a single man who is an ordinary devotee. When I interviewed him, he said that on every anniversary of Bhuvanagiri’s death, his descendants come from the Puducherry area and perform *pūjā* at the *samādhi*s that his grandsons fixed up in 2005. 

There is also a temple in Bhuvanagiri’s birthplace dedicated 

to the presiding deity Pattabi Rāmaṉ. Besides the main shrine, there are three smaller ones on the right, to Bhuvanagiri, Rāmānuja and Nammāḻvār.32



Nārāyaṇa Dāsar

Nārāyaṇa Dāsar \(1872–1912\) was born to Rāmadāsar, a staunch outcaste devotee of Lord Viṣṇu and Periyammā in the year 1872; they named him Nārāyaṇaṉ. The family lived in Kottaimedu, now known as Karunapuram. Rāmadāsar was a farmer, owned land, and had a kiln. According to Nārāyaṇa Dāsar’s biography,33 which often reads like a hagiography, Nārāyaṇaṉ helped his father. Rather than playing with other boys, he spent his time praying to the Lord.

Nārāyaṇaṉ went to Madras for teacher training. He lived in 

the Kontittoppu area, which was a stronghold of Vaiṣṇavism. Elsewhere, when hooligans mocked the Vaiṣṇava mark \(*nāmam*\) on his forehead, says the biography, he ‘conquered’

He wanted to be an ascetic, and so he left home and went by foot all the way south to the Vānamāmalai Mutt. On his way there, he met a group of yogis and learned astrology, medicine, and yoga from them. 

Nārāyaṇaṉ stayed one year at the *maṭha* to learn the works of

The *jīyar*, learning that the young man was married, refused to initiate him, commanding him to remain married and serve the Lord that way. Nārāyaṇaṉ returned home and lived devoutly with his wife. As I mentioned, Bhuvanagiri gave him *samāśrayaṇam* and the initiation name Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. Nārāyaṇa Dāsar used to send one-third of his salary to Bhuvanagiri every month, until his retirement. During holidays, he went on pilgrimage to sacred shrines.

Nārāyaṇa Dāsar was the headmaster of the government 

elementary school for outcastes in Virapandi, near Tirukkoyilur. The village had a separate government school for the upper castes too. Gradually, the villagers recognised Nārāyaṇa Dāsar as an able teacher and a man of noble qualities. Even high-caste students wanted to attend his school. 

The biography describes a miracle. Learning that Nārāyaṇa 

Dāsar was bedridden, Sundararāja Bhāgavatar, Bhuvanagiri’s son-in-law, went to see him. On his way, he stopped at the Chidambaram temple. While worshipping Lord Govindarāja there, he prayed for the early recovery of Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. Sundararāja was sitting on the edge of the sacred tank when a priest handed him a basket full of *prasāda* \(sacred food\) and a garland. The priest said that Nārāyaṇa Dāsar had come there in the early morning and worshipped the Lord, but had left the temple without taking the sacred food. When the priest went away, Sundararāja wondered how Nārāyaṇa Dāsar could have visited the temple when he was far away and bedridden. Not only was this a miracle, Sundararāja decided, so was the 

63







fact that the Lord himself in the guise of a priest had given Nārāyaṇa Dāsar the *prasāda*.

Sundararāja Bhāgavatar finally reached the house of

The Road to Recognition

It was difficult for Bhāgavatas to become religious leaders when Brahmins controlled the teaching of Sanskrit and the basic doctrines of the tradition, which were only taught orally by Brahmins. Oddly, non-Brahmin entry into this realm was facilitated by the need to address Dalit mobilisation and the attraction of people to other sects or even other religions. In other words, what started out as leadership by default for elite non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas turned into serious activism. Here is how that happened.



69







Gaining Esoteric Knowledge

The Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin challenge to Brahmin roles had several basic components. One was gaining access to esoteric learning in order to qualify as scholars of the tradition. This happened in several ways. Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, for instance, learned much by listening to lectures by Brahmins and learning from his Brahmin *jīyar*, Ciṟupuliyūr \(he would not have attended the school started by Bhuvanagiri, which taught Sanskrit to both Brahmins and non-Brahmins; it started only in 1912\). We are told that he had difficulty chanting Sanskrit and therefore could not learn that language in the traditional Brahmanical manner. Perhaps this was a circumlocution to avoid saying directly that no Brahmin would teach him. Whatever the case, he did not learn Sanskrit. The fact that the commentaries on the *āḻvār*s’ verses and many of the formative works about Śrīvaiṣṇavism were written in Maṇipravāḷam \(a combination of Tamil and Sanskrit\) meant that, without Sanskrit, he also could not read these works, and had to rely on oral sources.

By contrast, Rāmānuja Nāvalar learned Sanskrit from 

the Vānamāmalai *jīyar* and thus also had access to Sanskrit commentaries and treatises, as well as Maṇipravāḷa ones. His contribution, therefore, was showing that non-Brahmins were capable translators and interpreters of texts. Bhuvanagiri too had the advantage of learning Sanskrit from both Rāmānuja Nāvalar and the Vānamāmalai *jīyar*, which gave him access to all the *sampradāya* literature.

Therefore, a few Brahmin *ma ṭha*s were willing to help 

Bhāgavatas become educated in the tradition. This means that they deviated from traditional Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmanical control of the intellectual tradition through Sanskrit, Maṇipravāḷam, and schools only for Brahmins. No doubt the theology of inclusiveness in Śrīvaiṣṇava works, especially those of Maṇavāḷa-māmuni, was pressed into service to support the non-Brahmins, and counter the proselytism of other religions.

Harder to understand is why the Brahmin *maṭha*s encouraged non-Brahmin leaders to perform the initiation ritual, which had been an exclusive Brahmin ritual role that ostensibly provided access to salvation itself \(ultimately, salvation comes from God\). This takes me to the next topic. Should initiation be extended to Dalits?

In this context, it is important to remember that Dalits

Nevertheless, once these non-Brahmins had the expertise and the legitimation, they began to argue their right to do it for everyone, and thereby began to usurp what had been a carefully protected Brahmanical role. 



Struggling into the *Pulavar* World 

As described in early Tamil poetry, poets \(*pulavar*s\) praised rulers in oral performances to broadcast their fame and thereby increase their status in return for gifts, including economic support, food, and sometimes items of conspicuous 



73







consumption. By medieval times, patrons were not just rulers, they were also temples, *ma ṭha*s, or wealthy individuals. 

In *Colonizing the Realm of Words*,57 Sascha Ebeling \(2010\) 

gives us insight into the transformations of the *pulavar* ‘economy of praise’ as the poets adapted to colonial power and the transformation of an oral performance culture to one of print and a reading public. Although Ebeling discusses this *pulavar* world in the context of Śaivism, his analysis helps us understand the quandaries faced by non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas.

From the medieval period, Śaiva *maṭha*s were patrons of 

*pulavar* culture. On the model of royal and temple processions, monastic heads toured the countryside in ostentatious processions with other dignitaries, elephants, musicians and dancing girls. The birthdays of *maṭha* founders were celebrated with grand festivals, moreover, with poets performing their works, chanters reciting Vedic and T ēvāram verses \(the Śaiva *bhakti* verses of Campantar, Appar and Cuntarar\), learned men giving discourses, and musicians entertaining the crowd with concerts. 

The fact that there were major non-Brahmin Śaiva *maṭha*s 

meant that non-Brahmins participated in this transactional *pulavar* culture as patrons and poets. According to Ebeling, this ostentatious *pulavar* culture became even more important at the beginning of the nineteenth century as the Madras Presidency consolidated its power by turning independent rulers into subordinates, through curtailing warfare and therefore the status of military men, especially those who were victorious.

Some Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins participated in *pulavar*

cosmopolitan culture, both in their own *ma ṭha*s as centres of learning and also in royal courts, if the hagiographic stories of poetic competitions there reflect historical reality. In this context, poetic works such as Garuḍavāhana’s *Divyasūricaritam* might have been creations in a *pulavar* milieu.

Śaivism and Vaiṣṇavism Compared

Unlike the Vellalar Śaiva Siddhāntins who had wealthy non-Brahmin *maṭha*s that were involved in *pulavar* culture, Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin *maṭha*s—there were a few—were very modest affairs, without libraries, and more like the *rāmānuja-kūṭam*s, the charitable feeding houses. It was probably more an exception than a rule that the birthdays of their founders or leaders were celebrated on the same scale as the Śaiva ones, although we do have the case of the fifteenth-century figure Ka ṇṭāṭai Rāmānuja Ayyaṅkar, the manager of many *rāmānuja kūṭam*s in Tirupati and Tirumalai, who celebrated his birthday with processions of musicians, dancing girls, and others.

Bhuvanagiri lived a bit later thanāṟumuka \(he would have been nineteen whenāṟumuka died\); however, he probably knew of him. Chidambaram, whereāṟumuka spent considerable time, was not far from Cuddalore, near where Bhuvanagiri lived.

Here is another similarity.āṟumuka had built a small 

temple in Chidambaram.62 Soon after, the temple acquired an image ofāṟumuka.63 According to Hudson, elsewhere, images of him were likely worshipped. From 1910,āṟumuka’s birthday was celebrated like those of the early medieval Śaiva saints, the *nāyaṉmār*, who were worshipped in image-form.64 As I have noted, images or portraits were also made for Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ, to be worshipped in household shrines, temples, *rāmānuja kūṭam*s, or *samādhi* shrines. 

There are differences, too, betweenāṟumuka and the 

Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders I have discussed here. For instance, I can find no direct links between the latter and the missionary orientalists who promoted Dravidian nationalism or provided access to Western learning, even though Bhuvanagiri lived near Cuddalore, an important trade centre for the East Indian Company, and a Christian mission centre. Another difference was about caste.āṟumuka argued that *śūdra*s \(including Vellalars\) may not recite the Vedas or 

77







perform Vedic rituals. In the nineteenth century, Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins \(to my knowledge\) did not take such an explicit stand; they simply seemed more interested in access to Śrīvaiṣṇava *sampradāya* knowledge, and the right to perform *samāśrayaṇam*, a specific Śrīvaiṣṇava ritual. 

Even though Śrīvaiṣṇavism had anāgamic tradition—

the Pañcarātraāgamas—knowledge of this literature was of interest mainly to Brahmin temple priests. At that time, Bhāgavatas were not interested in becomingāgamic priests, even if they had been allowed.

Āṟumuka states that Śaiva non-Brahmins who have had the 

Āgamic ritual of *dīkṣā* \(which involves becoming vegetarian\) become twice-born, indeed Brahmins, if they practice the way of life appropriate to their *varṇa* andāgamic status. They have the authority to recite theāgamas, though not the Vedas, and to be *ācārya*s. 

Like the Śaiva *dīkṣā*, Śrīvaiṣṇava *samāśrayaṇam* involves 

becoming vegetarian; however, the ritual per se does not transform a Śrīvaiṣṇava into a twice-born or Brahmin. Rather, the ritual ostensibly provides access to salvation \(indirectly, it probably provides access to social or religious status as well\). If we understand an *ācārya* as a teacher and interpreter of the tradition, then Bhāgavatas certainly wanted to be *ācāryas*. The Śrīvaiṣṇava understanding of *ācārya* was more specific, however, than just being a teacher and exegete; it also meant belonging to one of the seventy-four Brahmin families supposedly appointed by Rāmānuja.

Finally,āṟumuka had quite a different position on Dalits 

than did the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders of his time. He thought that they were so innately impure that they could never have *dīkṣā* in theāgamic tradition. Although they could worship God and attain salvation, they could not enter the temples. By contrast, the nineteenth-century Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavata leaders considered Dalits worthy not only of *samāśrayaṇam*, but also of deep respect and honour. Yet, even the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins did not do much about temple entry until the mid-twentieth century.

The major difference between the non-Brahmin Śaivas and 

Śrīvaiṣṇavas was that the former had an important institutional base. Many Śaiva *maṭha*s, after all, were the exclusive domain of non-Brahmins. Bhāgavatas controlled no major *maṭha*s and thus had no established centres of learning, libraries, or formal leadership roles, although they did have *Rāmānuja kūṭam*s.



Influence from Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ? 

A comparison of the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders mentioned here with Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ \(1876–1950\), who wasāṟumuka’s disciple, is somewhat different. According to Ravi Vaithess, Maṟaimalai promoted reform from within the religion, and a neo-Śaivism that was pro-Dravidian, pro-Tamil, and anti-caste in the tradition of Rāmaliṅga.65 Sometimes Aṭikaḷ aligned with Periyar and sometimes he differed from him. Vaithees argues that nevertheless, we can see religious roots of the secular Self-Respect movement in Aṭikaḷ’s thought.66

Like Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ, Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins were 

against caste discrimination, which made some of them join the Self-Respect movement \(although others did not, because of its avowed atheism\). Nonetheless, most elite Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins of the time were not as anti-Sanskrit as Maṟaimalai. They wanted access to the world of Sanskrit learning, not its destruction. 

Finally, the fact that these non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava 

leaders did not claim that their religion could be found in Tamil origins, or that it was central to the nineteenth-century Tamil Renaissance, meant that their message was marginal to the great identity narratives of the day, which Śaivas had successfully appropriated.



Why Śrīvaiṣṇava Marginalisation?

It is fair to say that non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas in the late nineteenth and early twentieth century were not yet 

79







trendsetters, despite their inclusive religious position on caste and women and their growing self-confidence under British rule that broke down some traditional barriers to status. They were also out of the Tamil mainstream, in part because many had ancestors who had come from what became Andhra after independence, but was then part of Madras Presidency.

In the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, all 

these ‘Andhra’ connections were alive and well. In this context, remember that the ancestors of Rāmānuja Nāvalar and Bhuvanagiri were Naidus whose families had originally migrated from the Andhra region as warrior-cultivators.67 Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s family was possibly Sattada \(many Sattadas were originally from Andhra or Karnataka\). The *jīyar* at the *maṭha* in Tirukkoyilur, who performed initiation for Rāmānuja Nāvalar and Bhuvanagiri, belonged to a lineage that had originated in Andhra. The *jīyar* there sometimes sent his disciples to study with the *jīyar* of the Vānamāmalai Mutt in Nanguneri, which had Andhra connections as well. Both the *ma ṭha* in Tirukkoyilur and the *maṭha* in Nanguneri were located, moreover, in major cotton-growing regions, where many Telugus had migrated in Vijayanagara times to work as cotton-growers. Vijayanagara kings had been partial to Vaiṣṇavism and to Telugu-speaking warriors and other migrants, such as the Naidus, Reddiyars and Vanniyars, who accompanied them into the south. 

Non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavism was important in the 

Telugu-speaking northern parts of Madras Presidency too. I remember discovering in Salarjung Museum \(Hyderabad\) a set of miniature wooden figures with *nāmam*s painted on their foreheads. They were obviously from colonial times, given their dress. What intrigued me the most was the representation of Śrīvaiṣṇavas across a range of occupations and roles; the figures included policemen, house servants, ascetics, musicians in a band, learned Brahmins, women, labourers, farmers, and others.

The fact that most Bhāgavata leaders were Telugu speakers, 

not Tamil, helps explain why they were not in the intellectual

and literary mainstream, even though there had been Telugu Brahmin literati in the established cosmopolitan *pulavar* culture, including Carnatic music culture, in Tamil Nadu. Besides, for Śaiva non-Brahmins, Śrīvaiṣṇavism represented a Sanskritised Tamil tradition under Brahmin domination. This might have made it more difficult initially for Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas to be part of a Dravidian movement, which came to champion Tamil as central to identity. Finally, with the Tamil Renaissance and Madras as a new colonial centre of Tamil learning, Telugus were being further marginalised, and had little Telugu nationalism as a counterpoint. At the risk of digression, it is important to understand why.

According to Peter Schmitthenner, before the nineteenth 

century, Telugus had little sense of a common literary tradition, or even common identity, given their migrations and lack of a common caste, patron-client network, or religious sect.68 This began to change in the nineteenth century with the consolidation of the East India Company rule, and interest in Indian languages and literatures by Company officials and missionaries who collaborated with local scholars and brought Telugu literature into the public eye. 

Schmitthenner speaks of a Telugu resurgence developing 

at this time. One of its leaders was C. P. Brown \(1798–1884\). A British civil servant and private scholar, he wrote Telugu grammars and dictionaries. In addition, he collected, edited, and published Telugu literature and translated Christian literature into Telugu.69 Brown encouraged the appreciation of Telugu literature in Madras and elsewhere. He often drew on non-Brahmin informants to help gather and understand colloquial Telugu. He was especially fond of the seventeenth-century peasant poet Vemana, who adhered to Vīraśaivism, a Hindu sect mainly found in Karnataka that emphasised humility, tolerance, compassion, vegetarianism, and a casteless society that honoured women as well as men. 

Brown’s interest in non-Brahmin language and literature 

created tensions with Brahmin scholars who wanted to control Telugu literature. Eventually, it would be non-Brahmins who 

81







broke this control, although Schmitthenner thinks that they never became as anti-Brahmin as the Tamil non-Brahmins.70 In any case, although the foundations for a broad-based Telugu identity were being formulated in the nineteenth century, and a new mentality was emerging, it had yet to become the focus for Telugu-speaking non-Brahmins in Tamil Nadu. 

In addition, Madras had become the intellectual centre of 

Tamil Nadu, but as Eugene Irschick has said, educated Telugus considered Madras ‘a hostile city in which Telugu interests were not looked on kindly. This is not to say that Madras city did not contain many Telugus \[but\] .... It was the Tamils who tended to monopolize the educational facilities and the administrative and political opportunities in Madras.’71 \(Even Telugu Brahmins were marginal. According to C. J. Fuller and Haripriya Narasimhan, the reason Telugu Brahmins lagged behind Tamil Brahmins was because ‘they did not pursue medicine, engineering and administration to the same degree and because they did not have as large patronage networks and organization’.72\)

So Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins lost out on several fronts. 

They were not part of the mainstream *pulavar* and missionary cultures, for the reasons already discussed. As Telugu speakers, they had neither deep identification with the Tamil Renaissance \(focussed on Ca ṅkam literature\) and incipient Tamil nationalism \(focussed on Tamil\), nor did they have a vibrant Telugu identity with an institutional base in Madras as counterpoint. In other words, as Telugu speakers, the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders discussed in this chapter were once-removed Dravidians, notwithstanding many Telugu *pulavar*s in courts and Śaiva *maṭha*s.73 However, even though Telugu was their mother tongue, they did speak Tamil and identified deeply with the Tamil poems of the *āḻvār*s, their scriptural canon. This would pave the way for their eventual participation in the Dravidian parties to challenge old social hierarchies.

Despite their marginal status, the leaders discussed in 

this chapter were on the verge of creating a new orientation.

Since we cannot characterise it as reform without evidence that Bhāgavatas ever functioned as *ācārya*s or *jīyar*s in previous centuries, we must call this development a revolt, or at least the seeds of a revolt. Perhaps the participants were even on the verge of creating a new sub-sect. The description of miracles in the biographies of leaders such as Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, and the existence of temples and *samādhi*s that enshrined their presence, are signs of sainthood in the making. 

Even so, they did not attain the kind of sainthood or 

prophetic stature that Rāmaliṅga74 or Ayya Vaikuṇṭar did \(see Appendix 6\). Perhaps this was because they did not have what psychologists of religion call Credibility Enhancing Displays \(CREDS\), such as dramatic or costly rituals that attract attention, become memorable, and induce group cohesiveness. Neither did they have extreme messianic messages from someone believed to have the power to change the world,75 nor did they create an intellectual *sampradāya* for lack of institutionalisation; although it appears that the mantle was passed on for a generation or so in several cases. Nevertheless, a few Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas developed major scholarly skills, the power of oratory, and a message of justice, which paved the way for identification with Dravidian nationalism. 

As for Teṉkalai Brahmins, they had become moribund and 

could hardly lead the way to social justice. Because they had been put on the defensive to prove that they were real Brahmins, for instance, most were not willing to risk their reputations to adjust to social change, preferring to stay with ‘convention’, despite their caste-inclusive theology. Besides, there were conflicts within Śrīvaiṣṇavism that had been simmering for several centuries, and that led, by the eighteenth century, to a division of the tradition into the Te ṉkalai and Vaṭakalai sub-sects, as well as to fierce fights over control of major temples, battles that often ended up in court.76

Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins had little inclination to become 

involved in caste politics or issues of social justice. Many aligned themselves with other Brahmins to fight for independence 

83







from the British. They were open to a few reforms, such as improving the lives of Dalits and women, or shoring up the Śrīvaiṣṇava identity of non-Brahmins to prevent conversions. However, nothing too radical was advocated.

\* \* \*

This chapter has recovered a forgotten history of seven non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava leaders in the colonial period. Although some of them had followers, who kept their memory alive for a while with pamphlets, memorial shrines and discourses about them, almost all knowledge about these leaders has disappeared from historical memory, even many of their books from libraries. Still, through the memories of non-Brahmins involved in this study, pamphlets they provided and visits to their temples and *samādhi*s, we have found traces of a remarkable moment in the history of non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavism. 

By reading between the lines of more general discussions 

of colonial history, it becomes possible to situate their life histories and what little is known about them in the larger social, economic, and political currents of the late-colonial period. We know that this was a time of Dalit awakening, which led to the desire to redress past acts of discrimination at a time when the colonial government was addressing the need to abolish slavery and improve the appalling situation of the outcastes. It was also a time of growing literacy and demand by non-Brahmins for education. 

For most of the Bhāgavatas discussed here, their greatest 

grievance was lack of access to knowledge about their religion, because Brahmins had always had that firmly in control by allowing only Brahmins to learn Sanskrit and Maṇipravāḷam, the languages of the sect’s many erudite works, despite claims that the sect was inclusive of all castes. Once Bhāgavata leaders had access to Sanskrit and *sampradāya* works, and had opportunities to perform *samāśrayaṇam* for Dalits—because even the liberal *ācārya*s and *jīyar*s did not want to do this—they 

84

decided that they too could be *ācārya*s or *jīyar*s, and so they took on this role for other non-Brahmins and Dalits. More than that, they argued that anyone with the requisite knowledge and initiation, *śūdra*s and women, too, could be such religious leaders.

Despite the fact that they travelled about Tamil Nadu 

preaching about the sect, converting people and debating with Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins on the one hand, and with Śaivas on the other, the lasting impact of their activism, it seems, was the creation of access to Śrīvaiṣṇava literature for non-Brahmins. This led to roles as translators, commentators, and above all, teachers, about the religion, all of which paved the way to modern scholarship on the religion by non-Brahmins and the confident religious leadership that non-Brahmins practise today. The second major contribution was alerting the Śrīvaiṣṇava community to the fact that they must take Dalits seriously, not as mere symbols of inclusiveness, but as bona fide devotees. That change, however, has been much slower in coming.
