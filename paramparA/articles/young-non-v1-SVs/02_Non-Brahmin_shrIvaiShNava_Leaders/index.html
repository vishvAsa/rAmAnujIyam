Traces of Non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava Leaders

Research

My study of Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins was to be about recent changes in Tamil society. Perhaps it was fortuitous that I wanted to interview some elderly [[Śrī vaiṣṇavas|Śrīvaiṣṇavas]], because their stories would soon be lost to history. My questions about memories of grandparents and parents took me back into late colonial times.

This chapter is about the traces of some Bhāgavata leaders, ones that I discovered just in time—that is, before they are erased forever from the historical record. I searched for more details. Sometimes this took me to village shrines and tombs or memorial temples (samādhis), to photographs near descendants’ household altars, to pamphlets forgotten in personal libraries, and occasionally to a court case. One trace sometimes led to another, such as a booklet containing a short biography. Other vestiges led nowhere. These included many references to published books that no longer exist, even in the libraries of Tamil Nadu.

I am surprised that only traces remain, especially because the past century has been a time of non-Brahmin political awakening, ‘subaltern’ studies and robust scholarship on the colonial period in general and religions such as Tamil Christianity and Śaivism in particular. Given all the changes surveyed in the Introduction, how were Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas affected by these changes during the nineteenth century? And why do only traces remain?

Turning first to traces of seven non-Brahmin leaders, I analyse how they tried to make Śrīvaiṣṇava theological claims of inclusiveness more substantive than rhetorical, and therefore offer Bhāgavatas more support than before. At the heart of this Bhāgavata chutzpah were the ostensibly subversive acts of lecturing on the meaning of the Divyaprabandham verses and Śrīvaiṣṇava theology, as well as performing the initiation ritual (samāśrayaṇam), which only Brahmin ācāryas and jīyars performed. With these traces recovered, I discuss how the road to recognition for non-Brahmins was oddly connected to Dalit mobilisation, how they became part of pulavar (poet) culture, and how they eventually achieved recognition. After comparing Śaivism and Vaiṣṇavism on the topic of non-Brahmin leadership, I conclude the chapter by comparing the trajectories of Śrīvaiṣṇavism and Śaivism under late colonial rule, and offer reasons for why Śrīvaiṣṇavas were slower to awaken to possibilities for leadership.

A Forgotten Colonial History

The seven pivotal non-Brahmin leaders are Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, Nārāyaṇa Dāsar, (Kōyiḻ) Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, Gōpāla Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai, Appāvu Mudaliyār and B. R. Purushottama Naidu. Some of the following descriptions of their lives are based on booklets that their followers wrote about them. These combine the modern genre of biography with the traditional one of hagiography to emphasise the special status of these Bhāgavatas. Little is written about the last two leaders, however, and my information is garnered from remarks in the introductions to their own books.

Rāmānuja Nāvalar

A. V. Rāmānuja Nāvalar (1829–91), also known as Putuvai Navalar, was first known as Muttu Ramanuja. According to the brief biography on Ramanuja Navalar,1 he was born in 1829 to a Balija Naidu couple2 at Eppakkam, which is north of Puducherry.

After receiving samāśrayaṇam from the jīyar at Emperumāṉār Mutt in Tirukkoyilur, he went to Bhaṭṭarpirāṉ jīyar of the Vānamāmalai Mutt in the southern district of Tirunelveli. Mastering Tamil, Sanskrit and Telugu, he studied philosophical and other works about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. He strongly supported Teṉkalai views.

Because of the high standard of his scholarship and his status as a poet, orator and philosopher, Śrīvaiṣṇava scholars gave him the title Rāmānuja Nāvalar (one who is the master of his tongue, that is, a learned person).

Nāvalar’s biography describes how Poṉṉuraṅkam Piḷḷai, a devotee belonging to the Yadava caste, learned about the greatness of Nāvalar and asked him to live in Putuvai (Puducherry). After moving there, Nāvalar resided on Perumāḷ Kōyiḻ Street, where the Śrī Varadarāja Perumāḷ temple was (and still is) located, until the end of his life. He married and had one son, Śaṭakōpa Rāmānuja Naidu, who was also a good scholar and poet.

Once, when Nāvalar visited his jīyar at Tirukkoyilur, the jīyar gave him a copy of the Sanskrit Padmottara-purāṇa and instructed him to translate its 4,123 verses into Tamil and popularise it. When it was finished in 1869, Nāvalar presented it to a public gathering of Śrīvaiṣṇavas3 in Kanchipuram, which was customary for new works. It was then printed with the addition of 55 praise-poems in honour of the author and distributed by Poṉṉuraṅkam Piḷḷai. Because only Brahmins knew Sanskrit works, says the biographer, when Nāvalar translated some into Tamil, people began to think that he was an incarnation of God.+++(4)+++

According to the biography, sometime later Nāvalar dreamed that the Lord of Srimushnam temple had appeared [[P53]] to him and asked him to translate the Śrīmuṣṇa-purāṇa from Sanskrit into Tamil. He did that too, and then presented it to scholars in the presence of his jīyar. When Nāvalar was reciting the work, his disciples were surprised that a verse had forecast his birth: ‘though an eternal being (nityasūri), by the grace of God, Nāvalar was born on earth’.

This translation was followed by translations of two more purāṇas.4 In addition to these, he wrote forty-six prose works on Śrīvaiṣṇavism,5 and composed some devotional songs6 in Carnatic musical style about God and his divine places. The biography points out that these are sung in maṭhas in many towns and villages, because singing songs is the easiest way to pray during the Kaliyuga. Nāvalar himself travelled to many places where he taught about the tradition, gave lectures and led kīrtana-style songs for learned and ordinary people alike, just as Nampiḷḷai (1210–70) had done centuries earlier.7

Somasundara Nāyakar

Next, the biography describes how Somasundara Nāyakar8 (1846–1901), a Śaiva, mocked Vaiṣṇavism, the ācāryas, the āḻvārs and especially the sectarian symbol, the nāmam, in his Śaiva Cūtāmaṇi.

Nāvalar wrote a rejoinder to this, which he called the Hari Darśana. He filed a suit in the magistrate court, moreover, stating that he had been abused by Somasundara Nāyakar. After learning about the legal proceedings, Somasundara Nāyakar offered him 100 rupees, a written commitment that he would not abuse a Vaiṣṇava again, and an apology that appeared in a popular journal.9

Describing some miracles, the biography tells the following stories. One time, when Nāvalar was young and sleeping under a tree, his parents saw a serpent protecting his face from the sun’s heat by spreading its hood. Another time, when climbing Tirumalai hill, he became thirsty and lay down under a tree for a short nap. When he opened his eyes, he found a water pond and tulasī plant, an incident that he mentions in his Bhāgavatam Nāma-saṅkīrtana. On yet another occasion, when Nāvalar was giving a series of discourses on the Bhāgavata-purāṇa, a parrot used to sit on his lap and then fly away when he was done.

[[P54]]

In 1891, Nāvalar died. He was remembered for decades. A dictionary of Tamil literature,10 published in 1904, contains references to several of his books. Another work11 includes his 1914 Pañcaratnamālā about Nammāḻvār. Several poets composed works about him.12 In 1925, a scholar, K. Rāmānuja Svāmi, established an organisation in Pannurutti,13 west of Cuddalore, called Śrīmad Rāmānuja Nāvalar Svāmikaḷ Sabhai. This organisation celebrated his birthday every year, and also honoured him at an annual conference.

The biography says that his portrait, which was painted in Srirangam, was under worship in the house of the secretary, and that Nāvalar presided in this form over the annual conference. In Koyampalli village, where another image was established and daily pūjā performed on his birthday, people reported that a snake used to come and open its hood over Nāvalar’s image, staying for a week and not hurting anyone. There was yet another image in Vilakkoli Sannidhi, a rāmānuja kūṭam in Kanchipuram that had been built by a woman [[namedāṇṭāḷ-ammāḷ|named Āṇṭāḷ-ammāḷ]]. There, daily and annual pūjās were conducted.

I visited a temple dedicated to Rāmānuja Nāvalar in the village of Eppakkam, on the outskirts of Puducherry, where he had been born. Although this was once a Naidu village, most inhabitants today are Vanniyars and Yadavas. Śrīnivāsiṉ Piḷḷai, a wealthy merchant in Pannurutti, built the temple. He was an erudite scholar in the Divyaprabandham and would sing it with the melodic modes or pāṇs, not the Brahmin style of chanting.14 He would also conduct an annual Śrīvaiṣṇava conference in Pannurutti and invite important Bhāgavatas to lecture.

The Eppakkam temple, which Śrīnivāsiṉ Piḷḷai’s family still maintains, has Lord Śrīnivāsa for its presiding image. When facing the image, one sees in Image 2.1 a statue of Rāmānuja Nāvalar on the right.

He wears a turban like that found on the image of Maṇavāḷa Māmuni. The shrine’s external roof is adorned with images of Rāmānuja Nāvalar and famous historical figures of the Teṉkalai tradition: Nammāḻvār, Maṇavāḷa Māmuni and Rāmānuja. There is a monthly festival on Kārtikā (Ta. Kārttikai) [[P55]]

Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ

Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ (1860–1927) is formally known as Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Rāmānuja Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ (a name that overlaps with another figure from Srirangam whom I discuss shortly) and Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Dāsar.16 According to his biography,17 he was born in Bhuvanagiri, a village near Cuddalore, which was a centre of Śaivism and Christianity, to [[P56]] a Naidu family.18 His biography says that his original name was Raṅgasvāmi, the name of the God of Srirangam. Here, I will call him Bhuvanagiri. After marriage, he became a farmer and weaver to support his wife and daughter. Following his family’s tradition, he was initiated by Emperumāṉar Jīyar of Tirukkoyilur.

One day, continues the biography, Rāmānuja Nāvalar visited this family at Bhuvanagiri. Cinnaiyya Naidu and his son, the future Bhuvanagiri, prostrated themselves before him. Nāvalar selected a verse and asked Bhuvanagiri to read it. As if already familiar with it, he read it loudly and interpreted it. Impressed, Nāvalar decided that he should become a leader for the Śrīvaiṣṇava samprādaya and instructed his father to provide a good teacher, saying that his son would become a very important person.

Cinnaiyya Naidu sent Bhuvanagiri to study with Nāvalar himself, who was teaching other students. Besides Tamil works, Bhuvanagiri studied Telugu and Sanskrit. He so distinguished himself with his excellent memory, says the biography, that Nāvalar named him after Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Dāsar (Piḷḷai Perumāḷ Ayyaṅgar), author of the famous Aṣṭa Prapandham (Eight Books).19

Bhuvanagiri became very popular because of his teaching,20 composition of verses on particular temples, singing and rhetorical power. A number of people were jealous of him, says the biography, and challenged him to a debate. After he answered all their questions (which were later published as a small book called Kaṭāviṭai Cattai), his defeated rivals started verbally abusing him, and even physically assaulting him.

Vēnugōpāḷa Naidu, chairman of the Cuddalore municipality, intervened and struck a compromise. On another occasion, according to the biography, someone in Chidambaram composed a poem with the following line: ‘O god of death, be compassionate by ending the life of Bhuvanagiri’. Surprisingly, the person who composed that line became very sick. The doctors lost hope for him. Fearing that the bad karma created by insulting Bhuvanagiri caused his illness, he [[P57]] wanted to go to Bhuvanagiri and apologise, even though his condition prevented that. When he learned of Bhuvanagiri’s visit to Chidambaram, however, he waited at his house until Bhuvanagiri passed by and then prostrated himself, requesting forgiveness. Bhuvanagiri replied that the Lord would forgive him.

Bhuvanagiri decided to work full-time for the Śrīvaiṣṇava sampradāya. His wife went to stay with their daughter, who was now married. He travelled from town to town giving discourses in a style of mixed prose and music with percussive rhythm (the harikathā style of bhajana). Arguing that all religions have truth, [[Bhvanagiri|Bhuvanagiri]] said that one should follow one’s own strictly. In an age when Christian missionaries claimed that Christianity was the only truth, this was an attempt to keep Hindus within their own tradition.

Having heard about Bhuvanagiri’s discourses, Damodharaṉ Naidu, the sub-magistrate of Bhuvanagiri village, invited him home to lecture on [[Kampa ṉ Rāmāyaṇam|Kampaṉ Rāmāyaṇam]]. After Bhuvanagiri did this, the man was pleased and offered him a gift. Bhuvanagiri refused it, suggesting instead that he arrange for daily pūjā at the Rāmaṉ temple—presumably the Pattabi Rāmaṉ temple in Bhuvanagiri village that I refer to below. The sub-magistrate not only did that, but he also endowed the temple with 2 acres of land, a small tank and a grove of trees.

Once, the Vānamāmalai jīyar, by name Śaṭhakōpa Rāmānuja Jīyar, visited the town of Bhuvanagiri. After Bhuvanagiri prostrated himself, the jīyar, who was familiar with the work of Bhuvanagiri, advised him that he could not find liberation merely by harikathā and discourses on purāṇas. He would need to learn the basic principles of reality (tattva-jñāna) and customary norms (ācāra). The jīyar instructed him to study with Amarāvati Tiruvēṅkaṭācāriya and Chinna Kaliyaṉ Rāmānuja Jīyar, the twenty-fifth head of the Vānamāmalai Mutt. Both asked Bhuvanagiri to lecture and found his knowledge impressive.

In 1908, Bhuvanagiri went to Sriperumbudur during the festival celebrating Rāmānuja’s birthday and gave lectures. [[P58]]

After listening to one, a young man named Gōpāla composed two poetic works about Bhuvanagiri: Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Mālai and Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Antāti. (The works were never published and disappeared.) That same year, Bhuvanagiri decided to become an ascetic (ekāṅgi). Śrīnāth Bālakṛṣṇa Ekāṅgi at Sriperumbudur did the ritual and renamed him after his birthplace: Bhuvanagiri Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ.

To counter Śaiva critics of the Divyaprabandham, Bhuvanagiri wrote and published the Drāviḍaveda Vaibhava Prakāśika (1906),21 which explains the glory of the Divyaprabandham. In 1908, he wrote the Nālāyira Divyaprabandhamābhāsa Darpaṇa to argue against a Śaiva work22 that interprets the āḻvār’s hymns as if they were about the glory of Śiva. He established that one cannot interpret Vaiṣṇava works by relying on Śaiva ones, or vice versa.

In Kallakurichi, Bhuvanagiri’s followers constructed an ashram and provided a cook for him. When Bhuvanagiri was writing the Tirucitrakūṭa Mahātmyam, he appointed one Devarājulu to help him, yet when Bhuvanagiri gave discourses on the rahasya granthas (secret works), he would send him away, saying that he could not reveal the secrets to anyone except a Śrīvaiṣṇava who had completed the initiation ritual (samāśrayaṇam). Therefore, Devarājulu should go to the Emperumāṉār jīyar at Tirukkoyilur for initiation. After doing so, Devarājulu joined the group.

A member of the Tirukkulam (the sacred clan, a euphemism for the outcaste community) wanted to study with Bhuvanagiri, so Bhuvanagiri took him to Tirukkoyilur and asked the jīyar to initiate him. Instead, the jīyar gave Bhuvanagiri his own ritual utensils for branding, a key part of the ritual, and asked him to perform the ritual. Bhuvanagiri complied, and the outcaste was renamed Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. +++(5)+++ (This implies that by having a non-Brahmin initiate an outcaste, the Brahmin jīyar thinks he will not be caste-ist or polluted.) Nārāyaṇa Dāsar then studied about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. (I will discuss his biography in the next section.) To counteract the propagation of Śaivism in the area, Bhuvanagiri proselytised among the outcastes and initiated [[P59]] them into Śrīvaiṣṇavism. This activity generated enormous controversy, according to the biography.

Bhuvanagiri became famous for his recitations of devotional songs and talks on Śrīvaiṣṇavism. Even so, he provoked criticism within Śrīvaiṣṇavism for arguing against Brahmanical positions. When he gave lectures at Nammāḻvār’s birthplace (Alvartirunagari) in Tirunelveli district, for instance, the local Brahmin leaders refused to give him accommodation or organise the events.23 In response, Bhuvanagiri’s disciples raised funds and built a rāmānuja kūṭam, where he stayed and lectured.24

Since the Śrīvaiṣṇava schools (pāṭhaśālās)—which taught both the Vedas and the Divyaprabandham—were for Brahmins only, Bhuvanagiri founded his own school in 1912 in Srirangam. Called Rāmānuja Darśana Vidyāśālā, it taught Bhāgavatas the Divyaprabandham, Śrīvaiṣṇava theology, and Tamil literature. Negamam [[Zaimindar|Zamindar]] Ramakrishna Naidu rented a house for the school.25 The first year, it admitted ten students with full board and lodging. Many students attended, including B. R. Purushotamma Naidu (whom I will discuss).

Brahmins vociferously criticised it. Presumably this was because Bhāgavatas were receiving knowledge that customarily belonged to Brahmins alone. When students dwindled and costs rose, the Zamindar closed the school. Bhuvanagiri moved it to Chidambaram, where it continues to this day, only as an elementary school.

In 1914, in Tuttukkuti village, the Śrīvaiṣṇava Mahāsabhā was organised. When Bhuvanagiri gave a lecture, the Brahmin president Rāghava Ayyaṅgar, an authority on Tamil literature, found a mistake. At the end of the meeting, Bhuvanagiri stood up and said that there ‘was no mistake. I can support everything.’ The next day, in the Mahāsabhā, Bhuvanagiri refuted the president’s critique by quoting the relevant authorities. After listening to Bhuvanagiri, Rāghava realised his mistake and met him privately. Nevertheless, from that day on, Bhuvanagiri resolved not to give lectures in the presence of Brahmins. +++(5)+++ [[P60]]

When Bhuvanagiri went away on pilgrimage,26 Brahmins continued to criticise his views for threatening the duties of caste and stages of life (varṇa-āśrama-dharma) in general, and Brahmin control of Śrīvaiṣṇavism in particular. In fact, they complained to the Vānamāmalai jīyar. However, the jīyar, after scrutinising Bhuvanagiri’s activities, told him to continue his good work.27

A Srirangam jīyar, Ayodhyā Rāma Svāmi, sided with the opposing Brahmins. In 1918, he published Tattvatraya Vyākhyāna and Tātparya Dīpikā to argue that only Brahmin ācāryas had the right to perform initiations. Bhuvanagiri travelled around Tamil Nadu to counter the Srirangam jīyar’s attacks. His Tattvatraya Vyākhyāna Tātparya Tīpikā Vicāram (1918)28 and Samāśrayaṇam Sarvatikaram (1921) defend the idea that both non-Brahmins and women can perform samāśrayaṇam.29

One of the Brahmins with whom Bhuvanagiri debated was Śrīraṅga Rāmānuja Jīyar, who had argued in a book that because the ritual for initiation involved a sacred fire, the ritual was Vedic and therefore could be performed only by Brahmins. In 1919, at Tiruvahindrapuram, Bhuvanagiri countered this by arguing that the fire used for branding was a Vaiṣṇava fire, not a Vedic one, and so non-Brahmins could perform samāśrayaṇam.30

That same year, Bhuvanagiri gave a public lecture in Cuddalore on the Brahmins’ monopoly on initiation. Quoting elaborately from the Divyaprabandham and sampradāya works, he argued once again that initiation is not the exclusive right of Brahmins. Rather, anyone who has expertise in these works, is devoted to God, and follows the behavioural norms of the scriptures, has a right to perform it. That includes women.31 Although Brahmin scholars from several communities were invited to debate him, they requested a postponement and failed to show up even at the later date. [[P61]]

After meeting Govindarājulu from Manakkuppam (Cuddalore), who had become a government servant at Tirukkoyilur, Bhuvanagiri asked him to teach deserving devotees about Śrīvaiṣṇavism. On a later occasion, Bhuvanagiri asked him to initiate them and gave him the required conch and wheel-branding tools.

In 1926, Bhuvanagiri established his own [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] in Sriperumbudur. He called it Maṇavāḷa Māmunikaḷ Maṭam, after the famous ācārya Maṇavāḷa Māmunikaḷ (whom I date 1370–1443), and lived there until his death the following year (11 June 1927). In his will, he instructed that his samādhi should be built in the corner of a garden that belonged to his devotee Lakṣmiprapanna. Bhuvanagiri’s samādhi is now in an open field on the outskirts of Sriperumbudur, along with the samādhi of his disciple Prabandhasvāmi.

A [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] called Emmidi Maṭam, which a disciple established during Bhuvanagiri’s lifetime, exists in Sriperumbudur. Today, its head is a single man who is an ordinary devotee. When I interviewed him, he said that on every anniversary of Bhuvanagiri’s death, his descendants come from the Puducherry area and perform pūjā at the samādhis that his grandsons fixed up in 2005.

There is also a temple in Bhuvanagiri’s birthplace dedicated to the presiding deity Pattabi Rāmaṉ. Besides the main shrine, there are three smaller ones on the right, to Bhuvanagiri, Rāmānuja and Nammāḻvār.32

Nārāyaṇa Dāsar

Nārāyaṇa Dāsar (1872–1912) was born to Rāmadāsar, a staunch outcaste devotee of Lord Viṣṇu and Periyammā in the year 1872; they named him Nārāyaṇaṉ. The family lived in Kottaimedu, now known as Karunapuram. Rāmadāsar was a farmer, owned land, and had a kiln. According to Nārāyaṇa Dāsar’s biography,33 which often reads like a hagiography, Nārāyaṇaṉ helped his father. Rather than playing with other boys, he spent his time praying to the Lord.

Nārāyaṇaṉ went to Madras for teacher training. He lived in the Kontittoppu area, which was a stronghold of Vaiṣṇavism. Elsewhere, when hooligans mocked the Vaiṣṇava mark (nāmam) on his forehead, says the biography, he ‘conquered’ [[P62]] them by patience.

After marrying the daughter of a staunch Vaiṣṇava Dalit couple of his village, Nārāyaṇaṉ decided that he did not like married life. He wanted to be an ascetic, and so he left home and went by foot all the way south to the Vānamāmalai Mutt. On his way there, he met a group of yogis and learned astrology, medicine, and yoga from them.

Nārāyaṇaṉ stayed one year at the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] to learn the works of Śrīvaiṣṇavism and asked the jīyar to initiate him into asceticism. By this time, his wife had figured out where her husband had gone and came to the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] to bring him home. The jīyar, learning that the young man was married, refused to initiate him, commanding him to remain married and serve the Lord that way. Nārāyaṇaṉ returned home and lived devoutly with his wife. As I mentioned, Bhuvanagiri gave him samāśrayaṇam and the initiation name Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. Nārāyaṇa Dāsar used to send one-third of his salary to Bhuvanagiri every month, until his retirement. During holidays, he went on pilgrimage to sacred shrines.

Nārāyaṇa Dāsar was the headmaster of the government elementary school for outcastes in Virapandi, near Tirukkoyilur. The village had a separate government school for the upper castes too. Gradually, the villagers recognised Nārāyaṇa Dāsar as an able teacher and a man of noble qualities. Even high-caste students wanted to attend his school.

The biography describes a miracle. Learning that Nārāyaṇa Dāsar was bedridden, Sundararāja Bhāgavatar, Bhuvanagiri’s son-in-law, went to see him. On his way, he stopped at the Chidambaram temple. While worshipping Lord Govindarāja there, he prayed for the early recovery of Nārāyaṇa Dāsar. Sundararāja was sitting on the edge of the sacred tank when a priest handed him a basket full of prasāda (sacred food) and a garland. The priest said that Nārāyaṇa Dāsar had come there in the early morning and worshipped the Lord, but had left the temple without taking the sacred food. When the priest went away, Sundararāja wondered how Nārāyaṇa Dāsar could have visited the temple when he was far away and bedridden. Not only was this a miracle, Sundararāja decided, so was the [[P63]] fact that the Lord himself in the guise of a priest had given Nārāyaṇa Dāsar the prasāda.

Sundararāja Bhāgavatar finally reached the house of Nārāyaṇa Dāsar, where he lay in a semi-conscious state. Nārāyaṇa Dāsar woke up and sat on his cot. His guest gave him the prasāda and the garland of Lord Govindarāja in Chidambaram. Nārāyaṇa Dāsar was in trance. Uttering the Lord’s name, Nārāyaṇa, he died and attained liberation.34

After the news spread to surrounding villages, people came and placed garlands on his body. There was a funeral procession with chanting of the Divyaprabandham and Vedic mantras. On the way to the cremation ground, people worshipped him with lighted camphor. Finally, he was cremated with sandalwood. When the fire cooled, they created the samādhi (the memorial temple to honour him). This was followed by a thirteen-day funeral ceremony, during which people chanted the Divyaprabandham and Rāmānuja-nūṟṟantāti (a work in praise of Rāmānuja). The funeral was to conclude with a ritual, except there was no rice for it. Nārāyana Dāsar’s chief disciple (Āṟumuka Bhāgavatar) gave some paddy that he had saved for seedlings. Later, his seedling supply miraculously replenished itself and the paddy overflowed.

People believed that Nārāyaṇa Dāsar had performed many miracles and honoured him, especially during the month of Mārkaḻi. At his samādhi, devotees of many castes, especially the Vanniyars, worship him even today. Because he had forbidden his followers to place images of him in the shrine, there is only a nāmam image there, with the discus and conch on either side.

(Kōyiḻ) Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ

(Kōyiḻ) Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ (1850–1907) is also called [[Ekāṅgi Rāmānuja Dāsar|Ekāṅgi Rāmānuja Dāsar]], Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ and Kōyiḷ Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Rāmānuja Ekāṅgi. Given his many names and the overlap of one name with one of Bhuvanagiri’s, I will call him here simply Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ. Although we do not know his birth name, his biography35 notes that he was [[P64]] born in Srirangam to parents of a family ‘related to the Lord’ (bhagavat-sambandha). This, along with the presence of the word kōyiḻ (meaning temple, specifically Srirangam) in one of his names, likely alludes to a Sattada family identity, that is, a non-Brahmin family, which had the hereditary right to serve in the temple by protecting the deity and treasury, as well as by carrying processional torches and making special garlands for the pūjā.

The biography points out that during his school years, Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ preferred the company of Śrīvaiṣṇava elders who gave religious lectures. Very orthodox and pious, he was always keen to worship Lord Raṅganātha. With another Bhāgavata, Kōyiḻ Rāmānuja Dāsar, he visited many sacred places mentioned by the āḻvārs (divyadeśas) and then returned to Srirangam.

Following hagiographic precedent, the account describes several supernatural events. On one occasion, the pious boy was walking through the paddy fields and, like Nammāḻvār, fell down unconscious because of his eagerness to receive God’s vision.36 A Bhāgavata devotee helped him get up and return to the temple. Then the devotee revealed that he was God himself.

On another occasion, the Lord appeared in a dream and instructed him to become a disciple of the Brahmin Ciṟupuliyūr Jīyar (see Chapter 6). As usual, the future ekāṅgi went to the Kaveri, had his bath, put on his nāmam and worshipped Lord Raṅganātha in the temple. As he was returning, a jīyar came out of the temple tank. The future ekāṅgi saw that this was the same person who had appeared in his dream. He prostrated to the jīyar and requested samāśrayaṇam along with Śrīvaiṣṇava teachings. After he narrated his dream to the jīyar, the latter told him about having a similar dream. ‘I am pleased,’ the jīyar said, that ‘you are the one who appeared in my dream’. Then the jīyar initiated him and taught him verses from the Divyaprabandham.

The fact that the jīyar does not mention the name of his own preceptor might allude to the [[j īyar’s|jīyar’s]] self-initiation because [[P65]] the name (and lineage) of one’s preceptor is very important to an initiate, who must chant a verse about him daily. Of course, it might simply mean that the author of the biography did not know this detail.

The biography continues with a story about how the ekāṅgi-to-be wanted to learn the Sanskrit Veda but was unable to produce the correct intonation (svara). (Perhaps the real situation was that the Brahmins would not teach him Sanskrit and the Vedas in their schools.) Extremely disappointed, he tried to commit suicide on the banks of the Kaveri. Although he was seriously wounded, another non-Brahmin devotee got him treatment, and he lived.

The future ekāṅgi then went to Ciṟupuliyūr Jīyar and asked for initiation into asceticism. The jīyar did so, blessed him and gave him a Krishna image for daily worship (a very unusual act for a Brahmin). Now also known as Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, he built a small [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] for himself on the banks of the Kaveri river.

Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ gave samāśrayaṇam to everyone who wanted it and, as had Rāmānuja Nāvalar and Bhuvanagiri, lectured on the Śrīvaiṣṇava tradition, even though only Brahmins had done these things. He had hundreds of followers.37 When he went on pilgrimages to Srivilliputur, Alvartirunagari, Tirukkottiyur, Chidambaram and elsewhere, he gave lectures, performed initiations and defeated opponents in debate, which established his scholarly credentials. After a while, at the invitation of two pious Bhāgavatas, Rāghava Dāsar and Ceṅkalvarāya Nāyakar, he moved to an ashram that the latter had built for him in his own garden in Madras. From that time, [[Ekāngi|Ekāṅgi]] Svāmikaḷ gave religious lectures all over Madras. They were so popular that many people became Śrīvaiṣṇava devotees. His lectures on [[āṇṭāḷ|Āṇṭāḷ]]’s Tiruppāvai were especially popular.

The more famous he became, the more some Brahmins tried to prevent his radical behaviour. A Brahmin at Sriperumbudur, for instance, told him that he cannot give samāśrayaṇam. That was only for Brahmins to do. Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ refuted his [[P66]] arguments. When the Brahmins approached Empār Jīyar there and informed him about these arguments, Empār, though a Brahmin himself, replied that Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ was correct.38

As Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ continued his lectures and teaching of the Divyaprabandham,39 controversy flared again. One Brahmin, Cittākāra Śrīnivāsaācārya, told him that only Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas give initiation and call themselves ācāryas. Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ convinced the Brahmin that others could do so by using the same arguments that he had used at Sriperumbudur. Later, Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ went to Tiruchirappalli and many other Śrīvaiṣṇava sacred places to debate the matter of who could do initiations.

The biography concludes by reaffirming Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ’s teaching: that anyone may give initiation and recite the Divyaprabandham.40 Even though he was abused, he was a religious leader, an ācārya like Bhaṭṭar (twelfth century) and Nampiḷḷai (thirteenth century), two famous ācāryas. When he died in 1907, his body was placed in a samādhi in a western suburb of Madras.41 His disciple, Appaṉ Rāmānuja Swāmi, was persuaded to take over his role.42

I visited Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s samādhi in 2007 and 2008 (see Chapter 3), in what is now the neighbourhood of Ekangipuram. The samādhi looked like a small temple. The focal point was a painting of Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ, which was probably painted from a photograph.43 Beside his staff and sandals, the shrine had photographs of some disciples such as Appaṉ,44 along with a picture of [[āṇṭāḷ|Āṇṭāḷ]].

Gōpāla Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai

Gōpāla Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai (end of the nineteenth century) came from a Yadava (cowherd) family and was a disciple of the Brahmin ācārya Kōyiḻāṇṭāṉ Swāmi of Srirangam.45 We know almost nothing about Gōpāla Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai’s life, because no one wrote his biography. We do know that he was the president of the Varavaramunendra Sabhā near Triplicane, a section of central Chennai that borders the sea and is the location of the [[P67]] Parthasarathy temple.46 And we know that he published the Ācārya Hṛdaya Sāra Saṅgraham47 in 1892.

In his Foreword, he says that he wrote this work in simple Tamil so that everyone could understand Nammāḻvār’s Tiruvāymoḻi and the views of commentators. He then discusses the essence of the Ācārya Hṛdayam48 and provides several appendices that list major topics, supporting them with quotations from the commentaries about what ācāryas such as Rāmānuja had said, justifications for their interpretations, unusual interpretations of Nammāḻvār, his own contribution to the interpretations, the meaning of important Sanskrit words in the commentary, and a list of corrections.

How did [[G ōpāla|Gōpāla]] Kṛṣṇa Piḷḷai learn all this? He might have listened to discourses by various ācāryas, taken notes and collated them. There were other opportunities, which I have already mentioned. He might have studied with the jīyars at Emperumāṉār Mutt in Tirukkoyilur, or at Vānamāmalai Mutt in Nanguneri (Tirunelveli district), for instance, or studied with Rāmānuja Nāvalar or Bhuvanagiri.

Appāvu Mudaliyār

Appāvu Mudaliyār (end of the nineteenth century) was the first to publish the Divyaprabandham,49 thereby defying the attempt by Brahmins to control the Tamil scripture by not allowing its publication. The Brahmins had feared that non-Brahmins would read it and claim the right to chant it and lecture about it, which is exactly what happened.

B. R. Purushottama Naidu

The tradition of scholarship continued in Bhuvanagiri’s family. His brother Rājagopāla’s son was B. R. Purushottama Naidu (1901–?). Purushottama was initiated by Bhuvanagiri and considered him his ācārya. He spent three years in the Śrīvaiṣṇava school (pāṭhaśalā) in Srirangam, sponsored by the [[P68]] non-Brahmin Negamam Zamindar and run by Bhuvanagiri. After that, he studied with well-known scholars, some Brahmins and others non-Brahmins.50

His scholarship attracted attention. He became a professor of Tamil in 1935 at Government Tamil College, Tanjavur, and joined the University of Madras Tamil Department as a Reader in 1948. Editing important classics of Śrīvaiṣṇavism,51 he simplified the language of these works by translating the Maṇipravāḷam into Tamil. (In fact, this process of Tamilisation had been occurring for some time in Brahmin scholarship, too, and many scholarly aids had been prepared to make the works more easily understood.) At the beginning of his edition of Aḻakiya-maṇavāḷa-perumāḷ-nāyaṉār’s Ācārya Hṛdayam, he comments that his father’s elder brother Bhuvanagiri had prepared the notes for this work. Because he is using these notes, the entire work is really that of Bhuvanagiri. In his introduction to his edition of Piḷḷai Lokācārya’s Śrīvacana Bhūṣaṇam, he mentions that when ‘writing this book, I received the assistance of the University Grants Commission’.52

The Road to Recognition

It was difficult for Bhāgavatas to become religious leaders when Brahmins controlled the teaching of Sanskrit and the basic doctrines of the tradition, which were only taught orally by Brahmins. Oddly, non-Brahmin entry into this realm was facilitated by the need to address Dalit mobilisation and the attraction of people to other sects or even other religions. In other words, what started out as leadership by default for elite non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas turned into serious activism. Here is how that happened.

Gaining Esoteric Knowledge

The Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin challenge to Brahmin roles had several basic components. One was gaining access to esoteric learning in order to qualify as scholars of the tradition. This happened in several ways. Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, for instance, learned much by listening to lectures by Brahmins and learning from his Brahmin jīyar, Ciṟupuliyūr (he would not have attended the school started by Bhuvanagiri, which taught Sanskrit to both Brahmins and non-Brahmins; it started only in 1912). We are told that he had difficulty chanting Sanskrit and therefore could not learn that language in the traditional Brahmanical manner. Perhaps this was a circumlocution to avoid saying directly that no Brahmin would teach him. Whatever the case, he did not learn Sanskrit. The fact that the commentaries on the āḻvārs’ verses and many of the formative works about Śrīvaiṣṇavism were written in Maṇipravāḷam (a combination of Tamil and Sanskrit) meant that, without Sanskrit, he also could not read these works, and had to rely on oral sources.

By contrast, Rāmānuja Nāvalar learned Sanskrit from the Vānamāmalai jīyar and thus also had access to Sanskrit commentaries and treatises, as well as Maṇipravāḷa ones. His contribution, therefore, was showing that non-Brahmins were capable translators and interpreters of texts. Bhuvanagiri too had the advantage of learning Sanskrit from both Rāmānuja Nāvalar and the Vānamāmalai jīyar, which gave him access to all the sampradāya literature.

Therefore, a few Brahmin [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s were willing to help Bhāgavatas become educated in the tradition. This means that they deviated from traditional Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmanical control of the intellectual tradition through Sanskrit, Maṇipravāḷam, and schools only for Brahmins. No doubt the theology of inclusiveness in Śrīvaiṣṇava works, especially those of Maṇavāḷa-māmuni, was pressed into service to support the non-Brahmins, and counter the proselytism of other religions. [[P70]]

Why the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s? Maybe these jīyars were more entrepreneurial in recruiting new disciples because the lineage in [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s did not pass down through a family, with generational links to the families of their disciples. Because these [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s had strong Telugu and non-Brahmin connections, they might have been more relaxed about maintaining convention and took the inclusive theology to heart. They were also in remote agricultural areas, where non-Brahmin landlords such as Naidus were important patrons, and where it was important to attract agricultural workers so that they would not move elsewhere.

In any case, those non-Brahmins who had learned Sanskrit, Maṇipravāḷam, and the sectarian texts then began to teach other non-Brahmins. Bhāgavata support for these students and scholars helped extend non-Brahmin expertise. So did the use of the printing press to publish their works. (The printing press had arrived in Goa in 1556 and was used by Tranquebar missionaries in Tamil Nadu in 1714. Until 1835, Indians were not allowed to license printing presses from the Europeans. The latter, however, began themselves to publish Tamil texts.)

Besides, the fact that some non-Brahmins translated Sanskrit and Maṇipravāḷa works into Tamil dramatically democratised access to knowledge, as did Appāvu Mudaliyār’s publication of the Divyaprabandham—the key Tamil scripture.

Becoming Ācāryas by Default and then Revolt

In this context, it is important to remember that Dalits were mobilising. As Rupa Viswanath has documented in The Pariah Problem: Caste, Religion, and the Social in Modern India [[P71]] (2014), Dalits in the nineteenth century provided slave labour and were segregated in ghettos, but were virtually ignored by the colonial government despite agitation in Britain to do something about slavery. Only when Pariahs began to convert to Christianity en masse from the late 1870s did their plight begin to be addressed with more education, the right to own property, and by 1910, their own representation of their communities in governmental bodies.53

This period is exactly when our Bhāgavatas, with the blessings of some jīyars with [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s in agricultural areas, began to initiate Dalits. It is highly likely that there was a link between the desire to stop Dalit conversions, pressure to improve their conditions and Bhāgavata performance of initiations.

We know that Bhuvanagiri had connections with Dalits. According to his biography, on one occasion, some Śrīvaiṣṇava outcastes who had built [[āṇṭāḷ|Āṇṭāḷ]] temple asked for his help. They were being harassed by someone who did not want Śrīvaiṣṇavism to be practised by outcastes.54 To protect their worship, Bhuvanagiri not only went there immediately and drove the harasser away, but he also stayed a while to teach them more about Śrīvaiṣṇavism.

The biography describes how Bhuvanagiri preached about Śrīvaiṣṇavism to the Dalits of a Tamil village55 in order to counter the propagation of Śaivism there. After that, the Dalits wanted him to initiate them. He asked them to have the Vānamāmalai jīyar perform the ritual. The jīyar insisted, however, that Bhuvanagiri do it instead. After teaching Dalits about the importance of tāpa (branding the shoulders with conch and discus), Bhuvanagiri performed the initiation ritual.

Among the Dalits who became Bhuvanagiri’s disciples, as I have already said, was Nārāyaṇa Dāsar from near Tirukkoyilur. This time, Bhuvanagiri asked the jīyar at Tirukkoyilur to perform the initiation. Like the Vānamāmalai jīyar, however, he refused. Instead, he gave his conch and discus-branding tool to Bhuvanagiri and asked him to do it.

Although I found nothing about Dalits directly in Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s biography, I have found indirect evidence that he [[P72]] too had a special interest in them. His samādhi, for example, was located in a paṟaiccēri, that is, an outcaste neighbourhood, in what is now a suburb of northwest Chennai. There, he used to give lectures to the Dalits. This area is now called Ekangipuram after him.56

How do we explain the reluctance of Brahmin jīyars and ācāryas in the Teṉkalai tradition to initiate Dalits? After all, one āḻvār, Tiruppāṇ, was an outcaste. Between a rock (the inclusive theology of Śrīvaiṣṇavism and not wanting outcastes to convert to another religion such as Śaivism, Christianity, or Buddhism when Vaiṣṇavism was already a minority religion) and a hard place (Teṉkalai Brahmins’ loss of status in the eyes of orthodox Brahmins), Teṉkalai ācāryas or jīyars probably thought it best to encourage disciples such as Bhuvanagiri to initiate the Dalits. There was one Brahmin, and possibly two, however, who did initiate outcastes ([[Sāttvikaācārya|Sāttvika Ācārya]] [with spacing corrected], whom I discuss in [[Chapter6|Chapter 6]]), and possibly others.

Furthermore, it is likely that non-Brahmins such as Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ saw an opportunity for higher status and more disciples for themselves, because they now would have ritual roles that had once been exclusively Brahmin. So, Bhāgavatas gained the right to perform initiations initially by default, it seems, and not by design. The Brahmins needed the job done for outcastes, yet did not want to do it themselves, so they turned it over to a few learned non-Brahmins. Nevertheless, once these non-Brahmins had the expertise and the legitimation, they began to argue their right to do it for everyone, and thereby began to usurp what had been a carefully protected Brahmanical role. Struggling into the Pulavar World

As described in early Tamil poetry, poets (pulavars) praised rulers in oral performances to broadcast their fame and thereby increase their status in return for gifts, including economic support, food, and sometimes items of conspicuous consumption. By medieval times, patrons were not just rulers, they were also temples, [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s, or wealthy individuals.

In Colonizing the Realm of Words,57 Sascha Ebeling (2010) gives us insight into the transformations of the pulavar ‘economy of praise’ as the poets adapted to colonial power and the transformation of an oral performance culture to one of print and a reading public. Although Ebeling discusses this pulavar world in the context of Śaivism, his analysis helps us understand the quandaries faced by non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas.

From the medieval period, Śaiva [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s were patrons of pulavar culture. On the model of royal and temple processions, monastic heads toured the countryside in ostentatious processions with other dignitaries, elephants, musicians and dancing girls. The birthdays of [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] founders were celebrated with grand festivals, moreover, with poets performing their works, chanters reciting Vedic and Tēvāram verses (the Śaiva bhakti verses of Campantar, Appar and Cuntarar), learned men giving discourses, and musicians entertaining the crowd with concerts.

The fact that there were major non-Brahmin Śaiva [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s meant that non-Brahmins participated in this transactional pulavar culture as patrons and poets. According to Ebeling, this ostentatious pulavar culture became even more important at the beginning of the nineteenth century as the Madras Presidency consolidated its power by turning independent rulers into subordinates, through curtailing warfare and therefore the status of military men, especially those who were victorious.

Some Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins participated in pulavar cosmopolitan culture, both in their own [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s as centres of learning and also in royal courts, if the hagiographic stories of poetic competitions there reflect historical reality. In this context, poetic works such as Garuḍavāhana’s Divyasūricaritam might have been creations in a pulavar milieu.

A booklet58 published by the Bysani-Madhava Chetty Charity (1820–1900) provides an insight into Brahmin and non-Brahmin status in relation to sampradāya knowledge in [[P74]] the nineteenth century.

The booklet mentions the kinds of lecturer that are funded. These may be learned Brahmins or Bhāgavatas who have been initiated into the mantras by ācārya puruṣas (those belonging to one of the 74 ācārya families), more specifically, those who have had samāśrayaṇam in the Teṉkalai tradition.

In addition, the booklet offers support to Bhāgavatas who know Piḷḷai Lokācārya’s 18 Rahasya (secret) works and commentaries, and who can recite the 4,000 verses of the Divyaprabandham, poems on the lives of the āḻvārs and various praise-poems (stotrams). (These were demanding prerequisites for anyone!) The booklet mentions non-Brahmins in a separate section after the Brahmins, although it recognises their learning and supports them, whether as lecturers or students.

Śaivism and Vaiṣṇavism Compared

Unlike the Vellalar Śaiva Siddhāntins who had wealthy non-Brahmin [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s that were involved in pulavar culture, Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s—there were a few—were very modest affairs, without libraries, and more like the rāmānuja-kūṭams, the charitable feeding houses. It was probably more an exception than a rule that the birthdays of their founders or leaders were celebrated on the same scale as the Śaiva ones, although we do have the case of the fifteenth-century figure Kaṉṭāṭai Rāmānuja Ayyaṅkar, the manager of many rāmānuja kūṭams in Tirupati and Tirumalai, who celebrated his birthday with processions of musicians, dancing girls, and others.

Influence from [[fromāṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] Nāvalar?

In this context of pulavar culture, it is useful to think about the possible influence on Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins of the Sri [[Lankanāṟumuka|Lankan Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] Nāvalar (1822–79), who came to live in [[P75]] Tamil Nadu.

After learning Tamil literature from his father, a poet, [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] attended a missionary school to learn English. An excellent student, he eventually found work at the school as a teacher of English and Tamil, and then as an assistant to the missionary and orientalist Peter Percival, helping him to translate the Book of Common Prayer, the Bible, and other Christian works.

[[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] Nāvalar turned his attention next to editing and publishing Tamil works, for which he founded a press, the Preservation of Knowledge Press. In 1852, [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] brought out a Tamil prose version of the Periya-purāṇam (a medieval text in verse about the lives of the Śaiva saints), which he hoped would inspire Śaivas to maintain their traditions. He followed that with polemical tracts against Christianity, including The Abolition of the Abuse of Śaivism (1854). That same year (he was then thirty-two), he took an advanced initiation in Śaiva Siddhānta, which made him an ascetic.

[[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] spent the next twenty-five years in both Sri Lanka and Tamil Nadu publishing books (he himself brought out about ninety-seven works of various kinds), establishing more schools (eleven in Sri Lanka and one in Chidambaram), and battling against Christian missionary polemics.

Dennis Hudson puts his accomplishments this way:

Āṟumuka Nāvalar changed Śaiva Orthodoxy by creating a new Śaiva school system that fused modern knowledge with sectarian ideas and by revitalizing Śaiva culture through temple reform, preaching, and the printing press. He created an “evangelical” Śaivism by stressing scripture over custom and by seeking an individually motivated spiritual regeneration of culture.59

Āṟumuka fell into no conventional category of Śaiva scholar, comments Hudson;60 he was an independent, celibate scholar.61

I see intriguing similarities between Rāmānuja Nāvalar and [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] Nāvalar. Both belonged to elite non-Brahmin castes (Naidu and Vellala, respectively), both published many works (Rāmānuja Nāvalar about forty-six and [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] approximately double that number). Both were called Nāvalar because of their erudition, both knew Tamil and Sanskrit, and both resorted to the British legal system to fight their opponents.

I see parallels too between [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] and Bhuvanagiri. [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] became upset over Christian conversions of Śaiva youth. In 1848, he helped to found a school that would teach not only Western subjects with a Western pedagogy, but also Hindu ones such as the Vedas, āgamas and Śaiva Siddhānta. Like [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected], Bhuvanagiri started a school in Srirangam and then Chidambaram that included religious subjects, but to my knowledge no English or Western subjects. Although the Srirangam school soon collapsed for lack of funding, it set a precedent. Like [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected], moreover, Bhuvanagiri travelled around the countryside, preaching. Bhuvanagiri lived a bit later than [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] (he would have been nineteen when [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] died); however, he probably knew of him. Chidambaram, where [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] spent considerable time, was not far from Cuddalore, near where Bhuvanagiri lived.

Here is another similarity. [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] had built a small temple in Chidambaram.62 Soon after, the temple acquired an image of [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]].63 According to Hudson, elsewhere, images of him were likely worshipped. From 1910, [[āṟumuka’s|Āṟumuka’s]] [with spelling corrected] birthday was celebrated like those of the early medieval Śaiva saints, the nāyaṉmār, who were worshipped in image-form.64 As I have noted, images or portraits were also made for Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Swāmikaḷ, to be worshipped in household shrines, temples, rāmānuja kūṭams, or samādhi shrines.

There are differences, too, between [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] and the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders I have discussed here. For instance, I can find no direct links between the latter and the missionary orientalists who promoted Dravidian nationalism or provided access to Western learning, even though Bhuvanagiri lived near Cuddalore, an important trade centre for the East Indian Company, and a Christian mission centre. Another difference was about caste. [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] argued that śūdras (including Vellalars) may not recite the Vedas or [[P77]] perform Vedic rituals.

Even though Śrīvaiṣṇavism had an āgamic tradition—the Pañcarātra āgamas—knowledge of this literature was of interest mainly to Brahmin temple priests. At that time, Bhāgavatas were not interested in becoming āgamic priests, even if they had been allowed.

Āṟumuka states that Śaiva non-Brahmins who have had the Āgamic ritual of dīkṣā (which involves becoming vegetarian) become twice-born, indeed Brahmins, if they practice the way of life appropriate to their varṇa and āgamic status. They have the authority to recite the āgamas, though not the Vedas, and to be ācāryas.

Like the Śaiva dīkṣā, Śrīvaiṣṇava samāśrayaṇam involves becoming vegetarian; however, the ritual per se does not transform a Śrīvaiṣṇava into a twice-born or Brahmin. Rather, the ritual ostensibly provides access to salvation (indirectly, it probably provides access to social or religious status as well). If we understand an ācārya as a teacher and interpreter of the tradition, then Bhāgavatas certainly wanted to be ācāryas. The Śrīvaiṣṇava understanding of ācārya was more specific, however, than just being a teacher and exegete; it also meant belonging to one of the seventy-four Brahmin families supposedly appointed by Rāmānuja.

Finally, [[āṟumuka|Āṟumuka]] [with spelling corrected] had quite a different position on Dalits than did the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders of his time. He thought that they were so innately impure that they could never have dīkṣā in the āgamic tradition. Although they could worship God and attain salvation, they could not enter the temples. By contrast, the nineteenth-century Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavata leaders considered Dalits worthy not only of samāśrayaṇam, but also of deep respect and honour. Yet, even the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins did not do much about temple entry until the mid-twentieth century. [[P76]]

The major difference between the non-Brahmin Śaivas and Śrīvaiṣṇavas was that the former had an important institutional base. Many Śaiva [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s, after all, were the exclusive domain of non-Brahmins. Bhāgavatas controlled no major [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s and thus had no established centres of learning, libraries, or formal leadership roles, although they did have Rāmānuja kūṭams.

Influence from Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ?

A comparison of the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders mentioned here with Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ (1876–1950), who was [[āṟumuka’s|Āṟumuka’s]] disciple, is somewhat different. According to Ravi [[Vaithess|Vaithees]] [with spelling corrected], Maṟaimalai promoted reform from within the religion, and a neo-Śaivism that was pro-Dravidian, pro-Tamil, and anti-caste in the tradition of Rāmaliṅga.65 Sometimes Aṭikaḷ aligned with Periyar and sometimes he differed from him. Vaithees argues that nevertheless, we can see religious roots of the secular Self-Respect movement in Aṭikaḷ’s thought.66

Like Maṟaimalai Aṭikaḷ, Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins were against caste discrimination, which made some of them join the Self-Respect movement (although others did not, because of its avowed atheism). Nonetheless, most elite Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins of the time were not as anti-Sanskrit as Maṟaimalai. They wanted access to the world of Sanskrit learning, not its destruction.

Finally, the fact that these non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava leaders did not claim that their religion could be found in Tamil origins, or that it was central to the nineteenth-century Tamil Renaissance, meant that their message was marginal to the great identity narratives of the day, which Śaivas had successfully appropriated.

Why Śrīvaiṣṇava Marginalisation?

It is fair to say that non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas in the late nineteenth and early twentieth century were not yet [[P78]] trendsetters, despite their inclusive religious position on caste and women and their growing self-confidence under British rule that broke down some traditional barriers to status. They were also out of the Tamil mainstream, in part because many had ancestors who had come from what became Andhra after independence, but was then part of Madras Presidency.

In the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, all these ‘Andhra’ connections were alive and well. In this context, remember that the ancestors of Rāmānuja Nāvalar and Bhuvanagiri were Naidus whose families had originally migrated from the Andhra region as warrior-cultivators.67 Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ’s family was possibly Sattada (many Sattadas were originally from Andhra or Karnataka). The jīyar at the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] in Tirukkoyilur, who performed initiation for Rāmānuja Nāvalar and Bhuvanagiri, belonged to a lineage that had originated in Andhra. The jīyar there sometimes sent his disciples to study with the *jīyar of the Vānamāmalai Mutt in Nanguneri, which had Andhra connections as well. Both the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] in Tirukkoyilur and the [[ma ṭha|maṭha]] in Nanguneri were located, moreover, in major cotton-growing regions, where many Telugus had migrated in Vijayanagara times to work as cotton-growers. Vijayanagara kings had been partial to Vaiṣṇavism and to Telugu-speaking warriors and other migrants, such as the Naidus, Reddiyars and Vanniyars, who accompanied them into the south.

Non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavism was important in the Telugu-speaking northern parts of Madras Presidency too. I remember discovering in Salarjung Museum (Hyderabad) a set of miniature wooden figures with nāmams painted on their foreheads. They were obviously from colonial times, given their dress. What intrigued me the most was the representation of Śrīvaiṣṇavas across a range of occupations and roles; the figures included policemen, house servants, ascetics, musicians in a band, learned Brahmins, women, labourers, farmers, and others.

The fact that most Bhāgavata leaders were Telugu speakers, not Tamil, helps explain why they were not in the intellectual [[P79]] and literary mainstream, even though there had been Telugu Brahmin literati in the established cosmopolitan pulavar culture, including Carnatic music culture, in Tamil Nadu. Besides, for Śaiva non-Brahmins, Śrīvaiṣṇavism represented a Sanskritised Tamil tradition under Brahmin domination. This might have made it more difficult initially for Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas to be part of a Dravidian movement, which came to champion Tamil as central to identity. Finally, with the Tamil Renaissance and Madras as a new colonial centre of Tamil learning, Telugus were being further marginalised, and had little Telugu nationalism as a counterpoint. At the risk of digression, it is important to understand why.

According to Peter Schmitthenner, before the nineteenth century, Telugus had little sense of a common literary tradition, or even common identity, given their migrations and lack of a common caste, patron-client network, or religious sect.68 This began to change in the nineteenth century with the consolidation of the East India Company rule, and interest in Indian languages and literatures by Company officials and missionaries who collaborated with local scholars and brought Telugu literature into the public eye.

Schmitthenner speaks of a Telugu resurgence developing at this time. One of its leaders was C. P. Brown (1798–1884). A British civil servant and private scholar, he wrote Telugu grammars and dictionaries. In addition, he collected, edited, and published Telugu literature and translated Christian literature into Telugu.69 Brown encouraged the appreciation of Telugu literature in Madras and elsewhere. He often drew on non-Brahmin informants to help gather and understand colloquial Telugu. He was especially fond of the seventeenth-century peasant poet Vemana, who adhered to Vīraśaivism, a Hindu sect mainly found in Karnataka that emphasised humility, tolerance, compassion, vegetarianism, and a casteless society that honoured women as well as men.

Brown’s interest in non-Brahmin language and literature created tensions with Brahmin scholars who wanted to control Telugu literature. Eventually, it would be non-Brahmins who [[P80]] broke this control, although Schmitthenner thinks that they never became as anti-Brahmin as the Tamil non-Brahmins.70

In addition, Madras had become the intellectual centre of Tamil Nadu, but as Eugene Irschick has said, educated Telugus considered Madras ‘a hostile city in which Telugu interests were not looked on kindly. This is not to say that Madras city did not contain many Telugus [but] …. It was the Tamils who tended to monopolize the educational facilities and the administrative and political opportunities in Madras.’71 (Even Telugu Brahmins were marginal. According to C. J. Fuller and Haripriya Narasimhan, the reason Telugu Brahmins lagged behind Tamil Brahmins was because ‘they did not pursue medicine, engineering and administration to the same degree and because they did not have as large patronage networks and organization’.72)

So Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins lost out on several fronts. They were not part of the mainstream pulavar and missionary cultures, for the reasons already discussed. As Telugu speakers, they had neither deep identification with the Tamil Renaissance (focussed on [[Ca ṅkam|Caṅkam]] [with spelling corrected] literature) and incipient Tamil nationalism (focussed on Tamil), nor did they have a vibrant Telugu identity with an institutional base in Madras as counterpoint. In other words, as Telugu speakers, the Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin leaders discussed in this chapter were once-removed Dravidians, notwithstanding many Telugu pulavars in courts and Śaiva [[ma ṭha|maṭha]]s.73 However, even though Telugu was their mother tongue, they did speak Tamil and identified deeply with the Tamil poems of the āḻvārs, their scriptural canon. This would pave the way for their eventual participation in the Dravidian parties to challenge old social hierarchies.

Despite their marginal status, the leaders discussed in this chapter were on the verge of creating a new orientation. [[P81]] Since we cannot characterise it as reform without evidence that Bhāgavatas ever functioned as ācāryas or jīyars in previous centuries, we must call this development a revolt, or at least the seeds of a revolt. Perhaps the participants were even on the verge of creating a new sub-sect. The description of miracles in the biographies of leaders such as Rāmānuja Nāvalar, Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, and the existence of temples and samādhis that enshrined their presence, are signs of sainthood in the making.

Even so, they did not attain the kind of sainthood or prophetic stature that Rāmaliṅga74 or Ayya Vaikuṇṭar did (see Appendix 6). Perhaps this was because they did not have what psychologists of religion call Credibility Enhancing Displays (CREDS), such as dramatic or costly rituals that attract attention, become memorable, and induce group cohesiveness. Neither did they have extreme messianic messages from someone believed to have the power to change the world,75 nor did they create an intellectual sampradāya for lack of institutionalisation; although it appears that the mantle was passed on for a generation or so in several cases. Nevertheless, a few Śrīvaiṣṇava Bhāgavatas developed major scholarly skills, the power of oratory, and a message of justice, which paved the way for identification with Dravidian nationalism.

As for Teṉkalai Brahmins, they had become moribund and could hardly lead the way to social justice. Because they had been put on the defensive to prove that they were real Brahmins, for instance, most were not willing to risk their reputations to adjust to social change, preferring to stay with ‘convention’, despite their caste-inclusive theology. Besides, there were conflicts within Śrīvaiṣṇavism that had been simmering for several centuries, and that led, by the eighteenth century, to a division of the tradition into the Teṉkalai and Vaṭakalai sub-sects, as well as to fierce fights over control of major temples, battles that often ended up in court.76

Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins had little inclination to become involved in caste politics or issues of social justice. Many aligned themselves with other Brahmins to fight for independence [[P82]] from the British. They were open to a few reforms, such as improving the lives of Dalits and women, or shoring up the Śrīvaiṣṇava identity of non-Brahmins to prevent conversions. However, nothing too radical was advocated.

* * *

This chapter has recovered a forgotten history of seven non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava leaders in the colonial period. Although some of them had followers, who kept their memory alive for a while with pamphlets, memorial shrines and discourses about them, almost all knowledge about these leaders has disappeared from historical memory, even many of their books from libraries. Still, through the memories of non-Brahmins involved in this study, pamphlets they provided and visits to their temples and samādhis, we have found traces of a remarkable moment in the history of non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavism.

By reading between the lines of more general discussions of colonial history, it becomes possible to situate their life histories and what little is known about them in the larger social, economic, and political currents of the late-colonial period. We know that this was a time of Dalit awakening, which led to the desire to redress past acts of discrimination at a time when the colonial government was addressing the need to abolish slavery and improve the appalling situation of the outcastes. It was also a time of growing literacy and demand by non-Brahmins for education.

For most of the Bhāgavatas discussed here, their greatest grievance was lack of access to knowledge about their religion, because Brahmins had always had that firmly in control by allowing only Brahmins to learn Sanskrit and Maṇipravāḷam, the languages of the sect’s many erudite works, despite claims that the sect was inclusive of all castes.

Once Bhāgavata leaders had access to Sanskrit and sampradāya works, and had opportunities to perform samāśrayaṇam for Dalits—because even the liberal ācāryas and jīyars did not want to do this—they [[P83]] decided that they too could be ācāryas or jīyars, and so they took on this role for other non-Brahmins and Dalits. More than that, they argued that anyone with the requisite knowledge and initiation, śūdras and women, too, could be such religious leaders.+++(4)+++

Despite the fact that they travelled about Tamil Nadu preaching about the sect, converting people and debating with Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins on the one hand, and with Śaivas on the other, the lasting impact of their activism, it seems, was the creation of access to Śrīvaiṣṇava literature for non-Brahmins.+++(4)+++ This led to roles as translators, commentators, and above all, teachers, about the religion, all of which paved the way to modern scholarship on the religion by non-Brahmins and the confident religious leadership that non-Brahmins practise today.

The second major contribution was alerting the Śrīvaiṣṇava community to the fact that they must take Dalits seriously, not as mere symbols of inclusiveness, but as bona fide devotees. That change, however, has been much slower in coming.+++(4)+++
