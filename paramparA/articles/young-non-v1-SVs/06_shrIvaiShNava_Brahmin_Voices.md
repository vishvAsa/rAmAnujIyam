In my interviews with Bhāgavatas, the topic of Brahmins came up many times. Sometimes non-Brahmins viewed them negatively, sometimes positively, sometimes ambivalently. In this chapter, I begin with two Brahmins who lived during colonial times, and whose names had surfaced in my interviews and the biographies of the non-Brahmin leaders I discussed in Chapter 2. After this, I examine the field notes of Raymond Williams, who interviewed *ācārya*s and their relatives in 1988 and 1991 in order to detect changes that were occurring in their roles.1 Presenting my own interviews, I search for further changes over the past twenty-five years. Finally, I compare my findings with the study of Tamil Brahmins by C.J.Fuller and Haripriya Narasimhan, published in 2014.2



Nineteenth-Century Radical Brahmins

When doing research for Chapter 2, I encountered memories or biographical details about two Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins who had lived in the late nineteenth century and been vanguards of social change. Their own radical actions—both initiated 







Dalits—might have been inspired by religion, economics, or politics. 



Sāttvikaācārya 

Sāttvikaācārya lived in Tirumalicai \(between Chennai and Sriperumbudur\) in the late nineteenth century. When I visited Tirumalicai, a person who had ancestors related to Sāttvika3 told me that in Sāttvika’s time, a Dalit ascetic lived in a hamlet near Tirumalicai; after the ascetic’s death, a small shrine for him was built there.

Sāttvika began doing initiations for Dalits. When other 

Brahmins objected, Sāttvika argued with them. He quoted Nammāḻvār’s *Tiruvāymoḻi* 3.7.9, the now familiar verse about outcaste devotees being the paradigmatic preceptors.4 He also told them the story about Rāmānuja allowing outcastes into the Melkote temple and supported this story by referring to Śrīvaiṣṇava works that denounce discrimination.5

Appalācāri Svāmi, Sāttvika’s son, continued this tradition 

of initiating Dalits. His influence is still remembered. Śrīvaiṣṇava Dalits in a suburb of Chennai called Perambur received *samāśrayaṇam* from Sāttivka and his son. So too did the family of J. J. Thirunarayana Ramanuja Dasar \(whom I discussed in Chapter 3\).

I learned that someone else now does the ritual for those 

who want to have initiation; however, many Dalits are no longer interested in *samāśrayaṇam*. Still, if anyone were to ask, ‘who are the disciples of Svāmi?’, the Dalit disciples of Appalācāri would identify themselves and chant theirācārya’s lineage. A descendant of Sāttvika has a painting of him in his home shrine and still has all his books.

Ethnography from the Late Twentieth Century

We flash forward to the late twentieth century to learn about how the momentous changes of the non-Brahmin movement and Indian independence had affected the status and role of *ācārya*s. 

In 1988 and 1991, Raymond Williams interviewed *ācārya*s 

who were encountering many challenges related to the maintenance of orthodoxy, education, economic support, and the religious ideal of inclusiveness.

Through his interviews, Williams learned about orthodox 

rules in manuals from earlier times. These focussed on daily routines in connection with food and hygiene \(such as washing hands thirty-two times with a particular form of mud\). Despite the fact that many Brahmins once followed this code of 

229







conduct, most had abandoned its rules by the time of Williams’ interviews. Young Brahmins usually did not want to wear traditional dress, for instance, because that led to mockery in school or jobs. One *ācārya* said that wearing traditional dress and the *nāmam* marginalised him, except in Śrīvaiṣṇava circles during the performance of rituals.

The requirements of orthodoxy were especially strict for 

*ācārya*s. Informants from *ācārya* families told Williams that maintaining orthodoxy within the family made it difficult to arrange marriages for their sons. Young women now wanted refrigerators and other modern conveniences, and to watch movies, television shows, and videos. Even though it was prestigious to marry an *ācārya*, they did not want to maintain restrictions on food, such as not eating outside the home, or eating only food that orthodox Brahmins had prepared. Others disliked the fact that they could not continue to study in secular institutions or take jobs after marrying an *ācārya*. Moreover, future *ācārya*s were supposed to marry only daughters of *ācārya* families. That was becoming increasingly difficult because so few families were strictly following their *ācārya* traditions. 

Another problem for *ācārya*s who needed to maintain 

orthodoxy was travel. In the past, they would not travel locally by car, much less go abroad. One person told Williams about his own family. When someone travelled abroad, all four of his children died ‘in very mysterious circumstances’.9

Williams found that education presented a problem 

for *ācārya* families. Members of these families told him that disciples expected *ācārya*s to know both Sanskrit and Tamil and the Śrīvaiṣṇava scriptures. At the minimum, in order to take part in all the rituals and festivals, they had to recite from memory the 4,000 *āḻvār* verses, some Upaniṣadic passages and Sanskrit verses in praise of God, Rāmānuja, and other *ācārya*s. Moreover, they had to know the verses that constituted their own lineage of *ācārya*s.10 They had to know the Maṇipravāḷa and Sanskrit commentaries on *sampradāya* works and how to perform the rituals associated with daily, monthly, and annual

rituals. Most importantly, they had to be able to perform the *samāśrayaṇam* ritual. 

Not knowing what the future had in store for the *ācārya*

tradition, many *ācārya*s wanted their sons to benefit from both traditional and modern education. Because their fathers were living longer than ever, sons had to wait much longer to become *ācārya*s, and therefore needed gainful employment in the meantime \(although only some jobs, such as teaching Sanskrit in a college, were considered appropriate\). And families needed the income of the younger sons due to dwindling support from disciples.

It was hard for *ācārya* families to educate their sons in 

traditional lore. Many sent their sons to English-medium Hindu schools.11 After returning from school and changing into traditional dress, sons went to the Brahmin elders to learn Sanskrit and the Vedas. Williams found that some sons said that they did not want to study any of these things. Rather, they wanted to study what would lead to good jobs in the modern world: computer science, engineering, chartered accountancy, or business. ‘It was hard,’ they said, ‘to have both a classical Sanskrit education and a modern one.’12 Considering these changes, Williams suggests in his fieldnotes that ‘the more modern education young people receive, the further they go from traditional training and knowledge ... and so their religion becomes a kind of symbolic religiosity’.13

In the past, *ācārya* families had received lands, houses, and 

other endowments from rulers; they had also been supported by temples and their disciples, which gave them time for religious activities. Villagers were respectful to Brahmins and gave them rice and other produce from their fields. 

Now *ācārya* families were facing serious economic problems. 

Besides daily maintenance and medical expenses for himself and his family, an *ācārya* faced the costs of major events such as sacred-thread ceremonies, weddings, events to honour the birthday of his ancestor, his own birthday and his own death— all of which demanded conspicuous display, in line with the special status of *ācārya*s. People reported that it was now more 

231







expensive to bring disciples together for functions because they wanted modern facilities. ‘In olden days, they used to come and chant the ś*loka*s free of charge, and they thought it was a service. Now the *ācārya* has to pay for the chanting plus provide food and other amenities’.14 Although the incomes of *ācārya*s varied, one *ācārya* told Williams that at one time, ‘,we were starved for food, but we never complained’.15

People reported that traditional economic support for 

*ācārya*s had declined for various reasons. In some cases, *ācārya*s mismanaged these resources and major endowments declined, governments redistributed their land and the cost of farm workers rose. As one *ācārya* said, ‘We have only disciples who are not very affluent and depend on salaries to maintain their own families. Many young people do not want to contribute to the support of another family, moreover, when they themselves have enormous economic stress in a competitive and increasingly commercialized society’.16

Williams learned that some *ācārya*s had begun charging 

disciples for their activities, or gave honours to them in return for a gift. Since the 1960s, some disciples had established trusts to support *ācārya*s and their families. These trusts kept track of disciples and collected dues from them, which regularised their contacts with the *ācārya*. They raised funds for special occasions too and helped with organisational activities. Some *ācārya*s had a personal secretary who did much of the correspondence and other work as service \(*kaiṅkaryam*\). In addition, the interest from trusts helped pay for expenses. ‘It is clear,’ said one disciple to Williams, ‘that these organizations are the preserve of elderly retired persons. It gives them activity, and it fits the traditional pattern of devotion to religious matters in old age’.17 Trusts often died out. There was another source of financial support on the horizon, said one person: wealthy Indians living abroad who visit India, re-establish contact with the family’s *ācārya*, and have him perform their initiations. The *ācārya*s hoped their rituals would be reciprocated with gifts. 

Williams’ interviews reveal that Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins 

were aware of a decline. Of the seventy-four supposedly

original *ācārya* families, only twenty or so still maintained the tradition. Some people feared that the institution would die out altogether. Fewer Brahmins than ever could recite the Vedas, for instance, and only a few chanted in the group at the end of religious processions. Even among Vaṭakalais, fewer than ever before knew the Vedas, aside from a few verses. One individual pointed out that there was a separate street in Kumbakonam for *ācārya*s, Iyengar \(Ayyangar\) Street, where about 100 *ācārya* families used to live. Only two *ācārya* families still lived there. The others had migrated to cities for secular jobs.

Another person commented that Brahmins now perform 

the rituals in perfunctory ways, not as a way of life. Still another said that *ācārya*s used to tour villages, visiting disciples, lecturing and instructing. ‘Now,’ he said, ‘the *ācārya*s don’t do these things. Disciples did. Only if *ācārya*s are invited for special occasions would they go.’ ‘The times have changed,’ commented another informant, ‘there is no fear of excommunication among Brahmins to enforce the tradition.’18

Williams’ interviews provide an important insight into 

whether Śrīvaiṣṇavas, both Vaṭakalais and Teṉkalais, thought that their religion was once caste-inclusive, and whether it still was.

One Vaṭakalai informant challenged the claim that 

Rāmānuja had introduced a casteless Vaiṣṇavism. He also did not agree that Śrīvaiṣṇavas are a caste of servants, because they all serve each other. And he did not think that because everyone is a servant, everyone is equal. ‘Rāmānuja never wrote anything in appreciation of *āḻvār*s’ works,’ this person said, 

and never wrote any commentary on the *āḻvār*s’ songs. 

Rāmānuja maintained the Vedic tradition and accepted 

the caste system in total. All their \(Te ṉkalai\) ideas were 

only in books. They were not practised in actual life. If 

they had been practised, Śrīvaiṣṇavism would have 

stood at the forefront as a model for the reformation of 

Hinduism. What really happened was that the Brahmin 

Śrīvaiṣṇavas never mingled with non-Brahmins.19

233







In addition, according to Williams’ interviews, Brahmins also maintained positions of superiority based on control of knowledge. One of the people Williams interviewed commented, for instance, that only Brahmins attach *ubhaya vedānta* \(or u. ve.\) to their names and thus establish their attachment to bilingual status \(that is, to Tamil and Sanskrit traditions\). Because Bhāgavatas were not allowed to study the Vedas, they could not use the title. Another of his informants recalled that ‘our forefathers used to teach those works only to a select few who were formally admitted to the fold of Śrīvaiṣṇavism. Thus, they guarded the works as a great treasure, which had to be preserved with utmost care.’20 Yet another Brahmin commented that at one time, ‘non-Brahmins were not allowed to listen to the Brahmin lectures’.21

Informants told Williams about the special honours that 

temples gave to visiting *ācārya*s, such as receiving the five *mudrā*s \(emblems\) at five places on the body, or being the first to receive *tīrtha* and *prasāda*. The temple would send oil to an *ācārya* at home for the birth of his first son \(likely the future *ācārya*\). And when an *ācārya* died, the temple would send fire from the temple kitchen for cremation rites. 

Williams’ fieldwork reveals other changes were afoot. 

More and more non-Brahmins were becoming knowledgeable about sectarian teachings and were giving discourses on religious topics. For this reason, Brahmins had to recognise this new reality, and so a compromise was developing. One non-Brahmin teacher, for example, took his disciples to Brahmin *ācārya*s for the formal initiation—the branding and mantras. He, the non-Brahmin teacher, sat nearby and gave instruction in the scriptures. In another case, the compromise included having a pious non-Brahmin participate, not just for his learning and popularity in non-Brahmin circles, but also because he was an important patron of festivals and other religious activities. 

Therefore, Brahmin *ācārya*s were including non-Brahmin 

teachers in the initiation \(*samāśrayaṇam*\) ritual, albeit for different reasons. The *ācārya*s maintained as their exclusive

role the initiation ritual and the teaching of the mantras. In other words, because in the past the *ācārya*s who were doing the initiations also had the role of introducing the treatises to be studied by the disciple—but often turned the actual teaching of them over to other Brahmin teachers as a kind of secondary, ongoing role—now some *ācārya*s were willing to recognise non-Brahmins in the role of teachers for their non-Brahmin disciples, and to include this recognition by having them present at the initiation ritual itself. 

If, by the late twentieth century, Brahmins were sometimes 

willing to include non-Brahmins in their initiation rituals, the *ācārya*s still maintained distance from Dalits. One person told Williams that an *ācārya* would perform the ritual for Harijans at his house, although the Harijan must stay on the outside veranda. A non-Brahmin would transport the branding utensils from the *ācārya* inside to the Harijan outside and do the branding. Another person reported that *ācārya*s were not initiating Harijans ‘because they can’t go against society’. Some did not even like non-Brahmins initiating washermen at Srirangam, who were Harijans. As noted by one: ‘Harijans give initiation to each other. They are cobblers.’ Not all Brahmins held these positions. One told Williams: ‘Harijans should be made Śrīvaiṣṇavas. If we say that all souls are equal, why should they be rejected?’ Obviously, some Brahmins were ambivalent about who and what should be included in the religion.



*Jīyar*s and *Ācārya*s with Non-Brahmin Disciples

In the course of my own fieldwork, I had interviews with two Brahmin *jīyar*s and one *ācārya*: Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar of Sriperumbudur, Srinivasa Ramanuja Jiyar of Tirukkoyilur, and Parasara Badri Narayana Bhattar of Srirangam. These interviews helped me understand the important and challenging role of *jīyar*s and *ācārya*s with many non-Brahmin disciples.

Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar \(‘Ethiraja’\)22

Varataa Ethiraja Jeeyar \(1927–2009\) was the most radical of all the Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmin leaders whom I interviewed. After hearing about him from J. N. B. Chakravarthi Ramanuja Dasar, N. D. Sabari Natchi, Sudarshana Acarya, A. Pandurangan, and others, I became curious about his support for Bhāgavatas, including Dalits, and women of all castes. So, I interviewed him at his *maṭha* in Sriperumbudur. It was the closing day of the festival that celebrates Rāmānuja’s birth. 

At his *maṭha*, which is directly across the street from the 

temple’s pillared hall, Ethiraja greeted me. We entered a large room with many photos of the *maṭha*’s previous *jīyar*s and some of Ethiraja himself during his former service in the air force. Like Venkatachari, many Tamil Brahmins had lived through difficult times. During the non-Brahmin movement’s heyday, they experienced threats of violence and sometimes actual

violence. For over a century, they had been subjected to non-Brahmin critiques. They had experienced 250 years of bitter court cases to resolve disputes between Teṉkalais and Vaṭakalais. They had faced other court challenges from the government over matters such as allowing Dalits into the temples. When Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas told stories about Śrīvaiṣṇava equality, they were upbraided for their own hypocrisy. Besides, Teṉkalai Brahmins had long had to face criticism, first from Smārtas and then from Vaṭakalais, over the fact that they had never even been Brahmins. In other words, they had been under identity siege, and they sometimes did not have the moral high ground. 

Teṉkalai Brahmins knew, moreover, that there were no 

easy solutions to the conflicts that Śrīvaiṣṇavas faced. To be a religious leader, one had to have some distinguishing characteristics, but the *ācārya*s and *jīyar*s were gradually losing these. They knew that their own knowledge of the Vedas, *Divyaprabandham*, and other religious texts was waning. As they no longer controlled religious knowledge, most *ācārya*s clung to their right to perform *samāśrayaṇam*, which is linked in the hagiographies with Rāmānuja and salvation. Some clung to precedence and tradition, despite challenges from the Tamil Nadu government or the courts. As often happens in religious communities that face conflict, some leaders had maintained orthodoxy as the distinguishing characteristic of their special religious identity, and some had radically changed.

I found that occasionally, *ācārya*s even implied a hierarchy 

among the *ācārya*s themselves. This depended on whether they were initiation *ācārya*s or teaching *ācārya*s, whether they were well-educated or not, whether their families had maintained orthodoxy or not, whether they were of good character or not, whether they were wealthy and cosmopolitan or not, whether they spoke English or not, and whether they had only Brahmin disciples or not. The latter point is confirmed by K. Subramaniam, who notes that in some ‘Tamil Brahmin communities, it is a serious matter for a Brahmin priest to

As mentioned, I often encountered ambiguity in Brahmin 

views of non-Brahmins. One Brahmin told me, for instance, that ‘non-Brahmins are not supposed to learn the Vedas and Sanskrit’. However, he also admired non-Brahmins who had such knowledge. Another described the relationship like this: ‘there is a separate but equal philosophy; some non-Brahmins still give respect to Brahmins and engage Brahmins for the *pūjā*. Others were actively trying to bridge the gap. A retired

In *Tamil Brahmans: The Making of a Middle-Class Caste*,45

C. J. Fuller and Haripriya Narasimhan argue, on the basis of their interviews with Tamil Brahmins, that caste and a modern, upper-middle class orientation have fused for them. They explain this development as the result of maintaining or capitalising on some aspects of a secure traditional caste identity, in the sense of being vegetarians and guarding Sanskritic Hinduism, having wealth accumulated from former family positions as landlords or district administrators, and maintaining the value of education. 

Fuller and Narasimhan further explain the development 

of a caste-class orientation as a willingness to adapt to new circumstances. This began with colonial English education, government administrative jobs, and reforms to improve 

251







the status of women, such as eliminating child marriage and seclusion, decreasing the hold of purity rules, and encouraging women’s education, professional employment, and change of patriarchal marriage to a companionate model. After Indian independence, Tamil Brahmins pursued well-paying jobs such as engineering and information technology and participated in the market economy through investments and the housing market.

In addition, they fashioned a cultural identity as performers 

or connoisseurs of Carnatic music and *Bhārata Nāṭyam*. ‘As Brahmin identity formed around the idea of classical music \(and dance\)’, temple music and non-Brahmin temple musicians were ‘deemed inferior’.46 ‘Music,’ the authors continue, ‘was never crucial for the cultural identity of non-Brahmans in the early twentieth century.’ Rather, it belonged to the process of Sanskritisation for upward mobility. 

That, I think, is an overstatement. Music was absolutely 

central to the Icai Velalas for it was their livelihood, gave them public status, and defined their cultural identity. It was important for Naidu musicians and their families, too, although it was probably not a Naidu caste identity marker, in the sense used by Fuller and Narasimhan. Finally, music—*bhajana*s, including Tyāgarāja songs—was certainly a sectarian marker for both Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins and Brahmins. In any case, many non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas had practised Sanskritisation for centuries. This was not an accommodation to modernity.

Fuller and Narasimhan then comment that those Brahmins 

who remained in Tamil Nadu—a quarter had moved elsewhere in India or to other countries—skilfully avoided the political and economic problems created for them by the non-Brahmin movement. They avoided conflicts over reservations, moreover, by seeking employment in the private sector, especially with international companies. And they avoided the realm of politics altogether, unless it affected them directly, or they were Brahmin political leaders such as Jayalalithaa who held power by cultivating non-Brahmin support, especially that of the numerically dominant non-Brahmin castes.

When I compared the orientation of the Tamil Brahmins 

interviewed by Fuller and Narasimhan with the Tamil Teṉkalai Brahmins whom I interviewed, I found differences. Because my interest was in changes in religious identity as a component of caste identity, I found more Teṉkalai unease, sometimes even insecurity, over how to maintain identity and status in these changing times. Some wanted to maintain orthodoxy as a marker of Brahmin exceptionalism and religious leadership, and wanted to maintain control over religious roles and institutions, which was the basis for livelihood and status. However, there was also recognition that at least some members of their family had to have ‘modern’ education and jobs. I must add a warning here. My interviews were with those Tamil Brahmins still heavily identified with religious activities. Had I interviewed Teṉkalai Brahmins who had high-paying jobs in private industries, I might have found orientations closer to those described by Fuller and Narasimhan. It is also possible that the profile of Vaṭakalai Śrīvaiṣṇavas is closer to that of the Tamil Brahmins in the Fuller and Narasimhan case study.

In this chapter, I discussed two radical Brahmin *ācārya*s in the late nineteenth century, and then focussed on several ethnographies from the end of the twentieth century to detect changes that had occurred in Teṉkalai Brahmin—especially *ācārya*—lives. In my own interviews with several *jīyar*s and *ācārya*s, I found concern over how to handle the massive economic changes related to the huge scale of government reservations, and those related to their own financial support from temples and disciples. 

Even though Teṉkalai Bhāgavatas have lived through 

a stressful period, there are signs that they are beginning to adapt to changes. They are introducing, for instance, English education and secular professions for their sons and daughters. Many are open to ways to improve relations with non-Brahmins. Some Brahmins told me that Brahmins should 

253







welcome non-Brahmins into the community as true equals, although it was not always clear exactly what equality meant. Only one, Ethiraja, focussed on the importance of initiating Dalits and destroying hierarchy.

Finally, from my interviews, I discovered very different 

views regarding whether only Brahmins could be *ācārya*s or *jīyar*s. Parasara Badri Narayana Bhattar stayed with the tradition that an *ācārya* had to belong to one of the seventy-four Brahmin families legitimated by Rāmānuja. By contrast, Ethiraja echoed the position of the non-Brahmins Bhuvanagiri and Ekāṅgi Svāmikaḷ, who lived from the mid-nineteenth century into the twentieth, that *anyone* could become an *ācārya* or *jīyar* and give *samāśrayaṇam*.
