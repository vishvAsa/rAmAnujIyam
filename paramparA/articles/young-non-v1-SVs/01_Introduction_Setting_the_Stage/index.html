01 Introduction: Setting the Stage

What's in this page?
🐎💪 32%

The past two centuries have been a period of momentous religious, social, and political changes in southern India. These include the Tamil Renaissance, late colonialism in the Madras Presidency, 1 Dravidian nationalism, Dalit mobilisation, the Indian struggle for Independence, and then the new political entity Tamil Nadu.2

At the epicentre of these changes has been an awakening to injustices—those created by colonialism, but also for non-Brahmins, those long-standing ones created by the caste system. Non-Brahmins have dreamed that equality would create better futures for them. They have refashioned the past into new stories through recuperation, retrospection, and images of reform, revolution and utopia. With power shifting, hierarchies re-aligning and caste identities merging or colliding, they have re-imagined their own identities.

This book is about the turbulent transformations in modern Tamil Nadu from the perspective of non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas. Vaiṣṇavism is a pan-Indian umbrella term for the tradition that understands the supreme to be the one God with various names, such as Viṣṇu, Nārāyaṇa, Kṛṣṇa and Rāma, a cluster of deities with historical and regional overlaps and variations. Although some devotees, especially villagers, simply think of themselves as Vaiṣṇavas (Vaiṇavas in Tamil), others identify with a particular regional Vaiṣṇava tradition. In Tamil Nadu, this most likely would be Śrīvaiṣṇavism— the community of those who worship Viṣṇu and his consort Śrī, with its philosophical tradition known as Viśiṣṭādvaita passed down through a lineage of preceptors—although other Vaiṣṇava communities such as Mādhvas and Maharashtrian Vaiṣṇavas also exist.

Most of my interviews were with non-Brahmins who self-identify as Śrīvaiṣṇavas. They were from many castes or caste clusters, including Chettiyar, Dalit, Icai Velala, Naidu, Pillai, Reddiyar, Sattada, Vanniyar, Yadava, and Vellalar. I found a fluid use of the terms Śrīvaiṣṇava and Vaiṣṇava by non-Brahmins belonging to this sect today.3 Non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas most often refer to themselves as Bhāgavatas (devotees).4 One can sometimes identify non-Brahmins who have been initiated5 by the common suffix to their names: ‘Rāmānuja Dāsar’, servant of Rāmānuja. (Rāmānuja, who lived in the twelfth century, was the most important thinker—later considered a salvific figure—in this tradition.)

During my interviews, I never heard my informants

(whether they were Dalits themselves or belonged to another caste) use the word ‘Dalit’,6 which has pan-Indian currency today as the name used by former outcastes. Moreover, my Dalit informants did not self-identify as an oppositional group outside the non-Brahmin or Śrīvaiṣṇava nomenclature, although they spoke with concern of the special problems that their community faced, or spoke with pride about their roles in Dalit organisations. Still, I am well aware that many things might have been left unsaid because I was a Western researcher. Nor did I hear the Śrīvaiṣṇava circumlocution ‘Tirukkulattār’, which literally means ‘those of the auspicious clan or caste’, a Śrīvaiṣṇava euphemism for those once called outcastes.7 However, I did commonly hear the term Harijans, those born of [god] Hari/Viṣṇu, especially if they were followers of Gandhi. People also used the term Adidravidas,

2

the original Dravidians, in a positive or neutral way, to refer to those once called outcastes. When writing about outcastes in the past, I have used this pejorative term to be accountable to historical realities. Otherwise, I have maintained the term used by informants, such as Harijan or Adidravida. In general discussions, however, I have used the word Dalit. Despite the fact that Dalit voices in Tamil Nadu today have rightly drawn attention to how the term ‘non-Brahmin’ has been glossed in order to incorporate all those who are not Brahmins and how this has had a negative and even subversive effect on the emergence of Dalit articulation, I have often included Dalit Śrīvaiṣṇavas under non-Brahmin as an umbrella term, unless the context suggests otherwise.

I focus on the sect Śrīvaiṣṇavism, because far more has been

written on Tamil non-Brahmin Śaivas than on non-Brahmin Vaiṣṇavas. This is partly because Śaivism (the tradition that reveres Śiva as supreme god) has been the dominant sect in Tamil-speaking regions and has attracted the interest of Orientalists and other scholars.8 More specifically, I focus on the Te ṉkalai sub-sect of Śrīvaiṣṇavism, because its theology is one of radical caste and outcaste inclusiveness, although this has rarely been the sociological reality. Nevertheless, this theology remains for Teṉkalai non-Brahmins today a spiritual inspiration, as they aspire to justice and assume new religious roles.

Finally, I focus on Te ṉkalai Śrīvaiṣṇavism, because that is

my own area of scholarly expertise. I have made many research trips to Tamil Nadu and have had the privilege of studying with several hereditary religious leaders, and knowing their families over decades. This has facilitated my entry into the existential thoughts and feelings of my informants between 2006 and 2016, the period of my more than twenty interviews that took place mainly in eastern and central Tamil Nadu (in and around Chennai, Sriperumbudur, Puducherry, Tanjavur, and Tiruchirappalli).

Little is known about Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin history.

We often hear about how history disappears, especially in

3

the absence of institutions (which can include lineages of chanters or storytellers). Even manuscripts and books crumble from seaside humidity, or get eaten by ants. And temple renovators are notorious for covering over ancient inscriptions. Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin histories, which families try to maintain informally as their oral traditions, have disappeared over the centuries, and can be recovered today only by reading between the lines of Brahmin-authored texts or by studying inscriptions that have somehow survived the ravages of time. This is not unlike the loss and partial recovery of women’s history, which has preoccupied so many scholars for the past half-century and more.

Śrīvaiṣṇavism has a long tradition of commenting on its key

scriptures and writing works on its theological system. Asking questions about this tradition from non-Brahmin perspectives can provide clues to not only its religious ideals, but also its history of caste relations, and about what happened under Brahmin religious domination. And that helps us understand the interface of religious, social and political identities today.

But even modern non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava history is

disappearing because of a lack of non-Brahmin memoirs and scholarship on this tradition. This brings me to my task: to capture Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin oral histories. Oral history

is the collection and recording of personal memoires as

historical documentation. … it emphasizes the significance

of human experience. … Oral history is the best method to

use … to get an idea not only of what happened, but what

past times meant to people and how it felt to be a part of

those times.9

This characterisation of oral history is helpful but inadequate in the case of non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavism. The fact that many of my informants were very knowledgeable about their religious tradition means that they also offered important historical information. I complemented what my interviewees said with my own textual knowledge of Śrīvaiṣṇavism, as well as general historical and political background based on inscriptions,

4

gazetteers during the Raj, court cases, hagiographies, published works by non-Brahmins and newspaper articles.

When I found a conflict between informant and scholarly

accounts, I presented only the voice of my interviewee in that section, but sometimes offered a scholarly version elsewhere. I have not presented the textual origins of all the religious stories I was told. Tracing these through many hagiographies and other sources from the fifteenth century—some are even found in modern films—is a large research project in itself, which I must leave for others to do.

Sometimes I interviewed a person only once for several

hours; other times I followed that up with one or more addi-tional interviews. All interviews followed ethical principles regarding informed consent based on a discussion of what the project was about—that they could end the interview at any time they wanted, that they could request that some information remain off the record, and that they could choose whether to be identified and photographed. Because I was dealing with delicate topics, I used the most non-invasive interviewing methods possible. I took notes, I did not use recorders or video cameras, and I allowed the interviews to turn into conversations. Most people spoke English. When they did not, my travelling companions who understood the local Tamil or Telugu dialect became translators. My informants decided how much they wanted to reveal. Some willingly told me everything that they could think of; others were reticent and sometimes left their tales hanging. One informant asked me not to reveal his family’s caste identity. My research looks somewhat uneven, therefore, but is, I hope, valuable testimony nonetheless.

One of the topics on which my research is uneven is that

of gender. I did many more interviews with men than women. One reader has rightly commented that there is a lack of gender equality and analysis in this book. To my chagrin, as someone who has published a great deal about Hinduism and women, I agree.

5

Why did this happen? In part, because the public religious

leadership of Śrīvaiṣṇavism is still heavily male-oriented— ācāryas, jīyars, arcakas, sabhā leaders, publishers, trust officials, village elders, and so forth. I was in these male networks to locate informants and was therefore passed on from one manto another as the network extended. The interviewsoften took place in public places, such as sabhā offices and temple maṇṭapams. Only when the interviews were in homes did I encounter women, but even there, some women were ‘traditionally’ behind the scenes, and I could not break out of the interview process, which had been pre-arranged—usually with a time-limit related to the availability of my contact person and transportation—to develop an in-depth conversation with them, away from male ears. I unsuccessfully tried to set up appointments with several prominent Śrīvaiṣṇava women active in religious circles.

As a result of these problems, I decided that I would need

another ethnographic project10 just on Śrīvaiṣṇava women’s religion, especially their views on access to religious leadership, caste and politics. Nevertheless, some of my interviews with women gave me insights into women’s lives and their changing roles. I set the stage for understanding these changes by providing a summary of the history of Śrīvaiṣṇava views on the roles of women in a section of this chapter, and the changes that I found reflected in the interviews in the concluding chapter.

In short, because my research was focussed on the relation

of religion, caste and politics, it was based mainly on the male world of religious leadership in the public realm. I do not think being a woman and an academic had much influence on what men decided to tell me. For this research, I was an honorary male, as it were. Most were delighted that I was interested in non-Brahmins.

By way of introduction, further comment on the word ‘non-

Brahmin’ is necessary. To those unfamiliar with the modern history of Tamil Nadu, non-Brahmin sounds derivative, negative and patronising. Although the word non-Brahmin

6

had occurred occasionally since the late nineteenth century in the Madras Presidency, the Justice Party’s ‘Non-Brahmin Manifesto’ of 1916 used it thirty times, transforming it into a powerful statement of political goals. After the storm of protest subsided—the Manifesto had argued against the British giving self-rule to Indians, because that would simply re-entrench the power of Brahmins over other castes—the term non-Brahmin gained currency. It came to function as an invitation, as it were, to mobilise over the formation of a new identity, one that opposed the hegemony of Brahmins.

In caste associations and new non-Brahmin organisations,

non-Brahmins articulated their experiences of humiliation and deprivation. In almost all non-Brahmin communities, according to V. Geetha and S. V. Rajadurai, there were teachers, lawyers and other educated people who introduced to their communities ‘… a discourse of rights with which caste ranking could be questioned, privileges of higher castes criticised and a corporate caste identity established. Thus, supra-local sodalities emerged; an imagined fraternity of disparate sub-castes was forged.’11 The imaged fraternity was sufficiently flexible to engage local groups, yet could appeal evocatively to many groups.

The terms Brahmin and non-Brahmin remain deeply

lodged in Tamil consciousness and will continue to frame its discourse as long as there is mutual opposition or a memory of times when that famed the landscape of religion, caste and politics. Indeed, my informants freely used the word ‘non-Brahmin’ to describe themselves, but not always and not always in a context of anti-Brahmanism. Even so, people wanted to tell their stories of discrimination and of hopes for the future.

For many, such as Śrīvaiṣṇavas and those who live in Tamil

Nadu, the information in this chapter will be very familiar, and they may move quickly on. Others, however, will need some background to understand the interviews, and so I begin by introducing the religious orientation of this tradition. I continue with the examination of Śrīvaiṣṇavism’s position on

7

women, followed by a discussion of caste conundrums found in this tradition and the impact of modern politics, including an analysis of one of modern Tamil Nadu’s most famous leaders, Periyar, who came from a Śrīvaiṣṇava family but denounced it and was a central figure in the non-Brahmin movement.

A Sectarian Primer

One etymology of the English word ‘sect’ goes back to the Latin secta, which means a way or path, and by extension a following or school of thought from the proto-Indo-European sekw, to follow.12 We find sects in Indian religious history, defined as a school of thought or religious community with an established path to salvation, usually established by a founder. Śrīvaiṣṇavism is such a way or path. Its concept of founder, however, is traced back through a lineage of teachers and poets to the supreme God himself. The other etymology of sect is from the Latin secta from the Proto-European root sek, to cut.13 In this sense, a tradition is cut apart in the sense of being divided to form subsects or new sects. In the case of Śrīvaiṣṇavism, that would be the Teṉkalai and Vaṭakalai sub-sects.

Here, I offer a primer on mainstream Teṉkalai Śrīvaiṣṇava

views found in Brahmin-authored texts about the supreme deity, scripture, sacred places, religious leadership, initiation, service and temples. This will help us to understand non-Brahmin engagement with these ideas and gauge how their views are similar to or different from Brahmin ones on these topics. This will also help us to understand what has changed today.

The Supreme Deity

The supreme deity for Śrīvaiṣṇavas has many names and forms. His most common names are Perumāḷ, Nārāyaṇa, Bālāji, Viṣṇu and Kṛṣṇa, although he is said to have 1,000 names, many of which are the Tamil names of temple towns.14 He has many

8

forms, too, which are classified in the Pañcarātra texts15 as five types: supreme, cosmic emanation, incarnation in the distant past, incarnation in statue-form, and presence within human beings.16 He is a deity of royal power, yet also of tender grace and beauty. Descriptions of him and the landscapes where he lives draw on the poetic conventions of ancient Tamil Ca ṅkam poetry as systematised by the Tolkāppiyam (ca. fourth to sixth century ce).

Today, Kṛṣṇa, Rāma and Bālāji (Vēṅkaṭeśvara) are the most

popular divine names in villages. Tamils were worshipping K ṛṣṇa by the fourth century ce; this tradition is deeply embedded in many village folk traditions. Over the next few centuries, Rāma became part of folk religion, and Bālāji from medieval times attracted devotees throughout Tamil country, especially in its eastern region, and in the Telugu-speaking country to the north. Today, he is an important pan-Indian deity and his temple is the richest in India. Śrī, the goddess, who also has many names and forms, is the supreme deity’s consort.

Scripture

Śrīvaiṣṇava scripture includes both Tamil and Sanskrit texts. Tamil scripture consists of the collected works of 12 poet-sages called āḻvārs, who lived from the seventh century ce to the ninth (see Appendix 2). Their works were canonised centuries later as the Nālāyira-divyaprabandham, the divine collection of 4,000 [verses] or, for short here, the Divyaprabandham, also called the Tamil Veda. Sanskrit scripture includes the Vedas and post-Vedic texts. Among these are the Pañcarātraāgamas, manuals for temple architecture and rituals used by priests in many temples. Śrīvaiṣṇavas refer to these scriptures as ubhaya-vedānta (both vedāntas)17 to honour the legacies of Tamil and Sanskrit traditions.18

Until modern times, Brahmins generally controlled the

ritual and scriptural traditions by teaching only Brahmins Sanskrit, which was necessary for knowledge of the Vedas

9

and the Pañcarātraāgamas. Moreover, Brahmins controlled the teaching and interpretation of the many Śrīvaiṣṇava texts they wrote in Maṇipravāḷam (literally, that which consists of sapphires and corals; it refers to a mixed language, in this case of Tamil and Sanskrit19) because people needed to know Sanskrit to understand this mixed language. Since Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins (Bhāgavatas) did not have access to Sanskrit and the Vedas, the Tamil Divyaprabandham was important for their religious life and path to salvation. One cannot claim that Brahmins are just Sanskrit-oriented, however, because they too give importance to recitation of the Tamil Veda as a key part of many rituals, and over the centuries have written important commentaries on āḻvār works.

Sacred Places

There are 108 sacred places (divyadeśas),20 according to a list compiled from the works of the āḻvārs, most of them by far in Tamil Nadu, with a few in northern India and Nepal and two beyond the terrestrial sphere altogether (the Milk Ocean and Vaikuṇṭha, the Supreme Heaven).21 The three most important places in Tamil Nadu, according to works on the 108 places, have been Srirangam, Tirumalai-Tirupati and Kanchipuram.22 They have been especially important not only as locations of important temples that attract pilgrims, but also as intellectual and institutional centres of the tradition. In addition, the birthplaces of the āḻvārs and early ācāryas are important sacred places (Sriperumbudur, for instance, is famous as Rāmānuja’s birthplace).

Other places of sacred significance are the maṭhas (Tamil

ma ṭam). These are associated with lineages of Brahmin ascetics, some going back to the fourteenth century. In popular parlance today, maṭha might also refer to any Śrīvaiṣṇava building in which devotees meet. This building might function as a dharmaśālā or choultry, that is, a charitable guesthouse, usually at an important temple, where devotees stay and eat, especially

10

during festivals. A synonym in this sense is rāmānuja kūṭam (Rāmānuja’s house or hall23).

Bhāgavatas have established their own sacred spaces.

These range from house shrines (called kōyiḻāḻvār) and village or city temples with non-Brahmin priests, to maṭhas where non-Brahmin ascetics live, to places called bhajana monasteries (bhajana-ma ṭhas) or bhajana temples (bhajana kōyiḻs). According to Robert Leopold, bhajanas consist of ‘[m]en, women, and children meet[ing] in some structure …. Placed upon the bench is a framed, painted representation of the main deity to be worshipped … and the objects used for worship.’24 Sometimes, Bhāgavatas refer to these temple-like places by the term sabhā (Ta. sabhai), which is a more general term for a place where people meet. They might also be called rāmānuja kūṭams.

There are some village temples that have Śrīvaiṣṇava non-

Brahmin priests and non-Brahmin worshippers, others that have Brahmin priests but non-Brahmin worshippers, and still others where non-Brahmins function as priests not only for their own deity, but also for other village deities.

Religious Leadership

Śrīvaiṣṇavism is often described as a sampradāya in the sense of a tradition, religious school or system, based on teachings that have been transmitted from one teacher to another. Brahmin religious leaders of this sampradāya have been of two types: ācāryas and jīyars (cīyars; jeeyars; jeears). Since the relation of Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins (called Ayyangars, Aiyankars, Iyengars) and non-Brahmins has today been informed in part by the concept of Brahmin leadership, I will describe briefly the development of the concepts of ācārya and jīyar.

Ācāryas are the foremost of Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava

householders because of their knowledge of the sacred texts, their orthodox conduct, their right to initiate people and their special role in guiding disciples to salvation. As such, ācāryas are distinguished from other learned Brahmins who chant and

11

teach the Vedas or sampradāya works, those who are temple priests (arcakas), and those who perform the rites of passage such as weddings and funerals (purohitas).

The concept of ācārya is closely connected to the idea of

lineage. Between the eleventh and thirteenth centuries, an intellectual lineage of Brahmins in Tamil Nadu with family or teacher-disciple relations was developing a cohesive identity. The first chronological list of the important figures (Nammāḻvār, Nāthamuni, Yāmuna and especially Rāmānuja, the twelfth-century thinker who enhanced the philosophical dimension of the tradition) appears in the writings of Rāmānuja’s disciple K ūreśa.25 Other important figures were gradually included in the list (see Appendix 2).

By the mid-thirteenth century, some of these early ācāryas

were represented in statue-form in the temples and worshipped as divine, as were the āḻvārs. According to Erin McCann, by the end of that century, Pi ḷḷai Lokācārya had defined the ācārya as ‘both divine and human’ and ‘the tangible manifestation of the Lord’s compassion for the soul’, but also the ācārya as ‘always a soul’ who is ‘subservient and dependent’.26 Piḷḷai Lokācārya formalised, moreover, the concept of love of the ācārya alone as a true and independent means of salvation. Although the Lord and the devotee are eternally linked, this link may remain dormant until the ācārya spiritually awakens the individual and takes on his or her sins. The ācārya removes the fear of rebirth. Even today, Te ṉkalais argue that salvation will occur at the end of this very life, and they should not even contemplate rebirth. Having surrendered completely to God, they should live joyously as if in supreme heaven.27

The Guruparamparā Prabhāvam 6000 and the Kōyiḻ Oḻuku,

which I date in the seventeenth and eighteenth century, respectively,28 although they contain some earlier material, mention that the early (pūrva) ācārya group (which had come to include famous figures such as Vedānta Deśika and Maṇavāḷa Māmuni) had been extended to seventy-four (Brahmin) families whose lineage went back to Rāmānuja, or a figure associated with him.29

12

A tradition developed that the ācārya mantle was passed

from the ācārya to his eldest son. This ācārya institution was not lodged in the individual who might have faults; rather, it was lodged in the institution of the lineage of ācāryas. Some ācāryas had privileges and rights in particular temples and often lived nearby. In some families, younger sons migrated to new locales and became ācāryas, thereby creating sub-lineages and spreading the influence of Śrīvaiṣṇavism. This ācārya system could function because the relationship between the ācārya and his disciple was reciprocal. The ācārya looked after the disciple’s soul, as it were, by removing fear of rebirth and guiding the disciple toward salvation, and the disciple looked after the ācārya’s body (and his family) by providing all the necessities of life.

Even more classifications of ācāryas developed. According

to one, some ācāryas do the initiations only for their own families and others do the initiations for other people. Yet another distinguishes the ācāryas who give initiations and introduce the scriptures by reading the first and last stanzas, and those who teach the important works (granthas) of the tradition.

There is also a parallel ascetic system. In the early

period of Śrīvaiṣṇavism, some key figures such as Rāmānuja and Nañjīyar were ascetics. The ritual of renouncing the householder’s stage of life is called saṁnyāsa. According to the Dharmaśāstras, renunciation is a twice-born (in practice, usually a Brahmin) option,30 generally reserved for the last stage of life, yet allowed earlier as an exception.

The decision of whether to be a Brahmin householder or

an ascetic must have been originally fluid; there were many ascetics who had once been married in the early community. Although the early ācāryas included both householders and ascetics,31 from the fourteenth century ce, the Brahmin ascetic option was being formalised as the jīyar tradition, ascetics who were initiated members of maṭhas or monasteries. (Maṭhas were common among Advaitins, Śaivas and other religious communities.) Jīyars or tridaṇḍis (those with a three-pronged

13

staff) were Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmin ascetics32 who ministered to the intellectual and spiritual needs of their disciples, with roles very similar to those of the ācāryas.

Three important Te ṉkalai Brahmin-led maṭhas in Tamil

country that have had many non-Brahmin disciples have been the Emperumāṉār Maṭha at Tirukkoyilur (in the region west of Puducherry),33 which combined jīyar and ācārya traditions; the Vānamāmalai Mutt (in the southern district of Tirunelveli)34 and the Yadugiri Yatiraja Mutt (at Sriperumbudur, Rāmānuja’s birthplace near Kanchipuram).35

Other ascetics are called ekāṅgis. Ekāṅgi—from one (eka)

limb (aṅgi)—is a special term for any Śrīvaiṣṇava ascetic who carries a one-pronged staff and has not had sa ṁnyāsa (formal initiation into asceticism). He might be a Brahmin bachelor or a non-Brahmin ascetic (who is barred from the ritual of saṁnyāsa). Some ekāṅgis have had their own maṭhas, and this is true for non-Brahmins as well. Of particular relevance here is the fact that some non-Brahmin ekāṅgis have been religious leaders.

Non-Brahmins have been important patrons and

administrators for the temples, moreover, and some have performed non-priestly roles of temple service or have been priests in non-Brahmin temples. Non-Brahmin religious leaders who are householders are generally called mudaliyars. Today, they often give discourses on the scriptures or lead the chanting in Bhāgavata groups.

Initiation

In Śrvaiṣṇavism, another kind of initiation is called samāśrayaṇam. It includes both the concept of prapatti—taking refuge with God and totally surrendering to him36—and a ritual called pañcasaṁskāra,37 which consists of five acts: being branded with an image of a conch and discus on the shoulders; marking the forehead with the sectarian emblem, the nāmam;38 assuming a new name; being taught the three mantras;39 and worshipping an image. During this ritual, the ācārya introduces

14

‘the soul to God’, teaches the mantras to his disciple and explains them according to Śrīvaiṣṇava interpretations from secret works. Next, the ācārya recites the verses (taṉiyaṉs) for each of his ancestors back to the family’s link to Rāmānuja. The disciple then repeats a verse about his own ācārya (which he or she will do from this time on during morning worship). After this ritual, initiates are to be vegetarian, strictly do their daily worship at their household altar and maintain other orthodox practices.40

jīyars also perform samāśrayaṇam. There was once a

controversy over whether jīyars should do this ritual, which involves a sacred fire. Since, as ascetics, jīyars were to beg for their food, they should not touch fire, which was associated with the householder’s hearth. This controversy suggests that at some point ācāryas were resisting the jīyar claim on their role. Today, jīyars do perform samāśrayaṇam (a householder disciple prepares the fire). In any case, initiation by an ācārya or jīyar is popularly believed to guarantee liberation (although theologically, that is the prerogative of the gracious God).

Based on her interviews with Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins today,

Srilata Raman found that those Brahmin men and women who knew less about Śrīvaiṣṇava rituals and theology thought that the ritual signified a transition to a more orthodox life defined by dietary restrictions and enhanced daily worship at the household altar. By contrast, knowledgeable Śrīvaiṣṇava orthodox Brahmin men thought that the ritual qualified them for some temple and domestic rituals.41 My interviews reveal that many Bhāgavatas today share the first view—that the ritual is a transition to a more orthodox life-style—although some think that it is the ritual that makes them a Śrīvaiṣṇava rather than just an ordinary Vaiṣṇava, or that it is a ritual that gurantees salvation.

Service

Serving God and his devotees, called kaiṅkarya, is central to Śrīvaiṣṇavism.42 The āḻvārs often mention it. Nammāḻvār, for

15

instance, describes how he approached the Divine Mother as mediator and took refuge at the Lord’s sacred feet. With all obstacles now gone, he had desire only to do all loving service.43 Periyāḻvār calls serving the Lord a great privilege and declares that he will never serve anyone else so that there will be no blot on God’s fame.44 Tirumaṅkai comments that ‘out of my great love for you, I am eager to serve you’.45 Āḻvār poems often refer to worship as service: purifying the floor, decorating sacred places with auspicious designs, offering lamps and pots of water, growing, picking and planting flowers, and so on. In addition, they refer to service as humble solidarity with other devotees. Nammāḻvār, for instance, refers to himself as a servant of the servants of the servants.46

Ācāryas too mention service in their hymns of praise,

commentaries, and theological works. Rāmānuja, for instance, uses the analogy of God as Master and his devotees as servants.47 He links service with subservience or servitude to God as the soul’s essential nature. Service includes serving both God and also his community of devotees.

A corollary of service is humility. The āḻvārs castigated

those who are full of pride and egotism. This implies that to develop a community of devotees, rather than a hierarchy of castes with superior and inferior ranks, devotees must serve others humbly, do acts of humble service themselves, and insist on equal respect.48

Temples

Closely related to the concept of service is the concept of the temple as the locus for redistribution of material resources and services. These are culturally recognised and authoritative rights, in Arjun Appadurai’s words, ‘to offer service (kaiṅkaryam) to the deity, either through endowment (upayam) or through prescribed ritual function ….’49 The system coheres, generally, because it is premised on the idea of service to the deity (which, I would add, is anchored in the central idea of salvation), but also the recognition that all groups and individuals must

16

do their services to ensure that the redistributive and ritual process functions.

Services are recognised as honours. All those who serve—

such as priests, musicians, gardeners, weavers, those who light the lamps, bring water from the river, carry the palanquin in which the deity rides in processions, even those who are just present in the sanctum with devotion before the deity— are honoured. Some are honoured with special recognition (for instance, the garland that had been offered to the deity or standing closer to the deity, albeit within certain limits), others with just the simple offerings during temple worship of t īrtha (sacred water), prasāda (sacred food) and śaṭakōpa (the metal crown named after Nammāḻvār, which is embossed with the feet of Viṣṇu, symbolising surrender to him, and placed on a worshipper’s head). These acts of honour recognise the worshippers’ share (paṅku) in the process and binds them to the community. They also mark status, but that is certainly not their primary function. Because these rights/honours are to be carefully protected and recognised by the community as a whole, any abrogation is experienced as dishonour, disgrace, or humiliation (avamāna).50

Temple processions celebrate weekly, monthly, or annual

festivals. The paradigmatic procession is the movement of the deity out of his or her shrine around the temple corridors, or beyond to the streets surrounding the temple, or some destination a short distance away. Processions in major temples are accompanied by the auspicious sounds of drums and nāgasvarams in temples. Today, official Brahmin chanters of the Divyaprabandham lead the procession and official Brahmin chanters of the Veda follow it.

Processions are central to the system of temple honours.

Just as in the temple proper there are many people who serve the temple’s ritual process with designated rights to do their ‘share’, so too for processions, although on these occasions, those who serve with special functions extend beyond designated groups of temple servants to include other groups, such as Dalit Śrīvaiṣṇavas who have the honour of carrying

17

the palanquin, say, or those who have the honour of keeping the great temple carts moving safely through the streets, or others who hold lamps. As the procession moves through the streets, it stops to honour particular places, such as maṭhas and rāmānuja kūṭams. These honours, however, can be withheld by temple authorities to shame that establishment if something is deemed wrong.

Besides processions of deities, there are processions where

an image or picture of an āḻ vār, ācārya (such as Rāmānuja), or jīyar (such as the founder of a maṭha) is carried about. In addition, there are processions in which a living person—such as an ācārya or jīyar on his birthday—is carried on a palanquin. During processions, the leader of an important group of temple servants might be honoured by being carried in the palanquin for all to see.

Such processions are replicated on a smaller scale in villages

and neighbourhoods where the music is usually bhajanas or the songs of Tyāgarāja sung by members of a sabhā, accompanied by the harmonium and drums. Because temples are small or non-existent in many villages and neighbourhoods, the expression of religion in outside space is particularly important. These village processions function to make public the sect’s identity, often in competition with other sects. They are occasions, moreover, for proselytisation when the group stops for a short lecture. Processions send off pilgrims and honour their return. In fact, processions are central to the very idea of salvation: the final joyous journey to the ‘auspicious land’, the supreme heaven, is, after all, described as a grand procession.

A Note on Women in Śrīvaiṣṇavism

Since social justice includes justice for women, and reforms to improve the status of women became a major issue from colonial times, it is important to discuss briefly the position of women in Śrīvaiṣṇavism within a broad historical perspective.

18

Between the sixth century bce and the third century ce,

there was a decline in the status of women from that of the Vedic period. According to the Dharmaśāstras, a large corpus of texts composed between 600 bce and 600 ce, views on the proper behaviour of women (strīdharma) were structured by notions of auspiciousness and inauspiciousness, but also purity and pollution. Common norms for elite women included pativrata (fidelity to husband), pre-pubertal marriage, denial of education, segregation, no divorce, no widow remarriage and, in some contexts, sati (self-immolation of a wife on the funeral pyre of her husband). These texts also include misogynistic statements related to women’s ignorance, impurity and infidelity. These norms, although by no means universal even in Brahmanical contexts, influenced those of other castes. Women, moreover, were often placed in the same low category as śūdras in many texts. Women and śūdras did not have access to salvation in this life because they could not study the prerequisite, the Vedas, which was for Brahmin men only.

With the growing influence of the bhakti movements from

the fifth century ce, however, the position of women improved. Descriptions of women in the Tamil bhakti poetry of the āḻvārs from the seventh century is a case in point. One of the āḻvārs, for instance, was a woman,āṇṭāḷ, and the verses of the other āḻvārs view women positively. By the time that this tradition was institutionalised as the Śrīvaiṣṇava sect, we find in the writings an inclusive salvific theology. Rāmānuja, for instance, in his commentary on Bhagavad Gītā 9:32, says: ‘also women, vai śyas and śūdras shall attain the supreme goal’.51 Moreover, other ācāryas used metaphors to express the feminine aspect of God, drew on the relation of (female) lover and (male) beloved to express the devotee’s relation to God, and repeated the Mahābhārata’s story of how God saved Draupadī when she was menstruating in order to show that woman’s periodic ‘impurity’ was no bar to salvation.

In the sixteenth century, Piḷḷailokam Jīyar describes

Śrīvaiṣṇava views on sati and widowhood in the Śrīvaiṣṇava Samayācāra Niṣkarṣa (The Essence of Śrīvaiṣṇava Normative

19

Behaviour).52 He argues against sati, for instance, saying that the commandments for it pertain only to women who are rebirth-oriented, that this custom is against scripture, that it will prevent the woman’s husband from attaining heaven, and that it will turn the woman into a non-Vaiṣṇava—all ideas that Śrīvaiṣṇavism denies.

Nevertheless, in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries,

under colonial rule, the general position of women was again declining. As I have argued elsewhere,

During this period, the British contributed to brahmanical

conservatism by codifying its family law and applying it

to everyone, not just Brahmins. Throughout India we find

texts being written that enshrine conservative norms for

women. In the Śrīvaiṣṇava Tīpikai,53 for instance, we see this

social conservatism. It prescribes the proper behavior for

a woman in her daily activities, domestic roles, relation to

her husband such as prostrating to him when awakening,

strict observance of purity laws, eating only the remains

of food that had been offered to God, husband and other

devotees, staying in a ‘hidden place’ during menstruation

and observing orthodox norms for widowhood.54

Despite the growing conservatism, the Śrīvaiṣṇava Tīpikai remains supportive of women’s access to Śrīvaiṣṇava knowledge: they should know the lineage of teachers, the secret mantras, the 4,000 verses (the Divyaprabandham), and theological and philosophical treatises. Like Śrīvaiṣṇava men, women are to be devout, self-controlled, compassionate, egoless, and indifferent to wealth and material objects.

By 1930, the Śrīśaṇḍīlya Viśiṣṭa Parama Dharmaśāstra,55 which

has been influential in modern Śrīvaiṣṇava circles, shows that marriage and family life are to be situated within the larger framework of devotion to God. A husband’s responsibility is to turn his whole household toward the supreme Lord rather than to demand that his wife treat him as god, that a woman’s worth is defined by her religious behaviour, not her fertility, and that it is not incumbent on her to bear sons for the eventual performance of her husband’s death rituals and passage to

20

heaven. If she is infertile or bears only daughters, she is not to be abandoned. A widow, moreover, is not to shave her head, a common practice at that time in Brahmin circles, and a widow is not to be considered an inauspicious ogress or witch who had caused her husband’s untimely death, because death for all Śrīvaiṣṇavas is categorically auspicious.

In short, although Śrīvaiṣṇavism sometimes embraced

the conservative norms for women found in many Brahmin circles in Tamil Nadu and elsewhere, it was more liberal, at least in theory, regarding questions of salvation, education, norms for widowhood and sati. It did not overrule, however, the norm of marriage and at least nominal subservience to the husband. On these matters, Te ṉkalais and Vaṭakalais had minor differences. Those thirteenth and fourteenth-century ācāryas later considered Teṉkalai, such as Piḷḷai Lokācārya, Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Perumāḷ Nāyaṉār and Maṇavāḷa Māmuni, argued, for instance, that the sacred syllable OM could be uttered by women, whereas Vedānta Deśika and those ācāryas later considered Va ṭakalai did not allow that.56

Caste Conundrums

The classic model of social organisation found in many Dharmaśāstras posits four varṇas (groups or classes based on common characteristics). The first three, the ‘twice-born’ who are ritually superior to the fourth, include Brahmins (priests and scholars), kṣatriyas (rulers and warriors) and vaiśyas (traders). Below them are śūdras (servants of the higher varṇas). And below even śūdras are caṇḍāḷas (‘untouchables’ who are technically outside the ‘system’ altogether). According to M.N.Srinivas, caste is a ‘hereditary, endogamous, usually localized group, having a traditional association with an occupation and a particular position in the hierarchy of castes’.57 Although this definition has been debated and revised, it is still useful as a working definition. (For a brief history of definitions of caste and debates from colonial times to today, see Appendix 3.) For

21

some groups today, community is a euphemism for caste, but for Śrīvaiṣṇavas it is located in its ancient idea of the ‘family’ (kula), that is, the community of devotees.

In this book, I generally use the word ‘caste’ in the sense of

caste clusters such as Chettiyar, Dalit, Icai Velala, Naidu, Pillai, Reddiyar, Sattada, Vanniyar, Yadava, Vellalar, and Brahmin.58 As a result, I do not refer to specific jāti (sub-caste) identities and only occasionally, in discussions of texts, to the four varṇas ( brāhmaṇa, kṣatriya, vaiśya and śūdra). Sometimes I do use governmental administrative labels such as Scheduled Castes (SCs), Scheduled Tribes (STs), Other Backward Castes (OBCs) and Forward Castes when citing government documents, or media reports that use them. Occasionally, I use the term ‘Sanskritisation’, a process, according to M. N. Srinivas, by which middle and lower castes that desire upward mobility imitate the behaviour and customs (being vegetarian, say, or wearing the sacred thread) of those who have high ritual status, such as Brahmins and Ksatriyas.59

Although Srinivas suggests that Sanskritisation refers

to those with high ritual status, who are usually Brahmins or Ksatriyas, I prefer to use Sanskritisation for imitation of Brahmins, and the term Ksatriyasation for imitation of Ksatriyas. In my interviews, I found some castes practising Ksatriyasation by claiming they had ancestors associated with Pallava, Cola, Pandya, Viyayanagara, or Nayaka royal families. For instance, although the Vanniyars worship various deities, some sub-groups are exclusively Vaiṣṇava and connect themselves with Kulac ēkaraāḻvār who, according to tradition, had been a Cera king. Edgar Thurston (1855–1935) notes that at the Parthasarathy (Pārtasārati) temple in Chennai, Pallis (Vanniyars) celebrate Kulacēkara’s birthday with great éclat.60

The Śrīvaiṣṇava Concept of Inclusiveness

Already by the time of the Mahābhārata, worshippers of Nārāyaṇa-Viṣṇu-Kṛṣṇa in the north had reacted against the

22

disrespect and loss of dignity caused by the hierarchical social order. They carried their protest with them as migrants to the south (and elsewhere).61 In the verses of the āḻvārs (which became the Śrīvaiṣṇava Tamil scripture, the Divyaprabandham), we discover clues, for instance, that they came from different castes. We do not know the caste/occupational identities of the first four āḻvārs (Poykai, Pūta, Pēy and Tirumaḻicai). From descriptions in their verses, Tirumaṅkaiāḻvār seems to be a chieftain and perhaps Kulac ēkara; Nammāḻvār a landlord or administrator, and Toṇṭaratippoṭi and Periyāḻvār, Brahmins.62 Becauseāṇṭāḷ is the daughter of Periyāḻvār, she would be a Brahmin. The later tradition considered Madhurakavi a Brahmin too. In sampradāya works, Tiruppāṇ is described as an outcaste, but there is nothing internal to his own verses to suggest this.

The theme of inclusiveness, I will argue, is about including

everyone in the opportunity for salvation in this life and deep respect for those who worship Nārāyaṇa-Kṛṣna-Viṣṇu as the supreme God. That said, Śrīvaiṣṇava inclusiveness might have had economic motivations as well. The Divyaprabandham verses often refer to the landscapes of agricultural villages. Even though they do not mention irrigation, we can surmise that the religious orientation emphasised communal solidarity, mutual respect and economic cooperation, in part to facilitate the process of rice cultivation, which required village collaborative efforts and action to prevent agricultural workers from becoming disgruntled and moving elsewhere. Local temples and sectarian networks provided identity until some kings recognised their importance for making connections at the local level, which helped secure their powers. After that, kings occasionally became patrons, contributing monumental architecture, lands and other resources. Gradually, in the Tamil region, these temples became dramatic features of the landscape, as they grew in size with tall gopurams (gateways) that could be seen from a distance.63

The āḻvārs and those who came after them certainly knew

about the north Indian varṇa model of society, as it appears in

23

many Dharmaśāstras. The āḻvārs’ castigation of those Brahmins who are arrogant alludes to the idea of varṇa as hierarchical.64 Epigraphers have noticed, for instance, the lack of Brahmanical and royal domination at many early temples; rather, merchants, weavers, craftsmen and others were patrons.

Through subsequent history, some hierarchically-oriented

Brahmins joined the temple milieu, sometimes at the request of local rulers, whose patronage transformed them into wealthy landowners. Customs and gifts, which defined who received temple honours and allowed some groups to approach temple images of God more closely than others, indicate the incursion of northern forms of hierarchy and their increasing hold on identity and status. These developments led to controversies between those who followed the āḻvārs’ view of the community of devotees and those who supported the varṇa system.

For the āḻvārs, respect for other devotees and a sense of

the equality of all devotees did not mean elimination of caste. At least, there are no statements to that effect. The same must be said for the ācāryas. They defended ideas of respect and argued that non-Brahmins could attain salvation in this life. Periyavāccāṉ Piḷḷai, for instance, insists on deep and humble respect of other devotees, no matter what their caste. In his Arthapancakam, Pi ḷḷai Lokācārya says that insult to any devotee is a sin (bhagavatāpacāra).65 In fact, ‘to think of a devotee in terms of caste (varṇa) is said to be a heinous act, comparable to examining the genitals of one’s own mother; such a devotee becomes an outcaste (caṇṭāḷa)’.66

Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Perumāḷ Nāyaṉār in his Ācārya

H ṛdayam67 advocates respect for all devotees, also with strong words. He writes that ignorant people are confused about varṇa, stage of life (āśrama) and amount of knowledge, thinking that these define people as superior and inferior, even though this is not so. A Brahmin might study all the four Vedas, but without the knowledge of God, he is a donkey. A person who is born in a good family and who appears like a sage (muni) but has no god in his heart is worse than a person who eats

24

dog meat. One who is master of all learning that does not help one obtain salvation, moreover, is master only of that which is equal to repairing sandals. In spite of having all knowledge, not understanding the supremacy of Lord Nārāyaṇa, and taking his feet into one’s heart (that is, taking God as refuge) is a waste, like offering something into ashes instead of into the sacred fire. The author certainly puts emphasis on devotion to God and suggests that this mandates equal respect.

Despite this strong stance upholding inclusiveness

and respect, some Śrīvaiṣṇavas were more conservative. In the fourteenth century, Vedānta Deśika was reinforcing the orthodox Brahmanical stance on some issues, drawing on Dharmaśāstra and Pañcarātra passages. In his works, he argues, for instance, that only Brahmin men may utter the sacred syllable Om.68 In his commentary on Tiruppāṉ’s Amalanātippirāṉ, moreover, he avoids the controversy over worshipping an outcaste (all āḻvārs were worshipped as images in temples) by saying that Tiruppāṇ had been born to a Brahmin family, even though he was brought up by non-Brahmins, and is therefore not really an outcaste. That said, Vedānta Deśika does support the importance of the Tamil Veda, because it provides easy accessibility to the essence of the Vedas for everyone, including women and śūdras.

Maṇavāḷa Māmuni in the late fourteenth and early fifteenth

century once again defends caste inclusivity in no uncertain terms. In his commentary on Aḻakiya Maṇavāḷa Perumāḷ Nāyaṉār’s Ācārya Hṛdayam, he insists that in order to remove pollution caused by pride, knowers of the Veda of lofty birth should serve and teach low-caste devotees. In fact, they should worship the low-castes

… as a family deity, equal to the Lord …. The Lord has

revealed that the water in which [a Bhāgavata’s] feet are

washed is excellent tirtha [holy water], his leftover food is

the best purifier. Therefore, if those who are unclean and

drunk with egoism partake of this t īrtha or prasāda, their

essential natures (svarūpa) will be purified.69

25

In this way, Maṇavāḷa Māmuni used extreme rhetoric to underscore the importance of utmost respect for all Śrīvaiṣṇavas, of whatever caste.

In support of this view of inclusiveness, many ācāryas—

Rāmānuja, Periyavāccāṉ Piḷḷai, Piḷḷai Lokācārya, Aḻakiya Māṇavāḷa Perumāḷ Nāyaṉār, and even Vedānta Deśika, despite his conservatism—had explained how low-caste or outcaste figures such as Vidhura, Dharmavyādha, Pāduvāṉ and Vibh īṣaṇa, mentioned in the epics and purāṇas, were righteous, devout, wise, and learned. As such, they were not only included in the religion, but were exemplary figures.

This rhetoric of caste inclusiveness, a key strategy for

proselytism, attracted many non-Brahmins over the centuries, which can be detected in the inscriptions. In the fifteenth century, for instance, we have the famous figure of Kantāṭai Rāmānuja Ayyaṅkar,70 a disciple of Aḻakiya-maṇavāḷa-cīyar, the head of the Paravastu Maṭha (Vādhikēsari Aḻakiya-maṇavāḷa-cīyar Maṭha) in Kanchipuram, supposedly founded by Maṇavāḷa Māmuni. He also managed projects for the Vijayanagara emperors Saluva-nara-simha-deva-raya I (r. 1486–91) and then Saluva-nara-simha-deva-raya II (r.1491–1505). Kantāṭai Rāmānuja Ayyaṅkar is best described as a non-Brahmin entrepreneur at Tirupati and Tirumalai who organised irrigation works on land that donors had gifted to the temples, the income of which was used for the support of various religious activities such as temple rituals, festivals, and feeding and housing pilgrims in the rāmānuja kūṭams (choultries), which he managed.

All this made Kantāṭai Rāmānuja Ayyaṅkar a wealthy and

powerful man in the area. Of interest here is the likelihood that he enhanced the profile of the āḻvārs at Tirumalai by instituting directly or indirectly an annual cycle of festivals on their birth star (nakṣatra) and having their verses chanted by Sattada (non-Brahmin) chanters along with Sattina (Brahmin) chanters.71 From inscriptions, we know that his son, who went by the same name, and disciples72 also had influence at Kanchipuram and Srirangam. The chanting of the verses of the āḻvārs in temple

26

rituals no doubt attracted other non-Brahmins, who identified with the fact that the āḻvārs came from many castes.

Francis Clooney warns scholars today, who are predisposed

to think that caste is evil, not to read into this literature the idea that the āḻvārs and ācāryas wanted to eliminate the caste system altogether.73

Patricia Mumme makes much the same point.74 She sees

in Victor Turner’s theory of communitas—a religious modality of role reversals to glorify ‘the lowly, the liminal, and the subservient while castigating the privileged, the dominant, and the authoritative’75—a way to understand the Te ṉkalai mention of eating the leftover food or drinking the foot-washing water of low-caste devotees. Whether or not these were ever practised, the very rhetoric instigates communitas. Following Turner, she notes that communitas exists dialectically with structure, a structure in Śrīvaiṣṇavism that is best represented by Vedānta Deśika’s insistence on the duties of caste and stage of life. Mumme argues that it is highly unlikely that modern Teṉkalai Brahmins even read books by Bhāgavata authors or trained them in the commentaries, much less prostrated to them or had them as preceptors, as the sampradāya texts advocate. In other words, Te ṉkalai Brahmins did not treat Bhāgavatas in their own community much differently than other non-Brahmins, aside from communitas in devotional contexts, initiating them when requested, or allowing them to chant Om.

There is some truth to this comment, I think, but Rāmānuja

and other ācāryas certainly differed from many orthodox Hindus by arguing that women and śūdras could attain salvation in this very life, whereas orthodox Brahmins often held that being reborn was a minimum requirement. I would add that despite criticism from some orthodox Brahmins that Śrīvaiṣṇavas were, in fact, advocating destruction of the caste system, virtually all ācāryas up to today never advocated abandonment of caste, only expressions of superiority and inferiority, which were often its byproduct. By the eighteenth century, with general Brahmanical reinforcement of caste norms to provide a buttress against change and an escalation

27

of tensions between Va ṭakalais and Teṉkalais over temple control, some Va ṭakalais were also accusing Teṉkalais of advocating abandonment of caste, which could be read into scriptural and sampradāya passages.

Even though Teṉkalai Brahmins tried to finesse the

ostensible contradiction between denying caste and yet living by it by arguing that it was unnecessary to observe caste norms, although it was permissible to do so for the sake of convention, in point of fact they continued to observe Brahmanical norms unless there was a direct conflict with a Śrīvaiṣṇava one.76 Otherwise, they would have lost their status as Brahmins. Therefore, there was a tacit acceptance of many caste norms for pragmatic reasons, despite the inclusive rhetoric.77 An underlying problem, which Teṉkalais recognised, was that the arduous Brahmanical tradition of memorising the Vedas and other scriptures would most likely be maintained only if Brahmins themselves maintained the duties of caste and stage of life. To attract patrons, these Brahmins would need religious authority. And that relied both on their knowledge and their distinctive, orthodox Brahmin way of life.78

In the final analysis, even when Śrīvaiṣṇavism had

managed to create an ethos of respect, there were many structural aspects of discrimination that must have festered resentment in non-Brahmins, and especially in outcastes who, by the sixteenth century, if not much earlier, were not allowed to enter Hindu temples, including Śrīvaiṣṇava ones. Although Nammāḻvār in Tiruvāymoḻi 3.7.9 (discussed in Chapter 3) had proclaimed that the lowest of the low, the outcastes (Caṇḍālas) of the outcastes, are our lords, a verse often quoted by ācāryas (even today), the irony of this rhetoric would not have gone unnoticed. The Caṇḍāla as the key symbol of Śrīvaiṣṇava inclusiveness could not even enter God’s temple, so impure was he or she. No wonder Dalit anger was real and mobilisation was already occurring in the colonial period. And no wonder many Teṉkalais struggled from that time to make sense of their tradition.

The Politics of Religion

Religion became highly politicised in the colonial period, on account of criticism by missionaries, new concepts of Dravidian languages and culture, Dalit mobilisation, competition with Śaivas, tensions between Brahmins and non-Brahmins, and actions by the colonial government, such as the secularisation of space and alliances with elite non-Brahmin landowners (see Appendix 4.). This culminated in the non-Brahmin movement of the late colonial period and political populism after Independence.

Caste conflict between Brahmins and elite non-Brahmins

as well as between elite non-Brahmins and the lower castes spiralled, in the early twentieth century, over issues of precedent and respect, triggered in part by the census, which tried to document the hierarchy but had the effect of hardening it.79 The turbulence of Tamil politics during the early twentieth century and the dramatic political and economic changes, especially after Independence, which offered many non-Brahmins new opportunities, become the catalysts for social justice movements.

As a window on these changes, I focus on a key political

figure. He interests us not only because of his role in the non-Brahmin movement and his positions on caste and women, but also because he came from a Śrīvaiṣṇava family.

Periyar, a Śrīvaiṣṇava Renegade

Of great significance for non-Brahmins in general and Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmins in particular was the figure popularly known as Periyar or E. V. R. (1879–1973). He was born as Raghav Erode Venkata Ramasami Naicker in the town Erode (Coimbatore district), to a wealthy non-Brahmin (Balija Naidu) business family that spoke three languages: Kannada, Telugu and Tamil. A pious Teṉkalai Śrīvaiṣṇava, Ramasami’s father, whose ancestors had given an image of Lord Nārāyaṇa

29

to the Tirukkottiyur temple, would invite Śrīvaiṣṇavas to give religious discourses in the family house. Nonetheless, his son, who managed only a few years of primary school, delighted in being a prankster and broke taboos to disturb the orthodox household. He began to criticise religion.

Even as a child, Periyar was disturbed by Hindu

mythology’s many contradictions. A few years after his marriage, according to his biographer,80 he had a fight with his father and left home to don the garb of a holy man. With two Tamil Brahmins, he headed off to Hyderabad. The three became famous for their entertaining religious talks. These included stories, jokes, puns, irreverent questions and harangues. Periyar, the pseudo-ascetic, travelled as far as Calcutta and then the holy city of Varanasi, where he assisted the priest of a Śaiva temple. Eventually, the priest dismissed him for refusing to bathe.

Meanwhile, his dislike of Hinduism developed into

revulsion at the sight of corpses in the Ganges and beggars in the streets. His dislike grew when a Brahmin charitable guesthouse refused to give him the free meals that it gave Brahmin pilgrims. (Very hungry, he tried to gain entry again by dressing up as a Brahmin; his moustache, which Brahmins never wore, gave him away.) Fuelling his rage was the fact that a non-Brahmin from south India had founded this guesthouse. These experiences reinforced his antipathy to religion, turning him into an avowed atheist and rationalist.

In 1919, Periyar joined the Indian National Congress,

becoming vice-president of the Tamil Nadu Congress Committee.81 He trained volunteers for strikes and pickets to protest British rule and championed various Congress activities, such as wearing homespun cotton cloth, prohibiting alcohol and participating in satyāgrahas, such as the one that agitated in 1924 for the right of Dalits to enter Hindu temples, an event that led to both he and his wife being imprisoned.

Two pivotal episodes transformed him into an anti-

Gandhian. The first took place at a Congress-sponsored school near Madras. It became mired in a conflict over separate eating

30

places for Brahmins and non-Brahmins. The second took place at a Gandhi ashram in Tirunelveli district that was linked directly with some Congress leaders. Once again, Periyar was shocked that Brahmins and non-Brahmins could not eat together. These events probably reminded him of his own rejection in the Varanasi ‘Brahmin’ guesthouse, and cemented his contempt for Brahmins and their religion.

After these episodes, Periyar left the Congress Party,

pointing out Gandhi’s short-comings: deference to Brahmins and their Sanskrit texts, the varṇa system, caste rules forbidding inter-marriage or inter-dining, non-allowing Dalits to enter temples, and so on. All this led Periyar’s followers to argue that he offered Dalits more support than Gandhi did.

Periyar launched the Self-Respect Movement in 1925 for

non-Brahmins. It promoted reason over religion and real social equality over hierarchy as a way to destroy caste and orthodox gender norms. In the final decades of the Raj, Periyar became the key proponent of Dravidianism. The latter’s goals were an independent country for the South, to eliminate Brahmin domination through the caste system, and northern domination through Hindi, should it become the national language.

Of interest here, though, are Periyar’s views on caste and

religion. In 1929, he decided to drop his caste name, Naicker, in order to promote a casteless society. He became known, therefore, as E. V. Ramasami, E. V. R. for short. His followers knew him affectionately as Periyar (the great or elder one).

His views on religion tell us much about him. Periyar

was vehemently against all religions, especially Hinduism, ridiculing its myths and urging people to break images of deities, put the cherished Rāmāyaṇa in bonfires, and mock the gurus. Periyar, who had Communist connections and had visited the Soviet Union (along with many other European countries), argued that ‘people used religion only as a mask to deceive innocent people and therefore took it as one of his duties to life to warn people against superstitions and priests’.82 For him, the only necessary scripture was the Tirukku ṟal, which he considered a secular work. (Scholars have identified it as a

31

cosmopolitan Jain text, in the sense that its author was a Jain who celebrated religious diversity.) He was active, moreover, in helping Dalits gain entry to Hindu temples; this contributed to the Temple Entry Acts of 1924, 1931 and 1950.

As a propagandist, Periyar wanted to make atheism the

new dogma through public campaigns, and remove ritualism by advocating secular marriages without mantras and priests. Periyar’s greatest ire had been directed at the Brahmins. He sometimes incited people to kill Brahmins and burn down their homes. Despite the fact that such dire acts never occurred, Periyar lost a court case in Tiruchirappalli and was jailed for his extreme rhetoric (he spent his jail time in hospital). He ‘has not often called for the killing of Brahmins, but he has for many years called for their expulsion from Tamilnad’.83

The Justice Party, which began in 1917, advocated

reservations based on caste and educational reforms. But it favoured high-ranking non-Brahmins, a position criticised by the Dalits. Initially, there was hostility between Periyar’s Self-Respect Movement and the Justice Party. This was partly because of Justice Party’s views on Brahmins. It was not so harsh on them, unlike members of the Self-Respect Movement, who attacked the Brahmins as missionaries of a northern religion brought to the south, enslaving Tamils and destroying their indigenous culture.

Members of the Justice Party were often devout Hindus.

They imitated Brahmanical ways. Members of Self-Respect, though, were severe critics of this orientation. The parties found support, moreover, from different castes. Justice drew support from ‘forward’ non-Brahmins who owned land, had businesses, or practised professions. These non-Brahmins included Mudaliyars, Pillais and Reddiyars, about 19 per cent of the population. They mobilised partly to gain good government jobs for themselves and did little for the Backward Castes (49 per cent) and Scheduled Castes (17 per cent), even refusing to support a law against untouchability in 1922.84 In other words, the Justice Party sided with those who were only somewhat less privileged than the twice-born, whereas the

32

Self-Respect members supported Dalits and other ‘low’ castes as well.

Aside from changes advocated by the Self-Respect

Movement and the Justice Party, the Raj too was instigating change. ‘The management of temple trusts, the abolition of devadāsīs (temple dancers), and the legislative efforts to promote temple entry for untouchable groups,’ says Nicholas Dirks, ‘were all pitched in terms of principles having to do with fundamental human rights.’85 Ironically, the government’s attempts to reform Hindu institutions and control Brahmin power by setting up new agencies, such as the Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowment Board (1927),86 not only drew on Sanskritic interpretations and rituals, but it also placed Brahmins on temple-managing boards that had been under the control of non-Brahmin castes groups.87

By the late 1930s, Periyar was becoming less radical and

the Justice Party was soliciting his support in order to stem its waning political influence. The two became more closely allied in their common cause of avoiding Brahmin and Hindi domination after Indian independence. According to V. Geetha and S. V. Rajadurai, non-Brahmins did not want independence before the British could destroy Brahmin domination and the hierarchical caste system. They opposed the Congress Party, which advocated Home Rule88 and had many Brahmin leaders. Some who opposed the platform were Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins themselves.89 Others, such as Periyar and P. Varadarajulu Naidu, had been born into Śrīvaiṣṇava non-Brahmin families.

And then, in 1939, Periyar became head of the Justice

Party. This was after it had lost mass support. In 1944, he renamed the party the Dravidar Kazhagam (DK), which means the Dravidian Association. It attracted students with its demand for social justice; villagers with its demands for better education, communication and transportation; women with its demands for women’s education and reforms of customs governing marriage and widowhood; along with Dalits with its demand for the abolition of untouchability. ‘Its flag is black above to designate the injustice in society, and red below, to

33

show the revolutionary fervor with which the party tries to overthrow injustice.’90

After Independence, the party split in 1949 over the

issue of whether Tamil Nadu should be a separate country, or whether it should join the new country of India. Periyar stayed with his long-standing demand for separation, whereas C. N. Annadurai, who then formed the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK, the Dravidian Progress Federation), accepted the idea of union, albeit with increased rights for Tamil Nadu. Periyar and Annadurai also differed on whether they should just be social reformers, or whether they should try to form a government by gaining power through the vote. Periyar opted for the former and Annadurai the latter.

The issue of separation was finally laid to rest after

Independence by a 1957 law that banned public agitation for it, although Periyar periodically spoke about his dream. The DMK went on to become a well-organised party with a democratic constitution, moderate propaganda, and fewer attacks on religion in general and Brahmins in particular.

Political Populism

From the middle of the twentieth century, Tamil political power was being defined largely in terms of populism. Populism helps account for the politics of reservations in Tamil Nadu. Reservation policies had been first introduced by the Raj in the middle of the nineteenth century, and in earnest in the first half of the twentieth, thanks in part to the Justice Party, which introduced reservation of seats in educational institutions.

A big change occurred after Indian independence in 1947

with the introduction of democracy and the vote in 1950. In 1954, Tamil lower castes succeeded in ousting C. Rajagopalachari,91 the Brahmin Chief Minister from the Congress Party, and replaced him with Kamaraj Nadar—a Congress member from the once low-status Nadar caste (see Appendix 6)—who then held power for the next decade and did much for the lower

34

castes. Over time, the Backward and Scheduled Castes, which together constituted about 66 per cent of the population, exerted great influence on all the political parties with their large vote banks92 and astute political organisation.

The DMK had come to power in 1967 with considerable

support from Dalits, despite the latter’s stronghold in Congress. When Annadurai died in 1969, Muthuvel Karunanidhi (1924– 2018), a politician, playwright and screenwriter, became leader of the party and was elected thirteen times to the Tamil Nadu Assembly, and was Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu five times between 1969 and 2011.93 Together with relatives who have held other important positions in the Tamil Nadu and Central governments, not to mention the DMK party, he has wielded enormous wealth and power. His influence radiates out to the media and business, which has led to many allegations and some convictions of corruption, especially nepotism. Despite this strength, the DMK did not always hold power in the second half of the twentieth century and beginning of the twenty-first. Nevertheless, it has influenced many people. As we will see, some of my informants had brief flirtations with the DMK, one was the propagandist for the party and one is connected to Karunanidhi by caste and family.

One of the keys to Karunanidhi’s success was his

unabashed populism: various welfare schemes, besides reservations that touched many households and included food subsidies, midday meals for school children (which had begun under MGR), reduction in the price of rice, a health insurance scheme, and free electricity to farmers. His critics considered these crass attempts to secure votes; others saw them not as patronage politics, but as serious contributions to social development and social justice. Karunanidhi was especially attentive to addressing women’s needs with schemes such as securing property rights, free graduate education for girls, and support for inter-caste marriages and widow remarriage.

Another split in the Dravidian parties occurred in 1972,

when M. G. Ramachandran (1917–87), a popular film star, broke away from the DMK to form the Anna Dravida Munnetra

35

Kazhagam (ADMK), which would later be renamed as the All India Anna Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (AIADMK) when it established itself in other states. With his massive popularity and a platform that was not so anti-Brahmin and anti-Hindi as the DK and DMK had been, M.G.R. mustered political power, which led to his becoming the Chief Minister in the elections of 1977, 1980, and 1984.

After M. G. R.’s death in 1987 (followed by the very brief

rule of his wife), J. Jayalalithaa—a Tamil Śrīvaiṣṇava Vaṭakalai Brahmin originally from Melkote, Mysore (now Karnataka), who had become a Chennai-based actress—led the party. It was only after Karunanidhi’s government was dismissed in 1990 by Prime Minister Chandra Shekhar (an ally of Jayalalithaa) that Jayalalithaa came to power. She stayed in power in Tamil Nadu through much of the next two decades,94 thanks to populism in the form of various welfare schemes, free bicycles, colour televisions, mobile phones and cheap broadband Internet, and laptops to certain constituencies. Jayalalithaa also had influence in the Central government, thanks to Congress or Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) alliances.

Jayalalithaa was accused, eventually convicted (September

2014) and forced to step down, in a case that went on for eighteen years, over her amassing a large amount of properties, jewellery, luxury cars, cash and investments while she was Chief Minister during 1991–96.95 Nevertheless, by 2015, an appeals court overturned the judgment of the lower court. After her acquittal, she again became Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu on 23 May 2015. The saga was not yet over. There again was an appeal on the corruption charge, which went to the Supreme Court. The Court heard the appeal; however, there was no verdict in May 2016, when the Court went into recess.

Then Jayalalithaa died on 5 December 2016, followed eight

months later by the death of Karunanidhi. We will return to this turbulent period, the changing of the guard as it were, in the last chapter.

As this Introduction suggests, religion, caste and politics have been deeply intertwined in Tamil society. With our focus on Śrīvaiṣṇavism, several topics introduced here must be tracked carefully in the following chapters. More specifically, we must watch for whether sectarian

The ritual is next mentioned in eight stories found in the Guruparamparā Prabhāvam 6000, in the context of defining Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava male identity, more specifically as a mandatory ritual connected to initiation into Vedic study ( upanāyana), or for conversion of Brahmin householders and ascetics from Śaivism or Advaita to Śrīvaiṣṇavism. There is a brief mention that it could be done for women and śūdras (Raman, ‘Samāśrayaṇam’, 109), and could be performed at any stage of life. The lack of specific examples of women and śūdras in this text raises the question whether the brief allusion to them is just to appear inclusive, or whether it reflects reality. Because aspects of the pañcasa ṁskāra, such as branding on the shoulders, are ancient, there was surely some kind of initiatory ritual for women and śūdras over the centuries. Inscriptions from the fifteenth century, for instance, mention non-Brahmin disciples with initiatory names. It is reasonable to assume that there were two versions, one for twice-born men and one for others.

As for women, the Śrīvaiṣṇava Tīpikai, which I discuss in the section

of this chapter entitled ‘A Note on Women in Śrīvaiṣṇavism’, says that women should know the lineage of teachers, the secret mantras, and the theological and philosophical treatises. Because samāśrayaṇam is the initiation ritual into a connection with an ācārya or jīyar, and study of the tradition’s treatises, the mention of such a connection in the Śrīvaiṣṇava Tīpikai implies that women, at least Brahmin women, by the time this text was written, routinely performed samāśrayaṇam.

The history of Śrīvaiṣṇavism in the seventeenth and eighteenth

centuries is very poorly understood. At some point, there must have been major attempts by the ācāryas to recruit non-Brahmins to Śrīvaiṣṇavism, to counter the power of the Śaiva non-Brahmin

43

ma ṭhas that were actively recruiting non-Brahmin elites as patronage from kings waned. Given the new Teṉkalai-Vaṭakalai competition over control of the temples, moreover, it would have been important to attract wealthy and powerful non-Brahmins to the Teṉkalai community, and have them wear the Teṉkalai nāmam, a move that was likely extended over time to other non-Brahmins.

Samāśrayaṇam might have been extended from a Brahmanical

context to a more general one that included non-Brahmins as a formal entry of Vaiṣṇava non-Brahmins into a ‘Brahmin-style’ Śrīvaiṣṇavism to consolidate their allegiance. This was not a conversion per se, even for Brahmins, over hard sectarian boundaries (see Raman ‘Initiation and Conversion’, 281–89, for a discussion of hard and soft boundaries in this sect). Rather, it was a re-orientation of a Vaiṣṇava identity over soft boundaries within the Vaiṣṇava world itself, to a new orientation modelled on Brahmanical orthodoxy including vegetarianism, elaborate daily pūjās, and so forth. I would argue that when this ritual was extended to non-Brahmins, it served some purposes, but it created problems for Brahmin religious leaders because an important part of the ritual was to initiate the person into a study of the sampradāya texts, the knowledge of which had been carefully protected by the Brahmins. In Chapter 2, I trace the struggles by non-Brahmins to obtain access to this knowledge.
