Appendix 6



Two Nadars on Discrimination



At a Tamil workshop in Puducherry, I met two researchers with PhDs, T. Rajarethinam Chettikulam and M.Prabhakaran. After I gave a talk on my project on non-Brahmins, they told me how their experience differed from my description. They were from the Kanyakumari district, the southernmost part of Tamil Nadu, which borders the modern state of Kerala \(about 60 per cent of Nadars speak Malayalam\). Although they are not Śrīvaiṣṇavas, they are a kind of Vaiṣṇava that has links with Śrīvaiṣṇavism. For instance, the personal form of the supreme is Viṣṇu-Nārāyaṇ, who is also called Perumāḷ. Despite the risk of digression, I think the history of discrimination against this low-caste Vaiṣṇava community and its reaction is an important example of how some Tamil Vaiṣṇavas reacted to discrimination in one region of Tamil Nadu that is related to, and yet very different from, the Śrīvaiṣṇava case. 

Rajarethinam and Prabhakaran discussed how, in 

the nineteenth century, the Nadars had been subjected to discrimination by Brahmins \(both Tamil Brahmins and Kerala Nambudiri Brahmins\), as well as by Nayars, Kuruppas and Vellalars. Nadars were not allowed into the Travancore 







\(Trivandrum\) Padmanabhasvami temple \(a *divyadeśa*\). Nadar low status \(they were traditionally ranked just above the Dalits1\) was visually obvious, moreover, because their women were not allowed to wear sari blouses, men could not wear the turbans called *vastram*, a kind of cloth tied around the head, and Nadars could use only coconut leaves for their hut roofs. To make matters worse, the government had taxed them for these customs, which they were not even allowed to change. They had to pay a special tax, moreover, when their young girls attained puberty. All this led to the ‘Upper Cloth Controversy’ \(whether Nadar women could cover their breasts like upper-caste women\). Christian missionaries were the first to address the Nadars’ problems, but then used this support to convert many to Christianity. They attracted Nadars by allowing the women to wear blouses, the men turbans, and so forth. In addition, they developed quasi-ethnographic accounts of converts to help other missionaries. This provoked a pushback from the Nadars.2

The two young Nadar men, Rajarethinam and Prabhakaran, 

told me about a new religion3—summarised as One Caste, One Religion, One Clan, One World, One God—that had started in the nineteenth century with the Nadars in south India, and which had then spread to other castes and regions. It is called Ayyāvaḻi, which means ‘path of the father’. According to its scriptures,4 the father is Viṣṇu-Nārāyaṇa, a personal form of The Ekam, the One \(which is formless and absolute\).5 Viṣṇu-Nārāyaṇa remains in the sea, but is himself incarnated in Vaiku ṇṭar6 in order to destroy illusion \(*māyā*\) after a final judgement.7 Vaikuṇṭar becomes the supreme deity who will usher in the new age, the Dharmayuga. 

Over time, variants of this theology developed that looked 

more like other Hindu sects, and for some followers, Ayyāvaḻi came to resemble a polytheistic village religion, although followers commonly wore on their foreheads a flame-shaped *nāmam* rising from a lotus and celebrate the holy day called Ayya Vaikuṇṭa Avatāram, the day of Vaikuṇṭar’s incarnation.

My informants told me more about Vaikuṇṭar’s life 

\(ca.1809–51 ce\) from the hagiographies about him.

When Ayya Vaikundar was born in a town near 

Kanyakumari, his parents named him Muṭisūṭum Perumāḷ

because the family worshipped this deity, probably on 

account of the influence of the Padmanabham temple 

in Travancore. When the government heard the name of 

the infant, they sent soldiers to prevent the parents from 

using this name. He was then called Muttukuṭṭi, meaning 

the pearl that is the newborn calf.

Muttukuṭṭi had some kind of a disease, and so his 

parents took him to Tirucendur, a Murukaṉ temple. 

According to tradition, he went into the sea to bathe 

before going to the temple. After three days deep in the 

ocean, he emerged with a glorious light and, after three 

days, declared that he was the incarnation of Perumāḷ. 

This account probably had both Vaiṣṇava \(Nārāyaṇa is 

associated with the ocean\) and Christian influence. 

Followers of Muttu—now called Ayya Vaikuṇṭar—

worship the ‘formless form of God’. In the temple room, 

there is a stool, a *v*ē*l* \[spear\] and a dhoti cloth. People face 

the spear with folded hands. A leader of the community 

chants from the sacred book, and the congregation repeats 

a communal prayer, a tradition that might have Christian 

influence too. Prayers have integrated various names of 

God, such as Śiva. For worship, men won’t wear shirts, 

although they do wear turbans and a *nāmam*, which is a 

white vertical line symbolising fire or freedom.

Vaikuṇṭar had disciples called Gurus, who helped him and proselytised the new religion. One named Podukutty was the manager of the main temple in Swamithoppu \(where Vaikuṇṭar’s *samādhi* is located\), a role that was continued by his descendants. As the religion spread, there developed seven sacred places called *pathi*s; in addition, thousands of simple shrines were built by poor village people. Elderly village men led the simple rituals and the congregation in chanting. Everything was spoken or read only in Tamil.

Vaikuṇṭar instituted many social reforms to destroy the 

pernicious hold of the caste system. He had his followers wear the forbidden clothing, he had them refuse to pay penalty taxes or work without wages. And he encouraged them to undergo purification to eliminate meat-eating \(at least a few days a week; he himself was vegetarian\), toddy-drinking and tobacco-chewing. To break down the purity-pollution mentality, he designated a village well in Swamithope as the temple’s *t īrtha*; all castes could draw water for bathing and drinking, unlike other wells that could not be used by the lower castes. Its water supposedly had curative properties and prepared one for life in the paradise of Dharma Yuga. \(Vaiku ṇṭar was known as a miracle worker.\) 

Moreover, Vaikuṇṭar encouraged his followers to have 

meals with outcastes and to serve the festival food with their own hands. The religion operated an orphanage, an old-age home, and a charity that provided food for the needy. It promoted literacy and economic development, from agriculture to businesses and small-scale industries, in order to help eliminate the negative image of Nadars as toddy-tappers. Ethical principles included charity, tolerance, love, and peace. 

In the nineteenth century, Christian missionaries expressed 

concern over the growing popularity of Ayya Vaikuṇṭar, denouncing the movement as demonic and a threat to the spread of Christianity. 

After Indian independence, the Nadar situation improved, 

especially when Kamaraj, a Nadar, became Chief Minister.8 Although today Nadars are ranked as Other Backward Castes \(OBC\), they have achieved considerable upward mobility through education, economic development and astute political organisation. 

This was evident in the two young Nadar men from 

uneducated families \(albeit with good labour jobs\) whom I was interviewing. Both were the only ones in their villages to pursue higher education—thanks in one case to a youth spent in libraries and in the other, to an inspiring teacher—obtaining PhD degrees from the University of Kerala. Even though they

now had academic research jobs, they were sceptical about ever having a professor’s job because of the need to pay a huge amount of money just to obtain a post. They both admitted that they vote for individuals, not for parties, which are often corrupt. They admired the former Nadar Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu, Kamaraj.

The accounts provided by the two Nadars about growing 

up in Kanyakumari where their caste had experienced extreme discrimination by the Kerala elites, which had inspired the creation of a new Vaiṣṇava sect, has similarities with the Śrīvaiṣṇava story. The new religion Ayyāvaḻi emphasised equality and inclusiveness, and promoted social service. Of course, the fact that its members were based largely in one caste made it easier to create cohesiveness. Its religious leadership was Nadar, and it avoided a formal priesthood. It will be interesting to see if any of the Śrīvaiṣṇava castes whose religious identities function mainly at the caste level within their own caste *sabhā*s will develop a Nadar-like independent orientation in the future, or whether the time for such a religious reform dispensation is over now that many political rights and educational and economic opportunities are increasing.
