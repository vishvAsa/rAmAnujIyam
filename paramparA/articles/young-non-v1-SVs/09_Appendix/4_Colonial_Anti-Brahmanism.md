Appendix 4



The Colonial Cauldron of Anti-Brahmanism



Ferment in the colonial period created a powerful mix of forces that would eventually culminate in the non-Brahmin movement. One commonality among these forces was anti-Brahmanism.

Already in the nineteenth century, non-Brahmins had 

begun to protest exclusionary caste practices1 in Madras Presidency. This new self-consciousness was abetted by missionary critiques of the abuse, superstition, hypocrisy, and cunning of Brahmin priests. One of the first to adopt anti-Brahminism was Abbé J. A. Dubois \(1765–1848\). In *Hindu Manners*, *Customs*, *and Ceremonies* \(1806\), he praised Jains on the one hand and criticised Brahmins on the other. A more scholarly basis for anti-Brahminism was the concept of the Dravidian. In *A Comparative Grammar of the Dravidian or South Indian Family of Languages* \(1856\), Rev. Robert Caldwell \(1814– 91\) showed through linguistic and other methods that Tamil belonged to a family of languages that he called Dravidian. Caldwell then extended the concept of Dravidian to include culture and religion. He contrasted that with Aryan culture and religion, that is, Brahmanical culture and Advaita Vedānta, especially its modern version, known as neo-Vedānta. The 







word *drāviḍa* provided an umbrella for political mobilisation. Caldwell appealed to non-Brahmin sentiments by pointing out that Brahmins had lumped the Dravidians together as ś*ūdra*s at the bottom of the caste system. In this spirit, S. Sundaram Piḷḷai \(1855–97\), a Vellalar, wrote: ‘The Veḷḷālas who form the flower of the Dravidian race, have now so far forgotten their nationality as to habitually think and speak of themselves as Śūdras ....’2

A contemporary of Caldwell was George Pope \(1820–

1907\). Since he had translated Dubois’ work from French to English, he echoed Dubois’ praise of Jainism, noting that its *Nālaṭi-Nāṉūṟu* was so popular among the Vellalar that it was called the ‘Veḷḷāḷa-Vetham’. He goes on to champion south Indian Śaivism as the pre-Aryan religion of south India and the guardian of Tamil poetry.3 \(As the translator of Maṇikkavācakar’s *Tiruvācakam*, written in the ninth century, he had developed an interest in Śaiva religious poetry.\)

Śaiva non-Brahmins promoted this anti-Brahmin view in 

the Tamil weekly *Oru Paisa Tamilan*. Numbers and solidarity gave them power. They began to re-think history. The result was a renaissance in the scholarship on Tamil literature, history and Śaivism. Many elite Tamil non-Brahmins rallied around this idea of Śaivism, especially Śaiva Siddhānta, as the original Tamil religion. 

They took their inspiration also fromāṟumuka Nāvalar 

\(1822–79\), a Tamil-speaking Vellalar from Jaffna, Sri Lanka, where the landowning Vellalars dominated the caste hierarchy. He was instrumental in promoting a Śaiva religious identity and using the printing press to popularise his ideas. 

Elite Śaiva non-Brahmins have long had leadership roles 

in Śaiva Siddhānta. The thirteenth-century Meykaṇḍar, who was a Vellalar, for instance, was its first preceptor. He wrote the seminal *Śivajñānabodham*. Several later Śaiva monasteries had non-Brahmin leaders since the sixteenth century and Tamil libraries that became centres of Tamil learning. 

As Śaiva Siddhānta had accorded great importance to 

the Sanskrit Śaivaāgamas, some non-Brahmins had access 

341







to Sanskrit and its literature.4 Other Śaiva leaders wanted to promote only Tamil. Maraimalai Atikal \(Maṟaimalai Adigal; 1876–1950\), educated in Tamil literature, for instance, wanted to rid Tamil of Sanskrit vocabulary and influence. He started the ‘Pure Tamil’ movement. In this way, elite Śaiva non-Brahmins used the ideas of the missionary Orientalists and their promotion of Tamil language, culture and religion for their own purposes.5 According to the *Tanjore Gazetteer*, there were many more Śaiva Vellalars than Śrīvaiṣṇava Vellalars.6

V. Geetha and S. V. Rajadurai note that these developments 

provoked confrontations with Brahmins who did not appreciate rebukes and challenges to their authority. Despite being willing to accept some reforms in order to stop non-Brahmin conversions to other religions, some Brahmins mocked non-Brahmin aspirations. More specifically, they ridiculed the idea that non-Brahmins could create a common identity out of divergent castes and castigated elite non-Brahmins for their treatment of outcastes.7

After the Mutiny of 1857, when the British government 

began to administer its territories directly instead of indirectly through the East India Company, the government promised equality and religious freedom for everyone. This new policy made education, the legal system and the press theoretically available to all. The government passed a special Marriage Act in 1872, moreover, so that a person of any caste or religion could legally marry someone of any other caste or religion. It secularised wells, too, so that they would no longer be designated as polluted or pure. 

Secularisation speeded up with the advent of new markets, 

garrisons, schools, public meeting halls, libraries, and courts. The government’s authority was to rely on reason. Since this meant supporting merit over status by birth, remaining indifferent to matters of religion such as purity and pollution, and promoting the rights of all, non-Brahmins were attracted to the British values of equality.8

Furthermore, Madras Presidency needed alliances with the 

large landowners—which included the non-Brahmin Vellalars 

342







in Tamil districts, the Balija Naidus in both Telugu and Tamil districts, and the Kammas and Reddiyars in Telugu country— to facilitate tax collection in the villages. They wanted to curtail the Brahmins’ growing power in administrative jobs, moreover, which Brahmins easily obtained thanks to their long history of learning. In 1901, Tamil Brahmins were 17.88 per cent literate, Balija Naidus .98 per cent, Vellalars .19 per cent, and Reddiyars .04 per cent.9 A similar situation existed for government jobs. In Madras, Brahmins had 80 per cent of them; elite non-Brahmins had only 20 per cent.10 Brahmins, some of whom were large landowners, did not want opportunities to expand for non-Brahmins who far outnumbered them, for fear of competition.11

The advent of change for Dalits is a different story. In the 

eighteenth century and the first half of the nineteenth, many saw their lives improving, especially those who served in the armies of the Mughals and their allies, Marathas, and then the British East India Company. The Dravidian thesis was very appealing to Dalits who had long suffered under the authority of Brahmins and elite non-Brahmins who endorsed the caste hierarchy. Now the outcastes were claiming to have been the original Tamil inhabitants; the Brahmins, they said, were merely Aryan invaders. With this insight, they renamed themselves Adidravidas, the original Dravidians. 

One major development was the abolition of slavery. 

According to Eugene Irschick,12 Paraiyars \(untouchables\) and Pallis were bonded labourers for landowners, or those who had hereditary rights to the produce. These groups often became economically indebted to their employers. Despite insurrections, their lot was compelled into virtual slavery, sometimes enforced by violence. By the mid-nineteenth century, the British noticed the contradiction between their noble values and their ignoble actions. They had legitimated the Raj, after all, on moral grounds: a rule that relied on justice and fairness. This meant the elimination of master-slave relationships. In 1843, Act V made slavery illegal in India. In 



343







1860, the Indian Penal Code \(Act 45\) instituted a fine and up to seven years’ imprisonment for anyone dealing in slaves.

The practice of slavery changed very slowly. By the 

late nineteenth century, British utopian thinkers were still upbraiding colonial administrators in India for the continuing slavery and for increasing poverty. Several famines exacerbated the situation. Dalits were moving about, moreover, to escape indebtedness. Under pressure to collect taxes and trying to avoid reprimands from the mother country, colonial administrators had to improve the lot of these groups, including their status.

In Chengalpattu, for instance, colonial administrators 

began to give the Dalits free house sites and allowed them to record houses as their own property. This enabled the magistrates to praise Dalits as loyal and sedentary.13 Besides this, there was the emergence of organised labour. Becoming aware of their political and social rights, Dalits were beginning to protest their ritual status as impure. Colonial administrators used some of the Orientalist research produced by Christian missionaries to upgrade the status of Dalits. 

Some Hindu intellectuals began to publish articles on the 

problems that Dalits faced. Shri Kasi Viswanatha Mudaliar \(1806–71\), a Brahmo Samaji, propagated Ram Mohan Roy’s critique of caste in Madras. In 1870, an organisation14 was founded in Madras ‘to raise the consciousness’ of the Dalits. The Dalit John Rathinam started the popular magazine *Dravidian Pandian* in 1885. The next year, he started a school and then, in 1899, a hostel for Dalits in Madras. In 1889, Pandit C. Iyodhi Dass \(1845–1914\), an important leader of the Dalits in Tamil Nadu, and some theosophists15 started several schools for Dalits. In the 1890s, the Adi-Dravida Mahajan Sabha16 petitioned the government in Madras for agrarian reforms and lower test standards for admission to some medical schools. In 1893, Shri R. Srinivasan started a magazine, *Paraiyan*, to awaken Dalits in Madras, and in 1902, Colonel Olcott published *Poor Pariah*, which argued that long ago, the Pariahs \(an Anglicisation of Paṟaiyars\) had been Buddhists. 



344







Those Dalits who were unable to change their material 

conditions either started their own sects or, more often, converted to other religions. Pandit C. Iyodhi Dass converted to Buddhism. Many others converted to Christianity, becoming members of groups such as the Society for the Propagation of the Gospel, the London Missionary Society, the Madurai Church Council, or the Tamil Evangelical Lutheran Church.17 Dalits often represented half or more of the converts.18 Christianity offered material incentives \(mass conversions often occurring during or just after famines19\), and educational and social ones as well. By the late nineteenth century, therefore, life was changing for some Dalits.20

Although these forces during colonial times were often 

conflicting, some began to coalesce as the non-Brahmin movement developed organisational structures. But the tensions between Dalits and ‘caste’ non-Brahmins would remain a political issue long after Indian Independence.
