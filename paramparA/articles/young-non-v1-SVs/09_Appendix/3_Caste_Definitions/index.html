Appendix 3

Changing Definitions of Caste

As Nicholas Dirks has argued, we must separate the earlier caste system from the influence of colonial thought. The latter assumed that caste was a pervasive, totalising, and uniform system. He reminds us that before the colonial period, caste was not the key category or symbol of Indian civilisation, not even a system; it was merely one expression among many of social identities. Others included ‘temple communities, territorial groups, lineage segments, family units, royal retinues, warrior sub-castes, “little” kingdoms, occupational reference groups, agricultural or trading associations, devotionally conceived networks and sectarian communities …’.1

Dirks points out that the British observed, described,

enumerated, and portrayed caste in the eighteenth and early nineteenth century. Despite exotic or stereotypical features, this picture still reflected the diversity of life and occupation. Change was coming. The Tirunelveli Census of 1823 posited and ranked eight general categories: ‘Brahmans, Ksatriyas, Sudras connected to the religious establishment, Sudras of different denominations, “Muhamedans”, “Christians of different castes”, inferior Sudras, and the low castes (or untouchables).’2

Dirks notes that this list blends Manu’s concept of varṇa with revenue classifications.

The rebellion of 1857, continues Dirks, was interpreted by

the Raj as its failure to understand Indian culture (especially the caste system, but also religious and other customs), and not British complicity in troubling economic and political policies. As a result, ethnography in Madras under the leadership of Edgar Thurston between 1885 and 1908 led to the collection of data on physical measurements (anthropometry), as well as marriage customs, death rituals, evil eyes, and a host of other topics. More importantly, it led to district manuals and gazetteers that included all kinds of information about castes and customs, including some about Śrīvaiṣṇava practices and conflicts.

Under the leadership of H. H. Risley (1851–1911), an

attempt had been made to organise the groups by varṇa. In 1881, the question of size was formalised (100,000 or more). Ten years later, use of varṇa was replaced by occupation. However, as the Census of 1901 (which he was in charge of) itself admitted, it was difficult to classify castes in this way, and it would be ‘better to enumerate a few of the castes, which, from numbers or for any other reason, are of most importance in several provinces’.3 The census used a classification by occupation, yet even then had to comment that many groups no longer followed the occupation their caste title suggested.

Gazetteers from the late nineteenth century show that

even the śūdras were hierarchically organised. The elite śūdras in Tamil Nadu were the Vellalars, often landowners, village revenue collectors, and businessmen.4 They lived mainly in the districts of North Arcot, Coimbatore, Salem, and Tinnevelly, and came to prominence under British rule ‘by ousting [some of] their Telugu counterparts, the Kapu or Reddiyar cultivators, who had previously migrated into the area’.5 They often imitated orthodox Brahmin religious customs such as vegetarianism and daily ablutions. The Naidus, especially the Balija Naidus, a subdivision of the Kapu, were a Telugu

333

Vaiṣṇava Vellalar group, whereas most Tamil Vellalars were Śaivas.

Below the Vellalars, in the early twentieth century, were

other cultivating śūdra castes such as the Vanniyars in northern Tamil Nadu, southern Andhra, and Mysore (Karnataka), and around cities such as Madras, Kanchipuram, Chengalpattu, and Pondicherry. One of the largest caste groups in Tamil country, the Vanniyars, many of whom were agriculturalists, considered themselves descendants of the vassal chiefs of Pallava, Cēṟa, Cōḻa, Vijayanagara, and other past rulers. Some evidence backs up this claim.6 Alternatively, they claimed to be baniyas (vaiśyas), a twice-born group according to the varṇa classification.

It was the low-ranking and impoverished untouchables,

however, who performed the arduous labour of cultivation such as weeding, transplanting, and harvesting. In south India, they were the servants, even slaves, of landowning masters, both Brahmins and elite non-Brahmins.

The Census of 1901 emphasised ranking, supposedly as

understood by the castes themselves in each locale, although actually as understood by Brahmins and therefore based on gradations of purity and impurity. There had been protests against rankings—the Vanniyars had already petitioned to be counted as kṣatriyas in the 1871 census7—but now many groups sent in petitions to claim the status of a twice-born varṇa. Every caste had its own story about its origin and its own view of hierarchy, in which it was on top. The former outcastes, for instance, claimed to be the original (ādi) inhabitants of the southern land: the Adidravidas.

Even after the government eliminated ranking from later

censuses, agitation continued. So did the notions of pure and impure as the organising principles of caste.8 A 1906 British gazetteer’s description of Tanjavur, for instance, notes that

Brahmans wear the sacred thread, have gotrams, and

their touch or their presence in another’s house, so far

from polluting, confers honour. Their own sanctity is

so sensitive that the approach of a Paraiyan, Pallan

334

or Chakkiliyan … [outcastes or a rank above] … or his

presence in the Brahman street, pollutes them …. On the

other hand, these castes strongly object to the entrance

of a Brahman into their quarters, believing that harm

will result to them therefrom. The upper non-Brahman

castes generally only pollute them by touch, by entry into

their kitchens, or by seeing their food in the course of

preparation or after it has been cooked.9

The author remarks that this description would be characteristic even in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and in some other areas of Tamil Nadu.

In 1901, Thurston published his Castes and Tribes of Southern

India. As he soon realised, the identity of castes in Tamil Nadu was a tangled tale because of the myriad subdivisions (the jātis or exclusive groups into which people are born, intermarry, and inter-dine) and because of the overlapping titles that had become surnames, a situation that arose partly because of group attempts at upward mobility by changing their caste name to that of one with higher status.

A Pillai, for instance, often called himself a Vellalar.

In North and South Arcot districts, Tamil Vellalars called themselves Mudaliyars. Around Kanchipuram, however, weavers used the same name. The title Nayakar and its variant Naicker, which means ‘leader’ in Telugu, was used by people who speak Telugu, such as the Balija Naidus,10 as well as by the (traditionally) lower-ranked Vanniyars. As for the Vanniyars, they sometimes used names such as Varma, Pillai, Reddiyar, and Padayachi (army chief).

Classifying and ranking castes were such difficult

tasks that the British eliminated both from the 1931 census. Nonetheless, argues Dirks, caste became the central category for understanding Indian society; in addition, it established a pan-Indian system that relied on the Dharmaśāstras. Many modern scholars—notably Louis Dumont11—inherited this mindset, Dirks observes, and read it into the past as a pan-Indian system, even though many anthropologists disagreed.12

335

After Independence, the Indian Constitution (1950)13—

with considerable input from Dr B. R. Ambedkar, a Dalit educated at Columbia University in New York and a leader of the Dalits—addressed the problem of social injustice by calling for liberty, equality, and fraternity in its Preamble and special provisions to minimise inequalities in income, status, facilities, and opportunities with special attention to the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. In addition, the Constitution made provision for the reservation of seats in the Central and state governments for ten years to improve the status and well-being of these groups. Further, commissions were to report on progress, and lists of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes were to be created. This did not solve the problem, however. From the 1960s, there was considerable violence, for example, against Dalits in rural areas.

In 1982, an amendment to the Constitution stipulated

specific quotas in public-sector and government-aided universities: 15 per cent for Scheduled Castes and 7.5 per cent for Scheduled Tribes for five years. The amendment was then to be reviewed (it has been routinely extended since then). ‘Efforts to contain the political organization of depressed classes have continued to the present day,’ says Dirks, ‘but it is ironic that the very upper-caste non-Brahman groups who contested Brahman privilege were the ones who sought to keep the depressed classes in their ritual position of inferiority and subservience.’14

In the wake of the study of the plight of other groups by

the Mandal Commission15 and enormous political mobilisation of the groups themselves to be classified as ‘backward’, the Central government in 1990 declared that Other Backward Castes, an administrative term used originally by the Raj for the 3,000 sub-castes of Hindus who had suffered discrimination, would be given 27 per cent of government positions.

The early reservation policies for Scheduled Castes and

Scheduled Tribes had been generally accepted throughout India, including by the elite, as a way to end the well-known discriminatory abuses of the caste system, and so there had

336

not been major protest to the Constitution’s provisions. The introduction of 27 per cent reservations for the Other Backward Castes in the wake of the Mandal report, however, was another matter, for the so-called Forward Castes sensed that their opportunities would be dramatically curtailed. Protests erupted on the streets, with some students burning themselves alive in protest. This did not happen in Tamil Nadu, however, because the Forward Castes were only 13 per cent compared to about 36 per cent elsewhere in India.

The Supreme Court of India subsequently ruled that

reservations cannot exceed 50 per cent of the ‘vacancies’ in public institutions—even if the total number of competing candidates from the designated groups went above that number. In 1992, it also tried to prevent abuse of the reservation system in a ruling called the Mandal Judgment, which said that reservation for Backward Classes was valid, provided that the socially advanced persons or sections of this group, the ‘creamy layer’—those who were no longer disadvantaged because of their parents’ or their own type of jobs, such as doctors, lawyers, IT specialists, and so forth—were excluded.

Like many other state governments, the Tamil Nadu

government has had aggressive reservation policies in public institutions, which means that it has ignored the Supreme Court’s stipulation that reservations cannot go over 50 per cent of the vacancies. In fact, Tamil Nadu has the highest percentage of reservations at 69 per cent now that Andhra (which had 83.3 per cent) has been divided into two states (Andhra Pradesh and Telangana).

Tamil Nadu has ignored the Supreme Court’s ruling on

the reservations. A challenge to Tamil Nadu’s 69 per cent quota by two students—who had been denied admission to a public medical school despite having high scores—went to the country’s Supreme Court, which then requested the Tamil Nadu government to justify its high percentage. The Tamil Nadu government did so, saying that the population of the Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Other Backward Castes was really 87 per cent, and so the special treatment

337

was justified in this extraordinary situation. In 2014, however, the Supreme Court said that a meritorious candidate coming within the cut-off mark of 50 per cent reservation cannot be denied the right of admission.

The debates on reservations continue: What groups are to

be included, in what percentage, and for what opportunity? Some of these debates, for instance, are about reservations for women, others about promotions, and still others about whether new categories should be introduced.
