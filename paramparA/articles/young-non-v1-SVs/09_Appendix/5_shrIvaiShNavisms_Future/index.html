Appendix 5

Śrīvaiṣṇavism’s Future?

It is hard to predict what Śrīvaiṣṇavism might be like in another twenty years. Conceivably, this could go in several directions. One direction is a general decline of Ś r ī vaiṣṇ avism. The reasons that made it a minority sectarian tradition in Tamil Nadu throughout its history might contribute to such a decline. A few reasons, however, are new. People told me, for instance, that Śrīvaiṣṇavism demanded too much of people. ‘The dress of Tamils has changed,’ said K. K. A. Venkatachari.

Men once had long hair; then they started to cut their

hair. Tamil scholars once wore traditional dress; then

they adopted Western dress. Because Western dress is

not allowed in Vaiṣṇava temples, devotees can enter the

temple only up to a particular point. Śaiva temples are

more liberal; one can go in any dress, and so they are

more popular.

Initiation necessitates vegetarianism, moreover. Many people, even Brahmins, however, are no longer vegetarian or never were.

Many among even those Bhāgavatas who admired the

Brahmanical lifestyle noted that this lifestyle had declined in

their own family traditions since the days of their grandparents, albeit for various reasons: moving away from Srirangam, for instance, or a father who was profligate and irreligious, or attraction to Periyar’s anti-Brahminism, or the demands of modern jobs. There is no guarantee that a family’s religious identity will continue into the next generation and after, especially if the forces of secularisation take hold—so far there are few indications of that—or other religions or Hindu sects attract more converts. Even the traditional signs of Brahmin status are disappearing, except in some pockets of orthodoxy.

In addition, villagers complained that they could not find

Brahmin or even non-Brahmin priests to perform the rites of passage, such as marriages and funerals. They also could not find people to chant verses from the Divyaprabandham on these occasions. Nor could they find Brahmin or non-Brahmin priests to officiate at their village temples. Now that the Supreme Court has rejected the idea that temples must accept non-Brahmin priests who have the required expertise, the problem of finding priests to serve the many temples is not being addressed. This too might contribute to the decline of the sect in its incarnations as Te ṉkalai and Vaṭakalai.

Some people noted that despite the fact that Śrīvaiṣṇavas

have travelled about to spread the religion in the villages, the influence is usually short-lived. Others noted that sabhās, charities and trusts come and go, that temples, ma ṭhas and sabhās are mismanaged, that patronage that had supported non-Brahmin musical families has dried up, and that conversions are attracting people away from their traditional religion. Even though there are signs of a new vitality on Śrīvaiṣṇava Internet sites, these seem to attract mainly Brahmins who enjoy sharing their erudition on the tradition. True, people point out that there is also growth in tourist pilgrimage and many new temples are being built, a sign of improving economic conditions in the country. But is that enough?

Śrīvaiṣṇavism’s precarious existence can be attributed not

only to having passed through a century of turbulent times and transformations, but also to a general lack of institutional

structures that speak for the sect as a whole. This is true even for the Te ṉkalai and Vaṭakalai subsects. Indeed, the major institution in Tamil Nadu that addresses issues related to religion might be the Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Department, an arm of the government. Its influence could grow if the Te ṉkalai and Vaṭakalai orientations weaken.

There are other possibilities for the future of Śrīvaiṣṇavism.

One is the status quo, with the locus of non-Brahmin

Śrīvaiṣṇavism based in caste sabhās, which might periodically join cause with other sabhās. At least at this caste level there are deep roots for identity and continuity to counter the negative offshoots of modernity, such as hyper-individualism, consumerism and anxiety.

Another possibility is the formation of ‘new non-Brahmin

denominations’, perhaps a new Śrīvaiṣṇava reform tradition that furthers the original caste-inclusive message of early bhakti religion, the verses of the āḻvārs, and the works of some of the early ācāryas. That in turn would necessitate turning the rhetoric about sectarian unity into social reality. Such a tradition would need the composition of a new Āgama, called, for instance, the Śrīvaiṣṇava Reform Āgama, which would be written in Tamil for easy accessibility and again emphasise the ancient inclusive aspect of the religion. Such a movement would need to create its own traditions of precedent, moreover, with a clear record of its traditions and government registration of its priests, chanters of the Divyaprabandham, and so forth, so that there would be no denominational and legal confusion in the future.

Most religions have had histories of reform movements that

result in new institutions, so this trajectory is not inconceivable. At the moment, however, it is hard to imagine such a development in Tamil Nadu. The non-Brahmin identification as Śrīvaiṣṇava seems to operate mainly at the caste/sub-caste-cluster level, and not at the collective non-Brahmin level. Non-Brahmins, moreover, continue to have their own hierarchies of discrimination. More importantly, a new denomination

would need a visionary leader, one who knows Śrīvaiṣṇava history, theology and languages and can promote and defend the new movement and inspire people to join it. It would need schools to train priests and ācāryas from any caste, at least for the temples in its network. It would need to build new temples as well, which would need patrons, and it would need to make the Śrīvaiṣṇava reform tradition a vibrant religion for many existing village temples and the new ones being built. This is a tall order.

Perhaps people will decide that a more realistic scenario

would be for reform to take place within Śrīvaiṣṇavism itself, first by reintegrating the Teṉkalai and Vaṭakalai subsects, and then by reforming the existing Śrīvaiṣṇava temples, ma ṭhas, and other institutions. Even if the priesthood in many temples remains functionally a Brahmin preserve because of the Supreme Court’s position on ‘precedent’, people might try to ensure that each temple does a better job treating all worshippers equally. They might argue that a step forward would be to unite Śrīvaiṣṇavas by having temples invite non-Brahmin goṣṭhis to form, be registered in the temple and chant near the sanctum at certain times; or better yet, to create additional new ‘registered’ goṣṭhis that would have both non-Brahmin and Brahmin chanters. The Divyaprabandham, after all, is at the heart of Bhāgavata identity and to be excluded from chanting near the sanctum of major temples does not bode well for the long-term viability of Śrīvaiṣṇavism, especially given the strong competition with Śaivism and other Hindu sects onthe one hand, and the possibility of increased secularisationon the other.

In addition, Śrīvaiṣṇavas might propose more internal

temple committees consisting of both Brahmins and non-Brahmins to address complaints of discrimination, and work towards improving relations before conflicts end up in court. If problems are addressed internally, the government would have less reason to interfere in temple affairs and a reconciliation between castes might begin to occur, especially if behaviour is carefully monitored and court challenges remain an option.

Moreover, ācāryas and jīyars with a large number of non-

Brahmin disciples and close connections to major temples might take the initiative in building Śrīvaiṣṇavism as a community of devotees that moves beyond rhetoric towards social solidarity for justice. Teaching non-Brahmin and Brahmin youth together would be important. Significantly, some ācāryas and jīyars, especially those with many non-Brahmin disciples, are already doing this.

K. K. A. Venkatachari was quite adamant that the religion’s

future was in the hands of Bhāgavata Śrīvaiṣṇavas.

Three per cent of the people are Brahmins in South India

and of these, only 1 per cent are Śrīvaiṣṇava Brahmins. If

we do not accept the non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas today, we

have a very small group. Therefore, we need to include

them. In any case, this is the Teṉkalai tradition. Anti-

Brahminism has now died out because the Brahmins no

longer are a force to be reckoned with, although there are

still tensions.

That said, it is up to non-Brahmins to decide for themselves what they want for the future. They have other options and they would need to be convinced that the Brahmin domination of the past would not be reinstituted.

There is one other scenario for how Śrīvaiṣṇavism might

evolve in the future, and that is by being reshaped by the Sangh Parivar, the Hindu nationalist organisations such as the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS) and its affiliated political party, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP). One can see that the Sangh Parivar already has developed strategies that could respond to problems faced by Śrīvaiṣṇavism: (i) by the building or renewal of village temples with village involvement; (ii) through an ideology of caste inclusiveness, with temple committees comprising representatives of various castes and educational institutions that teach everyone; (iii) through social services in the form of charities; ( iv) by networking through pilgrimage tourism, print media and social media; and (v) through an appeal to a Hindu identity, with common enemies being Christianity and Islam. Given Tamil Nadu’s

strong sectarian traditions and the lingering effects of the non-Brahmin movement, the organisations of the Sangh Parivar might work at first within the sectarian traditions in this region to build its sense of Hindu community.

The fact that Tamil Nadu’s political parties, even the DMK,

have been increasingly willing to appeal to people’s religious interests, as have the national parties, suggests that a possible scenario could be greater collusion of politics and religion, not less. Is the fact that the Sangh Parivar has made deep inroads into other regions in India, despite many spirited contestations, a sign of things to come in Tamil Nadu? Sceptics will point to the fact that in the 2019 national elections, the BJP did not fare well in south India. In Tamil Nadu, for instance, the AIADMK– BJP alliance (with Vanniyar support) lost heavily (thirty-seven of thirty-eight Lok Sabha seats went to the DMK led by Stalin, Karunanidhi’s son, which had an alliance with Congress and the support of minorities, a breakaway party from the AIADMK, and the CPI, in addition to its traditional base).

At this point, it seems there is no clear way out of the

turbulence that has characterised the past several centuries.
