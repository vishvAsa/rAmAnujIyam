Tales from Srirangam, Tanjavur and Beyond

K. K. A. Venkatachari arranged interviews for me at Srirangam with non-Brahmin gardeners and Sattadas, as well as one Brahmin ā cā rya with many non-Brahmin disciples, whom I will discuss in the next chapter. Having deep family connections at this famous temple, Venkatachari also arranged for my own private darśana of Lord Raṅganātha— something extremely rare for foreigners. I then understood how the sight of Ra ṅganātha had so thrilled Tiruppāṇāḻvār that he broke into ecstatic song: ‘… My God! His lovely dark body of unfading beauty strung with pearls and big dazzling gems fills my heart!’ (Amalaṉātipiraṉ 9)1.

The focus of my second trip was Tanjavur, both the city

and the district. My guide was Dr B. M. Sundaram, the son of a famous Śaiva musician from this locale and a Śrīvaiṣṇava mother. We relived his family history. In addition, I interviewed several Bhāgavata musicians and bhajana singers. This gave me a better understanding of the link between non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇavas and the musical world of Tyāgarāja and Maharashtrian bhajana traditions.

Gardeners for the Lord’s Garlands

In Srirangam, I met C. Ramachandra, a Naidu, who received his samāśrayaṇam from a Vaṭakalai jīyar.2 Ramachandra is a trustee of the Negamam Zamindar Charity, which helps educate Śrīvaiṣṇavas. He described the charity as follows.

The charity was founded by the Zamindar Rama Krishna

Naidu, my distant relative, who lived at the turn of the

twentieth century and was from Negamam village near

Coimbatore. In the 1870s, the Zamindar founded a garden

called Tirunandavam at Srirangam near the Ammā

Maṇṭapam by the Kaveri river.

The Zamindar was close friends with an ekāṅgi named

Maturakavi after the āḻvār who was Nammāḻvār’s disciple.

This ekāṅgi used to do kaiṅkarya by making garlands

with flowers from this garden for Lord Ra ṅganātha and

Tāyār (the goddess) in the Srirangam temple. In 1908,

the Zamindar bought land at Srivilliputur and started

another garden. The Zamindar had two wives, but no

children. In 1915, he moved with his second wife to

Srirangam, purchased a house and built a r āmānuja

k ūṭam. At this time, he set up a trust in his will to support

the school established by Bhuvanagiri. Today, the trust

is administered by Srirangam descendants of Negama

Zamindar and his wife, some Chettiyars from Negamam

village and some Srirangam Bhāgavatas.

A few days later, I visited the garden at Srirangam. Two gardeners, Sriman Ramanuja Dasar and Sriman Venkattaramanuja Dasar, showed me around. They told me that they were non-Brahmin ekāṅgis who followed the example of Mathurakavi, the original gardener here. They described the religious significance of gardening.

Gardening is called puṣpa kaiṅkarya (service with flowers)

and refers back to the āḻvārs, who often mention worship

with flowers in their poems. We chant the Tiruppāvai

and other short Divyaprabandham works when picking

the flowers. We make twelve garlands daily for Lord

Raṅganātha and the goddess in the Srirangam temple

and many more for the sixty festivals that occur annually

there.3

For me, strolling through the garden and identifying flowers was a welcome respite from the hectic schedule of interviews. It also brought to life the many references in the works of the āḻvārs about gardening as service to God.

Srirangam Sattadas Speak Up

In Srirangam, I went to a Śrīvaiṣṇava bookstore4 to meet some Sattadas. One was K. P. Sundararajan, the supervisor of the Sattadas who serve in the Srirangam temple. As the senior Sattada, he carries the sceptre or silver stick in processions. This is what he told me:

For nine generations, someone in my family has been

honoured by the temple by being taken in procession on

an elephant around the temple. Despite the honours, my

family is very poor. We receive no salary from the temple.

Rather, we serve there for the sake of tradition. I have

four sons. Two are employed outside the temple (one in

the government, another in an electrical company); the

other two work in the temple. The family’s specialty is

making the garlands with leaves in the form of parrots

for Vaiku ṇṭha Ekādaśī and other special days. Two days

of the year we carry the Lord on our shoulders; one is the

procession on the first day of Brahmotsavam.

Nalan Cakravarti Ayyangar at Alvartirunagari initiated

me. Other Sattadas are initiated by the Vānamāmalai jīyar

at Nanguneri or someone in the Parāśara Bhaṭṭar family

in Srirangam.

Today, Sattadas wear the sacred thread elsewhere, but

not at Srirangam in the temple. Sattadas have no Sanskrit

and no Veda. They chant only Tamil for their daily worship

in the home shrine. They once had a Divyaprabandham

go ṣṭhi (a registered group in the temple for chanting), but

not now because they don’t have the sacred thread [this

will be discussed in Chapter 7].

Sundararajan gave me his version of the history of Sattadas at Srirangam. This service, he claimed, began at the time of Rāmānuja. At that time, Sattadas stopped wearing the thread because Rāmānuja said that the daily rituals should be done without it. The next important figure was Kaṇṭātai Rāmānuja Ayya ṅkar.5 He was an ascetic Sattada who served the temple and guarded the place, which is symbolised by an image of him with a knife at one of the entrances to the temple. He was with the deity from morning to night, even in processions. When the temple was closed, he was authorised to lock and unlock it.

I also met R. Andal. ‘My native place is Madurai,’ she told

me, and with great animation, leapt into her life story.

My grandfather had his own temple called Śrīnivāsa

Perumāḷ Kōyiḻ in Koyilpatti, three hours from Madurai.

It remained in the family. Then my father gave it away

when he moved the family to Madurai to improve our

opportunities by starting his own business selling sweets,

crackers, and so forth. It was hard work. By the grace of

God, he now has four savoury shops in Madurai. I am

one of five children. Today, the business is in the hands of

the kids. It is not doing so well. As a result of becoming

business people, no one in my family is now a Sattada

serving in a temple. Nevertheless, my father used to give

monetary help to the temple. This was his kaiṅkarya.

There are two types of Sattadas. Some speak Tamil

and do kaiṅkarya in the temple. They are conservative

and marry only within the community. The others speak

Telugu, generally don’t work in temples and go for better

jobs. I am from the Telugu community. There are now

thirty-six Sattada temple families in Tamil Nadu.

I went to Madurai Kamaraj University and then did

a B. A. by correspondence at Anna University in Tamil

literature. In 1977, I married an engineer who is from a

Sattada family in Salem. Some of my husband’s relatives

are still doing kaiṅkarya, making garlands and living

on their temple incomes. My husband’s family doesn’t

have deep knowledge of Śrīvaiṣṇava texts. Whereas my

teaching my elder brother’s daughter about the tradition.

My eldest son sometimes gives spiritual talks in various

places.

When a woman is pregnant, someone will come and

chant daily, from the third month to the time of birth, the

Mumukṣupati, Ācārya Hṛdayam and Śrīvacana Bhūṣaṇam so

that the child in the womb will obtain wisdom. Now, my

daughter in the United States is pregnant and is using a

CD for the same purpose.

At this point, Andal broadened her discussion to community activities.

Sattadas have an annual gathering in each district of

Tamil Nadu. I give lectures for women at these, as do

some men. The community has not had much inclination

to learn about Śrīvaiṣṇavism, although slowly awareness

and education are developing. When the community

gathers, it holds discussions, arranges marriages and

solicits funding for education. It holds competitions on

the Tiruppāvai and hosts general oratory events as well as

singing by children. It even holds fashion shows. When

any of these functions begin, someone will chant some

āḻvār verses from the dais. I am one of the few who does

this.

Sattadas attend an annual gathering in Tamil Nadu.

It is organised by T. R. Parthasarathy and Raja Gopalan,

who also put out a little journal in Triplicane. At this time,

we exchange horoscopes for potential marriages and do

charity work. The ladies wing is involved in a government

self-employment programme.

Although located mainly in south India, Sattadas have

spread everywhere, both in India and abroad. The All

India Sattada Srivaisnava Mahanatu brings them together.

Narmada Devi, a Sattada who is a medical doctor in Ohio,

collects donations for the community’s uplift. Many

donors outside India have good jobs. Here in India, many

don’t. Sattadas are not politically organised. They don’t

put pressure on the temples for better pay. They have no

union. They just want to serve the temple so that it will

develop.

The next day, I visited the Sattada Srivaisnava Matam; a sign calls it a rāmānuja kūṭam.6 The Srirangam temple procession stops in front, to honour the Sattadas. This Sattada organisation has a president and vice-president and a priest named Gopala, who is also the priest for the Amma Mantapam, a place where various rituals for non-Brahmins are performed, and where Sattadas gather on Vaiku ṇṭha Ekādaśi.

The Musicologist’s Memories of Temple Music

On the Internet, one finds articles about, and interviews with, music vidvāṉ B. M. Sundaram. They include ones originally written for newspapers such as The Hindu and The Indian Express, or the music and dance magazine Sruti. According to his official biography, ‘B.M. Sundaram is a leading historian of South Indian dance and music. Hailing from a traditional family of musicians that can be traced back over 40 generations, he has published extensively on the performance cultures and communities of South India.’7

When I first interviewed him, Sundaram, who was then

over eighty, showed no signs of slowing down, as documented by the public record of his recent accomplishments. His personal story shows a different side to this musicologist, one that is germane to the theme of this book: discrimination. He belongs to the Icai Velala community. First, some background.

As explained by Davesh Soneji, the term icai vēḷāla—

the cultivators of music—indicates a new jāti. As recorded in colonial census reports and ethnographies, Icai Velalas had described themselves as vēḷālas, piḷḷais, and mutalis. Traditionally, the elite of this group were female dancers (devadāsīs) and male musicians (and dance masters) who played the oboe-like nāgasvaram and tavil drum at the great temples of Śiva and Viṣṇu, as well as at marriages of elite castes. Some members of this community, however, had to eke out their living by combining these roles with other jobs.8 As such, there were many sub-groups of varying status.

The next day, we visited Sundaram’s home in Tanjavur. My

questions about the paintings and photos on the wall brought forth memories from his childhood.

My father had two wives. One had five daughters and

one son. That son who became a famous musician was

my half-brother. My mother also had five daughters,

who all died, and one son—me. My father named me

Sundaram, a Śaivite name. My mother was a Śrīvaiṣṇava

from Tiruppernagar.

205

Pointing to a stunning picture of her, Sundaram commented:

She was very fair, beautiful and cultured. My mother

would teach us Divyaprabandham verses, my father

Tevāram ones. People thought that she was an Ayyaṅkar,

a Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava. She even spoke like one. I learned

the Brahmin Ayyaṅkar speech from her. My mother had

had samāśrayaṇam. She chanted devotional hymns in

the morning, visited temples, and went on pilgrimages

to Srirangam and Tirupati. She was not a devadāsī (a

temple dancer), and nor was her mother. The family was

connected with Śrīvaiṣṇava temples because the men

were nāgasvaram players.9 They played in the temple,

for instance, after the priest recited the suprabhātam to

awaken God in the morning.

In the family, only my mother and I were vegetarian.

My father, his first wife, and her children were all non-

vegetarian. My mother wouldn’t cook non-vegetarian

food. Because my father had two wives, there were two

households. When my father was with my mother, he

would have one of his disciples bring food from the hotel.

I didn’t sit with my father for lunch, but with my mother.

My father was very happy that I was vegetarian. He

wanted me to fit into the Brahmin world.

That was also why he wanted me to study Sanskrit as

a second language and be able to recite religious verses.

Once, when he was very young, a Brahmin had insulted

him, saying, ‘Don’t talk to us. We are discussing important

things in Sanskrit, which you don’t know.’ Because of

this insult, my father wanted me to know Sanskrit. My

mother didn’t learn Sanskrit, yet she could recite religious

verses. Her behaviour was just like that of the Brahmins.

They told her, ‘You are more Brahmin than we are!’ Some

priests would even let her go into their kitchen, where

strict purity was maintained.

I wore a dhoti like the Brahmins. My father did not.

Brahmins used to call me ‘Sundaram Iyer’, Iyer being a

Brahmin name. We have only Vedic rituals with Brahmin

priests for weddings in my family. This has been a

tradition from the time of our ancestors. Because of these

Brahmanical influences, many Brahmins to this day think

that I am a Brahmin.

I think that all non-Brahmins should read Sanskrit

and become vegetarian. It would be a positive influence

and would help them control their senses and lead pure

lives. When the body is pure, the mind becomes pure

and service will increase. Discipline is important. The

Brahmins’ way of life is important because it teaches great

In Tamil University (Tanjavur), no Sanskrit is taught.

But in Karnataka, there is a Muslim village on a hill, where

everyone speaks only in Sanskrit. The government wants

non-Brahmin priests. For that they need to be trained in

rituals and Sanskrit.

Sundaram next described how his relatives had been connected with the Srirangam temple.

My mother’s grandfather was a musician who played

at the Srirangam temple. His wife called him a ‘water

buffalo’, because he would go to the temple and spend

the whole day bathing in the Kaveri river. Then, he would

head for the toddy (liquor) shop before coming home.

Another connection with Srirangam was my mother’s

sister’s husband, who was a nāgasvaram player there.

N āgasvaram musicians played during the daily pūjās

(at night, another musician would play the vīṇā). They

also played during the temple processions, when one

nāgasvaram and one tavil were mandatory, and at festivals,

when they were engaged only for that occasion. Generally,

there was only one family of musicians at a temple; they

would receive rice, oil, and food from the temple. Even

if offered more economic benefits elsewhere, they would

stay there. Each temple had a particular musical tradition,

although some compositions were played in most

Vaiṣṇava temples. The temples were mainly interested in

auspicious sounds, however, not composition. The only

ritual honour given to the musicians was offering them

the cloth that had been tied around the deity’s head.

Pointing to a small picture of his father with a large red boṭṭu (sacred forehead dot), Sundaram continued:

My father, Needamangalam Meenakshisundaram Pillai,

was dark-complexioned, a Śaiva, and a meat-eater. He

learned how to play the tavil from his uncle. All the men

in my father’s family were tavil players. At the age of

eight, my father was an accompanist. By the turn of the

twentieth century, he was the most famous tavil player

in south India and brought tavil playing to the stage. His

fame was mainly in Tamil Nadu, because the tavil is a

regional instrument.

Because of The Madras Devadasis (Prevention of

Dedication) Act of 1947, which made the tradition

offemale dancing at the temples illegal, the reputation of

my community was in decline. This Act had criminalised

professional dancer-courtesans. My father helped to

improve its status by introducing the tavil and nāgasvaram

to the concert stage. Unfortunately, he left few recordings.

On the way to Needamangalam, Sundaram’s father’s hometown, close to Mannargudi, Sundaram continued: ‘My father put that village on the map because of his fame. That is why my father was sometimes called Needamangalam. I am the son of a great musician. Today there is mere learning. Before that, there was real education because of one’s father’s expertise.’

Thinking about his family, Sundaram told me more about

his education, experiences at school, and desire to study music.

I did my B. A. in Trichi (Tiruchirappalli) at St. Joseph’s,

which had about 1,600 students. It was very difficult to

gain admission there because it was considered the best

school. The priests kept a very strict discipline. If you

were five minutes late, you had to pay twenty rupees.

Although I wanted to study math and physics, my grades

209

were not high enough, and so I took economics and

history. I liked history, not economics.

I was often teased for belonging to the Icai Velala

community. When my mother would put powder on my

face, my classmates would bully me, saying: ‘Who earned

the powder, your mother or your sister?’ [The implication

was that these professional dancing women earned their

money as prostitutes.] I would reply: ‘If you look like a

Brahmin, then speak like a Brahmin.’ Besides, in the past

Brahmin men had sexual relations with the devadāsīs. If

they had a child, it would be absorbed into the devadāsī

community. Some Brahmin women were even attracted

to the musician vidv āṉs.

I used to hear my cousin being taught music and started

to sing myself. Some people encouraged me to become a

performer. My mother wanted me to be a tax collector. My

father didn’t want his sons to become musicians because,

after the passing of the Devadasi Act, the reputation of

our community had greatly declined. He thought that it

would be impossible to have the same fame and respect

in the future. Even now, people say behind my back that I

‘belong to “that” community’.

The male musicians have fared better than the female

dancers. In fact, now some nāgasvaram musicians don’t

want to give their girls in marriage to families that once

had a devadāsī tradition because of the stigma. My mother

would let me go to listen to music. My father would not

allow even that.

I did odd jobs for Minakshi Sundaram Pillai, a

famous dance master with the same name as my father.

He arranged my marriage with his favourite student

Balachandra, a great dancer who performed in elite

homes in Tanjavur, but not in public because of the new

stigma. She had never been initiated as a devadāsī because

that tradition was on its way out. After our marriage in

1954, she decided not to perform again.

I didn’t tell my friends about my marriage. They would

think that I was not a real student. Once, I took my wife

to a film in Trichi and ran into some classmates there.

They thought that I was dating and went to my house

the girl had a bad horoscope. It forecast that her mother-

Fourteen days after my father died, my elder brother

picked up his drum and started to play. Within three

concerts, he was famous, although he had had no formal

In January 1963, my stepbrother was on his way back

from Chennai where he had given a concert. Drinking

heavily on the train, he fell down when disembarking

When she heard it, she fell down the stairs. After the fall,

Just after my mother died, I went away with my guru,

and now alone. My mother had left all the property to me,

despite vowing not to do so.

Once, my guru said that there were many vocalists but

no researchers in the field of music. I should forget about

a performance career because I could not expect money

and fame. As a researcher, I could become famous and

preserve our musical heritage. Then, my guru reminded

me of the vow that I made to my mother not to be a

performer. My guru knew that my research on music had

begun in high school. On Saturdays and Sundays, I used

to read manuscripts and books in the Sarasvati Mahal

Library in Tanjavur. And so, I devoted myself to research

on music.

I got my first job even before I went to St Joseph’s. It

was in 1951 at the All India Radio, Trichi station, which is

one of eighty-three stations. I had a contract to sing and

compose music. Sometime later, the director of All India

Radio wanted me to be a composer on staff in Delhi and

asked me to apply. I refused. The Pondicherry station

started up, and my guru asked me to apply. This time, I

did. There was a competition to select the music composer,

which I won. In 1980, I was promoted to producer and as

a government employee, I took care of an entire music

On 24 January 2011, we went to the Tyāgarāja festival, held every year since 1903 in Tiruvaiyaru, which is about half-an-hour by car from Tanjavur. ‘The festival,’ commented Sundaram, ‘which usually attracts 20,000 people a day, both musicians and the general public, is of pan-Indian fame and is televised.’ This day, the fourth in the five-day event, was very important because it commemorates the day on which Tyāgarāja attained enlightenment.

After watching the early morning worship on television,

we headed for the samādhi where Tyāgarāja is buried, and where the crowds had gathered. The highlight was the singing of five compositions. According to Sundaram, the musicians sit in front of the samādhi-shrine, competing for the first row in order to be visible on television. Guests sit in a large tent to the side. An image of Tyāgarāja is ritually bathed. Usually, the nāgasvaram players start the music. This year, it was a very popular clarinet player.

Sundaram commented: ‘Because of a dispute, I have taken

a vow never to join the main festivities.’ I waited outside the shrine, while Sundaram went for darśana. On the walls of the room where I waited were written some of Tyāgarāja’s songs.

After we left the festival, Sundaram explained the relation

between non-Brahmins and Brahmins in the music world. ‘Brahmins are usually the vocalists, violinists, mṛdaṅgam players,10 and bhārata-nāṭyam dancers. Those who belong to the Icai Velala community play the tavil and nāgasvaram. Because of the Devadasi Act, no women from our community now dance.’ ‘Why this division?’ I asked, and Sundaram replied:

In part, it is because the Brahmins don’t have the

technical skill to play the tavil and nāgasvaram, which

necessitates a training traditionally found only in the Icai

Velala community. The tavil, which is a very sensitive

instrument, has no pitch, unlike the mṛdangam drum,

and so the tavil artist must have mathematical prowess to

create rhythmic variations throughout the performance.

The tavil player accompanies the nāgasvaram, which is the

view these instruments as polluting. Saliva ends up on

the nāgasvaram, a wind instrument, and hands must touch

In general, Brahmins view the Icai Velalas as low status

and don’t want to be associated with either them or their

any competition from them was completely removed by

Sundaram pointed out that Tyāgarāja did not have this prejudice against non-Brahmin musicians. Even though he was a Telugu Brahmin, he had special relations with two Icai Velala nāgasvaram players: Swami Natham and Vedamswami Natham. They were probably his students. Sundaram continued his saga as follows:

I think that the Icai Velala musical tradition will die out

gradually. Because of their inferiority complex and their

lack of guts to stand up to the Brahmins, they can’t create

their own association. Given these conditions, it would

be a waste of time even to try. Because these musicians

can’t earn a living by playing only for bhajana ma ṭams and

sabhās, Icai Velalas will lose their professional status. In

the nineteenth and early twentieth century, Icai Velalas,

or maybe Naidus, dominated musical performance.

After that, there was a big decline. Today, non-Brahmin

musicians search for other jobs. Many temples have

stopped hiring nāgasvaram players or offer very poor pay.

If they play at weddings, they get much more. Still, the

non-Brahmin music tradition in general will fade.

As we walked the short distance from the Tyāgarāja samādhi to his family’s own private temple, Sundaram reflected on his experience with the modern Brahmin control over the music world.

In general, Brahmin-authored books avoid mention of

Tyāgarāja’s relations with non-Brahmins. The books on

Tyāgarāja had been written by Brahmins, until I myself

wrote one. Brahmins even now do not acknowledge my

scholarship. Take the issue of where Tyāgarāja was born.

They think that he was born in Tiruvarur. I have provided

evidence to the contrary from manuscripts, compositions,

old invitations to the festival, and logistics that it must

have been in Tiruvaiyaru. For example, Tyāgarāja’s father

had been appointed by the Maharaja of Tanjavur to give

daily lectures on the Rāmāyaṇa; therefore, he had to live

a short distance from Tanjavur to travel back and forth.

Tiruvarur is too far away.

We arrived at Sundaram’s family temple. ‘This temple was constructed by my mother’s mother’s father, who was Lakṣmaṇa Piḷḷai, a student of Tyāgarāja,’ commented Sundaram.

Once a year, all members of my family attend a festival

here. Also, for any major event in the family, we go first

to this temple. The deity is Aṅgala Parameśvarī. I don’t

know why my mother’s family had this goddess shrine.

They were considered Vaiṣṇavas who were connected

with the Tiruppernagar temple, a Śrīvaiṣṇava divyade śa.

In fact, they were such strict Vaiṣṇavas that my mother’s

father, after returning late from the temple on Vaikuṇṭha

Ekādaśī, would take so long putting on his nāmam that

his wife would complain. She couldn’t eat until he had

eaten, and he wouldn’t eat before putting on the nāmam.

Maybe the family initially was a goddess family, but

when Lakṣmana Pillai got a job as a nāgasvaram player at

the Tiruppernagar temple, they became Vaiṣṇavas.11

The next day, Sundaram took me to a Naidu musician neighbourhood, the Mutthu Krishna Naidu colony,12 which has about 100 houses. We located the house of Lakshmana Naidu, now an old man, whose family members had been m ṛdaṅgam players for several generations. After introductions, he described his family’s musical tradition.

In the early twentieth century, my grandfather was the

famous singer and mṛdaṅgam player Mutthu Krishnan,

who had studied with well-known musicians.13 The

family was very wealthy at that time and had lots of land

in Tanjavur. Although my grandfather himself had taught

many Brahmins, they refused to acknowledge him when

they in turn became famous. Over time, my family was

marginalised from the music scene. I myself had a mental

breakdown because of all this, and my son has had to take

another job to keep going. He now teaches mṛdaṅgam only

on the side for money or plays the mṛdaṅgam for other

communities’ bhajanas.

My family has had a Śrīvaiṣṇava connection. Mutthu

V ēṅkaṭeśvara is the family’s deity. Mutthu’s son Virasami

used to sing Divyaprabandham verses regularly, as did

other members of the family. This tradition was given up

by my time. I have not had samāśrayaṇam. But the family

goes to the ma ṭha near the first lane of the neighbourhood.

It is like a temple with its own Hanuman festival.

Lakshmana still wore a large nāmam on his forehead. Later, Sundaram suggested that this Naidu music tradition had been influenced by the Tyāgarāja tradition. ‘Perhaps earlier generations were students of Tyāgarāja’s disciples because Tyāgarāja’s compositions were in their musical repertoire. Or perhaps it was the Telugu connection that attracted them to Tyāgarāja. Or his iconic status.’ Hearing about these Naidu musicians reminded Sundaram of the difficult relations that Icai Velala musicians have had with Naidu musicians.

Some Naidu musicians have discriminated against Icai

Velalas. Nevertheless, some Icai Velalas have taken on

the name Naidu, for example, Tirupati Narayana Swami

Naidu, whose mother was a devad āsī. Many Naidus don’t

like their name being appropriated in this way. They call

a man in the Icai Velala community the ‘prostitute’s son’,

even if his mother is married. When I hear this, I ask, ‘Can

you know for sure if your own wife is not a prostitute?’

Sundaram returned to this problem of prejudice against the Icai Velala community many times in the following days. The old insults that devadāsīs were prostitutes haunted him, even though he knew that they had been famous dancers and were wealthy and proud women outside the institution of marriage.

In the following days, we visited many maṭhas around

Tanjavur. These belonged to the Maharashtrian Vaiṣṇava tradition that had come south with migrants after the establishment of Maratha rule in 1675. The new Maratha rulers enticed the Maharashtrian kīrtaṅkars (performers of songs about Rāma or Krishna14) to stay in their kingdom by offering them houses in Tanjavur, Mannargudi, and Konur. These houses became maṭhas and very popular places for worship and musical performances.15 Between 1700 and 1830, the Marathi kīrtana tradition developed into performances with both devotional sermons and singing (harikathās), and performances with song, dance, and dramatic renditions of purāṇic stories accompanied by music. The Rāmadāsi sampradāyas16 helped to popularise this movement.

Of relevance to me was how these kīrtanas and harikathās

became an important part of the Śrīvaiṣṇava tradition, especially for non-Brahmins. The Śrīvaiṣṇava ācāryas and jīyars integrated their tradition into the kīrtana traditions of some Maharashtrian villages. Or the kīrtaṅkars integrated their bhajanas and harikathā performances into those of Tamil Śrīvaiṣṇava communities. ‘The Śrīvaiṣṇava Empār Vijayarāghavacariar of Srirangam,’17 said Sundaram, ‘was the greatest harikathā performer.’

I saw also that the Śrīvaiṣṇava tradition and its link to

the Maharashtrian bhajana tradition in some villages was in decline. One man said that his grandfather, father, and he had had samāśrayaṇam, but now that he was an old man, he had given up its required lifestyle because its orthodoxy was too demanding. Another said that his grandfather had sung bhajanas in street processions, although no one does that now. Young people refuse even to wear the nāmam.

One day, I went with Sundaram to Tirukkandiyur and

interviewed Kulacekara Ramanuja Dasar. First, we visited his prosperous-looking house. Because he was not home, we went to his office near the court building. There, we encountered an imposing businessman with a big nāmam and a big moustache. After introductions, he began his life story.

My parents and grandparents were Śrīvaiṣṇavas. When

I joined the DK and then the DMK, I became an atheist

and started to eat meat. I wanted to have a ‘Member of

the Legislative Assembly’ seat. When I didn’t obtain

one, I reverted to Śrīvaiṣṇavism. My original name was

Bhagavat Singh; I was initiated by Parasara Ranganatha

Bhattar of Srirangam. He gave me the initiation name

Kulacekara Ramanuja Dasar. Since then, I have been

vegetarian. To be called a Bhāgavata, one must not only

be vegetarian, but also wear the nāmam and have the

conch and discus branded on the shoulders. Initiations

for the non-Brahmins of this area are now done by the

Vānamāmalai jīyar in Nanguneri, the Emperumāṉār

Mutt jīyar in Tirukkoyilur, and Sri Chinna Jeeyar in

Hyderabad.

Kulacekara was impeccably dressed because it was his birthday, and he was going to do kai ṅkarya in the form of feeding orphans. He added: ‘I also do kai ṅkarya by feeding devotees three times a day during the twenty-five days of the Adhyayanotsavam festival and on Vaiku ṇṭha Ekādaśī.’ After showing us a carefully prepared ledger with the names and addresses of the Bhāgavatas he invites on these occasions, he remarked:

There are 150 Bhāgavatas is this area. Brahmin

Ayyaṅkars used to give classes on the 4,000 verses of the

Divyaprabandham and on the Śrīmad Bhāgavatam. They no

longer do this. They also used to come to do the death

ritual (the tirucūrṇa-paripalanam or removing the power

of the conch and discus branded on the shoulders).

Although Dāsaris (non-Brahmin mendicants) could do it,

the Ayya ṅkars did it in a more elaborate manner. Now,

they rarely come, and so the death rituals are done by

Bhāgavatas who have had samāśrayaṇam. The sixteenth

day, when the ritual of tirun ārāyaṇa bali is performed, is

especially important. Some of Nammāḻvār’s verses are

chanted. Often a group of Bhāgavatas who know how

to chant them will come from Srirangam. Alternatively,

one Caturvedi Brahmin named Rajaraman Swamikal,

who knows the 4,000 verses, will come and chant. He

is planning to take 1,000 Bhāgavatas to all the northern

sacred places, even the ones in Nepal.

Sundaram adds:

Before the removal of the mudr ās, non-Brahmins sit the

dead person in a chair wearing a garland. Someone

performs a harikathā for the crowd; the story mentions

that everyone who hears this will go to heaven. Musicians

who don’t get many commissions do this for a fee. They

are commissioned also to perform at weddings and other

events such as those at temples.

After interviewing Kulacekara, we went to Ammapet.18 The inhabitants of the Maharashtrian section of this village are silk weavers, who came to this region in the early Maratha period as part of a larger migration of Maharashtrians to all parts of India for the sari market. They are Vaiṣṇavas. Today, some have adopted high-status caste names, such as Iyer, Iyengar, and Chettiyar, and wear the sacred thread.

We found a Rāmaliṅga Swami maṭha. Inside, we saw a

painting of him wearing a white robe, large vibh ūti, and red dot. The maṭha is also used by the villagers for Śrīvaiṣṇava bhajanas. These begin with short Divyaprabandham works, such asāṇṭāḷ’s Tiruppāvai and Periyāḻvār’s Tirupallāṇṭu, and end with Rāma and Krishna folk songs. These bhajanas occur on Saturdays, Ekādaśī days, and festival days.

I noticed at this Saurashtra maṭha that men wore the

Vaṭakalai nāmam, which also adorned nearby houses. In fact, around Tanjavur, I saw more Vaṭakalai influence than Teṉkalai in non-Brahmin communities. The current leader of the bhajanas, who performs the rituals for them, wore a Vaṭakalai nāmam. I heard no discussion of specifically Vaṭakalai

identity, however, and so these villagers probably knew little about sectarian differences. In any case, it was obvious that Śrīvaiṣṇava influence was declining in this village; only three people have had samāśrayaṇam.

The next day, we went to Ammaiyappan, a village very

near Tiruvarur. We found the shrine, also a Vaṭakalai one. It features a large picture of Tyāgarāja and small pictures of bhajana processions.19 At that moment, a small group of men carried Tyāgarāja’s picture in a street procession. We followed them for a short distance.

On the way to Needamangalam, where Sundaram’s father

grew up, the conversation turned to politics and Sundaram’s relation to Periyar. Sundaram began by saying:

Periyar was a Tyāgarājarī Vaiṣṇava Naiyakar. Despite his

avowed atheism, he would do his morning pūjā wearing

the nāmam. When he came out of the pūjā room, he would

wipe it away. When travelling away from home, he

might have done mental pūjā. Periyar’s chief agent was

Ramanuja Iyengar, a Brahmin with a big nāmam. Periyar

initially wanted to ignore caste, but he increasingly spoke

out against Brahmins, at first mildly, as in ‘If you boast

as a Brahmin, be a Brahmin. Don’t do other vocations.

Just do the duties prescribed by the śāstras (religious

texts).’ Then the mild rebukes became harsh. When he

was abusing Brahmins, Periyar admitted that ‘without

abusing Brahmins, I won’t survive’. And then he would

say, ‘If you find a Brahmin, kill him. There is no god,’ and

‘Tamil is a savage king’. After repeating this many times,

he would call his audience ‘sheep’. Still they adored him.

Sundaram remembered a day in 1957. He was staying in a room at a cinema building on Mount Road in Chennai, next to Periyar’s house.

One day a cinema poster was plastered on Periyar’s

house. He was angry. Periyar’s man came, demanding

that the manager go to meet Periyar. The manager was not

there, so he dragged me there instead. I was presented to

Periyar, who was eating his breakfast. He demanded that

the poster be removed. I said: ‘I had nothing to do with

the cinema. I was just renting a room in that building.’

Periyar continued eating, not saying anything. Then he

asked me if I were a Brahmin. I said, ‘No.’ Then he asked

me why I was wearing Śiva’s emblem, the vibhūti, on my

forehead. I said, it is my family custom. My father wore it,

and so did my brother. Next, Periyar asked me if I knew

ask me about the vibhūti, with questions such as: ‘When

should it be mixed with water?’ I could see that he had

considerable knowledge of these matters. Finally, he let

Periyar was a hypocrite because he was the leader of the

Justice Party, which had made atheism a cornerstone of

its ideology, and yet he secretly practised Śrīvaiṣṇavism.

He was politically expedient, moreover, because he said

contradictory things such as, ‘I oppose only Brahmanism,

not Brahmins’. Still, he terrorised Brahmins enough to

bring about social change. For that reason, we have to

thank him.

Sundaram paused and then continued. ‘Many Vaiṣṇavas were attracted to Periyar, but were also afraid of his atheism, and so they would remove their nāmam to hide their religious identity. I myself was never attracted to him.’20 This prompted Sundaram to describe another incident with Periyar.

Periyar’s first wife had died. He then married into the

devad āsī community. This is not widely known even

today. There was a big difference in age between him

and his second wife—he was seventy-seven and she was

thirty—even though Periyar himself had preached against

marriages with a big age difference. This wife’s name was

Mani-ammai. My family knew hers. Mani-ammai told me

that she had been formally dedicated to a temple. One

time, she had a big argument with Periyar. His driver,

C. Cittrarasu, who later became a great orator, told me

about the fight. Periyar had said to Mani-ammai: ‘I have

given you life. I have rescued you from prostitution.’ She

retorted, ‘No, you are such an old man, you have not

given me life; you married me out of your own interest!’

My father was dead set against Periyar because he

abused Brahmins. Brahmins, my father said, patronised

our music and appreciated us. Therefore, we shouldn’t

abuse them. This perspective made many Brahmins see

my father as both a great musician and a great man. In

short, there has been a love-hate relationship between the

Brahmins and the Icai Velala community.

For me, the caste inclusion expressed by the āḻvārs and

nāyaṉmārs is important. It makes the Brahmins appear

hypocritical. It also makes the elite non-Brahmins appear

hypocritical. They don’t treat the Harijans very well.

Sundaram changed the topic and spoke more generally about Hinduism.

To retain Hinduism, equality is good, but we shouldn’t

dilute the religion to globalise it. People are becoming

too materialistic. Chandrasekhara, my guru, says that we

should fly with two wings, the spiritual and the material.

Now, however, with heavy materialism, there is no

balance. This will cause us to fall and bring destruction.

A devotee of Mahatma Gandhi, my father wore only

khadi shirts and fasted, not taking even a drop of water,

when Gandhi died. I myself met Gandhi when I went

with my father to Delhi on 15 August 1947, Indian

Independence Day. My father played for an hour with

Gandhi present. I also used to know Dr Radhakrishnan

when he was President of India. He had seen my father

perform many times in Chennai, and called me the son of

a great Vidvāṉ.

We were momentarily distracted from these reminiscences when Sundaram saw the village temple where his mother used to pray for a child. The conversation moved on. Because the topic had been political leaders, I asked him, ‘Have you ever met Karunanidhi?’ The answer was ‘yes’.

Karunanidhi is much older than I am. His mother is

Anjukam Ammaiyar, from a devadāsī community in

Tiruvarur district, where he grew up. He was at first a

storywriter. He would come to Tanjavur and stay with my

relatives. My mother-in-law’s uncle was Karunanidhi’s

father-in-law. Karunanidhi joined Periyar and became a

politician, and eventually Chief Minister.

As our trip together neared its end, Sundaram took a long look back.

Chandrasekhara is my guru and my god. I met him first

with my mother at his maṭha in Kumbakonam when

I was eleven. I had been told how my mother had had

five daughters, all of whom had died when young.

Chandrasekhara had blessed her, saying, ‘You will have

On 10 October 2006, my music guru M. Balamurali

* * *

Reflecting more generally on the region of Tiruchirappalli and Tanjavur that I had just visited, my thoughts began with Srirangam, called the ‘capital’ of Śrīvaiṣṇavism. Srirangam is famous, after all, because of its antiquity as a sacred place and impressive architecture, rituals, and festivals, the fact that all the āḻvārs had sung about it with the total number of verses amounting to more āḻvār verses about any other divyadeśa (Tirupati being second and the temples of Kanchipuram third), and the fact that it has been a main seat of the Śrīvaiṣṇava sampradāya from its earliest days.

223

Non-Brahmin connections with Srirangam mentioned

to me ranged from a Zamindar trust’s patronage of religious schools and gardens, to Sattada service in the temple, to the many non-Brahmin disciples living not only in the nearby city of Tiruchirappalli, but also villages throughout the greater Tanjavur region and beyond. As I found in other regions, there was a link between the religious centre and the villages, thanks to the proselytism of Srirangam ācāryas and jīyars over the centuries, but that there was now little contact, making Śrīvaiṣṇava identity vulnerable in some villages.

I found that the second major focus for Śrīvaiṣṇava identity

in the general vicinity is Tiruvaiyaru, where the samādhi of Tyāgarāja is located, and the annual festival associated with the famous composer is celebrated. The importance of Tyāgarāja for non-Brahmin Śrīvaiṣṇava identity, especially those who speak or identify with Telugu, cannot be overestimated. I had discovered that his devotional music formed the spiritual core of many religious sabhā events and processions throughout Tamil Nadu. It is therefore not surprising that devotees, not only from the surrounding villages but also from across Tamil Nadu, are attracted to his shrine. The importance of music for religious identity was highlighted in these interviews by B. M. Sundaram’s life story and his family’s connections with traditional music. The life story of Laksmana Naidu highlighted the importance of music too.

My research opportunities for interviews did not take me

directly into the Madurai and Kanyakumari areas, which have their own Śrīvaiṣṇava divyadeśas, such as Tirumaliruncolai, Srivilliputur, and Alvartirunagari, and a powerful maṭha, Vānamāmalai, the history and pervasive influence of which I have already described. But I did glimpse Śrīvaiṣṇava life in this area through the reminiscences of Dr Pandurangan, my own previous fieldwork on the divyadeśas of the area, and my travels there with Sundaram and Davesh Soneji to study Maratha Vaiṣṇava influence.
