Paramatabhaṅgaḥ
Śrīmadācārya has authored a work called Paramatabhaṅgaḥ in
the maṇipravāḷa form of Tamil. In this work he has strongly
refuted the other doctrines and established his own doctrine. In
each of the “bhaṅgas” or refutals he has, in the first and lastTamil
pācurams, briefly presented the subject matter of the respective
Adhikāra. There are 54 such pācurams in all. He has explained
their content in the following manner:

By presenting just the summary of each doctrine and pointing out
its shortcomings, and setting forth his own views on the subject,
the author has made this composition grow and shine brilliantly.
Due to space limitations, we have refrained from discussing the
complete work in great detail but instead explained only what is
contained in the respective pācurams. If one wants to understand
everything in detail, one should see Svāmi Decikan’s works like
“Nyāyasiddhāñjana -Tattvamuktākalāpa”, etc. We have now
presented only what refers to this particular composition.4

even the Jñyāna is also going to exist only for one moment before
perishing, it has to receive the colour information from the object
in the next moment and cognize it in the next moment after that.
This is also impossible, i.e, “Object Realization” cannot occur.
Thus, this theory of the Sautrāntika is rejected in this Adhikāra
as being absolutely unrealistic like a child drinking milk from the
breasts of a mother who has no breasts, or one without a mouth

Once while Śrī Kṛṣṇa had taken with him the cows and calves to
the forest for grazing, Brahma, with the intention of making
himself as well as the world understand the greatness of God,
made all the cowherds, cows and calves disappear. Then, Śrī
Kṛṣṇa, deciding to fool Brahma and also to protect the cowherds,
Himself took simultaneously the forms of cowherds, cows and
calves and carried on in this manner for a full year.

actually became the cow or the calf. His form is distinct from
every object. However, when he appeared to behave like the cow
or the calf, it was not because He became their body. His Svarūpa
is not the same as theirs. When scriptural statements appear to
identify God’s Svarūpa (which is quite distinct from the objects
of the material universe) with the objects of this universe, our64

is only because God has made those objects His Śarīra (body)
and remained inside them as Antaryāmi”. It therefore means that,
unlike as with the Bhaskara-Yāadava doctrine, we do not have to
accept stupidly the mutually contradictory Bheda-Abheda
principle in our doctrine.

Summary:-
The mīmāmsakas do not accept Īśvara; they
postulate that an entity “Apūrva” is created when men perform
rituals like yāgas (sacrifices), which bestow the boons
appropriate for the ritual performed. They do not accept devatās
like Indra etc.; instead they call Śabda itself as Devatā. From this
it follows that in this system, there is no form for the devatās, nor
is there any thing like the devatās receiving the Havis (the
offering in the Homam), or devatās attending at the same time
different yāgas taking places at different places, or the concerned
Devatā getting satisfied with the offering and granting the fruits
appropriate for the ritual performed.82
If one can invent a power called “Apūrva” that is the
Super Master that is propiated by all rituals that can bestow full
benefits for all the good acts performed by every one, then
performing the good rituals as prescribed in the vedas undergoing
enormous physical strain and spending a lot of money actually
becomes useless; thus the people who are advocating all this are
like a person wasting a lot of energy watering a barren piece of
land hoping that it will yield good results.
Our Svāmi Dēcikan, the “Crown Jewel of the ācāryas” has
said that he would first pray to the Almighty God that He may
grant good sense to these mīmamsakas, who have lost their mind
and are saying whatever that comes to their mind; that he would
then refute their doctrine with the help of the very best logical
arguments from Vedānta Śāstra.

Summary:-
At the very beginning of this work, Svāmi Dēcikan
prayed indeed to Śrī Sudarśanadeva only, in his MaṅgaLa Śloka
(Benedictive Verse). Even while endng the work, he prays to that
same Sudarśana Bhagavan, by celebrating the greatness of his
(Sudaraśanadeva’s) sixteen arms in one verse of sixteen lines.
This Sudarśana which is regarded as the “King of all weapons”
shines brilliantly like the personification of the fiery energy of Śrī
Raṅganātha Himself. All the worlds in the universe are protected
by nestling within these sixteen arms of this Cakradeva, like tiny
bells arranged to form a garland. Those arms very much love to
perform eternal service to God like Ādiśeṣa, and extend their
grace and protection to all the bhāgvatas coming seeking refuge.
They destroy the asuras who come to loot the wealth of the
devas. They will dessimate the enemies of the vedas. With their
fiery energy, they dispel the darkness of ignorance that pervades
the world, and keeps them safe. With such being the case, how
indeed is it possible for one to adequately describe the greatness
of Sudarśanadeva?
