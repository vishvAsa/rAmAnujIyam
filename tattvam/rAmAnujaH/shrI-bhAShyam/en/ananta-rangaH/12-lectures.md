Shri Bashya Upanyasa Manjari - Sri Dr. N.S. Anantharangacharya

## Avataranike

Na vedaanthath shastram na madhumathanaath daivamadhikam  
Na thath bhakthaath thirtham na thadhabhimathaath saathvika padham  
Na sathvaadhaarogyam na budhabhajanath bodhajanakam  
Na mukthehe saukhyam na dvayavachanathaha kshema karanam

There is nothing greater than Vedantha shastra. There is no greater God than Madhusudhana. There is no place more holy than the place of devotees. There is no better form of health than the satvik (pure and virtuous) state. There is nothing happier than mukthi. There is no greater utterance than dvaya mantra for wellbeing.

Vedanta means the end of the vedas. It contains the essence of vedas. Upanishads come under vedanta. Upanishads talk about creation, protection and finally dissolution of this universe. It describes how we should understand Parabrahma’s swarupa, do upasana and with his blessings realize Brahman.

There are 2 parts in vedas: Purva Bhaga and Uthara Bhaga.

Purva Bhaga talks about karma, rituals, yaga and the fruits of yaga.

After doing many yagas and experiencing the fruits of these yagas, one realizes that these fruits are temporary in nature and begins to follow jnana marga to attain eternal benefit and worship the sachinanda swarupa of Paramatma. Here jnana comes in the place of karma. Jnana refers to Atma Jnana and Brahma Jnana.

In Katopanishad, Yama Dharmaraya offers the following advice to Nachiketha “Na saamparaayaha prathibhaathi baalam pramaadyantham vitta mohena moodham.”

Due to lack of wisdom, we are unable to have the sight of Atma swarupa and para loka. Due to intense delusional attachment to wealth, without knowing what to do, people are running helter skelter. “Ayam lokaha na para iti maanee punaha, punaha, vasham aapadyate me. They think that this is the only loka, no other loka exists and keep getting caught by me.

In this purva bhaga, people who realize that karma phala is alpa (trivial) and asthira (impermanent) begin to look for phala that is anantha(eternal), shaashvatha (permanent) and sthira (stable) and starts Brahma Jignaasa (enquiring about Brahman). This is mentioned in Vedantha. Brahma jnana is the parama nischaya (definitive conclusion) of Vedas and Vedanta.

Darshana means to see Atma swarupa. Yaajnavalkya advices Maitreyi to seek a Guru and understand Atma Swarupa as told in “Drashtavyah, srotavyah, manthavyah, nidhidhyasitavyah.” One should listen and understand. Then think and reflect on the teachings. Finally do dhyana (deep meditation) and perceive the supreme as though He is in front of us.

Darshana means to have sight of Atma swarupa. Darshana provides a pathway to a human being. Darshana asks human beings to stop doing the inevitable and unavoidable tasks for a minute and think about who are you, how did you come here and where are you going?

Yajnavalkya asks Janaka “kwa gamishyasi?” A traveler is going in a chariot. What is his end point, destination? Janaka did not know the answer. Then Yajnavalkya replies “he is going to a place to see Paramatma who is eternal, blemishness and the embodiment of the bliss of knowledge. This is called darshana.

There are two types of darshanas: Nastika and Astika.

Naastika Darshana: They have not accepted Vedas as authority. They include Jainism, Buddhism and Charavaka (Materialism)

Astika Darshana: They have accepted Vedas as authority. There are 6 types in this category. Sankhya, Yoga, Nyaya, Vaisheshika, Purva Mimamsa and Uthara Mimamsa.

Sankhya Darshana: This is the foremost darshana. They talk about the transformation of prakruthi and say that one finally attains kaivalya which is different and distinct from prakruthi.

Yoga Darshana: Sage Pathanjali came up with yoga sutras. He accepted all tatvas(principles) mentioned in sankhya darshana and included Paramatman along with them. He said one should get atma sakshathkara (self-realization) with the blessings of Paramatma.

Nyaya Vaisheshika Darshana: These two darshanas are grouped together. They use nyaya (logic) to determine everything.

It is said that one gains moksha from tatva jnana (knowledge of the principles.) With the principles of pramana(means of knowledge) and prameya(object of knowledge) nature of realities was established with valid reasons. We identify ourselves with our sharira(body). We should overcome dehaatma bhrama (false perception of identifying self with body), worship Ishwara and realize self.

Purva and Uthara Mimamsa Darshanas: 

Purva Mimamsa: Mathum itcha mimamsa. Vedas mention how to perform rituals and the fruits for these rituals. Vedas talk about different yagas like Darshapurna Maasa, Agnihothra, Aswamedha etc. One who wants to go to swarga(heaven) should do Jyothishtoma yaga. Only those who seek this fruit should do this yaga. It is not mandatory for everyone. Purva Mimamsakas established karma through kriya (procedures).

Uthara Mimamsa: They use Upanishads as the authority. They talk about how one should learn about Atma and Brahman and realize Brahman. This is not possible by just doing karma.

Parikshya lokaan karmachitaan braahmano nirvedam aayaath. No matter how much punya we accumulate, they get used up. Whatever we earn from doing karma is alpa (trivial) and asthira (transient). One can stay in swarga loka (heaven) as long as his punya (good deeds) quota is available. Aaye dukham, vyaye dukham, rakshane dukhameva cha. There is suffering involved in earning, spending and protecting money, so one gets dukkha or Nirveda. kruthena karmana - Earning money etc. does not help to realize self and Paramatma. It does not provide lasting happiness. Hence, even if it is delayed, one should try to get Atma jnana. One should go to a Guru and seek his advice. This is the fundamental reason to learn Vedantha.

Ithihasa Puranas (ancient Indian epics and narratives) talk about Brahma swarupa (essential nature of Brahman), what should an aspirant seek, how should one seek etc. If we think for a moment about who I am, I am not Indriyas, Manas or Prana. Then who am I? Aham Atma na dehosmi Vishnu sheshaha aparigraha. Nothing is related to me. Once we realize this, we begin to wonder who we are. What is Parabrahma Swarupa?  What is the upaaya (means/method) to realize Brahman? What are the impediments in this process? How to overcome them? What is the prayojana (purpose) of this goal? These five questions are called artha panchaka. All these questions are answered in Uthara Mimamsa.

Uthara Mimamsa means Vedantha Mimamsa and Brahma Mimamsa. Acharyas and Rishis would provide advice on these matters to students in the form of sutras which are in a concise form of just two or three words. These sutras are not available in any other literature. It is available only in Sanskrit literature. Sankhya sutras, yoga sutras and mimamsa sutras are examples of sutras. Sutras help to recall the advice of Acharyas. Alpaaksharam, asandigdham saaravath vishwato mukham. Ex: There is a sutra “A AA” (in Ashtadhyayi) and there is a commentary on this sutra. It is difficult to understand the meanings of sutras. We should try hard and learn about these sutras. Jaimini wrote sutras for Purva Mimamsa and Baadaraayana for Uttara Mimamsa.

Baadarayana looked at the intricate aspects of Upanishads, explained and drew conclusions in a logical way. Each sutra is backed by a statement from Upanishads.

As time passed by, due to ashakthi (incapacity) to understand these sutras, and unavailability of Acharyas, Bhagavad Ramanujacharya came up with a commentary on the Brahma Sutras called Shri Bashya and provided the highest level of service to mankind. We are able to study Vedas and Dharma sutras because of this. He talks about other Acharyas and their opinions too. He talks about Baadari, Audulomi etc. Many sutras are not available to us. We only have Baadarayanaa’s Vedanta sutras now.

Brahma sutras are divided into 4 adhyaayaas (chapters). Each adhyaaya is divided into 4 parts called paadaas(quarters). There are 16 paadaas in total. Each paada contains sutras. The number of sutras is the same for all commentators. There is no difference in the alphabets of the sutras either. Vedantha tatvas (principles of vedas) are expounded using these sutras as reference.

Prasthaana Traya: Prasthaana means prakarsha sthaana (superior standing). Traya means three. Shruthi Prasthaana, Smruthi Prashtaana and Sutra Prashtaana.

1. Shruthi Prasthaana: This is the foundation for everything. Tatvaas are present in Upanishads. The language of the Upanishads is difficult to understand and complex. We need to understand them with the commentaries.

2. Smruthi Prasthaana: Bhagavad Gita is a smruti sourced from Upanishads. This is called Smruthi Prasthaana and specifically Gita Prasthaana. Sarvopanishado Gaavaha. All Upanishads are cows and the milk drawn from these cows is Bhagavad Gita. Whatever advice is provided in Upanishads is explained in a simple way by Krishna Paramatma.

3. Sutra Prasthaana: Sutras are developed using Shruthi and Smruthi. There are 545 sutras that are categorized into 4 adhyaayaas (chapters) and 16 paadaas(quarters).

 Many Acharyas have written commentaries for these sutras. Shankaracharya’s commentary is the earliest commentary. Ramanujacharya’s commentary came later. In between there were commentaries from Bhaskara known as Bhatta Bhaskara Bhashya. Yadava Prakasha wrote a commentary too but this is not available. Madhavacharya’s bhashya came much later. The commentaries that came later are influenced by Ramanuja’s bhashya.

Advaita, Dvaita and Vishistaadvaita use sutras as their source material. Darshanas were developed using shruthi, smruthi and sutras. People have professed their doctrines using these sutras. There are lots of commonalities in these doctrines. Shankaracharya came up with Brahmasutra Bhashya; Bhaskaracharya proposed Bhedaabheda Bhashya and Ramanujacharya presented Shri Bashya.

Shri Bashya:

The specialty of this commentary is that it was developed with the thought that the universe is real, Jiva is real and everything is real. Ramanujacharya wrote based on what he realised and experienced. He has formulated the darshana in an authentic manner. He did not mislead anyone.

People refuted other’s doctrines and came up with solutions. This is how many doctrines came into being based on kriye (action) and prathikriye (reaction). Finally what stands are the important tatvas (principles) proposed in these darshanas.

Shankaracharya talks about vruthikara (specific commentator who wrote a commentary called vrutti) and so does Ramanuja. Ramanuja talks about Bodhayana vruthikara. There is a bhedha(difference) between Jiva and Paramatma. Paramatma is the prabhu (Lord) of Jivatma. Since Bodhayana vruthi (extensive commentary) was very vast, purvacharyas condensed it. But it was condensed so much that people could not understand. Then Ramanujacharya came up with Sri Bashya which was neither too vast nor too brief and easy to understand. It was written in agreement with what was mentioned by Bodhayana and purvacharyas. Tanmataanusaarena suthraaaksharaani vyaakhyaasyanthe. He stated that he will formulate as mentioned in the sutras without any distortion.

This Bhashya is like a vast ocean. It has been presented here with limited time and knowledge.

There were two bhashyas before Sri Bhashya: Shankara Bhashya and Bhaskara Bhashya.

Ramanujacharya examined these two bhashyas and evaluated if their doctrines portray what is mentioned in the sutras and offered his opinion. His opinions are based on pramana (evidence). Most importantly we have to understand what is Paramatma swarupa, Jivatma swarupa and Prakruthi swarupa as mentioned in the Brahma sutras. How should Jivatma attain Paramatma? What is the upaaya (means or method) to reach the goal? What are the obstacles? These topics are discussed and Brahma swarupa is determined based on sutras. Adhikaranas (sections) mention what is written in the sutras.

Athasmin thath buddhihi. Pramana (means of knowledge), prameya (object of knowledge) are all adhyastha (super imposed) and nirvishesha Brahma (undifferentiated consciousness) is the reality according to Shankaracharya. Ramanujacharya does not agree with this stand. Adhyaasa Bhashya was written by Shankaracharya and he first defined what adhyaasa is. What is unreal is being superimposed as real. Only nirvishesha Brahman is real according to Shankaracharya. Ramanujacharya says think about this independently and seek truth. In respect of vyakarana (grammar) sutras it is said as aadesha. It has to be followed as told whereas this is jignaase (inquisitiveness) and we should enquire based on pramana (pramana includes pratyaksha (perception), anumana(inference) and shabdha (Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita etc.)

Ramanujacharya says the following:

1. There is no contradiction between karma kanda and jnana kanda. Both together is one shastra.

Karma kanda talks about the swarupa(nature) of yagas. This refers to various rituals and procedures that are modes of worship of Devathas and Bhagavan. All yagas, no matter what is said in the yagas constitute the worship of Bhagavan only. When we do sankalpa (resolution) we say Bhagavath kainkarya rupena bhagavath preethyartham. Hence there is no contradiction.

Also, there is no contradiction between karma kanda and jnana kanda. Jnana kanda talks about the swarupa of Paramatma. Upanishads talk about the swarupa of Paramatma who is worthy of worship. Karma kanda talks about the various modes and ways of worshipping Paramatma. So, there is no incompatibility. We should understand that both these together constitute one shastra.

2. Jivatmas are different from Brahman. This is Ramanuja’s doctrine. There is a sutra that states “na itharaha anupapathehe”. It is not possible to say that there is identity between Jivatma and Paramatma. The universe is not false. The universe is real, Jiva is real, Jiva and Brahman are different. The most ancient Alwar says “avan avane ivan ivane.” 

In some places, identity is mentioned, sarvam khalvidham Brahma, tatvamasi etc. But when bheda(duality) is mentioned in several places, it is not correct to say that it is sopadhika (limiting adjunct). In order to support some phrases, we cannot ignore many phrases. When we come up with a sidhantha, we should be able to explain all sutras using mukhyaartha (primary direct meaning).

Even Shankaracharya has to accept Ishwara because creation should happen. He said Ishwara is apara Brahma (lower or manifested aspect of absolute reality) and is shubhopadhi (auspicious limited adjunct). Apara Brahma possesses aishwarya (wealth), jnana (knowledge) and vairagya(dispassion). But he is sopadhika (limited adjunct). Only pure consciousness is real.

Akshara, antaryami and jiva are mentioned in Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Shankaracharya says akshara is paramatma, antharyami is shubhopadhi (auspicious limited adjunct) and jiva is ashubhopadhi (inauspicious limited adjunct). He says that one tatva has 3 avasthe (stages).

Ramanujacharya opines that it is not correct to disgrace Ishwara by saying Ishwara is apara Brahma. Two Brahmas are not there. Para Brahma is Para Brahma only and everything else is apara (different).

Secondly, when talking about mokshopaya (means/methods for salvation), it was opined as aikya (identity) in three ways.

1. Nishprapancheekarana Niyogavaadha: In Gita Bhashya, Shankaracharya says everything should be superimposed (aaropa) and removed. We cannot say “idam Brahma” because Brahman is avigneya (cannot be known) and abaadhitha (unrefuted). When everything is Atma, there is no jnana(knowledge)- jneya(to be known) division.

2. Dhyana Niyogavaadha: It is not enough to listen to the statement” tat tvam asi.” One should do dhyaana and realize it.

3. Shankaracharya talks about vaakyaartha jnana - aparoksha jnana (perceptible knowledge), This does samanvaya of above two opinions. When one listens to the verse “tat tvam asi” since one has had the samskara, he is able to realize the true meaning.

Ramanujacharya does not agree with the above three points. He stresses that one should do upasana (meditation). Bhakthi (devotion) and upasana are the pathways to moksha. One needs upasanathmaka jnana (devotional meditative knowledge).

Avidya does not mean devoid of vidya. It is bhavarupa (positive entity). Shankaracharya says Jiva bhava (individual soul) arises from this. Ramanujacharya comes up with sapthavidha anupapathis (seven objections) like swarupa anupapathi etc to Shankaracharya’s doctrine of avidya. There is no source for avidya with this nature in sruthis. Sruthis do not accept that avidya has this kind of power. Avidya means karma, says Ramanujacharya.

Shankaracharya talks about two different levels of existence: sopadhika (having an upadhi or adjunct) and paaramarthika (highest level of reality). But Ramanujacharya does not accept the concept of vyavaharika- sopadhika (worldly plane of existence is because of limited adjuncts). According to Ramanujacharya, everything is real. Brahma sutras expound the concepts of Brahma swarupa, Jiva swarupa, Prapancha swarupa and Moksha swarupa. It states that one finally goes in archiraadhi gathi (luminous path to the divine abode of Brahman) and attains the attributes of Brahman.

Sribashya has sections called adhikaranas. Adhikarana means vishaya (subject). Each adhikarana includes sutras on a certain topic. Five aspects are addressed in it. ViShayaha vishayaschaiva purva pakshaha tathottaram sidhanntaha. All these agree with nayasutras.

There are five components in a proposition:

1. Vishaya/Paksha/Sadhya: Subject

2. Hethu: Reason

3. Drushashtaantha: Example

4. Upanaya: Relating drushtanta to paksha

5. Nigamana Sidhaantha: Conclusion of the doctrine.

All upanishad verses that may raise doubts are studied using these five components and meanings are established conclusively. Hence, they are called naya sutras. Since they propound para-Brahman they are called Brahma sutras.

Shaareeraha para Brahmaath thath pratipaadakam shaastram shaareeraka shaastram. All Acharyas talk about the first four sutras in detail. They are called chatussutri.

    1. Athaato Brahma Jignaasa: (Then, therefore, an enquiry into Brahman should be made).

One should add injunction and say “Brahma Jignaasaa karthavyaa.” There were different opinions on this.


Athaha means ananthara (afterwards) and everyone agrees with this. Athaha also signifies auspicious. After what? Shankaracharya says after sadhana chathushtaya such as understanding what is nithya and anithya etc. one should do enquiry of Brahman.


Ramanujacharya says karma vichaaraanantaram - first one should enquire into karma as mentioned in purva mimamsa. After realizing that the fruits of these actions are alpa and asthira (little and impermanent), ataha (for that reason) one should do Brahma jijnaasa.

2. Janmaadhyasya Yataha (Brahma Lakshana): Janma aadi asya yataha. Brahman is that from which the origination and others of this universe happen.

Purva Mimamsakas say that everything should be denoted in action. Otherwise, there is no meaning for shabda. Vedas do not do bodha (instruction) of Brahman. Brahman is already siddha vasthu (realized). It need not be realized. The Karma (action or rituals) part is important for purva mimamsakas. Upanishads are like a desert for them. They say in order to qualify to do karmas only one should understand Atma swarupa (Brahman’s nature). Karma eva Mimamsakas. They down played the role of Brahman.

 Ramanujacharya says the nature of Brahman is asya (this universe) janma aadi (cause of origination, sustenance and dissolution of this universe) is Brahman. Brahman is the cause for creation, sustenance and dissolution.

    3. Shastra yonitvaath: Brahman can be known only from the scriptures because scripture is the source of the knowledge of Brahman.

Nayyayayikaas (logicians) said one can establish Brahman from inference. But Ramanujacharya states as per the third sutra that one can know about Brahman from Vedas, shastras and upanishad. Yato vaa imaani bhuthaani jaayante is the vishaya vakya (verse that is the subject of discussion) for this sutra.

4. Tat tu Samanvayaat: Connection with the ultimate end of man.

What is the prayojana (benefit) of knowing Brahman who is the cause of this universe?

Purvapaksha (prima facie view): One need not study about Brahman. Shabda does not teach Brahman at all. Brahman does not have a definition. Knowledge of Brahman is not purushartha (goal of human life). There is no benefit in studying about Brahman.

Ramanujacharya says samanvayaath means anvaya (connection) is there as stated in Katopanishad. “Tadh Vishnoho paramam padam” after traversing a long path, one reaches the holy destination called “parama padha”. One who is samanaskaha (balanced mind) and sadhashuchihi (always pure) will reach this parama padam (supreme abode). This is clearly mentioned in Katopanishad.

The Acharyas wrote elaborate commentaries for the above four sutras and expounded the prominent aspects of their doctrine. They have to answer these four questions. Why should one make an inquiry of Brahman? It is because when we understand Brahman, it brings peace of mind.

Shankaracharya talks about adhyaasa bhashya in Jignaasaadhikarana. First, we should understand that everything is adhyasa (super imposition of the unreal on the real). Then all descriptions are understood by individuals as sopadhika (limited adjunct).

 

When Ramanujacharya talks about this, he portrays pradhana prati tantra, sharira shariri bhava. Paramatma is Atma, shariri for everyone.


Everything is one, there is one tatva says Shankaracharya. Ramanujar says everything in one Brahman is the only tatva. This is vishishtavaadha Advaita (qualified non dualism). Names which denote sharira also denote Jivatman and that inturn denotes Paramatman who is the antaryami. Sharira vishishta - Jiva vishishta is Paramatman. This is the doctrine of Vishishtadvaita.

Mokshopaaya (means for liberation): Upaasana of Paramatma is the path to Paramatma. This is the absolute path. In maha purvapaksha - maha sidhantha, he demonstrates swarupa of Paramathma. Laghu sindhanta is about upaaya swarupa (the means) and mahaa sidhanta is about upeya swarupa (the goal).

Purushaartha (ultimate goal of human life): Reaching Paramatma is the ultimate goal of human life. Just like how Yajnavalkya tells king Janaka, attainment of Paramatma is the supreme benefit and this is portrayed in samanvayaadhikarana.
