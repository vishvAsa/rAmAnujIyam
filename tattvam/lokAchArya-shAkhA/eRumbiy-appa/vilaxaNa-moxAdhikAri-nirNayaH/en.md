**Vilakshana Mokshadhikari Nirnayam**



Obeisance to Sri Ramanuja.

Determination of the distinctive persons qualified for moksha.

"I bow to Devaraja Guru, auspicious, the giver of divine knowledge, who is like a bee at the lotus feet of the great yogin, the noble bridegroom."

"I take refuge in the true preceptor Samarapungava, the abode of virtues such as sama and dama, whose mind is unmoving at the final stage, who is like a bee at the lotus feet of Yativara."

The good disciples beginning with Senapati Alvan, joining their hands, bowing at the feet of Erumbiyappa, who was a disciple of Srimad Varavaramuni, requested: "For us, your servants, who desire moksha, please graciously determine the nature of the distinctive ones who are qualified for moksha."

Then Appa said: "What is there now for us to determine in this matter as though it were something new? Is it not something that all the sastras, both general and special, the Gitacarya who is known through the Vedas and Vedanta, the rsis, the Alvars, and the acaryas have all made clear, like a lamp placed on the peak of a mountain? Even so, for the benefit of the world, I shall submit the special meanings of the words of our acarya in response to the question you have asked. Listen."

Determination of the distinctive mokshadhikari:

The Lord, the consort of Sri, the one whose auspicious qualities are opposed to all defects, the Lord of all, absolutely independent, and supremely compassionate, is one alone. As it is said, "He has become the many living beings of the earth, and delights in this play through manifold bewilderments." Thus, through many kinds of deluding acts, He remains playing with the conscious beings in His lila-vibhuti. When His divine will arises, "Let me uplift certain rare persons from samsara," then, according to the statement:

"When the jiva, standing in the wheel of samsara, wandering because of his own karmas, and afflicted by sorrow, is seen, a certain compassion arises in Vishnu,"

that conscious being is in the wheel of samsara and, through the punya and papa he has himself acquired, is undergoing the sufferings of birth, death, falling into hell, and so on. Seeing the distress he is undergoing, compassion arises toward him. And, as it is said:

"Not through purushakara, nor through any other cause; solely by My own will do I, at some time, look upon someone,"

He performs a special glance of grace through His own causeless compassion, like a king and the elephant fit for royal consecration.

And as it is said:

"The person upon whom Madhusudana casts His glance at birth is to be known as sattvika; he becomes one who thinks of moksha,"

by that glance which arises at birth, he becomes firmly established in supreme sattva, and the desire for moksha is born in him. But, according to the statement, "By knowledge, moksha; by ignorance, samsara," that moksha is something obtained only through knowledge of the fivefold truth, extending up to practice. Therefore, in the absence of that, only the sorrow of samsara in the form of repeated birth and death grows further; there is no way to be saved.

Therefore, as the sruti says:

"For the sake of knowing That, he must approach a guru alone, with fuel in hand, one who is srotriya and established in Brahman. To him who has properly approached, whose mind is pacified, who is endowed with sama, that knower teaches in truth that Brahma-vidya by which one knows the imperishable true Purusha,"

that mumukshu-jiva, for the sake of knowledge of reality, must approach a true acarya: one who knows the essential meaning of the Vedas and Vedanta; one who is, preceded by detachment from everything other than the Supreme Self, exclusively established in the experience of Parabrahman.

That acarya, after thoroughly testing the mind of this person who is endowed with qualities of the self such as sama and dama, should teach him the knowledge that is the means to attaining Brahman, and should make him clearly understand the practices corresponding to it. Then, obtaining these, he becomes one who has accomplished what had to be accomplished, becomes qualified for moksha, and is saved.

In this sruti, in the places where it says "whose mind has properly become tranquil" and "endowed with sama," the two qualities, sama and dama, are only examples among the qualities of the self. Therefore, by mentioning these two, all the marks of a true disciple are indicated by implication. Likewise, where it says "srotriya" and "brahma-nishtha," these two expressions are only representative indications of the marks of an acarya.

What, then, are the marks of a true disciple?

As it is said: he is the one who does not think that body, life, house, field, sons, wife, and such things exist for his own nourishment or benefit; rather, he makes them all subordinate to the acarya. He has no separate effort of his own; he remains absorbed in the acarya's thought, fit to be bought and sold by him.

And as another text says: when a disciple has arrived who possesses these qualities - his intellect inclines toward true matters; he is connected with the inquiry into reality and with the corresponding practice; he has the desire for knowledge of tattva; he is firmly devoted to service to the guru; he is without self-conceit; he performs pranipata, namaskriya, and service; he waits for the proper occasion; he has restraint of the outer and inner senses; he cannot bear separation; he has taken refuge; and he has firm trust in the sastra - then, after examining him, the acarya should give him instruction in the truth.

Thus, hita-upadesa - instruction that is truly beneficial - should be given to one endowed with the marks of a true disciple.

One should not accept just anyone in this manner.

For the sake of one's own fame, worship, material gain, and so forth, some teachers go about attached to other deities, other sastras, and other subject-matters. They gather worldly samsarins, converse with them, and, as it is said, "speaking what merely remained in one's own mind," they abandon the words and practices of the ancients. They speak meanings that appeared to them through their own slight understanding, acquire wealth and other benefits from those people, and wander about in that way.

Of such an acarya, it is said: "The sin committed by the patron remains attached to the wealth." Since the defect of the conscious being clings to his wealth, it is also said: "The disciple's sin belongs to the guru also." Through the wealth received from an unqualified disciple, that disciple's sin rises up against him. Joined with his own sin and that person's sin, it will overtake and destroy him.

Therefore, just as connection with a true acarya is to be accepted, connection with a true disciple too must be regarded as something to be accepted only when it is distinctive. One should accept only distinctive persons.

For this reason, it is well known that even Emperumanar, who is celebrated as the giver of moksha to former and later acaryas, transgressed the word of his guru and, in the temple of Tirukkottiyur Nambi, publicly revealed the meaning of the Tirumantra to all the Srivaishnavas who desired it. Because of that offence, he determined that evil destiny was certain for himself. Yet, by the connection of his true disciple Kurattalvan, moksha became as though held in his own hand; it is famous that he put on the ochre garment and danced. As the verse says: "Having resolved that transgressing the guru's word would bring evil destiny to himself, because he could not bear the suffering of others, the author of the Bhashya spoke the mantra."

Thus, just as connection with a distinctive disciple is salvific, connection with a non-distinctive disciple ends in harm. Connection with a good son becomes the cause for lifting up the father and his ancestral line; connection with a son given to wrong conduct, because of the prohibited acts he performs, becomes the cause of destruction for the whole ancestral line, as the sastra says.

And the relation between disciple and acarya is itself spoken of as the relation between son and father: "For he gives birth to him through knowledge; that birth is the best birth; for mumukshus, the guru is indeed the father."

Therefore, the connection of a disciple who proceeds in what is harmful, through the accepting of his wealth, becomes the cause of every harm and ruins the guru. If even disciple-connection itself is dangerous in this way, there is no need to say separately that connection with a guru who lacks knowledge and practice is a cause of destruction. It is said: "Having reached a guru devoid of knowledge, how could one attain moksha?" Such a connection is declared to be a cause of harm.

Therefore, only connection with the supremely distinctive one - one who is marked by true sampradaya and by the marks of a true acarya - becomes the bridge to moksha and carries one across.

As it is said: one should seek as desika the one who is established in the true sampradaya; who has sastric understanding; who is srotriya and brahma-nishtha; who is established in sattva; who is truthful in speech; who is joined with proper discipline and pure conduct; who is free from arrogance and envy; who has conquered the group of sense-objects; who is enduring; who is compassionate; who corrects slips; and who is devoted to the good of himself and others.

Accordingly, the acarya is one who is established in the true sampradaya; one who possesses sastric understanding; one who is free from the mistaken thought that the fruit of instruction is worldly advantage and the uplift of disciples in a merely visible sense; one who has understood the purport of Vedanta; one who is pre-eminent in exclusive steadfastness toward Bhagavan.

He is not like those under the control of the three gunas, who experience elation and depression in pleasure and pain. As the Gita says: "He who is the same in pain and pleasure, established in himself, to whom a lump of earth, stone, and gold are alike, who is the same toward the pleasant and the unpleasant, firm, equal in blame and praise, equal in honor and dishonor, equal toward friend and enemy, abandoning all undertakings - he is called one who has gone beyond the gunas."

Thus, he is one who has the sattva appropriate to one who has gone beyond the three gunas. He speaks according to dharma, according to justice, and according to what has truly been seen. He possesses food-discipline, association-discipline, and conduct-discipline, by means of the sadhvi-vrtti that accords with the conduct of the earlier acaryas. He is free from deceit, ostentation, envy, and the like. He is not attached to sense-objects such as hearing, seeing, and embracing what is pleasing.

Even if the disciple abandons him, he does not abandon the disciple. He is supremely compassionate, unable to bear the suffering of another. When the disciple slips, he is capable of restraining him - even, as it were, tying him by the neck and begging - so that the disciple does not proceed into what is harmful. He conducts himself with such care that prohibited conduct does not appear in him even through negligence. He is beneficial to himself and to others.

A disciple who takes hold of such an acarya and acts according to his command becomes most beloved to Bhagavan and becomes liberated. As our acarya has said: "When one has reached a guru who possesses both knowledge and practice in fullness, the Lord, the consort of the honey-filled lotus Lady, Himself grants Vaikuntha."

The acarya's being established in true sampradaya means this: as it is said, "by the flawless course of conduct," beginning with Alvar, who is the root-standing point of the prapannas, and continuing down to one's own acarya in the flowing succession of the lineage, no connection with any false doctrine has entered; he remains faultless in that lineage.

His being free from the fault of mistaken understanding regarding the fruit of instruction means this: he does not think, "The fruit of giving instruction is that visible benefits such as the disciple's worldly uplift and association with him will be achieved." Rather, he thinks, "This person must become fit for mangalasasana in the matter of Bhagavan." He does not look upon himself as "the acarya" and the disciple as "my disciple"; he regards him as a fellow-brahmacarin.

Discipline regarding food means this: rejecting what is defective by kind, defective by source, and defective by occasion, he accepts only those things that are pure, specially received, and clean, and that serve the nourishment of Bhagavan and the bhagavatas.

What are defective by kind? They are the prohibited substances spoken of in sastra as things to be abandoned. Those who eat such sastra-condemned, inedible things go to a state of eating filth, urine, and worms.

Now one may object: "But Perumal, who is the model of conduct, is described, at the time of separation from Sita, as giving up meat and honey. Since it is said that he avoided meat and honey only then, does it not appear that he enjoyed them when united with her? And since he is said to be the one who establishes dharma for the world, should not the dharma practiced by him in his incarnations for the establishment of dharma be accepted?"

The answer is this: Perumal was established in common dharma. Therefore he accepted such meats as were appropriate to the kshatriya lineage, and such honey as kings accepted; but he did not take meat and honey contrary to his own varna-dharma. For those who live according to varna-dharma, some things are to be abandoned and some things are to be accepted according to the nature of that dharma. But for the paramaikantins, who are abundant in sattva, since the sastra says, "having cast off both punya and papa," everything other than the experience of Bhagavan becomes something to be abandoned; it cannot be called something to be accepted.

Substances that are prohibited even for brahmanas, who are abundant in sattva, and whose violation requires severe expiation, are not even to be thought of by pure-sattva-nishtha paramaikantins, who are not defined by distinctions such as village and family, who stand beyond all varna and asrama distinctions, and who are to be designated only as servants of Hari.

Nor is the accepting of such prohibited substances forbidden merely because of varna. They are condemned because they belong to prohibited acts connected with other deities, external agamas, and ways contrary to the Lord's exclusive service. Therefore, even if Bhagavan in His avataras worshipped Rudra, Indra, Aditya, and others, it does not follow that those whose selves have been wholly offered to Him, together with Piratti, may worship other deities.

Further, if the actions connected with avataras were to be treated as direct examples for ordinary practice, then since Rama slew Ravana, who was born a brahmana, even brahma-hatya and the like would become acceptable. Likewise, by following Krishna's avatara-conduct, violence toward others, contact with others' wives, and taking others' wealth would also become acceptable. But that cannot be so. Otherwise even great sins would have to be practiced as the dharmas of prapannas.

If one says, "In the first word of the Charama Sloka, in order that the word 'all' should not be restricted, should the abandonment of dharmas not include even the dharmas of abstention from prohibited acts?" - this too has already been set aside in the Mumukshuppati by considering oneself, Isvara, and the fruit. If prohibited conduct were taken as included in the meaning of the Charama Sloka, then contempt for guru, mantra, and deity, wrong understanding of meaning, and contempt for prapatti itself would follow. Since prapatti is free from being either an upaya performed by oneself or an apaya to be avoided by oneself, such contempt would become a cause of ruin.

Therefore, as the Gita says that food which is stale, impure, and unfit is dear to tamasic people, impure things are to be abandoned by those established in sattva. Things are to be accepted or rejected according to the gunas only for embodied beings whose actions are under the three gunas.

One might ask: "For those who understand all things as having Brahman as their self, according to 'All this indeed is Brahman,' can anything be something to be rejected?" The answer: only after one has been freed from prakrti, which consists of the three gunas, does the whole lila-vibhuti become fit for experience. Even while still in prakrti, persons such as Alvar, who received faultless knowledge, and Prahlada Alvan, who had direct realization, saw the Lord in all things. For them, even things such as poison, weapons, and fire became favorable. They were equal toward all because, in the Supreme Self Govinda, who is the self of all beings, there is no distinction of friend and enemy.

Alvar sang, "Shall I call Him the one of matchless fame? Shall I call Him the earth of peerless excellence?" and, "He is all things and all persons," and, "He is poverty and wealth, hell and heaven," and, "He is water, earth, fire, and wind." In these ways he realized that the Lord pervades the elements beginning with earth, the things made of those elements, and all conscious and unconscious beings, and that all of them are modes of Him and subordinate to Him. But he did not enjoy impure and rejected things as though they were directly to be enjoyed as Brahman.

When Alvar prayed, "Come so that I may see You," and the Lord showed the cosmic form, one may ask, "Can he not sustain himself by seeing that?" The answer is: just as all the wealth of a king, even his prison-house, may be considered the father's wealth by the prince, so too the worlds, the deities within them, heaven, hell, the fields of enjoyment, and the punya and papa that cause them are all understood as having no independent existence and as being modes of the Lord. But beyond that, the cosmic form made of prakrti - a body full of impurity, a thing to be rejected, a veiling of the true self, and born of wrong knowledge - is not something enjoyable to him like the Lord's radiant divine body.

Therefore Alvar, in the Tiruvaymoli beginning "as water, as earth," prayed that the Lord should come with His extraordinary divine form, bearing the sharp discus and white conch. Though Alvar could realize all things as having Bhagavan as their self, in the state of experiencing Him like Piratti, the nitya-suris, and the liberated ones, he still rejected prakrta objects, beginning with the mother's breast milk, as things to be abandoned. He declared, "The rice I eat, the water I drink, the betel I chew - all is Kannan," and understood that He alone is support, nourishment, and enjoyment. Unable to bear seeing samsarins who live by prakrta things, he prayed for release from the body, saying, "Do not show me this cruel world,"

"What is this worldly nature?" and "End my dealing with these forest-like people; command me, O Lord." Thus one must not say that rejected prakrta things are acceptable for a mumukshu on the ground that they have Brahman as their self. Therefore, things opposed to knowledge are among what must be abandoned. For those such as Alvar, because the Lord had granted faultless, unclouded knowledge, support, nourishment, and enjoyment could all be Him alone. But for other mumukshus, only objects in which sattva predominates are fit to be accepted. Hence things defective by kind are to be abandoned.

Defective by source means things connected with those who are fallen, impure, candalas, or egoistic in the wrong way.

Defective by occasion means things arising from occasions such as sraddha and similar rites, and things spoiled by hair, leftovers, improper contact, and the like. Rejecting these three kinds is food-discipline.

Discipline regarding association means this: when one sees those who have bodily self-conceit, those attached to other deities, other goals, and other means, those who regard themselves as independent, and chief opponents of the truth, one should be afraid to approach them just as one would be afraid to approach snakes. One should instead live with true persons from whose very form knowledge and practice can be received as a gift.

Discipline regarding following means this: one should not crowd at royal gates for the sake of earning wealth and other such things, following samsarins. Since visible results depend on karma, one should not be anxious for them, but should be wholly intent on following the true acarya.

Thus, one who possesses both knowledge and practice in fullness is the one fit to be called a true acarya. One without knowledge is like a blind man; one without practice is like a lame man. Only when both are present do he himself and the one who has taken hold of him reach the shore. As it is said: "Just as the movement of birds is by means of the pair of wings, so Bhagavan Hari is attained through knowledge and action together."

It is said: "Abandon dharma and adharma; free from upaya and apaya, remain in the middle state. When there is connection with upaya and apaya, your firm state is diminished." Taking such statements to mean that, for a saranagata mumukshu, both injunction and prohibition are to be abandoned, some, being confused by rajas and tamas, are unable to determine things as they truly are. As the Gita says, when the intellect knows dharma and adharma, what is to be done and what is not to be done, wrongly, that intellect is rajasic; and when, covered by tamas, it thinks adharma to be dharma and sees all things in reverse, that intellect is tamasic. Such a person lives in contrary practice and teaches the same to others.

Against this, it is said: "Sruti and smrti are My command; he who transgresses them acts as a violator of My command. Though he be My devotee, he is not a Vaishnava." And again: "One dear to Vishnu, one devoted to the Lord of gods, should not transgress Vedic conduct even in the mind." Just as even a favorite of the king becomes unfavored if he transgresses the royal command laid down for the good of the world, so too one who transgresses the Vedic boundaries established by the Lord becomes displeasing to Him. Therefore, by crossing the Vaidika limits that are forms of Bhagavan's command, and by engaging in acts contrary to them, such a person becomes displeasing to Bhagavan. He destroys himself, destroys the disciple who has come to him, and causes others who see him also to fall, some by imitating him and some by being unable to bear what they see.

Such a one regards himself as acarya, desires fame, gain, worship, and following, gathers disciples who are themselves acting wrongly, accepts the wealth polluted by their bad self-conceit, and maintains his body by that. This is not the conduct of a true acarya.

The true acarya is otherwise. He examines and follows the words and practices of Nathamuni, Yamunacarya, Yativara, Kurattalvan, Govinda Bhattaraka, Lokaguru, Varavaramuni, and the other elders. He does not transgress the boundaries laid down for the uplifting of samsaric beings in the divine prabandhams of the Alvars, who received faultless knowledge, nor in the rahasya works such as Srivacanabhushana, which reveal the meaning of those prabandhams. He thoroughly abandons false practices opposed to Veda and the ancient authorities. For the sake of the uplift of disciples and sons, and for the protection of the world, he performs the prescribed karmas accepted by the sistas, but without any thought that they are the upaya; he casts off that upaya-buddhi as one would cast off association with great sins, and acts in the proper way.

He thinks of himself as dependent on the acarya, thinks of the disciple as a fellow-brahmacarin, and thinks of the fruit of instruction as the disciple's becoming fit for mangalasasana. The good disciple established by him submits everything he has to the acarya, thinking, "All this is subordinate to the acarya," and remains dependent. The acarya examines all of it and gives it back to him, saying, "Since your self and all that belongs to you have become dependent on the acarya, use them properly in matters of Bhagavan and the bhagavatas, and in maintaining your body." Acting according to that command, the disciple applies them as appropriate to Bhagavan, the bhagavatas, and his own bodily maintenance.

The acarya himself, having no possessiveness, thinks, "You have taken as Yours even the self and possessions that were mine," and when wealth that has been offered even a little is not to be accepted, he lets it go; or else he uses it in service to Bhagavan, the bhagavatas, and the acarya. As it is said, "A prapanna should live, as long as life lasts, by rta and amrta," and "rta is known as gleaning; amrta is what comes unasked." Therefore he lives by uncha-sila and by wealth offered without asking by pure persons established in sattva. He has compassion toward the disciple, dependence toward his own acarya, and distaste regarding maintenance of his own body. He is not one who serves arrogance, wealth, and desire; he accepts only what is needed for the sake of the world. If, because of prakrti-vasana, some lapse arises in the disciple, he grieves, corrects him according to his true nature, does not separate from him, and proceeds with his eye fixed on the disciple's svarupa. Such a one is the acarya.

This acarya's function is of two kinds: ghatakatva and upayatva.

Ghatakatva means this: regarding those who are fit to be corrected and engaged in service, he examines their heart for a year, or half a year, or three months. When he sees that their prakrta-vasanas have subsided, that they are true seekers, that they have no other goal, and that they are devoted to mangalasasana, he joins them to Bhagavan, makes them perform sarana-varana, and instructs them so that they live according to their svarupa.

Upayatva means this: there are children, the mute, the dull, the blind, the lame, the deaf, and others who lack the knowledge and detachment needed to understand when special modes of conduct according to svarupa are taught, and who lack the strength to practice them. Yet Bhagavan's causeless glance may have fallen upon them; detachment from samsaric karmic activity may have arisen; fear of the sorrows of birth, old age, death, and so on may have arisen; they may have become distressed. Seeing such persons, the acarya, moved by compassion, understands that they are not fit for instruction in knowledge and practice. Therefore, for their sake, he himself performs sarana-varana. Just as, when a nursing infant incapable of taking medicine becomes ill, the mother herself takes the medicine and removes the child's delusion through her breast milk, so the acarya takes responsibility for them and, by the greatness of his tirtha, prasada, and service, makes them cross beyond samsara.

There is an incident: seeing that a mute man was distressed, Emperumanar, filled with compassion, showed him his holy feet and by hand-sign instructed him to place them in his heart. The man placed them on his head. Seeing this, Kurattalvan lamented: "Alas\! We were born in the Kura lineage, studied the sastras, and were ruined. Had we too been born mute, we would have obtained Bhagavan's acceptance."

Even though such persons are unfit for knowledge and practice, they are still conscious selves, capable of being the basis of pravrtti and nivrtti. Therefore, the removal of pratikulya must occur. The pratikulyas are: ahankara and mamakara; harm to others; blaming others; praising others wrongly; taking another's wife; taking another's wealth; and other such acts; foods and drinks that harm the self, that increase rajas and tamas, and that are not to be eaten or drunk; attachment to other deities, other means, other goals, and other objects; offences against Bhagavan and the bhagavatas; and the like. If these are not removed, even the abhimana of a true acarya will not protect.

If a young crop is watered as much as one can, and if proper water is lacking, it may dry up before fruiting. But if weeds such as red amaranth grow tall, take root deeply, and spread, then even abundant proper water cannot save the crop. So too, if hostile tendencies grow, acarya-abhimana will not bear fruit.

One may object: "Is it not said that for all embodied beings, whether fit or unfit, the auspicious path is only the word of the true guru, just as the alchemical pill makes gold out of both kinds of base material without distinction?" The answer is: in that example, the fuel-stick and the stone are unconscious things; their becoming gold depends on the sentient agent and on his power. Likewise, if this person becomes subordinate to his own acarya's command, does nothing of his own, and becomes wholly dependent on the true one, then the defect that he lacks knowledge and practice is removed, and through connection with the true acarya he becomes liberated. But if hostile conduct exists, it bears fruit without fail. Even the king, though he had connection with Kurattalvan, who is the very ground of the marks of disciple and acarya, went to ruin through offence against a bhagavata. Therefore, for one who performs prohibited conduct, it is not possible to be saved merely by saying, "I have connection with a true acarya."

If one asks, "But is it not said that whether one is pure or impure, one becomes liberated?" - that concerns previous conduct. As it is said, "Whether one was a great sinner, or bound in another way, or one who had done meritorious acts, like Pundarika, by obedience to the acarya one becomes liberated; therefore one should be subject to the acarya's word." Thus, whether before samasrayana one was sinful or meritorious, if one takes refuge in the true acarya and becomes firmly turned toward him, then the punya and papa done before are destroyed; like Pundarika and others, such persons become liberated through connection with the true acarya. That is all that is meant.

Likewise, when it is said that even one without conduct, having formerly committed all sins, should take refuge in the Lord with faith until death and should be known as faultless by the power of the Supreme Self, the word "formerly" is decisive. The same is the meaning in the phrase "whoever they may be, if they possess the wealth of association." If, even after taking refuge, one deliberately performs prohibited acts, then the taking of refuge itself is absent. Therefore one cannot remain bound to samsara, identifying the body as the self, attached to wife, sons, and so on, inclined toward sense-objects, joined with attachment and hatred, and still, with wrong self-conceit, think: "I have true acarya-sambandha; I have Bhagavat-sambandha."

Therefore Tirukkottiyur Nambi said that, for a prapanna, the seed of samsara must be destroyed. The thoughts "I am the self in what is not the self" and "what is not mine is mine" - these alone are the seed of samsara. These two are the root of every harm. As long as these two remain, there is no way to think that samsara-sambandha has been destroyed and that attainment of Bhagavan will come, however great the adhikari may be.

It is with this meaning in mind that Alvar said that one should uproot "I" and "mine," together with their very root, and then join the Lord. Even if one performs Bhagavad-asrayana, if these two seeds of samsara remain, it will not accomplish the result. Therefore, one must uproot them together with their taste and latent impressions, and only in that opposite state perform Bhagavad-asrayana.

Even if ahankara and mamakara, and the faults that arise from them - attachment to wealth and pleasure, offences against bhagavatas, and so on - are not actually present, still, merely because one remains in a body that is capable of producing them, the great ones became troubled and prayed for their removal. Nammalvar prayed in this way: "I remained saying, 'I, mine'; will You show many worlds and still destroy me? Will You still ruin me? These five senses, which destroy even the desire to serve the Lord of the dwellers in heaven - what will they not do if they obtain me here on earth? Grant me the boon that will destroy the five, which bind one in the terrible pit of karma beginning with family; show me the way by which I may be saved." Likewise the great acaryas and our own acarya prayed for the removal of pratikulyas. From this it is shown that Bhagavat-sambandha and sadacarya-abhimana do not become effective where there is contact with pratikulya.

Therefore, since disciple and acarya are both fellow-servants, each should watch over the other. If improper conduct arises on the disciple's side, the acarya should restrain him and correct him. And since the acarya too is still connected with prakrti, if some lapse arises in him through negligence, then, according to the rule "one should instruct the guru in private," the disciple should, in solitude, hold his holy feet and submit beneficial words. He should pray to Emperuman: "By Your special glance, let this bad thought in him be purified; let him see purely with his eyes." In this way each must remain vigilant, seeking the other's good.

For such a qualified mumukshu, marked by the signs of a true disciple and joined to a true acarya, Bhagavan gives His own attainment, as it is said: "He Himself will grant Vaikuntha."

For conscious beings who take hold of an acarya, knowledge, firm resolve, love, and proper conduct are needed for the joy of the acarya's face. Within these, removal of pratikulya is needed both for those who do not know and for those who are not yet liberated, and also for those established in acarya-abhimana. But for births such as animals, trees, and plants, since there is no scent of pratikulya, mere abhimana toward them is salvific.

Once, when Nampillai was travelling from Perumal's temple toward the temple, he crossed a long distance and became troubled by hunger, thirst, and heat. A banyan tree stood there, spread wide and leafy, giving shade sufficient to remove the fatigue of the elders and of himself. All sat beneath it and rested. There they had food offered to Perumal, ate themselves, stayed there that night, and the next day prepared to continue the journey.

Then Nampillai looked at the elders and said, "See how this has removed all our afflictions - hunger, thirst, heat, and so on. What have we done for this?" Saying this, he cast a special glance upon it and embraced it with both holy hands. A brahmana from a nearby village saw this and went away laughing. Nampillai then went on to the temple. That night, with a great festive sound like that described in "Sul visumbu," while the brahmana who had laughed and all the villagers stood watching, a divine radiance appeared in the sky. As they were all wondering at it, that tree split into two with a great roar; a divine light came forth, rose into the sky, and merged into the radiance of the sun. Those who saw it were astonished. When they asked what this meant, the brahmana told them how Pillai had cast his glance upon it. Hearing this, all were filled with wonder. The next day, when Pillai heard of it, he performed the proper honoring rites and had sacred recitation conducted. So the Jiyar used to relate.

One should not object: "This flawless purushartha must be attained by a person endowed with sastra and anushthana; how can such an attainment come to an immovable being?" The Supreme Lord is endowed with divine power, utterly independent, and supremely compassionate. If He thinks, "By My abhimana I must uplift this self," anything can occur. Karma, jnana, bhakti, prapatti, and acarya-abhimana - all these are accepted by Him as some pretext, so that there may be no excessive extension without distinction. When He decides to accept someone, He accepts. As it is said, "You thought of me in Your mind"; His very thought is what uplifts.

After bestowing the first causeless glance, He purifies the person through turning toward Him, association with the good, instruction from a true acarya, knowledge of the meaning of the mantra, and practice corresponding to it. He makes the person's pleasure and pain depend solely on union with and separation from Himself. He makes everything other than Himself appear almost like hell. Just as a person intensely attached to a worldly beloved suffers states such as longing, thought, wakefulness, emaciation, shame, madness, and fainting when the desired object is not obtained, so too this adhikari, being utterly turned toward Bhagavan, reaches the states sung by Alvar: longing to see Him, eyes and mouth drying up, the mind fixed on Him alone, tears swelling, standing bewildered, saying "I have no one but You," and so on. In that way the Lord makes him fit to experience Himself, the one who is opposed to all defects and who is the sole mass of auspicious qualities, and joins him to Himself.

So too, in Ayodhya, when Rama wished to take with Him all moving and unmoving beings, there was no cause on their side. The beings of Ayodhya were made to have pleasure and pain only through union with and separation from Him, without distinction between moving and unmoving. Alvar therefore sang that, without leaving out even grass, ants, or anything else, all the moving and unmoving beings dwelling in good Ayodhya were raised to the good state in the realm obtained by Brahma. One should not doubt whether this can happen in this vibhuti. The omnipotent Lord, possessing the capacity to accomplish the impossible, when He wills to employ someone in His lila, acts according to karma, removes what cannot be removed by that being's own effort, and when the flood of grace breaks its banks, even the most sinful obtain these states and are saved.

When He thinks, "I shall gain this person," the beginningless karmas that obstruct attainment cannot obstruct Him; they fall away by themselves. He makes that person perform good acts and causes him to attain Himself. The sruti too says, "He Himself makes him perform good karma whom He wishes to raise from these worlds." Alvar says that the wondrous Lord made the sweet poem spoken by Alvar's words into His own praise, spoke through His own words without defect, gave poetic power, and saved even "me, the incomparable sinner." This shows that, for those whom He thinks to uplift, He enters within, regulates them so that they know what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted, and makes them liberated.

Therefore, since none of these matters can be known except through the instruction of a true acarya, He brings the person within acarya-abhimana, produces the corresponding knowledge and practice, and saves him.

If one asks, "Are such adhikaris not rare in this age?" - yes, they are indeed spoken of as rare: "Vasudeva is all - such a great soul is very rare," "One among many knows Me in truth," and "If one or two exist, that is all." Therefore, those who have performed acarya-varana, bear the name and marks of panca-samskara, and yet remain opposed to the meaning of the mantras - holding bodily self-conceit, servitude to others, self-independence, ahankara, mamakara, hatred, desire, anger, pride, jealousy, attachment to sons and family, competition with one another for wealth and pleasure, and conduct unfit for service - such people will still remain for some more days as objects of the Lord's lila.

But if, at the time of the body's fall, they have become detached from pratikulyas and have become true persons, then, just as water in a river shows its marks, the marks of a liberated person will be visible to all. Then one may infer, "This is the final body." It is also said in the Prameya-ratna that, because bhagavatas do not go to the realm of Yama, even if one was undisciplined, there is no attainment of Yama's world.

A certain Srivaishnava once came to Periyavaccan Pillai and asked, "Svami, are we objects of lila or objects of grace?" He replied: "If we are attached to the body, we become objects of lila. If we are attached to the self, we become objects of grace." The meaning is this: if, with bodily self-conceit, one nourishes the body with oil, ghee, and the like, becomes inclined toward sense-objects, is deeply attached to bodily relations such as wife and sons, does not submit to sastra, and wanders as one pleases, such a person becomes an object of lila. But if one is connected, at least a little, with what accords with the nature of the self; knows the truth of the artha-pancaka; knows what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted; abandons all pratikulyas opposed to one's svarupa; sees this body, which cannot simply be cast off, as one would see a deadly enemy; does not nourish it; accepts regulated food only as support; submits to sastra; and acts without ego - such a person becomes an object of grace.

Therefore, one becomes fit for moksha only by performing sadacarya-varana and by becoming endowed with the knowledge and practice of the mantra-meaning taught by that acarya. The adhikaris who perform this acarya-varana are of two kinds: the first prapanna and the final prapanna. The first adhikari, according to what his own acarya has taught, is utterly dependent on Bhagavan and exclusively turned toward Him, like Alvar, who said that women with fish-like eyes, great wealth, good children, noble mothers and fathers - all these are He, and also as it is said, "Vasudeva is all."

The second adhikari is like Madhurakavi: the extraordinary greatness of Bhagavan's matter, taught as a favor by the acarya, has entered deeply into his heart. Thinking of the great favor by which such an utterly distinctive object was given to him, he becomes bewildered, asking, "What can I do in return for this?" He knows no other object; he is far removed even from the scent of other deities; for him the acarya is mother, father, beloved, and everything. For both these kinds, apart from difference in degree, all the special qualifications beginning with removal of pratikulya are required.

But then, one may ask: How is it said that Bhagavan-asrayana is difficult, while this acarya-asrayana is easy?

The meaning is only this. Even for persons who are not fit for ordinary instruction - those who lack the knowledge to understand when a matter is explained, and lack the strength to practice it - the true acarya's conduct can become effective. This is because the acarya is not merely one known through sastra, but one directly accessible before the eyes. If the disciple proceeds into what is harmful, the acarya does not merely look at his taste and approve it. Rather, he restrains him firmly until the end, corrects him, and makes him fit to be used in the way desired by Bhagavan. It is only in this sense that the service rendered by a true acarya is said to be unfailing.

It is not possible to say: "Even if a person is capable of being disciplined, and even if the true acarya disciplines him according to the way taught in sastra and by the elders, still, if he does not stand in that discipline, but lives lazily and according to his own will, the connection will nevertheless accomplish the result." If one said that, then it would follow that even pratikulyas such as offence against bhagavatas do not matter. That would contradict the words and practices of the earlier acaryas.

When Bhagavan's matter is called "difficult," the meaning is this: He cannot be seen by ordinary sight; even when He comes to be seen in the arca form, He does not open His mouth and speak a word. If this person, through bad latent tendencies, becomes inclined toward sense-objects and proceeds in what is harmful, Bhagavan may look at that person's taste, give permission accordingly, and grant fruits in accordance with karma. To Bhagavan, both the person fit for His enjoyment and the person fit for His lila are possible; if the person becomes subject to sastra and qualified, He joins him to enjoyment; if he transgresses command and proceeds wrongly, He employs him in lila.

But the true acarya does not abandon his dependent disciple so that he may become fit only for lila. His task is to make him fit for enjoyment by Bhagavan. If a king sees a courtesan who is diseased and commands a physician, "Make her fit for enjoyment," the physician does not simply apply outside decoration - turmeric, saffron, sandal, collyrium, garlands, garments, and ornaments - while the disease remains within, and then send her to the king saying, "She is fit." If he did so, the matter would end in danger for both. Instead, he gives proper medicine, places her under the required regimen, nourishes her well, and only after she becomes free of disease does he present her as fit.

So too, one's own acarya, being dependent on Bhagavan, must make this conscious being fit for the Lord who is opposed to all that is rejectable. If, while the defects called ignorance, karma, and latent impressions remain inside this person who has long wandered as servant of another, the acarya merely produces the external name and marks and then offers him, thinking "that much is enough," it will not bear fruit without danger for both. Therefore he must properly correct him and offer him. That is why connection with a true acarya is said to give the fruit surely.

It is told that Emperumanar, seeing a man called Arangamaligai who had connection with him yet lived inclined toward sense-objects, examined him carefully, taught him the eighteen chapters, made their meaning enter his heart, and by grace made him detached, without regard even for food and sleep. Therefore it is true that even one who is undisciplined can be saved.

This is the meaning of the song beginning "There is no sweeter place even in heaven." Amudhanar says, in effect: "If, after declaring Emperumanar as refuge, he would leave us in our own state and allow us, through our own sensual tendencies, to wander in evil ways and enter heaven and hell, then what use would that be? Since he corrects us so that we proceed in the right path and accepts service from us, we need not be anxious for the attainment of Bhagavan."

Moreover, even if some slight defect occurs unintentionally or through negligence in one who is a prapanna of Bhagavan, it is said that the Lord, being omnipotent, utterly independent without any restrainer, and supremely compassionate, may consider even a mere friendly disposition sufficient. He may argue even with those who oppose Him, place them behind Him, and save them. But the acarya is a conscious being dependent on Bhagavan. Therefore, just as a goldsmith, when he performs service for the king, places the gold in the fire and purifies it before presenting it to the king, so too the acarya must turn this person away from prakrta enjoyment, make him fit for Bhagavan's enjoyment, and produce in him all the required ornaments of a prapanna.

If the disciple remains dependent on the acarya's command, the acarya casts a special glance so that he may be joined to Bhagavan's matter. But if he becomes hostile, then the matter becomes otherwise. Therefore, the one who is an acarya-prapanna must possess adhikari-sampat - the full qualification of a true adhikari - in an especially high degree. Bhagavan is more pleased with the adhikari established by a true acarya than with one He accepts merely by His own independence, because such a person knows well what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted, and serves in a way pleasing to Him. Hence He Himself has said: "My love is greater toward the devotees of My devotees." Therefore the final prapanna must be endowed with distinctive practice.

Before such an adhikari contemplates the three rahasyas, he must possess the marks of the Vaishnava adhikari described in the Mumukshuppati, beginning with: "letting go, together with their taste and latent impressions, the attachments that have arisen." These are the marks: father, mother, wife, children, relatives, fields, friends, wealth, cattle, kin, and all such things must appear like fire and be abandoned together with their taste and latent impressions; all these must be taken as belonging to Emperuman; when such abandonment and acceptance have arisen, he must remain convinced that the fruit will not fail; he must be eager for attainment of the goal; while still in this body, he must have attachment to the divine places; he must spend his time in experience of His qualities and in service; he must look upon distinctive persons of this kind as visible before his eyes; he must be firmly established in the three mantras; he must live in association with the acarya; and so on.

Now, the persons qualified for the Alvars' divine utterances are jivanmuktas. Who are they?

It is said that one who has taste for prakrta sastra, one attached to household possession, one absorbed in food, clothing, women, and such things, and one interested in worldly sinful action is not fit for moksha. The one fit is the one who has abandoned these, who does not mingle with samsarins, who remains inwardly solitary, whose mind is firm in disciplined conduct so that not even Isvara can shake it, who is detached from external sense-objects, who is engaged in inquiry into the sastra concerning the self, who is not harmful to living beings, who is supremely compassionate, whose inner qualities such as sama and dama are complete, who is compassionate toward all beings, who is detached from sensual pleasure, who is filled with knowledge, who remains unshaken and tranquil even if cruel samsarins insult him by harsh words or acts, who has no rigid craving that only luxurious things must be enjoyed, who accepts pure things merely as necessary for maintaining the body, and who has no elation or depression over the gain or non-gain of desired results. Even if such a one lives in a house, he is called a jivanmukta. Pinpalagiya Nayanar declared in the Guruparamparaprabhavam that only such an adhikari is fit for the experience of the Alvars' divine utterances.

A prapanna, whether reciting mantras or prabandhams, must perform the recitation together with contemplation of their meaning. Emperumanar and Alvar have both taught this: "Contemplate the meaning of the Dvaya in solitude," and "Those who can recite this decade daily are nectar to the dwellers in heaven." In the case of the upasaka, japa, homa, and such acts protect by the power proper to those practices. But in the case of the prapanna, they protect by revealing their meaning: they produce discrimination between what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted, make him abandon the rejectable, and make him accept the acceptable. Protection means making him a fit recipient of the Protector's grace.

Since even a prapanna remains connected with prakrti, things opposed to his true nature may enter. When such ailments arise, he must contemplate the meanings of the words of the mantra in which the remedies for those ailments are present, and he must remove them. If he does not remove them, then, as before, ignorance alone will bear fruit.

If the thought "the body is the self" arises, he must contemplate the meaning of ma in the mantra and remove it: "Am I not of the nature of knowledge and bliss? Am I not distinct from body, senses, mind, life-breath, and intellect?" To remove it means this: when bodily relation and the conceit of varna and asrama rise up, producing arrogance such as "I am wealthy," "I am learned," "Who is equal to me?" and when he thinks those who insult this body with harsh words are enemies and, through bodily self-conceit, feels "he has insulted me," he must put an end to that ahankara.

To remove bodily self-conceit means this: when identification with body, caste, and asrama rises up, and with it the arrogance expressed in such thoughts as "I am wealthy," "I am learned," "Who is equal to me?" - and when, because of body-identification, one thinks of those who insult this body with harsh words as enemies, saying, "He is insulting me" -that ahankara must be removed.

One must contemplate: "I am not a brahmana, nor a kshatriya, nor a vaisya, nor a sudra. The varnas such as brahmana are modifications belonging to the body. Since we are distinct from the body, none of these belongs to us. We are Vaishnava servants." One must think in this way.

And when one sees those who reproach this body, one should regard them as supreme relatives. One should think: "This body is external to us; it is to be abandoned; it is contemptible; it is born of contrary knowledge; it is the hostile group consisting of desire, anger, and the rest; it destroys the true nature of oneself and others; it is like a snake-pit, a cause of harm. Is it not our own task to afflict this?" Toward such reprovers there should arise patience, compassion, smiling, joy, and remembrance of the help they have done. Only when these exist does the knowledge of the meaning of ma become complete. This removal of body-identification is the first qualification. When it arises, the seed of samsara is shaken loose, and realization of one's true nature arises.

If the thought "I am independent" arises, one must contemplate the meaning of a, which denotes refugehood, protectorship, and nourishership, and think: "Are we not beings who exist as sesha to Bhagavan? Can independence be fitting for us?" In this way that thought must be removed.

If the thought arises that one is nourished by another, one must contemplate the meaning of u, which denotes being fit for no one else, and think: "Are we not fit for no one but Him? Can being nourished by another be fitting for us?" Thus that thought must be removed.

If the thought arises that one has a role in protecting oneself, one must contemplate the meaning of namah, thinking, "Bhagavan alone is my protector; not I," and remove that thought.

If, toward those who appear as protectors in danger, the cognition "these are my relatives" arises, one must contemplate the meaning of the word ayana, and think: "The One who is the goal alone is every kind of relative." Thus that thought must be removed.

If fickleness toward sense-objects arises, one must contemplate the dative sense - the "for Him" sense - and think: "Are we not fit to be enjoyed by Him? We must be fit only for use according to His will. Can any separate experience of sense-objects be proper for us?" Thus that must be removed.

In this way, through knowledge of the mantra-meaning, one knows what is to be abandoned and what is to be accepted. If one abandons body-identification and the rest, accepts seshatva, dependence, and service, and conducts oneself accordingly, then one attains establishment in one's true nature, becomes a recipient of Bhagavan's grace, and becomes qualified for moksha.

But when there is no practice corresponding to that meaning, even if one spends time every day in exposition of the mantra-meaning and in ritual service, such persons become only eternal samsarins - so Bhattar graciously said.

Now, what is the fruit of learning the prabandhams?

As the prabandhams themselves say, "If they learn this and become capable," the attainment of Vaishnavatva occurs only when one becomes capable of knowing and practicing. Then, as it is said, "Those who can dance and sing in this way will ascend to Vaikuntha," one becomes liberated. If it is merely recitation, then, as in the saying, "like a ladle in the cooking," it will become useless. It will become only another cause of arrogance: "I know the Four Thousand; I know the Three Thousand."

If one asks, "But is it not said, 'By recitation alone I am pleased'?" - when there is no knowledge of word and meaning, there is no fitness to know clearly what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted, and to conduct oneself accordingly. Therefore there is no removal of tamas. Such statements are statements praising the greatness of recitation.

When it is said, "If there are those who can recite this decad, they will reach Vaikuntha," and when it is said that those who meditate on that decad become liberated, that cannot mean that those who recite it and then act contrary to it by engaging in offences against bhagavatas and the like become liberated. If pratikulya has been removed, one may even say that recitation alone is enough. Therefore, the expression "those who know these ten" means "those whose knowledge of the meaning and practice according to that meaning are complete."

Why say that knowledge and practice are intended, rather than merely the literal act of reciting as stated? Because when the fruit is stated in phrases such as "only for the lovely ones" or "only for those with lightning-like waists," one cannot take the literal surface alone as the meaning. Just as lovers are objects of enjoyment for their beloved women, those who are capable of practicing this decad become objects of enjoyment for the nitya-suris. That is the intended purport. So the meaning extends up to knowledge of that meaning and practice corresponding to it.

In this same way, the Vedas, the rsis, the Alvars, and the acaryas state fruits according to the occasion, often as praise of greatness. For example, when a text says that by merely wearing the heated discus one attains the supreme abode reached by detached renunciants, this cannot be extended to those devoted to other deities or to those engaged in prohibited acts such as harming others. It only says that such a mark has that form or character; it cannot mean that this alone is sufficient. Such statements must be understood as praise-passages describing the greatness of that thing.

Likewise, when it is said that even very sinful persons such as Sisupala attained liberation after repeatedly speaking harshly out of enmity, that too is spoken because of the greatness of remembrance of Him; it cannot mean that hostile offenders against Bhagavan obtain that attainment simply as such.

When Alvar praises lofty mansions as "joining the moon's sphere," or says that the ramparts of Tirukkannapuram touch the silver-like moon, these are not to be taken as contradicting direct perception. They are praise-passages. Their purport is in the greatness of that place.

Likewise, when the greatness of anjali is praised, and it is said that even once, somehow, by someone, anjali brings benefit, this does not mean that one who once made anjali and then lives transgressing the Lord's command, proceeding wrongly, and committing offences against bhagavatas, obtains all desired good preceded by the removal of all undesired things. If that were said, then since there is hardly anyone who has never made anjali, all conscious beings without distinction - those devoted to other deities, those devoted to other goals, those practicing prohibited acts, those committing bhagavata-offences - would become liberated. Then the distinctions between Vaishnava, non-Vaishnava, and qualified person would all become useless. Therefore such statements are to be understood as praise-passages; there is no fault in that.

What, then, is it to know and practice the prabandhams?

It is to understand, from "false knowledge," that the untrue knowledge called body-identification, the activity of samsaric bad karma, and the impure body are all things to be abandoned; and to pray to Sarvesvara that they be removed.

It is to follow "Give up completely; making your self His, place it in Him," by abandoning father, mother, wife, children, house, field, and all things apart from Bhagavan, and by performing self-surrender.

It is to follow "cut these from the root" by uprooting, with their very root, ahankara and mamakara, the seeds of samsara.

It is to remove the disease of looking at one's own virtues and others' faults.

It is to contemplate one's own defects, saying, "I of hard karma," "I the low one," "I the sinner."

It is to grieve, "When shall I come and join You?" praying that the adverse body, senses, and the rest be removed, and longing, "When shall I unite with You?"

It is to have taste for every form of servitude, as in "May we perform flawless service."

It is to know, "All things and all persons are for Him alone," understanding everything as having Bhagavan as its self, and so becoming free from attachment and hatred.

It is to understand the fullness of qualities in the arcavatara.

It is to regard the supremely distinctive bhagavatas who are absorbed in the experience of Bhagavan's qualities as the very objects to be attained.

It is to withdraw the mind from unworthy dealings, saying, "I cannot speak green, fresh lies for the sake of worldly attachment."

It is to make aisvarya and kaivalya insignificant, saying that the pleasure seen by the five senses is "immeasurable petty pleasure."

It is to feel intense disgust toward worldly dealings, saying, "What are these worldly things? Do not show me this cruel world," and desiring that this world not show its form.

It is to contemplate akincanya, saying, "I have no noble lineage; I have no good conduct."

It is to suffer when shown objects other than Him, saying, "Will You show me many things and ruin me? Will You still destroy me?"

It is, through the distress of not attaining Bhagavan, to lose taste for sleep, food, and such things, as in "Night and day she does not know sleep in her eyes."

It is to cry out, "Father, grant that I may see You."

It is, in the heat of separation from Him, to find all enjoyable things like fire, as in "O breeze fragrant with jasmine\!"

It is to refuse by oath, praying, "Do no magic; by Your command, by Your own oath, I know none but You," and thus to have Him bring about release from connection with prakrti.

This is the true fruit of learning the prabandhams.

This, then, is the fruit of learning the prabandhams.

But there are people who do not know the meanings of these dharma-prabandhams at all and remain bound in samsara. There are also people who have learned these meanings, yet whose minds remain attached to sense-objects; they live joined to wives, children, and the like; they remain bound samsarins; they are devoted to ahankara, wealth, and desire; they do not fear offences against bhagavatas; they compete with one another; they defeat others by sastric disputation; and they fail to keep in their heart the warning that one who dishonors the guru, triumphs over a brahmana by argument, and so on, becomes a brahma-rakshasa. Such people lie about at royal gates, engage in improper service, and, as Alvar says, lose their truthful speech by praising as "generous" the useless wealth of people whose riches are like heaps of refuse. They praise a very low man, a petty village-lord who gives a little wealth, as though he were the highest, while concealing or glossing over his bad qualities. For such astika-nastikas there is no remedy. An ignorant person can be corrected, but such a person cannot be corrected. For a learned man's transgression, the offence is greater. Therefore one should never associate with an astika-nastika. If one associates with him, then, just as one thing standing near something foul becomes foul itself, that person's evil vasana will come upon this one too, and he too will become a bound samsarin.

This meaning is established as Bhagavan's own intention. He says: "Service to the great ones is the door to liberation; association with those attached to women is the door to darkness." Service to mahatmas is the gateway of mukti; association with samsarins attached to wife, children, and the like is the gateway of the darkness of anartha. Who are these mahatmas? They are those who are equal toward enemy and friend, honor and dishonor, cold and heat, pleasure and pain, gain and loss, blame and praise; who are tranquil, free from anger, friends of all beings, supreme sadhus, filled with intense love toward Him; detached from wife, children, house, field, and wealth; and content with whatever comes unasked. Bhagavan Himself has declared that association with such mahatmas is the door to liberation.

Therefore, our acarya Periya Jiyar taught: distinguish carefully between the nastika who has no sastric knowledge and practices prohibited conduct; the astika who follows the good path taught by the essential sastra; and the astika-nastika, who knows the meaning of sastra but wanders according to his own will. Reject the fools mentioned before and after; follow always the astika spoken of in the middle. Therefore the one who longs for moksha should associate with the supremely distinctive ones who follow the path of the essential sastra.

One must not trust teachers of contrary meanings. Some take the title "acarya," though they have no good quality at all. They do not listen properly to the words spoken by the elders; they do not examine later, so that the meaning may not conflict with what came before and after; they merely speak whatever appears in their own mind and call it "pure sampradaya instruction." Jiyar says that such people are fools. They cannot establish matters as they truly are. They abandon whatever portion they happen to know, saying, "That is not needed." If, in one place, a supreme truth is stated, they imagine contrary meanings against it. They do not abandon pratikulyas such as bodily self-conceit. Remaining bound samsarins, they say, "We have the glance of a true acarya; what fear is there for us?" Deluded in this way, they themselves become fearless in sin and ruin the disciple who has taken hold of them. Thus both become joint offenders. By prohibited conduct, which is harmful to all three objects to be served, they kill the disciple and destroy themselves. Do not trust such teachers of distorted meaning. Instead, take refuge in the true acarya described earlier, marked by the proper signs of an acarya, and remain fully dependent.

There are also bound samsarins who wear the name and outward marks of Vaishnavas: they wash hands and feet carefully, arrange their garments neatly, apply white clay marks and urdhva-pundra, wear sacred garlands and pavitras, hold themselves as ritual specialists, and appear externally very attractive, like prostitutes adorned with ornaments. Yet they are filled with ahankara and mamakara; they are attached to house, field, wife, and children; they are joined with attachment and hatred; they pursue wealth; they seize one another's property; and, driven by arrogance, wealth, and desire, they commit offences against bhagavatas. Seeing such people, one should be afraid to approach them. Since they bear the names and marks, one should not despise them; according to one's capacity, one may show slight honor, be humble, and gladden them by pranipata and the like. But one must not mix inwardly with them, serve them closely, or associate intimately. One must stand apart, behave with caution, and join instead with mahabhagavatas who know the artha-pancaka and who are beyond the bondage of "I" and "mine."

In the company of such mahabhagavatas, one should, as the Gita says, "instructing one another," spend the few days one remains here in mutual instruction, so that no conduct contrary to one's svarupa may arise. One should pass the time in experience of Bhagavan's qualities and in service, free from ahankara and far from bhagavata-apacara. Our acarya has said that those who live so are the ones qualified for moksha.

Now, one may object: "Except for two or three names among the Alvars and acaryas, many lived in samsara with wife and children. Were they not qualified for moksha?"

The answer is this: all the acaryas, beginning with Sriman Nathamuni and reaching down to our own acarya, must be understood as renunciants in truth. They were free from attachment; without ahankara and mamakara; endowed with self-qualities such as sama and dama; compassionate toward all beings; equal toward enemy and friend; indifferent to wealth and desire; living by saintly means such as uncha-vrtti; and, for the sake of pleasing the acarya and conforming to what is favorable, they may have lived for some days with wife according to dharma, abandoning the thought of pleasure and desire. If the wife herself was a jnanin capable of correcting lapses, then both, being mutually detached from sense-objects, lived like Alvan and Andal, engaged in experience of Bhagavan's qualities and in service. They were not eternal samsarins.

But ordinary bound persons are otherwise. They produce ten or twelve generations; they cherish the wife, who is an object of extreme rejection and rooted in bodily life; they cherish children, who increase samsara and are utterly contrary to the self; they remain completely dependent on them and deeply attached until the body falls. They feel no grief that these are opposed to their svarupa. They have cast away shame before the sistas who reveal the true nature of the self. They have cast away fear of the Lord who wants them to take refuge in Him, thinking nothing of this difficult samsaric activity that is displeasing to Him. They then reason falsely: "Poison, weapons, fire, and all conscious and unconscious beings are His; all bhagavatas in the world are His; then why are wife and children not also His?" Such thinking is contrary knowledge.

One should be afraid of one's own body as though seeing a snake; and one should be afraid of those connected with it as though seeing serpents and fire. Instead of this, some go repeatedly to royal gates for the sake of protecting wife and children, perform improper service, seize others' property, appoint unqualified people, and accept wealth polluted by the conceit of unqualified disciples. Such people cannot cite the elders as examples and say, "What is wrong if we too live in samsara?" If a kshatriya were to drink poison because Rudra drank the kalakuta poison, he would perish. In the same way, destruction of the svarupa will not fail for those who imitate the elders wrongly.

If one asks, "Since wife and children are also His, can they not be treated as tadiya?" - the answer is this: when it is said that everything is Vaishnava, or everything is His, the purpose is only to remove the thought that anything exists for oneself or for another independent master. It teaches that nothing is for oneself; everything is for Him. It is not taught in any rahasya that one should cherish one's own body and the wife, children, and other bodily connections, which are the very field where opposition to the svarupa grows. If that were so, then since hell and heaven also are His, the twenty-eight great hells too would become things to be experienced as His. That would be the work of contrary knowledge. The required cognition is simply this: "Everything belongs to Him."

There is no one higher than Nammalvar in experiencing everything under the form "this belongs to Him." Yet, after Alvar had performed sarana-varana through Piratti as purushakara, he saw that the Lord did not take him to Himself. He concluded: "He is not granting His own attainment because I still have some desire in my self and in what belongs to me." Therefore, in the decad beginning "O parrots with whom I have no further relation, O mynahs, O cuckoos, O peacocks," he said, in effect: "You birds whom I have reared, and all things that belong to me, and all that is in my mind - He will not allow me even to see the places to be attained, beginning with Paramapada, until I have abandoned even the faint traces of desire toward you. Therefore from today there is no business between you and me." Thus he graciously declared that desire toward oneself and toward one's own possessions is an obstacle to Bhagavat-prapti. So one must not remain deluded, saying, "These too belong to Him."

If one says, "But Alvar is said to have folded his hands and bowed to the pet parrot; if they are jnanins, what fault is there in supporting them?" - the answer is this. A wife inclined toward sense-objects and sons intent on their own affairs may see that this person has become detached and think, "If we behave contrary to him, we shall be ruined; let us deceive him." Like the bird-trap, they may speak one lowly word - "We are your servants; we are your servants." Hearing that, if one becomes bewildered and says, "Even if they are disciples or sons, if they are jnanins, they are to be supported as tadiyas; what fault is there in associating with them?" and then becomes useful for their desires, wanders in sense-inclination through deep attachment, all former samsaric bondage will rise up and he will fall. Therefore, as it is said, "not regarding the abasement of small-bangled women," one should not go to associate with such deceivers.

But if they are truly of another kind - if their bodies visibly show knowledge and practice, with joy, tears, horripilation, and the like; if they are supremely detached; if they are deeply turned toward Bhagavan; if they know what is to be abandoned and what is to be accepted; if they live like a model laid down in sastra - then one may regard them with special honor, just as one recognizes all the jnanins in a forest. But one must not, being filled with desire and inclined toward sense-objects, associate intimately with them.

When Alvar says, "Give up everything," and when he explains the manner of that renunciation by saying, "Destroy from the root these things called 'you' and 'yours,'" is it enough merely for the attachment called "I" and "mine" to be broken, and for the cognition "this is His" alone to remain? No. Those such as Bharata and Saubhari had outwardly given things up, but samsara later increased for them. Therefore the special sense is that these things are to be abandoned.

If one asks, "If this meaning is clearly understood, is that alone enough?" - no. When one makes a candala settlement fit for brahmanas, one cannot remove only some filth and keep the rest. In the same way, if everything is to be abandoned, then wife, sons, house, field, and the like, which are polluted by mamakara, must indeed be abandoned. But what is meant by abandoning the body, which is hard to separate from oneself? Physical abandonment is not abandonment. The thought "the body is the self" and the thought "what belongs to the body is mine" - these alone must be abandoned.

As the Gita says, one who restrains the organs of action outwardly while thinking of sense-objects inwardly is called a false practitioner. Therefore merely restraining the body is not enough; abandonment in the intellect is necessary. Because those such as Bharata and Saubhari abandoned outwardly but later developed attachment and bondage, external abandonment is not complete abandonment. The mind alone is the cause of bondage and moksha. The point is only that mental abandonment is superior to bodily abandonment. It is not that one may simply know things to be rejectable and then wander as a bound samsarin, inclined toward sense-objects. The fruit of knowing them as rejectable must be the corresponding conviction and practice.

That conviction is this. The body is the field in which ahankara, sense-inclination, offences against bhagavatas, and other destroyers of one's true nature grow. Therefore, when one sees one's own body, one should fear it as one fears a murderous enemy approaching with a tightened waist-cloth, fierce moustache, rolling eyes, lowered shoulder-cloth, and a sharp dagger held firmly in the hand. Since this body kills, and since one has no power either to remove it or to attain moksha, one should cling to Him alone and pray, as the Alvars prayed, "Command me; let me die," "Call me to Your feet and accept my service," "Is the time for You to call and take me not yet near?" and "When shall I, Your servant, call out to Your feet?" As a loved guest is expected, one should pray for the removal of this body and await its death.

When one sees wife, sons, and other bodily relations, one should remember the statement that, for a mumukshu in this samsara, the society of bodily relatives is sorrowful like Sita's dwelling among the rakshasi women headed by Ekakarna in Lanka. They should appear intolerable. Like Piratti praying, "When will my Lord slay these rakshasas, destroy Ravana with his sons and relatives, uproot Lanka, and protect me?" one should pass the time praying for deliverance. Keeping all this in mind, Alvar said "destroy them from the root," meaning that one should abandon them by cutting them off together with their root.

Ahankara is of two kinds: the form of identifying the body as the self, and the form of thinking oneself independent. If one identifies the body as the self, one becomes a manifest Buddhist and the lowest among samsarins. If one thinks, "I am independent," then, since the self is Bhagavan's property, one becomes a thief who has stolen himself. As it is said, "No sin is greater than that committed by the thief who steals the self," he will perish like Ravana, who stole Sita, the Lord's possession.

Without understanding the cruelty of such ahankara and mamakara, if people say, "It is enough to know that these are to be abandoned," and then fatten the body with oil and other things, growing large arms and a thick neck, and wander about inclined to sense-objects, they will become eternal samsarins.

If one asks, "In this age, even those who are jnanins seem attached to sense-objects, intent on wealth, and settled in samsara. Are they not jnanins? If this conduct is contrary, why do they live that way?" - that is the power of Bhagavan's maya. Alvar says that His bewildering mayas, greater than the sky, can delude even the deathless ones whose knowledge is free from confusion; and that even the celestials do not know His mayas; and that the five senses, the mighty maya He has set in order to remove one, are hard to resist.

Therefore, however great a jnanin may be, if, though knowing his true nature as one requiring exclusive orientation, he lacks detachment from what is other than the Supreme Self and has a mind attached to sense-objects, then, if Sarvesvara thinks, "Let Me remove him," He will make him deeply inclined toward those harsh objects through the maya-power that can delude even the nitya-suris. Through that, he becomes attached to wife, sons, and the like; through that, intent on earning wealth for their protection; through that, engaged in improper service; through that, joined with attachment and hatred; through that, harmful to beings. Then, from ignorance, the intellect is ruined, and, as the Gita says, from attachment desire arises, and finally the intellect is destroyed. Thus he reaches destruction.

Therefore this only shows the power of Bhagavan's maya, which can delude even the nitya-suris whose knowledge is unobstructed. One must not attribute this samsaric bad conduct to the jnanin himself as though it were simply a defect of prakrti and then dishonor him; that too will become a cause of harm. Nor should one take mere jnana without practice as authoritative, saying, "The conduct of the sista is authority," and "Whatever the best person does, others follow." With bad latent tendencies, some speak contrary meanings that delight the ears of listeners, treat those swollen with learning and argument as knowers of dharma, and, being deluded by maya, regard the samsaric, low conduct they practice as sistacara. If one acts in that way, it will bear fruit without inquiry, like entering a hidden pit. Therefore two things must both be avoided: contempt for the conduct of the great, and imitation of what is not their true good conduct.

As the sastra says, one should follow only the good conduct of the great. The meaning is this: the mahatmas, being filled with the essential meanings of sastra, with knowledge and practice, do not merely speak lofty words outwardly; they graciously act for the good of conscious beings, taking care that no harm comes to themselves or others. One must carefully take up those good practices, just as a swan separates milk from water. One should leave aside statements that may be spoken through fatigue, negligence, and the like, and actions that occur merely because of bodily nature, and should practice their good conduct.

If one asks, "Can maya delude even a bhagavata?" - yes. Tondaradippodi Alvar, though an unbroken brahmacarin and one engaged in service, saw the courtesan Devadevi, became deluded, followed her, lost the service that had properly come to him, became deeply attached, and underwent all sorrows. Garuda too opposed the Lord on account of Sumukha. This indeed is the power of maya. Therefore one desires the true acarya's abhimana because, if one lives without separating from it, like a shadow at his feet, he will make there be no room for that person to be used merely in the Lord's lila.

But if one is sheltered only by one's own self-conceit, can one still be used in lila? Yes. Unless one is free from all other attachments, without ego, and established in the supreme truth, then even if one has taste for prakrta things, or pride that one is superior, or attachment to the body and bodily relations, there is no obstacle to being used for some time in lila. Alvar says that the Lord corrects those who stand without deviation in clear understanding and grants them the blissful state. This means: He corrects the sesha-bhutas who have clearly understood artha-pancaka and stand according to that understanding, who are ananya-prayojanas, and who are fit for His attainment, and He accepts their eternal service.

Did He not cast Jaya and Vijaya, who had the arrogance "We are the gatekeepers of Bhagavan's house," into births as rakshasas by the pretext of the sages' curse? Did He not cast the king called Indradyumna, when the thought arose in his heart that he was meditating only on Sarvesvara and that there was no one equal to him in devotion, into elephant-birth for a long time by the pretext of Agastya's curse? Then, when he was seized by the crocodile in the pond and cried "Save me\! Save me\!" in the form of praise, the Lord came with His garment and hair disordered, saying "Obeisance to Bhagavan's haste," in a way fit to be sung by jnanins, and protected him.

Therefore, if there is involvement with ahankara, sense-inclination, offences against bhagavatas, and the like, then even knowledge and devotion become useless, and such people become fit for lila. Among them, if there is exceptional devotion, He makes them pass some time in lila and then grants grace. But if there is no true knowledge and detachment, then, as it is said, "not even in crores of kalpas," such a person remains fit for lila forever and becomes an eternal samsarin. Therefore one must not sit carelessly, saying, "Are we not included in Bhagavan's abhimana? What fear is there for us?" and then involve oneself in ahankara, sense-inclination, bhagavata-offence, and the like. One must not remain a bound samsarin.

In former times, though the fifty thousand rakshasas were devoted to Him, when they became hostile to others, He caused delusive sastras to arise so that they would go away from Him, made them become established in contrary meanings, and cast them off. Likewise, even if people are born in Vaishnava households through the release of accumulated merit, if they have asuric natures, harm beings, and commit offences against bhagavatas for the sake of wealth and desire, He will produce contrary knowledge, make contrary meanings appear for all authoritative matters, cause them to begin prohibited practices, and cast them away so that they become unfit for Him.

There are three causes by which He casts a person out: inclination toward other deities, inclination toward other means, and inclination toward other objects of enjoyment. Alvar speaks of these when he says, "You cast me out to worship another deity," "Will You show me a path and remove me?" "The mighty five senses, the maya You set to remove me," and "Will You show me many things and lay me low?" These three, even individually, are enough for Him to let a person go; they are also mutually rooted in one another. By "You cast me out to worship another deity," he speaks of contact with other deities. By "Will You show me a path and remove me?" he speaks of being pushed into other means. By "Will You show me many things and lay me low?" and "Will You ruin me?" he states unmistakably that contact with other objects makes one's svarupa perish.

Here, the other object that destroys the svarupa is attachment to wife, sons, and the like. In worldly conduct, if a woman under the protection of a kshatriya becomes attached to another man, he does not tolerate that; he punishes her. In the same way, this conscious self has taken refuge in Sarvesvara, the Lord of all, distinct from everything else, the supreme object of enjoyment, omnipotent, and has come under His abhimana. If, being deluded by maya, he makes both Him and wife, sons, and the like objects for his own desire, then, just as a king's wife who looks toward a man other than the king is subjected to punishment, the Lord restrains him and casts him away; He does not include him in His own enjoyment. Since the elders understood that contact with unattainable objects is extremely painful for such a person, they taught: "Give up everything," "Destroy from the root these things called 'you' and 'yours,'" "Enter at the root of the one who is without attachment," and "Meditate on and destroy the three - thought, word, and deed - and enter within Him." Thus they prescribe complete abandonment of association with external sense-objects.

The acaryas who followed them also taught acceptance preceded by the abandonment of all that is other than Him, beginning with "father, mother, wife, sons, relatives." Therefore there is no use in outward forms, nor in pitiable speeches spoken by false persons, if the meaning taught here is not practiced.

One may object: "But the knowers say, 'We shall utter many words of obeisance to You,' and 'The folded hands once offered to Your feet are the supreme seal that quickly destroys sin,' and 'Even to write this is excessive.' The Lord Himself, the Alvars, and the wise have said that anjali alone is enough, and even that is excessive; that He Himself comes; that the matter is extremely easy. Why then speak this way?" The answer is this. Such statements speak of the greatness of the refuge so that a conscious being who has turned toward refuge, seeing his own fault and akincanya, will not become discouraged and turn back. They are not statements made at the stage of protecting one who has already surrendered, as though no discipline were needed. Once self-nourishment and other-support have been removed, and once he has placed his hand in Bhagavan's matter, then, by the compassion of the omnipotent Lord, who can make even an untrue favorable word spoken by someone running away become true, all the required qualifications will gradually arise of themselves. It is in that sense that the matter is spoken of as easy.

If one does not understand it this way and says, "That itself is the literal meaning," then karma and jnana are unnecessary; bhakti and prapatti are unnecessary; acarya-abhimana is unnecessary; Vaishnavatva is unnecessary; cessation from other deities is unnecessary; the meanings of sastra are unnecessary; there is no need to fear prohibited conduct extending up to offences against bhagavatas; one may act as one likes and, if once one performs anjali, it will accomplish the result. Then moksha-sastra becomes useless, the spotless words and practices of the Alvars and acaryas become vain, and the distinctive greatness of this matter is lost. If the Veda says something, one may call it mere praise; if the rsis say it, one may allege errors such as delusion and deceit; if Isvara says it, one may call them words meant to delude tamasic people. But Alvar, who received flawless knowledge and acted for the good of conscious beings, spoke with exact capacity and truth according to what he saw. In the prabandham that he himself called "the thousand songs without falsehood," he said such things as "Worship and rise, my mind." Are these false? No.

The answer to this is given by Madhurakavi Alvar in Kanninun Siruttambu. When he says, "He has the nature of being mother and father and ruling me," the meaning is this. First, this Alvar, seeing the state of the conscious being, acts like a mother: he speaks of the greatness of the refuge, invites him, and wins him over by tenderness. Then, so that he may not remain a bound samsarin, he acts like a father: he speaks of the protection of the surrendered, draws him in, and makes him obey. Just as, when treating a child's disease, the mother puts milk in the mouth and the father stands nearby, and both together soothe the child, saying, "It is gone, it is gone," while gradually curing the disease through medicines and operations, so too Alvar first says, "Worship and rise," meaning that worship itself is enough, and afterward says, "Give up completely," commanding the abandonment of wife, sons, friends, family, action, field, and every other non-divine thing.

If one says, "How can we abandon objects in which we have had beginningless habitual attachment? Since we have taken refuge in Him, shall we not gradually leave them?" - that too is not right. When one makes a place inhabited by candalas fit for brahmanas, does one keep some things and remove others? Since ahankara and mamakara are candalas, all things polluted by them are to be known as adverse. Therefore Alvar says: "Leave them all, make your self His, and place it at His feet," meaning: abandon them all and perform atma-samarpana. If one asks how to abandon the body, which is hard to distinguish and separate, and wife, sons, house, field, and so forth, which are connected with it and hard to leave, Alvar says: "Destroy from the root these things called 'you' and 'yours'; then join Him." That means: remove, together with their root, the thought that the body is the self, the thought that the self is independent, and the thought of "mine" in what is connected with the body, and then join the Lord.

What does it mean to destroy these from the root? It means regarding this body, the dearest enemy, as an enemy that harms oneself; not nourishing it with curd, milk, ghee, and such things; offering pure substances without the defects of kind, source, and occasion to Perumal; accepting only a little prasada with reverence, merely to sustain existence; not giving room to the senses; regarding those who restrain one's mind as supreme relatives; and regarding those who increase attachment to the body as enemies. It means thinking, "This self exists for Him alone and is wholly dependent upon Him alone." This is the conviction that ahankara is to be abandoned.

Next, one should regard wife, sons, and other bodily relations as harmful, like the rakshasi women headed by Ekakarna who harmed Piratti in Lanka. According to the statement that one should be free from clinging to son, wife, house, and such things, one should not have ordinary pleasure and pain in union with or separation from them. One should feel that living with them is like being in fire and should suffer inwardly. This is the conviction that mamakara is to be abandoned. Like Piratti, who longed, "When will Rama come with arrows, destroy Lanka's mighty fort, and take me?" one should pray: "When will He remove this prison-house called the body, this delusion like Ravana that steals the self, and this association with wife, sons, and the like, who are harmful like rakshasas, and cast His glance upon me?" This is what is meant by uprooting ahankara and mamakara. If such a way of life is not born, mere verbal statements will not bear fruit. A snakebite is not removed by wiping the cloth.

One may ask: "Since namah teaches non-ownership and dependence, can one compel Him and act for one's own purpose? Would not praying for the removal of obstacles amount to involvement in protecting oneself?" No. This is like praying for service in a service-context. The prayer for the removal of avidya, karma, vasana, and prakrti-sambandha, which are opposed to service, is connected with one's true nature. Just as the prayer for service is expressed in its proper place, the prayer for the removal of these obstacles is expressed in the word namah. The Mumukshuppati teaches that through this, avidya and the rest are removed. Emperumanar too, in explaining the meaning of namah in the Gadyatraya, prays for the removal of these obstacles beginning with mind, speech, and body.

Self-involvement in protection would arise only if one undertook karma-yoga and other means such as sacrifice and austerity with the thought, "I must protect myself." For one who is dependent, that is improper. But if one gives up the prayer for service and the prayer for removal of what is opposed to oneself, the very self perishes and there is no connection with either the means or the end. Therefore "absence of self-involvement in protection" means the cessation of one's own activity that is opposed to Bhagavan's activity. It must not be taken to mean that one may abandon the prohibitions themselves, saying, "I have no involvement in my protection; is not abandoning forbidden acts a kind of self-protection?" If one acts so, the three qualifications - for svarupa, upaya, and upeya - collapse and are uprooted. Therefore what must be accepted is only the cessation of self-activity apart from service.

If one asks, "Then why does Srivacanabhushana say that prayer for release from prakrti-sambandha is not pleasing to Him?" - that statement is for supreme persons like Alvar. Because, as in "O Tirumaliruncolai hill\! O Tirupparkatal\!" they performed many services in divine places by means of their bodies, Sarvesvara finds the divine body of such a supreme person extremely dear, as it is said that separation from even a quality of such a one is painful to Him. The statement means only that. It does not mean that one should not have distress over bodily bondage. If there is no distress, if there is no state of rejecting sense-objects, one is not an adhikari but a fool. The very Alvar who said that such a prayer is not pleasing to Him also swore, "By Your command, by Your command," prayed to be freed from the body, and made Him release him from it. It becomes pleasing to Him only when this person has such distress. If instead one is overcome by desire, he is merely one among samsarins.

If one has joy in the body and in what is connected with the body, ahankara and mamakara remain. If not removed, the tree of avidya grows; the seed of matter sprouts; desire for wealth and pleasure, attachment and hatred, delusion, punya and papa, pleasure and pain all increase. However much of a jnanin one may be, one becomes a bound samsarin and is ruined. Thinking of all this, Alvar, in the very place where he teaches the mark of the saranagata, said: "Destroy from the root these things called 'you' and 'yours.'"

After speaking of His ease of access - "He is easy for devotees who possess attachment" - and after saying that one should stand in the sastra-taught paths of bhakti and prapatti, and after declaring His being easily worshipped, Alvar says: "O servants, abandon; after abandoning, worship." That means: if one worships after cutting off association with external objects, He will give His own attainment. Alvar also says, "He will come even if thought of," and, "If you and I stand in this state, O mind, He will not allow any other disease to approach," and, "Do not desire," and, "He is the life-support even of those without vileness," and, "When death comes, He is the Lord who gives Vaikuntha," and, "He becomes true to all who worship with desire; He becomes false to all who worship false things." In this way, first he speaks of the greatness of the refuge. Then, because the protection of the surrendered person's svarupa is being examined, these other requirements must be understood as things that must arise in that person's own nature.

If one asks, "Then is the greatness of the refuge false?" - no. As it is said, "Even if one is of very evil conduct, if he worships Me with no other orientation, he is to be regarded as a sadhu," even if he was previously of extremely bad conduct, if he worships Him as one who belongs to no other, he becomes distinctive and is saved. Even if some of that old vasana continues later, He bears with it. He may delay for some days until the person becomes fit for enjoyment, but there is no loss in the accomplishment of the fruit.

A prince may take young girls under his care and, when they attain maturity, bring them into enjoyment. A farmer, when the seed of paddy sprouts in the field, does not immediately eat it; he grows it, removes the weeds, waits until it bears fruit, cooks it, and then eats it. In the same way, Sarvesvara employs a person according to his distinctive ripeness. Therefore, whether in this birth at the final moment, or, if not then, in another birth, He corrects him, makes him fit, and joins him to Himself. If there is real sambandha, He will not abandon him forever as a bound samsarin inclined toward sense-objects. He has said, "My devotee does not perish." Alvar too says that He will not allow the five snake-like senses and fierce karma to devour His servant.

Once, when Nampillai was staying in the matha of Pinpalagiya Perumal Jiyar, the Sripada servants submitted: "When the prapyam is to be attained, must not Alvar's state arise? What should be done for those whose legs are still joined with bodily relations such as wife and family?" Pillai replied: "Even if that state has not arisen here, when the gross body falls, before granting the attainment, Emperuman will produce that state and accomplish His work."

When asked, "What is His state in His avataras, since He does not obtain the proper adhikari?" Pillai said: "His body becomes pale and His tongue becomes dry." The meaning is: because He does not obtain the adhikari, His body becomes pale; and even when He obtains some, because they are attached to other fruits, His tongue becomes dry, as one's tongue dries on seeing rice polluted by hair. Therefore the adhikari who is ananya-prayojana, one who has no other goal, is very rare, hard even for Emperuman to search out and find. The Prameya-ratna says that one may see an ananya-prayojana on the far side of the Viraja, but he is hard to see in this vibhuti. Thus his rarity is established.

If even for a prapanna there is such rarity, why is this called an easy means? Because, unlike other means, it does not require difficult practices to be performed; it requires cessation. The difficulty appears only because of the strength of his own attachments. There is no bodily torment here: no sacrifice, gift, austerity, renunciation, krcchra, candrayana, pancagni, or similar effort. What is required is the cessation of all these adverse things. If even this much is absent, the result is only becoming an eternal samsarin.

If this is easy, why is it said, "This self is hard to obtain," and, "Only one or two exist"? Because, as the Gita says, "All this world is deluded by these three guna-made states," the power of His maya, made of the gunas, does not allow this state to arise easily. Therefore it is called rare.

How, then, can this qualification arise in one who is bound by prakrti? It cannot be produced by oneself, nor can another ordinary person produce it. But if it comes through Bhagavan's glance, by the grace of a true acarya obtained through that glance, there is no deficiency. It is said that even trees melt in the presence of a true acarya; even immovable beings may become receptive to self-qualities. By Bhagavan's grace alone, the moving and unmoving beings of Ayodhya, Gokula, and the like became qualified for liberation through connection with Him.

One may ask: "If what comes through His glance is independent of this person, why do sastras, rsis, Alvars, and acaryas command, 'Do this; do not do this'?" The answer is this. When Sarvesvara saves certain selves, He must produce self-qualities in them and then accept them. Since He does not stand visibly before them and speak with His own mouth, He gives general sastra, like a support-pole for a vine to climb, so that those whom He wishes to save may know what is to be abandoned and what is to be accepted. He also gives special sastra so that He may be attained.

Creation, preservation, destruction, samsara, and liberation are all His lila. Just as He brings forth the four-faced Brahma, becomes the inner controller of Brahma and Rudra, causes creation and destruction through them, and Himself stands as the preserver, so too, for liberation from samsara, He brings forth Parankusa, Parakala, Nathamuni, Yativara, and others. Through them He sets the special sastra in motion. Because He has resolved to bring certain souls to Himself through them, those who are fit for liberation and dependent on grace become bearers of pramana; and He, as the indwelling Self, impels sastras and sistas to state those meanings so that others may trust them.

For those whose inner being is polluted by bad vasanas, instruction is useless. Even though His compassion - "this person must be saved" - is eternal, the jivas' previous punya and papa must be experienced, and they must act according to their vasanas. Therefore His glance does not immediately take effect in every case. He waits until there is a place suitable for it. When He obtains that opportunity, without delay He makes them fit for liberation.

For those who are established in general sastra and wander as sadhakas, He casts His glance: "Should I not quickly bring them to My feet?" He casts a special glance so that they may become established in the special sastra. Then they become paramarthikas; they clearly understand the special meanings practiced and taught by the Alvars and acaryas; they completely abandon pratikulyas; they become established in His qualities and service alone; they become ananya-prayojanas; and they are saved.

For one established in another sadhana, it is said: "Only for those whose sins have been exhausted through tapas, knowledge, samadhi, and the like over thousands of births does devotion to Krishna arise." Such a person does not easily obtain a transformation of the heart in which the form of the self, uninterrupted meditation, supreme dearness, and ananya-prayojanatva arise. Even an upasaka who has practiced for a long time, because anga-bhuta-prapatti-nishtha exists in him, may, when purity of mind does not arise because the mind is polluted by sense-objects and by the great mass of beginningless sins, pray: "This disturbed mind of mine is hard for me to remove. You alone must be the means." Gradually he becomes established in the siddhopaya.

Yet even then, if bad vasana rises up, and he becomes devoted to other fruits, sends his mind into worldly enjoyments, becomes attached to wife and sons, and enjoys evil objects, then, because the siddhopaya does not tolerate alien mixture, the joining of upaya and apaya loosens that nishtha. As it is said, "When upaya and apaya are joined, one's nishtha is diminished; when apaya arises, one should perform the proper expiation." He must perform prayascitta again and again. If bhagavata-apacara and the like arise, he may wander, being consumed, for a long time. Only after much labor, when the siddhopaya bears fruit, will he become a paramarthika - like Tirukkannamangai Andan - and later, like Tirunaraiyur Araiyar, abandon attachment to his own household and gradually become liberated.

But for the one who is the recipient of the special glance mentioned earlier, paramarthatva arises from the very beginning. From the beginning he becomes detached from "false knowledge, evil conduct, and the impure body," and he waits, saying, "Is the time for You to call me not yet near?" Since none of these anarthas enter him, he becomes liberated immediately after release from the body.

If one asks, "Even after accepting the siddhopaya, which is valid for all, can there still be occasion for delay?" - if he is qualified for that acceptance, then afterward, at the proper time, He quickly accomplishes the matter without dependence on anything else. But if there is deficiency in the qualification for the upaya, it will not become effective until he becomes qualified. Since this acceptance of the upaya is a common cause for all fruits, it may also occur for those who seek aisvarya or kaivalya; in their case, because of their bad vasana, it culminates in self-experience or petty enjoyment. Therefore, only if this person turns away from samsaric evil activity and becomes ananya-prayojana does the desired fruit come.

An ananya-prayojana is one who abandons all other fruits - beginning with wife, sons, and the like, extending up to the lordship of Brahma, and even including self-experience - and takes Him alone as the goal. Therefore association with wife and sons and enjoyment of impure objects are other goals. If one has these, it cannot be said that he has accepted the siddhopaya. One devoted to another fruit has no fitness for attainment.

Those who are attached to other objects, though those objects are supremely rejectable, and who cannot abandon wife and sons, say "we do not want them" only because they have not obtained them. If they obtained distinctive pleasures beyond this world, would they abandon them? Therefore, for one inclined toward sense-objects, there is no participation either in the qualification for the upaya or in the qualification for the upeya.

Ammangiyammal, in the Sarartha-catushtayam, explains the nature of the upaya-adhikari so that contrary meanings may not enter. The knowledge connected with the upaya is of two kinds: upaya-jnana and upaya-yathatmya-jnana.

Upaya-jnana itself is fourfold: sambandha-jnana, sambandha-yathatmya-jnana, sambandha-svarupa-jnana, and sambandha-svarupa-yathatmya-jnana.

Sambandha-jnana means clearly knowing His nourishership and one's own state of being nourished by Him, and being fit for the use He desires. Sambandha-yathatmya-jnana means knowing clearly that the relation between Him and oneself is not external, like the relation between master and servant, father and son, or owner and house, but is the relation of body and self. Sambandha-svarupa-jnana means knowing clearly that it is not like body and self, which appear as distinct substances, but that He is the dharmin and oneself is His dharma. Sambandha-svarupa-yathatmya-jnana means knowing clearly the unity of dharmin and dharma: in the qualified whole, it is He alone, so that one may say that there is no independent "I." These four are upaya-jnana.

Upaya-yathatmya-jnana is nishtha in Him. That means knowing the cessation of doership in four places: in being nourished, in knowing, in acting, and in enjoying.

Doership in the state of being nourished is this: like ministers who use the king's authority to damage the kingdom, one uses Bhagavan's authority to loosen sadacara, saying, "Are we not Bhagavan's seshas? What fear is there for us?" Thus one becomes fearlessly inclined toward prohibited objects, and desire for service to Bhagavan and the bhagavatas does not arise. The cessation of that doership is this: even in accidental contact with sense-objects one is frightened and bewildered; even in accidental separation from service one suffers.

Doership in the state of knowing is this: "Because I am a knower, He accepted me." One becomes proud on the basis of one's own knowing. The cessation of that doership is this: "I was lower than those who identify the body as the self; I was ignorant; I was destroying myself from the root. Like one who makes iron into gold, He removed my ignorance and gave me knowledge."

Doership in the state of acting is this: "Only because I abandoned prohibited acts and took up prescribed acts did He accept me." One becomes proud on the basis of one's own actions and abstentions. The cessation of that doership is this: "There is no activity of mine. Like one who makes a wooden puppet move, He Himself made me leave prohibited acts and made me take up prescribed acts."

Doership in the state of enjoying is this: "I am doing service to Him." The cessation of that doership is this: if one hand pulls the hair of one's own body, the hand does not think, "I have done a favor to myself." In the same way, this self, being an organ-like part, is lifted and extended toward Emperuman, who is the whole. That is all.

Thus one must remain as one who is nourished by Him and not become inclined toward sense-objects. One must think: "I am not a knower by myself; I am without knowledge; this is knowledge given by Him." Even when one abandons prohibited acts and takes up proper conduct, one must think: "I am dependent like a wooden puppet; both this abandonment and this acceptance have come from Him." Even while performing every kind of service, one must think: "He Himself is causing it to be done." This is the mark of the upaya-adhikari.

Though this is the true state, fools say: "Those who have known the body-self relation and the unity of part and whole may perform prohibited acts; those acts too belong to Him, and there is no defect." One must not say this. The body-self relation is taught so that one may know that being supported, being controlled, and being nourished are one's true nature. The unity of part and whole is taught so that, in the service one performs, ahankara and mamakara - "I am the doer; this is mine" - may not arise, and so that one may stand in dependence free from doership and enjoyership. It is not taught so that whatever one does by one's own desire, while inclined toward sense-objects, becomes His. Such a thought is a cause of destruction.

Nahusha, Nrga, Yayati, and others performed many sacrifices, gifts, and austerities for a long time. But because of the pride "we did these," all of them became useless in a single moment, and they fell. King Citraketu ruled for a long time, later became detached, practiced Bhagavad-upasana, and by the fruit of that upasana traveled with a divine vehicle through the higher worlds. One day, on the road to Kailasa, seeing Rudra seated with Parvati and surrounded by the ganas, he became proud of his own detachment and mocked him as shameless for sitting with his wife. By Gauri's curse, all that upasana was uprooted, and he was born in a rakshasa womb.

If, while performing the six karmas, one thinks, "I am doing them," they do not merely become like dust thrown on a peacock; they rise up and bring harm. Therefore the prapanna must place upon Him all the knowledge, resolve, love, and good conduct he has. As for prohibited activities that arise through karmic bondage, if he laments, "I, not knowing myself, remained saying 'I' and 'mine,'" then the svarupa can be saved.

If one objects: "But is it not said that whatever one does with body, speech, mind, senses, intellect, or self should be offered to Him, and therefore all that this person does belongs to Him?" - the answer is: first, by the single expression "one who is exclusively established in Narayana," the adhikari is specified. Those who have abandoned every other dependence, including dependence on dharma, and who experience Him alone as everything, like Alvar, are the ones exclusively established in Narayana. Such adhikaris do not engage in prohibited conduct.

When Alvar says, "I shall cry; I shall bow; I shall dance and see; I shall sing and wail; through karma I shall look to the side and remain ashamed," all those states arise because of love directed toward Him. Therefore they are pleasing to Him and become causes for the blossoming of His face, like desired services. But if people like us - inclined toward bad objects, saranagatas only in name, and bringers of sorrow - perform violence toward others, theft of others' wealth, and acts of lust, and then say that these too become service to Him, we become worthy only of ridicule.

If one says, "But Ajamila and others lived in evil ways, uttered the name through attachment to a son, and He made that a pretext and graced them," the answer is this: He restrained the servants of Yama from taking him away and released him. Then Ajamila became clear, was filled with wonder, understood the greatness of Bhagavan, became detached from other matters, fixed his mind on the Supreme Self, and was liberated.

If one asks, "Why speak in this severe way? Since all the Alvars are speaking only of His ease of access, can Bhagavad-vishaya be described as difficult?" - I have not said that He lacks the qualities of accessibility. His nature is indeed "easy even for the lowly." Yet this person must conduct himself according to his own svarupa. Because He protected the crow that committed offence against Piratti, should this person too commit offence against the Mother? The meaning is only that, if he stands without destroying his own svarupa, he becomes pleasing to Him. It is not that, because He once protected someone by His independent grace, others may take that as license to act against their own nature.

One may ask: "Is it not said, 'He does not fear, saying, "Why did I not do good? Why did I do evil?"' Does this not say that even deliberate wrongdoing need not be feared?"

The answer is this. In the Anandavalli, what is being taught is that, through meditation on the Supreme Brahman qualified by the quality of bliss, the upasaka obtains the complete removal of all afflictions. It is not saying that a prapanna may deliberately engage in prohibited conduct and be fearless. The upasaka, from the very beginning, is one who has become disciplined: "having become tranquil, self-controlled, withdrawn, enduring, concentrated, he should see the Self in the Self." Such an upasaka will not later perform sin.

If one says, "Self-control may be necessary for the upasaka, but it is not compulsory for the prapanna," the answer is: even if it is not required as a condition for accepting the means, it is absolutely required for qualification for the goal. Alvar prayed, "Grant me the boon that will destroy the strong root." Therefore the removal of the root of adverse conduct is required for the attainment to be fitting.

Again, one may object: "But it is said that even those devoted to sin, if they become My devotees, are released from all sins like water from a lotus-leaf. It is also said that even cruel, evil-souled, sinful people attain the supreme state if they take refuge in Narayana. If He Himself says that even a great sinner who takes refuge in Him becomes liberated, why do you frighten us?"

The answer is this. It means that, even if a person was a very great sinner, once he takes refuge in Him, by His grace he quickly becomes dharmic, attains peace, and becomes liberated. It does not mean that he remains in the same condition until the fall of the body and is liberated while remaining so.

If this meaning does not appear right to you, and you wish to proceed in prohibited conduct, then do not ask me. You do not need Vaishnavatva; you do not need acarya-abhimana; you do not need the words and practices of the Alvars and acaryas. Do not enter this sacred matter and spoil its distinctiveness by forcing contrary meanings into it, saying, "Did He not save Ajamila and Ghantakarna? Is He unable to save us?" If you continue to perform acts such as brahma-hatya, theft, and other sinful conduct, becoming cruel, evil-minded, and sinful in practice, then at that time He will use you according to His divine will. If He chooses by His independence to make you liberated, He can do that too. He can also restrain and punish.

When He, by His causeless independence, makes someone liberated, He may grant His own attainment without considering, "This person was devoted to other deities," "This person was inclined toward other objects," "This person committed sins such as brahma-hatya," or "This person committed offences against bhagavatas." But if that is taken as a general rule, then why do you speak of restrictions such as "Do not touch other deities," "Do not have the scent of other means," "Do not commit bhagavata-apacara," and "Do not associate with prakrta persons"? Do not speak a few rules as though you were a non-Vedic teacher who holds doctrines such as the body-sized self, non-violence as an absolute, nudity, or eating from the hand. Give up everything and remain just as it appears to you.

He showed grace to the moving and unmoving beings of Kosala and Gokula, and to persons such as Vidura, the garland-maker, and Dadhyang. Did they have panca-samskara and acarya-abhimana? Still, our earlier acaryas did not desire moksha merely as something He gives; they stood rooted in what pleases Him and in the purity of their own svarupa. They were detached from everything, absorbed in experience of Him alone, complete in true knowledge and practice, and became exceedingly dear to Him. If the path walked by them is desirable to you, then follow what they graciously taught and become fulfilled.

Do not merely take the name "we are established in the fifth means," leave aside the words and practices of the former acaryas in which prohibited conduct never entered, speak only of the wondrous acts of omnipotent Sarvesvara and of praise-passages declaring His greatness, imagine contrary meanings for them, and go about arguing, "What is the meaning of this? What is the purport of that? What is the basis of this?" while not abandoning even one pratikulya. Do not keep your eye fixed on prohibited conduct, saying, "Everything will fit somehow." You must walk in some true path.

When the disciple again offered obeisance and said, "Because I am ignorant, and because you are mother-like in affection, please do not be angry. I am only submitting whatever has arisen in my heart. Please, out of mercy, resolve all these doubts," the acarya smiled and said, "Listen."

One may ask: "Is it not said that the man of knowledge is not subject to injunction and prohibition, and goes about like a child, because he turns away from prohibited objects and does not perform prescribed acts for the sake of merit?"

The answer is this. Such statements mean that, when knowledge has arisen, he becomes unable to distinguish in the ordinary way between punya and papa, enemy and friend, honor and dishonor, pleasure and pain, praise and blame. Intoxicated by the honey of the experience of the one, non-dual Brahman, he forgets his body; his mind and intellect become absorbed in Bhagavan; he has no deliberate self-directed activity; he wanders like a child, a madman, or one possessed. They do not say that a man attached to wife and sons, lying at royal gates for wealth, ruled by arrogance, wealth, and desire, may go about without regard for injunction and prohibition. The example of the child itself shows that the activity is not deliberate.

One may say: "If a person has placed mind and intellect in Him, and has become devoted, then even if, forgetting his body, he does difficult acts, there is no fault. Did not Sarvesvara Himself say, 'He whose sense of "I am the doer" is gone, whose intellect is not tainted, even if he kills these worlds, he does not kill and is not bound'?"

Then one may ask further: "Fear arises only when one takes the independent Lord as the means. But if one rests in acarya-abhimana, did not the elders say that one need not fear one's own acts and omissions?"

We have already answered this. When it is said that one established in acarya-abhimana has no fear, the meaning is this: the acarya clarifies for him what is to be rejected and what is to be accepted, causes him to act accordingly, watches him as though applying butter to the eye, purifies him so completely that there is no room for him to be used in Bhagavan's lila, and joins him to Bhagavan. That is the meaning. It must not be taken to mean, "Even if he remains undisciplined, there is no defect." If that were the meaning, there would be no reason to say, while examining the nature of the final prapanna, that knowledge and practice must arise for the delight of the acarya's face, or that prohibited conduct must be abandoned.

Acarya-abhimana is effective for animals and immovable beings, because they have no pratikulya. But for conscious beings such as humans, even if knowledge and practice are absent, it will not become effective unless there is removal of hostile things such as ahankara, mamakara, inclination toward sense-objects, harm to others, praising others wrongly, taking another's wife, eating what must not be eaten, drinking what must not be drunk, devotion to other deities, other means, and other fruits, offences against Bhagavan, offences against bhagavatas, and the like.

Madhurakavi, who took refuge in Alvar so that no fear might arise from taking the independent Lord as means, did not say, "I have connection with Nammalvar; what fear is there for me?" and then perform prohibited conduct. Rather, he said, "He corrected my actions and accepted service," showing how Alvar corrected him so that he became complete in knowledge and practice. And by saying, "I stood as the loving servant," he showed that he himself became detached from other objects, abandoned everything other than that matter, and became good.

Thus fearlessness belongs only after one has been graced by a true acarya who is detached, who can cross himself and make another detached and carry him across, and after one has abandoned dharma and adharma in the proper sense, taken Sarvesvara alone as the means, and laid down one's burden. It is not possible for one to remain a bound samsarin, devoted to another fruit, engaged in prohibited conduct, and yet be fearless merely because "I have the acarya's glance."

If one asks, "Then what is the meaning of saying that it is enough, while bathing or studying, to be pleased and say, 'Bhattar's holy feet alone are refuge'?" - it means only that one should not engage in activities of self-protection. It cannot mean, "Bhagavad-kainkarya and bhagavata-kainkarya are unnecessary; removal of pratikulya is unnecessary; go about devoted to the belly." If that were said, both the svarupa of the refuge and the svarupa of the one who has taken refuge would be destroyed.

One may ask: "Did not Emperumanar, when Pillai Uranga-villi Dasar became bewildered thinking of samsara, say, 'Do not fear; if I have obtained it, there is no lack in your obtaining it too'?"

Pillai Uranga-villi Dasar's fear was of the same kind as the self-abasing utterances of the pure-souled Yamunacarya, Kurattalvan, and others. When they say, "I am the first among sinners," "I constantly dishonor the guru and mantra," and similar words, they are taking upon themselves the defects seen in samsarins. It is not that they actually committed contempt toward guru, mantra, or deity. Such self-abasement shows absence of arrogance. Persons such as Ponnacciyar, Alvan, and Andal were detached, had crossed samsara, and existed to speak benefit to all Srivaishnavas. Because they were such adhikaris, those words were spoken to display humility.

Karanci Somayaji, though a bound samsarin, thought of worshipping the divine arca-form; because of that, Emperumanar joined him to the holy feet of Sri Krishna. Embar was supremely detached; when the taste for samsara had disappeared, even if he was restrained by command, once detachment arose, he was made to give up that support and was established in renunciation, according to the rule that one should not remain without a support. Mudaliyandan too, after accepting household life, rejoiced, saying that now the tridanda had entered his hand. These elders, though they lived outwardly as householders, were supremely detached and were saved. Even now, if one lives free from ahankara and mamakara, free from the confusion of attachment, hatred, greed, delusion, and pride, indifferent to wealth and pleasure, equal toward enemy and friend, pleasant and unpleasant, blame and praise, there is no defect.

Then one may say: "Even if this is so, is there not Piratti's purushakara, which says, 'whether they are sinners or the worst of sinners'?"

Her purushakara functions toward the conscious being who is turning toward refuge. Seeing his offences, she says, "I forgive." By her intervention she asks Sarvesvara, "Shall You punish one who has turned toward refuge? If You punish him, how will Your qualities such as compassion live?" By her instruction, mediation, beauty, and mercy, she causes His qualities of grace to arise and makes Him protect. But it is not that, because of her purushakara, He tolerates all the beginningless sins of this person, cherishes him as dearer even than Piratti, and then, if through old vasana the person again becomes inclined toward other objects like a king's wife attached to a low man, and enters into ahankara, mamakara, and bhagavata-apacara, He will still accept him as desirable. Such a person becomes undesirable to Him and fit for punishment. Therefore neither the acarya, who is dependent on Him, nor Piratti, who is His sesha and wholly dependent on Him alone, will protect one who later falls away and becomes a bound samsarin ruled by ahankara and mamakara.

One may object: "It is said that even the son of a royal servant who does not know the king's way enjoys all pleasures. So even if there is no qualification, will not the fruit be obtained without obstruction?"

If there are no pratikulyas, yes, there is no defect. But if in this person there are things contrary to the mantra-meaning and destructive of the svarupa - ahankara, mamakara, inclination toward sense-objects, bhagavata-apacara, and so on - then fruit-accomplishment cannot be spoken of. Even if the servant's son does not know the details of royal service, he must behave without doing what is displeasing to the king. If he acts in things contrary to the king's desire, harm comes both to him and to his father.

If one asks, "Did not Piratti save the crow? Did not Alvan save Naluran?" - the crow committed offence once, lost all recourse, and fell at the holy feet; Piratti prayed to the Lord, "Protect him," and had him saved. That does not mean that repeated offence will be tolerated. Naluran, not knowing that trouble would come, uttered an offensive word for the maintenance of his own dharma and later repented. Alvan, not considering it an offence, bore with him; and through his affection for Alvan, the Lord was entreated so that Naluran might obtain the realm Alvan obtained. It is not said that Bhattar interceded for Virasundara, who committed deliberate offence.

One may ask: "Still, does He not have forgiveness, affection, and the thought 'he is Mine'? Will those not be effective?"

His sambandha is effective for one who knows the sambandha he has with Him, who cannot bear separation from Him, and who is distressed. One must examine one's own state and be frightened, saying, "What is this state in which I stand?"

"Today He has seized me; for so long I went astray," "Behold these bodies," "Will You still destroy me?" "It is hard for me to remain in this body," "Remove the conduct of this wild world," and so on. For such a conscious being, who has become unable to fit into this world, Bhagavat-sambandha may be spoken of. Thus it is taught in Manikka-malai. Otherwise, Narayanatva-caused sambandha is common to all and cannot be specially spoken of; it becomes undistinguished.

Therefore, if this person himself is distinctive in the world, he becomes the recipient of a special glance and becomes fit for moksha. If he is devoted to another fruit, yet has sadacarya-sambandha, then, after the removal of pratikulyas, as with yogins who have had slips, one or two births may arise; in the final body, detachment from all prakrta and aprakrta things is produced, he is made a paramarthika, and he is liberated. He is not abandoned forever like ordinary samsarins. The acarya's word says that, after becoming a Vaishnava, even if further births occur until he becomes exclusively devoted to Bhagavan, his earlier devotional vasana does not leave him; gradually he becomes liberated.

If one becomes qualified in this very birth, then what is required for acarya-abhimana is this: one must perform sadacarya-varana; make everything one has subordinate to that acarya; abandon all pratikulyas; and, like Arulalapperumal Emperumanar, Embar, Alvan, Andan, Vaduga Nambi, Nanjiyar, and others, live without separating from the acarya even for a moment, like a shadow at his feet.

Even if that qualification is not present, at least at the time of death, according to "even at the place of dying," one should remember the pratikulyas one has performed from beginningless time and condemn one's own defects, saying, "I am low; I have no good quality; I myself have committed sin fit to throw me into hell," and, "I am the first among sinners." One should not expose or proclaim the faults of others. One should not think that the knowledge, devotion, and detachment born in oneself through the acarya's glance came from oneself. One should not become arrogant. One should regard oneself as lower even than bound samsarins. When one sees a fault either among bhagavatas or among samsarins, one should think, "This is my own fault," and not reveal it aloud to anyone. One should be extremely afraid of bhagavata-apacara. If the causeless glance of Bhagavan bears fruit so that ahankara and mamakara are removed together with their vasanas, one becomes liberated at the end of this body.

But if, even at that time, one's mind is attached to the body, wife, sons, and the like; if the vasana for external objects has not been removed; if one has gone on acting with ahankara and mamakara, then, like Jada Bharata, one obtains another birth. It is not possible to be saved merely by saying, "I have acarya-sambandha." The acarya's function is to remove the defect that comes from lack of knowledge and practice. Piratti's function as purushakara is to have the sins accumulated before taking refuge forgiven. The Lord's function as upaya is to forgive and protect from all previous acts and accidental later faults. This is the true meaning. All statements that exceed this are praise-passages and must be taken as such.

Whether it is acarya-sambandha or the siddhopaya, it becomes effective only when the inner instrument is made to return from external objects and when the person is made intensely attached to Bhagavan's matter, as in "my heart also remained there." Therefore Alvar begins, "Worship and rise, O my mind," and then prays, "There is no life living in this flesh," "There is no heart in me," "If you and I stand in this state, He will not let any other disease approach," and "I have said this heavy word; do not forget." In the song beginning "the servants who receive grace," he says, in effect: "He is showing grace; I too have become detached from this samsara. Even if He intends to give and I desire to obtain, if you, O mind, are adverse, the matter will not succeed. Though the outer object is not permanent, because of your pratikulya I have lost beginningless time. At least now, give up ignorance and take hold of His holy feet." Thus Alvar teaches that without purity of mind there is no qualification for Bhagavat-prapti.

The way in which Sarvesvara corrects the impurity of the self whom He intends to join to Himself, and then makes that self fit for His use, is stated in the curni beginning, "In the great wasteland, the kshetrajna..." In the vast waste-field of the embodied self, powerful marauders have settled and multiplied, consuming the crop from within. The Lord, the farmer of devotion, guards that field, cuts down what has to be cut, tears out and uproots the mass of karma, removes the weeds with their roots, clears and prepares the soil, sows the good wet seed, destroys the roots of the seven births, prevents what is harmful from remaining, lets the crop grow full like the sea and bend its head, cuts it at the proper time, threshes it, removes the chaff and husk, washes it in pure water, places it in another vessel, and prepares it as nectar fit for the celestials. Thus Acarya Hrdayam explains how Sarvesvara, the bhakti-farmer, undertakes this whole cultivation for the salvation of the self.

By this it is shown that mere sambandha is not enough. For the one whom He intends to join to Himself, He must make that person ripened, purified, and fit. Alvar says that He remained watching him as one who does not abandon something he has found, and that by His special glance He removed the inclination toward other objects. Therefore the meaning is this: after He has cast His special glance and made the person pure, He joins him to Himself.

If one asks, "Then what is meant by the statement that He grants grace freely?" - that refers to the first glance, when He looks without cause, as it is said: "Not through purushakara, nor through any other cause; solely by My own will I look upon someone." It does not mean that, even at the stage of attainment, if a person remains a bound samsarin, inclined toward bodily objects, full of ahankara and mamakara, and committing offences against bhagavatas, He will still "freely grant grace." If that were so, even Vaishnavatva would be unnecessary. For a conscious being to become fit for Bhagavan's enjoyment, Vaishnavatva alone is not enough; he must become a prapanna. That too is not enough; he must become an ekantin. That too is not enough; he must become a paramaikantin. Ahankara and mamakara must be removed together with their latent impressions. Inclination toward prescribed and prohibited worldly objects must cease. The state described in the verse beginning "fields, friends..." must arise.

For the sake of establishing the soul's true nature, the Alvars and acaryas did not say this once and leave it there. They labored for the good of the conscious being, and in many prabandhams, not merely in one or two, each echoed the other with one voice, calling out and prescribing the abandonment of everything other than Him. There is no reason for them to prescribe total abandonment unless it is truly needed.

Yet some, relying on the strength of mere learning, argue perversely: "Did He not say that even a defect in His dependent one becomes a manifestation of His qualities? Therefore, if His devotee performs prohibited acts, that too must be pleasing to Him." Since Veda, sastra, Purana, and Itihasa are like a Kaveri without banks, outsiders and wrong-seeing people can extract contrary meanings from them. In the same way, these people say: "The mantra teaches the cessation of self-effort in self-protection. If one abandons prohibited acts thinking 'these are not to be done,' then that becomes involvement in one's own protection. Therefore the real meaning of the mantra is that even this abandonment is not to be undertaken. The Dvaya and Charama Sloka, which explain it, have the same purport." They twist even statements such as "Do not teach this to one without austerity or devotion," asking, "Otherwise would one grieve as though a treasure in hand had been thrown into the sea?" They say, "Dharma is what is indicated by Vedic injunction; the subtlety of dharma is hard to know; therefore even prohibited conduct, since it removes ahankara, is a subtle dharma." In this way they speak many contrary meanings that burn the ears of those who hear them. They refuse to abandon wife, sons, sense-inclination, ahankara, and mamakara, and they reduce to ashes the earlier auspicious conduct of the Alvars and acaryas. This must be understood as the work of Bhagavan's maya.

If that view were true, there would be no reason for Sarvesvara, the Alvars, and the acaryas to labor and call out for the uplift of conscious beings. Does joy arise in a man when his most beloved woman desires another? When He repeatedly says, "ananya, ananya, ananya" - "not for another, not for another, not for another" - would the touch of another not plainly appear to be the work of contrary knowledge?

Therefore it is said: "As long as there is no non-attachment toward sense-objects, one is not pure. Only one free from attachment to them is called pure." Unless attachment to other objects ceases, one does not become pure; unless one is pure, one does not become fit for enjoyment by Him. To establish this purity of mind, one must spend one's time contemplating the meanings of the three rahasyas and the meanings of the Alvars' divine utterances. One should remain inwardly solitary, in a place not known to samsarins. If one lacks the qualification for such contemplation of meaning, one should not associate with unauthoritative people who call whatever arises in their own minds "paramartha." One should live in association with those established in supreme sattva, who are free from arrogance, wealth-pride, and desire, and who are complete in knowledge and practice.

His quality of forgiveness operates only when, after performing sarana-varana, one does not proceed into deliberate prohibited conduct. If, instead, one says, "Since He is forgiving, He will forgive all the deliberate pratikulyas I have accumulated from beginningless time; so I need not fear committing them again," and repeatedly enters offences, falsely saying "I take hold of Your feet after abandoning fields, friends, father, mother, wife, and all pratikulyas," while not actually abandoning them, then forgiveness will not survive. Such a person says pleasant words to the Lord whenever he comes to surrender, but remains one who does what is not to be done, one inclined toward sense-objects, one unable either to hold on properly or let go properly. Forgiveness does not live for such a person.

Next, what is vatsalya? It is like the affection of a cow for its calf. While the calf is still in her belly, and while she licks it at birth, she accepts all its dirt as something enjoyable and lives united with it. But after it leaves her udder and wanders eating grass and drinking water, if it comes with filth smeared upon its body, she does not accept that. Likewise, when this conscious being comes to take refuge, the Lord accepts the defects present in him as objects of enjoyment and bears all his burden, as He says, "I carry his yoga and kshema." But afterward He does not accept deliberate actions as enjoyable; if the person becomes constantly inclined toward sense-objects, He does not take that person's burden upon Himself.

Therefore, unless one abandons everything and becomes a paramarthika, the Lord has no taste for him. The token for His protection is arti, distress. Accan Pillai has said, "If this is deficient, the svarupa of both is deficient." Therefore, for one who lacks removal of pratikulya and lacks arti, sadacarya-abhimana, purushakara, sambandha, and even His qualities such as vatsalya do not become effective.

Therefore, for a prapanna, the fourfold prohibited conduct beginning with doing what should not be done, the acceptance of other means, worldly enjoyments such as wife and sons, heavenly pleasures, and even self-experience must all be abandoned. He must remain as Alvar says: "Women with fish-like eyes, great wealth, good children, noble mother and father - all these are He alone."

The meaning that both involvement in other means and involvement in apaya are to be abandoned by the prapanna was taught by Alvar himself through both self-instruction and instruction addressed to another. In the self-instruction, when the proper beneficial means does not immediately bear fruit, a person might begin other upaya-practices: worshipping other deities, seeking purification through rites such as soma-sacrifice, and joining with practitioners attached to such means. But when sambandha-jnana rises strongly, the confusion of such resolve is removed. One then thinks: "How can I involve myself in self-protection? For the pure self, whose nature is exclusive seshatva and which belongs to no one but Bhagavan together with Piratti, are not other deities, other means, and association with those attached to them destructive of the svarupa?" Thus, because it removes activity directed toward self-protection, other upaya-practice is not fit for a prapanna.

In the instruction addressed to another, the nayika lies deluded by love, unable to attain the Lord. The mothers, confused, think that the girl's delusion should be cured by worship of a low deity and by offerings dear to fierce deities, such as liquor and meat, as a fortune-telling woman advises. When they are about to begin prohibited conduct, the friend comes running and says: "Alas, alas\! Knowing this girl's nature, can you engage her in prohibited acts that destroy the svarupa? Can this girl have any connection with a low deity? Can she bear contact with utterly forbidden substances such as liquor and meat, which make the one touched by them like themselves? Can she bear contact with those who teach contrary meanings, such as the fortune-teller? Apart from contact with Bhagavan's name, and apart from contact with the dust of the holy feet of the great bhagavatas who are supremely Vedic and dependent on pramana, can anything else be fitting? Can prohibited conduct enter the discipline of our Vaishnava lineage?" In this way the friend restrains them.

If the girl's illness is examined properly, she has become deluded because of dependence on the Lord who is dependent on His devotees; if His names are spoken, her delusion will end. Therefore one must not follow the words of the false fortune-teller who desires the opposite of her good. One must not engage in acts destructive of Vaishnavatva, sprinkle liquor and meat, and say, "This alone is the divine medicine by which she will live." One must not arrange forbidden items dear to rajasic and tamasic deities, and make her dance like one possessed. If there has been such low contact, place the dust of the holy feet of bhagavatas upon her as expiation. Is this your method of curing a girl equal to Piratti - to destroy her with liquor and meat? Abandon the contradictory words of that unauthoritative woman. Take as purushakara the knowers who are foremost in Vedic knowledge and dependent on pramana; take refuge in the Lord who conducts the nitya-suris; cure her illness. Praise the feet of Kannan; then her illness will end, her svarupa will be attained, and she will live, worship, and dance. Thus the prabandham teaches that prohibited conduct and association with those established in such prohibited conduct are to be abandoned by the prapanna.

One may object: "But the pramanas say that one who knows the body-self relation between Him and himself, and the unity of part and whole, has all his actions belonging to Him; the punya and papa connected with them do not come to this person." We have already explained this meaning. If one is beyond the three gunas, and if both mind and intellect have come to rest in Him, and if one has forgotten the body and has no deliberate action of one's own, then, as it is said, "He whose thought is not 'I am the doer,' whose intellect is not tainted," those acts do not come to him. But mere knowledge of this exists in all who have the latent impression of Vedanta-knowledge. Only if practice corresponding to it exists can the fruit be experienced. Ravana too had this knowledge, yet in the Itihasas we see him continuing in acts such as harming others, taking another's wife, seizing another's wealth, eating what must not be eaten, and conduct that must not be followed.

Only the paramaikantin who possesses the marks of being Bhagavan's body - existing solely for Him, being wholly dependent on Him, and being sustained by Him alone - is spoken of as His body in this special sense. One whose mind is polluted by grief and other defects does not become His body in that sense. The paramaikantin who is His body regards even heavenly enjoyments such as Indra's lordship, and even the joy of self-experience, as equal to hell. He does not, like fools, become attached to the body, which is full of impurity, crowded with worms, naturally foul-smelling, impure, unstable, and made of urine and feces. He does not perform the prohibited acts that even samsarins consider rejectable.

Therefore it is said that even the Vaishnava must abandon doing what should not be done, because it contradicts the sastra; the ekantin abandons it because it transgresses Bhagavan's command; and the paramaikantin abandons it because it is tasteless and wholly unfit. For the final adhikari, since taste for the prapyam exists without obstruction, he does not give it up merely out of fear; he gives it up because he has no taste for it. Only in the acts of such an adhikari does He regard the act as His own. If this were not the meaning, then since all conscious and unconscious beings are His body, all their acts too would become His acts. If one says, "But these people have knowledge," then even the wicked acts of Ravana and others, who possessed Vedanta-knowledge, would become His. Therefore mere sambandha is not enough; mere knowledge is not enough; practice corresponding to that knowledge must arise.

In the Charama Sloka, the adhikari's duty is firm determination in Him alone as the upaya, preceded by the abandonment of all dharmas. The removal of obstacles belongs to the function of the upaya. If one asks, "Even when this resolve exists, if arti is absent, why is he said to be unfit for attainment?" - the answer is: for upaya-adhikara, firm resolve is enough; but for upeya-adhikara, detachment from all worldly and otherworldly attainments is also necessary. Just as one abandons other means and takes hold of the supreme means, one must abandon other goals such as aisvarya and kaivalya and desire the final prapyam alone. Because this upaya is common even for other goals, mere resolve may exist even in those attached to other fruits. By resolve alone one does not become fit for Bhagavan's world. Alvar himself says that He will not show His realm except after inferior attachments have been abandoned. Therefore what is spoken there must be understood as upaya-adhikara alone.

If one asks, "Why is this not explicitly stated in that sloka?" - the first half of the sloka states the abandonment and acceptance required for the upaya; the second half states, as its fruit, the removal of obstacles to attainment. Though attainment itself is not stated there, it is understood from the statement "you shall attain Me" in the related teaching: "Set your mind on Me; be My devotee; sacrifice to Me; bow to Me; you shall attain Me alone; truly I promise you, for you are dear to Me." There, He teaches: "If you are one whose mind is set on Me alone, one joined with love for Me alone, one devoted to My service, and one who has Me alone as refuge, then you need not do anything by yourself for the removal of your sins; you shall attain Me." Thus the two slokas are mutually dependent and have one meaning.

The teaching "Abandon all dharmas and take refuge in Me alone" states the abandonment of other means required for the upaya-qualification that was implicit in the previous sloka. And the statement "I shall free you from all sins" states the removal of obstacles to attainment required for the fruit expressed there as "you shall attain Me." Therefore the two slokas are explanation and explained, having one meaning. Since the fruit in the earlier sloka is the fruit here also, the qualification for the goal taught there must be brought into the meaning here.

Moreover, for upaya-adhikara, firm resolve alone is not enough; abandonment of pratikulya is also necessary. In the places where the nature of prapatti is stated, it is said: "Free from upaya and apaya, remain in the middle," and, "When upaya and apaya are joined, one's nishtha is diminished." After abandoning these and accepting Him, if one conducts oneself without falling from upaya-adhikara until the time of attainment, then, at the final moment, He removes obstacles in accordance with that adhikara and makes the person liberated. Alvar says that He grants the blissful state to the one who stands without confusion.

What does it mean that He removes obstacles according to the adhikara? By grace He allows the already-accepted prarabdha karma to be experienced in such a way that detachment from prakrta and aprakrta enjoyments is produced. He causes all previous karmas other than that to be burned away. He forgives later faults that arise through negligence and prevents them from clinging to the person. In deliberate acts, He may make the punishment light, like the offence of a prince; if the offence is heavy, He gives the appropriate punishments and removes it.

If one asks, "When He Himself has cast His glance and this person has performed upaya-varana, if some lapses arise through prakrti-vasana, will He make him an object of lila?" - even if He cannot let the matter fail entirely, if there is room in this person for an offence in the form of harmful conduct, there is no defect in His using him in lila for some time. The independent Lord does not let go even when He obtains occasion to employ those who are otherwise qualified and free from offence. This meaning is seen in the case of Tondaradippodi Alvar, who sang, "Removing my bonds, taking me wholly for Yourself, You performed mayas and set them before me - what bewilderments are these?"

The meaning is this. A king may greatly value a courtesan skilled in dance and song. Until the proper time comes, he may employ her in dance and song. If she is very delicate, he brings her gradually; if she is strong in body, he employs her in play as long as she herself desires, and afterward grants favor. Similarly, Sarvesvara, if persons are unfit for the journey of the world beyond and are not distressed, may sometimes grant grace immediately; or, if they are satisfied with this world, He may use them in lila for some time and accept them when they become distressed.

One may ask: "But is it not said that even for those who are satisfied, another body is prevented?"

That statement means this: although, at present, because of long association with the body, they may seem satisfied and may endure the prarabdha body, at the final time Sarvesvara will make them arta - distressed for release - and will accept them. The one called "satisfied" here is not someone truly content with worldly life. For this adhikari, the only remaining defect is that he is still joined to this body. The other qualities - renunciation, knowledge, and the rest - are common to him and to the one already distressed.

Such a person cries out in the manner of the Alvars: "How long shall I remain here and lament? Has the time for You to call and take me not yet come? What are You doing to me, O lotus-eyed one? Will You show me another path and remove me? Will You show me many things and lay me low? Shall I wander in this world melting like wax joined to fire?" He becomes like the heroine of the prabandham who knows neither night nor day nor sleep; who stands helpless, wherever she is placed; who joins her hands toward every direction; who weeps, bows, dances, sings, and cries aloud. He loses taste for food and sleep; his eyes fill with tears; his hands and feet cannot move properly; he faints, becomes clear again, cries, bows, dances, trembles, and laments, "Seeking everywhere, I do not find Him." This alone is the excellence proper to the arta.

For both kinds of adhikari, these are common: removal of ahankara, mamakara, desire, anger, greed, delusion, and the rest; detachment from wife, sons, house, field, relatives, and so on; and the firm resolve that Kannan alone is support, nourishment, and enjoyment. Therefore, even if one remains joined to the body for a few days because of long bodily association, at the final time He produces distaste for all these things, makes that person distressed, and accepts him. Seeing that arti, the person himself gains confidence, "This is the final body," and others too accept it. But if, even then, there remains dependence upon sons, servants, and such connections, then it is clear both to himself and to others that Sarvesvara has not yet resolved to accept this self quickly; according to the word of our acarya, attainment of another body will not be avoided. This, he declared, is the supreme meaning.

Hearing this, Senapati Alvan too became distressed and said: "Who are fit for this arti? Who can abandon the things that must be abandoned?" Like a tree whose root has been cut, he fell at Appa's feet and began to weep. Appa lifted him, embraced him, wiped away his tears, and said: "Do not grieve. If such helplessness and burning distress arise toward unattained Bhagavad-experience, then Sarvesvara Himself will produce all that is needed and will accept the person Himself."

"Even if you are not yet able to abandon all the things that ought to be abandoned, you must at least know them as rejectable. You must abandon the prohibited things that certainly must be abandoned: ahankara, mamakara, desire, anger, offences against Bhagavan, offences against bhagavatas, offences against the acarya, and the like. As far as needed for the protection of the world and for the uplifting of disciples and sons, you may perform varnasrama-dharmas out of compassion, without being a slave to arrogance, wealth, and desire. Associate with those supremely distinctive persons who are complete in knowledge, devotion, and detachment. Spend your time continually in experience of His qualities and in service. Cut off association with bound samsarins. Know acarya-abhimana alone as the uplifter. Understand that all these qualities of the self have arisen only through his grace, as in the words, 'You made me good; You gave me the great excellence of joining and worshipping.' If one remains without ahankara in this way, then the all-knowing Lord will, at the final stage, produce paramartha-nishtha and protect him. Do not think this is rare."

No self-quality can be produced by this person, who is subject to prakrti. Once He thinks, "I must uplift this self," then this person becomes liberated. When He is bringing him to liberation, since it belongs to Him to produce the qualities of the self corresponding to that liberation, there is no difficulty. Did He not produce, in all the moving and unmoving beings of Ayodhya, the self-qualities by which their pleasure and pain depended solely on union with and separation from Him? Then what difficulty is there in producing them in a conscious being who has placed his burden upon the all-knowing Lord?

But if this person remains subject to prakrti, identifies the body as the self, fattens the body with curd, ghee, and other things, does not join the sattvika assembly, remains a bound samsarin, inclines toward enjoyed objects, and lives under the sway of ahankara and the rest, then He will simply conduct him according to karma, thinking, "They have obtained what suits them." But if one becomes truly dependent, knows what is to be abandoned and what is to be accepted, understands that the abandonment and acceptance of these are necessary for himself, and laments: "These small five senses, if they obtain me here on earth, what will they not do to me, even though I am one who would serve the Lord of those in heaven? I have no place; I have no support; I cannot conquer the five senses; I have not even served by plucking flowers each day at the proper time; You alone must join me to Yourself" - then the Lord, affectionate even toward the lowly, will not abandon him.

Alvar says that the Lord does not allow fierce karma, like a strong tiger, to devour His servants. He will not let them remain experiencing sense-objects through the five senses. Those who become recipients of His grace perform good acts and become liberated; they do not remain inclined toward sense-objects. He Himself performs this work, and nothing is difficult for Him. Standing within the heart, through the practice of the former good conduct and through the meaning of the gracious utterances, He produces clarity; through knowledge and devotion He removes the darkness born of ignorance, makes the person proceed in proper practice, and protects him. There is no doubt about this. If this conscious being abandons pratikulyas and has even the mere resolve toward what is favorable, He Himself produces all these things and carries the matter through.

Therefore, one must not merely recite praise-passages about the greatness of refuge - "Even if there is some defect in the one who has sought refuge, I shall not abandon him," and "Whoever he may be, even if sinful, by taking refuge attains liberation" - without understanding their purport. One must not remain a bound samsarin, attached to sinful objects and ruled by ahankara, while citing those statements. One must not be deluded by the words of those who do not know the conduct previously practiced by all the acaryas. One must keep in the heart the excellence of the former teachers.

The earlier acaryas, seeing the danger of being shown many other objects, prayed for removal from them. They knew the spotless good conduct practiced by Parakala and the other elders, so that such things might not enter even through negligence. Those who say, "I have Bhagavat-sambandha; I have sadacarya-sambandha," yet do not abandon pratikulyas, become fearless themselves and teach the same fearlessness to others, thereby ruining others too. Do not mix with such destroyers of themselves and others; do not listen to their instruction and perish. Instead, take the words and practices of Nathamuni, Yamunacarya, Yativara, and the other elders, and practice accordingly. Teach others only the right practice that begins with the abandonment of prohibited conduct that obstructs the prapyam, and thereby carry them across.

Nammalvar possessed knowledge of past, future, and present matters concerning Bhagavad-vishaya. Seeing that, in the strength of Kali, teachers of contrary meaning and practitioners of contrary conduct would become powerful, he wished that they should not enter the Vishnu-siddhanta. Therefore, looking toward the future avatara of Emperumanar, he performed mangalasasana, saying, "May it flourish, may it flourish, may it flourish." Saying, "O servants, rejoice," he pointed to him as the one to be taken as refuge by all mumukshus. He cast a special glance upon Madhurakavi, Nathamuni, and others, so that they might come to see that avatara. He showed that Emperumanar would be both servant to us and the one responsible for our welfare; that this very avatara would uplift those before and after; and that he is the one anointed with the status of guru for all our refuges.

Therefore, from Nathamuni down to our own acaryas, Emperumanar is to be accepted as the supreme saving acarya; his words and practices are to be accepted as pramana. The true acarya is the one who shows him as the one who uplifts those before and after, and who is himself joined with good conduct. Such an acarya is to be understood as the acarya who does the greatest good, and he should instruct others also in this way. If the word "guru" is applied to some other matter in this same sense, many contrary meanings may enter through ambiguity. Since the future fault was foreseen, other meanings are rejected; the words and practices of those elders, as preserved in the texts, alone are to be accepted.

Therefore, the acarya who teaches, "The holy feet of Emperumanar alone are refuge," and who himself remains sheltered in that acarya-abhimana, should be thought of as everything: mother, father, wife, worldly and otherworldly good, guru, Vaikuntha, Venkata, and all the rest. One should know Emperumanar, the uplifter of those before and after, as the saving acarya, and regard the prapatti he performed at the final time as the protector. One should pray: "O acarya, establish in me knowledge of truth and the nishtha corresponding to it." Then, gradually, one should practice the good conduct taught there, live with paramarthikas endowed with that conduct, and bring that conduct into one's own life.

One should fear ahankara as one fears the touch of fire; fear contact with sense-objects as one fears poison; fear bhagavata-apacara as one fears a burning brand that reduces everything to ashes; fear the fault of seeing others' defects. One should be compassionate toward all beings, perform prescribed duties only so far as needed for the protection of the world, maintain rules concerning food, conduct, and association, give up attachment to the body, wife, sons, and the like, and, if a bodily disturbance arises, stop it only in a sastra-allowed way, without the thought of pleasure or dharma-merit, treating it merely like a bodily discharge. Such a one, living in this manner, becomes most dear to Bhagavan; by His grace, at the final time, he obtains extreme arti and becomes filled with bliss in the land that has no defect. As the Lord says: "Declare it, O Kaunteya: My devotee does not perish."

Hearing this truth, they were satisfied and stood with their staves and water-pots. Then Appa said: "There is still one more supreme meaning that I must tell you. Even if all these qualities of the self have arisen, if even the slightest thought appears in one's heart, 'These qualities are present in me,' then, like the sacrifices, gifts, and austerities of Nahusha, Nrga, Yayati, and the others, they become useless, and one becomes an object of Bhagavan's displeasure. Therefore one must regard oneself as lower even than eternal samsarins and live without ahankara. This is what all our elders constantly contemplated in passages such as 'I am low; I have no good at all,' 'I am beyond the boundary,' 'When You are joined to me, the lowest,' and 'I am the foremost among sinners.' Although it is proper to regard oneself as lower than samsarins in this way, if one becomes arrogant toward a bhagavata, thinking, 'I am equal to him' or 'I am greater than he,' there is no need even to say that this destroys the self."

If, while it is proper for one to regard oneself as lower than even samsarins, one becomes arrogant toward a bhagavata, thinking, "I am equal to him," or "I am greater than he," there is no need even to say that this destroys the self.

Then the disciple asked: "Svami, please graciously explain the order of conduct toward bhagavatas." Appa replied: "Nampillai used to say that this is very clear. They are of three kinds: satkara-yogya, those fit to be honored; sahavasa-yogya, those fit for association; and sadanubhava-yogya, those fit for constant experience."

The satkara-yogyas are those in whom the Lord's name and marks are prominent. As the verse says, those who wear tulasi garlands, lotus-seed rosaries, the conch and discus marks on their shoulders, and the bright urdhva-pundra on their foreheads are Vaishnavas who purify the world. Therefore, whatever their birth or conduct may be, one must not dishonor them. According to one's capacity, one should render some small service, and just as one fears committing offence against the image of Bhagavan, one should fear offence in this matter too. One should act toward them with arghya, padya, and other honors, so that their holy mind becomes pleased.

If one dishonors them, then, as it is said, one who reviles My devotee, even if that devotee is a dog-eater, does not reach Me even in many crores of births. And as another text says, one who reviles My devotee - even if that devotee is of improper conduct, bad conduct, or unknown lineage - becomes a true candala. Just as, for a brahmana, merely wearing the sacred thread is enough for brahma-hatya to attach to the killer, so too, whatever the birth and conduct of these persons may be, their Vaishnava marks alone are enough for offence to arise if they are despised. Therefore one must not dishonor them. One should gladden them through small honors and gentle, pleasing words, and conduct oneself carefully. But they are not thereby fit for intimate association or constant experience.

The sahavasa-yogyas are those in whom knowledge is prominent. As the cited verse says, they are those joined with Nampillai's lineage, established in the final means, having understood the supreme meaning, without desire for wealth and pleasure, acting for the good of the world, having conquered anger and greed, and serving Varavaramuni. These are complete in discriminative knowledge of what is to be abandoned and what is to be accepted. They abandon the things to be abandoned, hold to what is to be accepted, and are established in the final means. They are fit for association, and fit for mutually instructing one another, as in "instructing one another," so that impurity of mind caused by contact with prakrti may not arise.

The sadanubhava-yogyas are those in whom practice is prominent. Who are they? They are those in whom disgust has arisen toward this lila-vibhuti, which is full of things to be rejected; those who, through the inability to attain Bhagavad-experience, have become paramarthikas; those who, like persons possessed by a spirit beyond this world, forget their own bodies and cannot remain where they are; those who cannot even bear to see samsarins; those for whom living among such people is extremely unbearable, like being shut inside a cage of blazing fire.

As the prabandhams say, they are sleepless night and day; their tears pour out from their eyes; they call out, "O jewel-hued Lord\!"; they cry, bow, dance, seek to see Him, sing, and wail. Looking at the people nearby, they heave hot breaths, their bodies weaken, and they dance while worldly people laugh. Thus they go about day and night, with eyes filled with tears, abandoning shame, uttering the divine names, crying, bowing, fainting, singing, becoming bewildered, and doing acts of worship. Worldly people mock them as mad. Yet they are equal in pleasure and pain, blame and praise, mother and friend, clod and gold; they understand the whole universe as having Bhagavan as its self, and they are free from opposition toward all things.

Because of their longing to see Him, they treat even the great attainments such as anima and mukti as straw. As the verse says, after beholding such mahatmas who long to see His form even once, Bhagavan Himself cannot bear separation from them even for a moment. Such mahatmas are exceedingly dear to Him. Persons of this kind are the ones fit to be chosen as acaryas, fit for the acceptance of their tirtha and prasada, and fit to be trusted as able to carry conscious beings across.

Among these three, the first category do not, merely by that status, carry one across in this birth. Their qualification is that they are capable of causing forgiveness for those who have committed offence against them. The second category, being fit for association, can assist another through the illumination of knowledge, and, through the power of their own acarya-lineage, can cross themselves; that is their excellence. The third category is supremely dear to Sarvesvara like Piratti, brings Sarvesvara under control as though by the movement of the eyebrow, and can uplift even those connected with them, as is said in the verses about seven generations above and below becoming pure and gaining the life that belongs to them. Such a one is the true master.

Why does one perform acarya-varana and accept tirtha and prasada? It is so that, by that connection, one's avidya, karma, vasana, and taste for them may be forgiven, and that one may be saved. Those who themselves desire ahankara and sense-objects and are perishing, and those who, like the shade of a palmyra tree, are themselves without refuge and unable to save themselves, cannot make this person cross over or cause the fruit to ripen for him. Therefore connection with an unqualified person is declared to be a cause of destruction, like entering a cracked boat or a leaking vessel.

It is said that receiving tirtha, prasada, and bodily contact from those whose knowledge of Bhagavad-seshatva, taught by the pranava, has been damaged by ahankara; from those established in other means, who do not have the renunciation of other means taught by the middle word; and from those devoted to other goals, who are attached to wife, sons, and similar objects and remain bound in samsara though they lack the knowledge of ananya-bhogyatva taught by the final word - all these become causes for dullness of knowledge.

One may ask: "If we avoid these, will ahankara not arise in us, and will their holy minds not be troubled, so that fault comes from that?" Fault arises only if one thinks, "There is defect in them, and there is superior virtue in me." Without thinking so, one should think: "What can they do? This is the power of avidya." According to what is beneficial for oneself, one should honor them without attributing the defect to them and without reviling them. If one behaves humbly with pranipata and the like, no fault arises. If one regards oneself as lower than samsarins and thinks that whatever virtues one has are only "His causeless grace," then ahankara will not arise.

If one says, "Their minds will be troubled, so we must act to satisfy them," then, if they order one to perform some prohibited act, one would have to do that also. That cannot be. Therefore one should not act merely because their mind may be disturbed. What is required toward them is only to conduct oneself so that no opposition arises from oneself toward them. In the case of the sahavasa-yogya, accepting tirtha and prasada has neither special merit nor fault. In the case of the final adhikari, acceptance itself becomes the cause of uplift. For this there is no rule based on varna, asrama, or corresponding conduct; there is only the rule of adhikara.

This is the meaning of Alvar's words: "Whoever they may be, if they possess the wealth of habitual devotion," and, "If those who say, 'O faultless one, O imperishable one, O Lord of the celestials,' give the remainder of food they have eaten, is it not pure?" Whatever their low birth or conduct may be, if they have connection with Bhagavad-experience and are qualified with respect to the upaya, they are our masters and fit for the acceptance of tirtha and the like.

If one does not understand the distinction just stated, and instead says, "So long as they are bhagavatas bearing the names and forms, whatever condition they are in, they are to be accepted," then the instruction of the elders becomes useless. For the elders said: "O mind, reflect well: leave aside the fools mentioned before and after, and daily follow the one spoken of in the middle." If all who bear the form are equally to be accepted in every way, this warning would have no purpose.

The saying that even a mleccha, if he is a devotee, is a bhagavata, is meant to establish acceptance of the distinctive sambandha of bhagavatas, in obedience to Bhagavan's command, for the good of the world. But no earlier acarya, whether in one place or another, ever said that, for the removal of ahankara, or for the abandonment of all dharmas, or because of the marks of bhagavata-sambandha, one should accept prohibited conduct as desirable.

One might say: "Even if they did not say so explicitly, if there is no fault in it, could it not be accepted as useful for removing ahankara and for practicing extraordinary conduct?" The answer is no. In prabandhas and divine sastras, acts such as doing what should not be done are repeatedly condemned. Therefore such prohibited acts must be regarded as the play of Bhagavan's maya, not as sadacara. One must not call them authoritative conduct. If a statement in some text teaches a supreme meaning, one may gather its necessary limbs and auxiliaries through tradition and reasoning; but one must not therefore take all things condemned by sastra and by the sistas as though they were received through lineage and fit to be practiced.

By sastra one must understand that things born of rajas and tamas, food-defects opposed to knowledge, things opposed to knowledge of artha-pancaka, substances defective by kind, things touched by prakrti, things utterly to be rejected, and things rejected even by worldly decent people, are not to be accepted under another name. To say that such prohibited substances can become acceptable through some different interpretation is only a display meant for people whose nature is impure; it is not suitable for the distinctive jnanins who are under the control of Bhagavan.

Some may create an opening for this through external doctrines. They say: "The sastra commands the cessation of self-activity that opposes Bhagavan's activity. Therefore even abandoning prohibited acts would be self-effort, and so it too must be abandoned." Without acceptance by sastra, without the utterance of Bhagavan, without the practice of the elders, such people, like Buddhists and other outsiders, speak contrary meanings from their own minds. They show weak perceptions produced by Bhagavan's maya and bewilder others. The words and actions of such unauthoritative people cannot be accepted as pramana by those who desire liberation.

One may object: "But they are not inventing conduct independently. They accept the conduct of the great as pramana. It is said, 'Whatever the best person does, others follow.' It is also said that by trust in one's own acarya, one should act according to his command. If so, will acting according to such an acarya's command not save?"

The answer is this. To accept one's own acarya's command means accepting the command of one who is marked by good qualities, one who holds sastra in his hand, one who is dependent on pramana, and one who has carefully examined the matter according to divine sastras such as Srivacanabhushana. It does not mean accepting one who speaks what appears in his own mind and calls it "pure instruction." If he invents contrary meanings and, as in the prabandham's warning about those who say "this is the medicine for the afflicted girl," secretly teaches that prohibited conduct itself is the siddhopaya, then those who follow him fall by the blind-leading-the-blind rule. Entering such a teacher's path is like entering a cracked boat; both teacher and disciple drown. Therefore such association is to be completely avoided.

The acarya is not one overcome by ahankara, wealth, and desire. He is one who desires the restraint and welfare of the world. As the cited sastra says, he takes a mortal form, carries sastra in hand, and out of compassion uplifts the world. Since sastra itself is of many kinds, one must abandon what is non-essential and accept the essence, just as one takes nectar from the ocean. Leaving aside the external sastras without essence, the earlier portion that bears only lesser fruit, and whatever is not the highest essence, he instructs the great Tirumantra, which removes samsara and is the essence of the Vishnu-gayatri; he prescribes conduct corresponding to it; and he himself practices without deviating from what the Alvars and acaryas taught in their texts and lived in their conduct. Only then does liberation arise for both teacher and disciple. Accan Pillai therefore said that guru, mantra, and deity connected with poison are to be abandoned.

When reality is this, one cannot merely say, "The acarya is the one who gives the root-mantra that removes samsara," and stop there. If such a teacher himself is sunk in the ocean of samsara, unable to cross over, and if he makes the person who has taken hold of him into a bound samsarin, then the connection is useless. If, saying "we have acarya-sambandha," he removes fear, shame, and restraint even while sin is being committed, and thus destroys even the little life of discipline that remained, his words become laughable and fruitless, like the talk of chastity spoken by an unchaste woman.

One may say: "Even if there is no affection for such a teacher, will not firm trust itself give fruit?" But such trust exists even among external, non-Vedic sects. They too have deep faith in the guru's path, practice the mixture of knowledge and action taught by him, and firmly resolve that moksha will not fail. Yet supreme moksha cannot be established on that basis. For one who depends on pramana, the acarya must be a knower of sastra, complete in knowledge and practice, and established in true sampradaya. Outsiders have no dependence on valid pramana; therefore their acarya-nishtha may be intense, but it does not establish attainment of the supreme purushartha.

Even in the matters of guru, mantra, and deity, great attachment without true trust is ineffective; but false trust does not make a false object true. If someone mistakes a shell for silver and firmly trusts it to be silver, that does not make it silver. In the same way, firm trust in a wrong teacher or wrong meaning cannot become saving truth.

One may ask: "If the acarya is a jnanin, very dear to Bhagavan, and if, though some lapses arise on the disciple's side, there is well-being on the acarya's side, will not Bhagavan consider the matter and will not that acarya keep the disciple behind him and protect him?" The answer is: the acarya's task is precisely to discipline him so that such lapses do not arise, to make him fit for the world and for Bhagavan, and then to protect him. One must not think: "Even if the disciple remains undiscerning, the acarya can save him merely because there is affection." The acarya is called the helmsman only if he is firmly connected with the boat that goes toward Vishnu. If this person lets go of that support, falls into the unfathomable ocean of samsara, and cannot remain inactive, then only the omnipotent Lord can rescue him.

Alvar cries out in this sense: "Calling and calling, I, the sinner, have long wandered, having lost the path, while cruel karma blocks me. O Lord who protected the cows, who measured all the worlds - how shall I attain You now?" The meaning is: "Even if You, omnipotent Lord, through forms such as Krishna and Vamana, made divine service easy, gave beneficial instruction through sastra, and stretched out Your hand to take me, because I stood among the thorns of evil karma for a long time, crying out, I, the sinner, lost the path and wandered. If I have failed even in relation to such an all-powerful Lord, how shall I attain You now?" Thus it is not possible to be saved by an acarya who does not truly correct.

That same Sarvesvara incarnated as Rama, Krishna, and so forth and gave much beneficial instruction. Yet, in order to make adhikaris think, "This is not for me, because I am unlike the Lord," and in order to correct them through those who are of their own kind, He caused Alvars and acaryas to incarnate. They too correct well and then accept service. Only if the acarya is capable of correcting in that way does the disciple become liberated. Since this is the real state of the matter, one cannot say that non-distinctive teachers can save. Emperumanar labored hard and disciplined persons such as Arangamaligai. Even Emperumanar and the other great ones, who made children, the mute, the dull, the blind, the lame, animals, and immovable beings fit for liberation, found it difficult to save conscious beings in whom pratikulya was present. How then can ordinary teachers save them? That would be only name and imagination.

Sarvesvara alone is completely independent, omnipotent, delighted in lila, and supremely compassionate. He alone is the final cause of creation, preservation, destruction, samsara, and liberation. Even though He entrusts protection to another, creation to another, and destruction to another, He Himself remains the true agent. Just as He creates four-faced Brahma, enters him as inner Self, and performs creation through him; just as He creates Rudra and, entering him as inner Self, performs destruction through him; so too, seeing conscious beings afflicted by sorrow, He creates wooden images, as it were, bearing the name "acarya." Like placing gold plating on wood, He adorns them with the Vaishnava ornaments called knowledge and practice, stands within them as inner controller, and through them reforms conscious beings and protects them. Therefore there is no separate, independent principle called "acarya" apart from Him.

From the surface view, creation and destruction seem to belong to those particular agents. But when examined deeply, it is as the sastra says: Vishnu is the inner Self of all, and all power belongs to Him. In the same way, in the matter of liberation too, He takes Alvar as His divine body, possessing the marks of being supported, ruled, and nourished by Him, and takes Emperumanar, the abode of kshama and other qualities, as His holy feet; assuming the avatara of the acarya, He Himself carries out the whole work. His wish is everyone's wish; all others are eternally dependent on Him. Without His thought, not even a blade of grass can move.

The acarya's beneficial instruction is not an independent achievement added from outside. First, the Lord gives the instruments of body and senses, produces the capacities for action and restraint, provides Veda and sastra so that one may not go astray, incarnates as Rama, Krishna, and others and gives instruction. When, because of their own slight understanding, people still do not enter the supreme good, His compassion overflows. He waits for a moment when His own lila-ordinance can be transcended by grace; then He casts a special glance, produces favorable impressions, brings about the rise of sattva, creates desire for moksha, adorns someone as "acarya," produces in this person qualities such as sama and dama that are useful for taking refuge in that acarya, and creates taste for joining him. Only after that does the acarya give beneficial instruction. After instruction, the Lord Himself, standing in the heart, prevents it from becoming fruitless, produces self-qualities, creates detachment from what is other than the Supreme Self, makes love for His own matter swell like an ocean, makes the person a paramarthika, grants grace so that all obstacles to attainment are removed, and carries the chain of results all the way to release from prarabdha and the attainment of eternal service. These are His extraordinary acts.

Even though this is the ultimate truth, the first help that appears in this birth for the uplift of this beginningless samsarin is the help of one's own acarya. Isvara is present; Piratti is present; the Alvars and all the acaryas have appeared for the protection of the self. Still, in this birth, the actual benefit arose for this person through his own acarya-sambandha. Therefore gratitude begins toward one's own acarya. But when the final ground of that help is examined, it ends in the Lord, who is the cause of everything. For this reason the elders said: "The first step in remembrance of help is gratitude toward the acarya; the final resting place is gratitude toward Emperuman." Since even the state of being acarya belongs to Him, there is no contradiction in saying, for the fully qualified one, "The acarya is the supreme Brahman," "the guru himself is the supreme Brahman," "the guru is the visible deity," "he is all good in this world and the next," and "he is mother, father, and beloved."

Therefore, in order that contrary practices may not enter, all the earlier acaryas contemplated and taught the inimitable acarya-status of Emperumanar. Their intention was this: people should not hear the contrary meanings spoken by their own teachers under the influence of maya and proceed wrongly; instead, all should know that Emperumanar's own words and conduct alone are to be taken as the aim, and should be saved. Therefore, if someone practices something and says, "This is the practice taught by Emperumanar," one should not merely listen. One should examine the texts, beginning with the Guruparamparaprabhavam, determine the meaning carefully, and accept only the proper conduct of those supreme Vaidikas whose conduct does not contradict the words and practices of the former gurus.

Nammalvar, who received faultless knowledge, established the authority of the Veda in many places; he explained, through Dravida speech, the Vedanta statements such as "All this indeed is Brahman," "There is no multiplicity here,"

"Narayana alone was," and "Neither Brahma nor Isana existed." Through his own prabandham he revealed all the special meanings that mumukshus need to know. Therefore, he is the foremost among the supreme Vaidikas, the root-standing point of prapannas, and the leader of all good practice. Thus, if even highly learned seekers teach or act contrary to Veda and Vedanta, they become fit only to be objects of lila, not objects of grace. Even if they appear very sattvika and extremely learned, if they are connected with sinful acts, one should think, "Sarvesvara has regarded them as assistants in His lila," and stay away from them.

Sarvesvara is independent, supremely compassionate, and Lord of the lila-world. Sometimes He is full of compassion and makes certain persons liberated by Himself; sometimes He produces distinctive sambandha in certain souls and uplifts others through them. When, through the force of Kali, Vedic and Vaidika boundaries are transgressed and all begin to move toward nastikatva, He, out of compassion, places Parankusa, Parakala, Yativara, and others as His glance, and tries to correct the world through them. When the defect of Kali is removed, then everywhere one looks there are Vaishnavas; by the abhimana of the great, even those without the full set of qualifications become fit for liberation.

But He also preserves the order of lila. If merely by the removal of pratikulya, and merely through the abhimana of the great, children, the mute, the dull, the blind, the lame, and even immovable beings all became liberated, then the boundaries of sastra would collapse and the lila-vibhuti would be diminished. Therefore, unless prohibited conduct exists, there is no occasion for using someone in lila. In former times, when the asuras and rakshasas had Vedic rites and Vaidika forms but were hostile to the devas and to the good, He Himself, taking the guise of the Buddha and other forms, deluded them, removed their Vedic faith, made them established in contrary meanings, and set them aside. Likewise He caused Brhaspati and others to become teachers of false meanings for those who were fit to be deluded. He created many external doctrines and false views, and through persons fit for each guna caused those doctrines to spread, so that asuric natures would be separated from His true matter.

So too, in this very tradition, some seek fame, gain, and worship; they bear the names and marks, but have no conduct corresponding to the svarupa. They do not abandon anything opposed to the svarupa; they are body-identifiers, seized by ahankara. They are like Paundraka Vasudeva, who put on Krishna's form and opposed Him; like Rahu and Ketu, who entered the line of the devas for the sake of nectar; like Suka and Sarana, who took monkey forms for their own purpose. When asuric natures, devoted to violence, taking others' wives, and stealing others' property, come mixed in this way, the Lord may cast His glance upon some as assistants in lila, show lower practices through them, make others follow prohibited conduct through them, and use them in lila. Those who depend on pramana must discern this clearly.

Even if Sarvesvara Himself, or Emperumanar himself, were to open the mouth and speak something, or command something in dreams, if that instruction contradicts pramana, one should think: "This is the maya-work of the Lord of countless wondrous mayas, just as Indra once came in disguise to disturb Ambarisha." One must not take unauthoritative acts as sistacara. Nor should one revile those persons. One should stand apart and flee, just as Alvar said, "See His maya; knowing it, run away." One should not blame the Lord's activity performed for lila; that too is not pleasing to Him who delights in lila. Instead, among trustworthy adhikaris who submit to pramana, one should speak this meaning and go on one's way.

There is an old account concerning this. In the time when Emperumanar was maintaining the darsana, some saw his greatness and, by the word of their mother and by Bhagavan's command, took refuge in him and entered the Srivaishnava siddhanta. Yet their followers could not bear that, and contrary teaching began to arise in this darsana. Our former acaryas accepted the distinctive sambandha of bhagavatas because of Bhagavan's command, but some made that very acceptance a pretext for mixing up all distinctions, and then misread the Charama Sloka's abandonment of all dharmas as though both injunction and prohibition were to be abandoned in the same way. They introduced prohibited practice and composed texts claiming that this was the final dharma. Because such teaching assisted people ruled by wealth and desire, it spread like wildfire.

Even when Emperumanar restrained such tendencies, the fault of Kali caused them to grow again. Some did not make them public, fearing the reproach of the sistas; they said only to those favorable to their own circle, "This is sistacara," and wandered as agents of that contrary teaching. Even in the time of Pillai Lokacarya, some raised contrary questions of the kind you have raised. Some knowers say that, for this reason, the divine sastra Srivacanabhushana arose by Bhagavan's command. The earlier teachers, in their prabandhams, often stressed the greatness of the refuge, thinking, "If we teach the full nature of the adhikari, no one will remain." Thus they created abundant trust in His qualities and showed the nature of the adhikari only briefly. Because people did not clearly understand the real requirement, they looked only at the greatness of the refuge, let their own svarupa perish, became bound to samsara, attached to wife and children, and inclined toward bad objects; except for names and marks, there was no distinction between them and ordinary samsarins. To refute such contrary practices, Srivacanabhushana was revealed. Thus, when it says, "I speak to you who desire to be saved," it teaches that those who desire salvation must be established in the nishtha taught by Srivacanabhushana and the other rahasya works.

One may object: "You say that even those who have taken refuge in Bhagavan may be unfit for close association. But did not Sarvesvara Himself say that one must have vatsalya toward My devotees, and regard their defects as enjoyable?"

The meaning of that is not that doing what ought not be done, failing to do what ought to be done, offences against Bhagavan, offences against bhagavatas, and other prohibited acts are to be enjoyed. The intended meaning is that one must not revile them; if one reviles them, one becomes fit for punishment. "Taking as enjoyable" means this: just as a cow bears with the calf that kicks her, pushes her with its head, or bites her with its teeth, because the calf lives by her; so too, if a bhagavata insults oneself or speaks harsh words, one must bear it.

Or again: in worldly love, if a beloved woman in the privacy of the bed kicks, pinches, bites, speaks harsh words, or does many acts of contempt, the lover does not proclaim these to others; he regards them as supremely enjoyable. In the same way, if one is a bhagavata, then even if one has committed no offence, and others speak against him, and on hearing that, others abuse, threaten, strike, or insult him with harsh words, he should regard it as something beneficial to himself. He should not proclaim it before the all-knowing Bhagavan and bhagavatas. Instead, he should say that he cannot live apart from them, grieve that their holy minds have become clouded, blame his own ancient karma, and conduct himself as Nampillai did when he was insulted without cause. Not merely should he avoid retaliation; he should regard such treatment as enjoyable. This alone is what is meant by the mark of being His devotee.

It does not mean this: that if people knowingly do what ought not be done, dwell in the houses of courtesans, insult others for the sake of wealth and pleasure, live by unworthy means, speak dry and pleasing words such as "this alone will make us yours," refuse even a small offering, do not make one fit for even a single service, and yet distribute sripada-tirtha and prasada, one must accept them and behave according to their pleasure. The Gita and other sastras say that even if one was previously of bad conduct, if later one becomes ananya-bhak, exclusively devoted to Him, he is to be honored. This must be understood in that way. If this were not the meaning, then the acaryas who knew Bhagavan's intention would not have rejected nastikas and astika-nastikas as fools, and would not have said that only the astika who knows the essence of the sastra is to be honored.

Therefore, even if such people bear the names and forms, one should not revile their conduct; if they commit a fault against oneself, one should take it as something to be borne and even as beneficial. That alone is Bhagavan's intention. But if one says that the prohibited acts they perform are themselves enjoyable, and that those very acts should be contemplated as one's protector, then what is the meaning of Nammalvar repeatedly saying, "Abandon, O servants; after abandoning, worship," "Do not value the baseness of young women," "Do not increase evil by your strength," "Think 'alas\!' and do no low act," "Do not let fierce karma surround you," and "Do not cheat or gamble"? Why did he cry out, "Uproot them from the root," and "There is no happiness here, alas\!"? He was lamenting precisely because one must not sink into the region of doing what ought not be done.

If one asks whether this cry was directed only to ordinary samsarins and not to Vaishnavas, the answer is in the phrase "O servants." He speaks to those who have eagerness to obtain Bhagavad-vishaya: "Abandon; after abandoning, worship." Do not think, "Let us worship first and abandon gradually." One must certainly abandon and then worship. The word "abandon" shows that detachment from other objects itself is required. If one worships after such abandonment, He removes the powerful karmas that stand in the middle and obstruct Bhagavat-prapti, and grants moksha, marked by non-return.

One may object: "But did He not say, 'If they have done it, they have done well'?" The answer is this. When Piratti intercedes, she does not make the fault into something to be charged against the devotee. The words "they do not do it," and "if they have done it," themselves show that the chaste Mother does not cause faults to be spoken of, and that the adhikari whom she recommends does not perform what ought not be done. The statement only reveals the greatness of refuge: even if impossible contradictions somehow occur, He does not give him up. First He says, "My servants do not do that," thereby defining their nature. Then He says, "If they do it, they have done well," thereby showing His own nature. Not doing such acts is their svarupa; not abandoning them even if they do them is His svarupa. The statement "they did well" is only His expression of partiality toward them, so that others may not speak fault against them. If a fault exists, He Himself bears it like the offence of a prince; it is not for others to speak of it.

If this is not the meaning, then there is no reason for the mantra, His own statements, and the words of the Alvars to prescribe that one must abandon other deities, other means, and other objects and then take hold of Him.

One may ask: "Why do you especially take Alvar as trustworthy and the Tiruvaymoli as pramana?" The answer is this. Bhagavan took Alvar as an especially desired body, even more intimate than His divine arca-body. In him there was no mixture of delight in lila and dependent mercy in the ordinary way; rather, by sheer compassion, holding sastra in hand, he appeared for the uplift of the people sunk in samsara. Unlike the avataras that speak falsehoods for the delusion of tamasic persons, he mingled without falsehood. Bhagavan did not cause Vyasa, Parasara, the earlier Alvars, and others to sing this matter; instead, He made Himself sing Himself through Nammalvar, by the Tiruvaymoli, the king of prabandhams, the essence of all the Upanishads, like the Saman born from the Chandogya.

This Alvar was not one sustained, nourished, and delighted by prakrta things as others are. For him, "the rice eaten, the water drunk, and the betel chewed" were all Kannan. From childhood itself, beginning with the mother's milk, he was indifferent to other support; he was solely attached to Him. He was free from the defects of exhaustion, delusion, negligence, and confusion that arise through connection with prakrti. All the other Alvars were like limbs of him. Therefore his words and conduct are to be accepted as pramana. This is the purport.

Now, as for the svarupa of the acarya, the sastras say, "Regard the acarya as deity," "the acarya is directly Narayana,"

"the acarya is Hari Himself," and "the guru alone is the Supreme Brahman." Some say that, for mumukshus, taking hold of Sarvesvara is like grasping His hand to have the work done, while taking hold of the acarya is like grasping His feet to have the work done. Thus he is spoken of both as one with Him and as a distinct helper for both sides. How can both be true?

The answer is this. One must not say that the acarya is merely Bhagavan's svarupa; nor should one say that he is merely the pure self's svarupa. He is something wondrous, like Narasimha, in whom the natures of man and lion are both joined. If one says he is merely Bhagavan's svarupa, then the whole guru-parampara would become one single entity, and the fault of identity of svarupa would arise. If one says he is merely the self's svarupa, many pramanas would be contradicted. Therefore, just as Balarama, though an avatara of Sesha, is included among Bhagavan's avataras because he is mixed with Bhagavat-svarupa, so too, when Bhagavan presides over the distinctive paramaikantika selves who possess the marks of being supported, controlled, and nourished by Him, the result is a distinctive Bhagavat-svarupa qualified by those distinctive selves. Like a heated iron ball, it shines outwardly with great brilliance. This is the Visishta Bhagavat-svarupa called acarya.

Jagadacarya taught that this is a distinctive avatara of Bhagavan, different even from the five forms. Because there is a relation of qualifier and qualified between Bhagavan's svarupa and the acarya-svarupa, there is both distinction and non-distinction: distinction through the qualifier, non-distinction through the qualified whole. This acaryatva exists uniquely in Alvar; and Emperumanar, who stands in the place of Alvar's feet, is therefore the acarya who uplifts all. This meaning is established by the intention of all the earlier gurus.

Since Alvar himself taught that this person is related to us and is responsible for our work, one should meditate on the forms of his being exclusively fit to be enjoyed by Him, supported by Him, and nourished by Him. One should pray: "In the Srivacanabhushana composed by the Jagadacarya, may the acarya establish in me knowledge of truth and firm nishtha." If one lacks strength for favorable conduct, one should grieve, "I have not even a single good quality," abandon all pratikulyas decisively, and remain with the conviction: "The holy feet of Emperumanar alone are refuge." One should associate with those most distinctive adhikaris who live in that way, spend time through the strength of the rahasya-meanings of Srivacanabhushana and the like, and, according to "Do not teach this to one without austerity," not reveal these supreme meanings to the unqualified. They should be spoken only to those of extreme purity. In this way, as the verse says, "Having taken refuge in Ramanuja, the directly manifest treasure of compassion, placing oneself beneath him, they go forth fearless."
